Humor tells determine fittedness

We can also think about fittedness with humor tells.

Humor tells

brightening, energy, expansive posture, eyes twinkling, flushing, higher speaking register, informal posture, informal movement, laughter, loosening, reduction of physical tension, relaxed posture, smiling, touching others, touching self, efforts to spread humor, fast thinking, fast speaking, focusing on present, high energy, increased agency, loss of situational awareness, non-normative behavior, optimism, raised spirits, reduction of stress, social integration, social seeking, social tightening.

This lets us break apart questions of fittedness into behavior.

For instance:

Instead of asking “where does humor fit?”, we can ask: “where does brightening fit? Where does brightening not fit?”—and the same of smiling, informal movement, social integration, and so on.

When there’s disagreement over where humor is “appropriate,” the disagreement isn’t always about the general idea of humor—it’s often about specific humor tells:

Owen and Emma are playing a co-op game where teamwork is essential. Owen keeps laughing and dropping putting a hand on his head, even during gameplay.

Emma: *alarmed* "Dude, stay focused!" (←Emma thinks touching self is unfitted in this situation)

Trevor is trying to practice a presentation with Yasmin, but everytime he starts, Yasmin breaks into giggles.

Yasmin: *laughing* (embarrassed) "I'm sorry!" (←Yasmin and Trevor think laughing is unfitted in this situation)

Zoe, Owen, and Emma are working on a group project, but they can’t stop laughing and getting distracted. Owen wants to make progress.

Owen: “Okay for real, we have to get serious.”

Everyone agrees and they all sober up and nod. (←study group thinks laughing, loss of situational awareness, loosening, and so on are unfitted in this situation)

Owen flips open his textbook, which causes Emma and Zoe to burst into laughter.

Above, it's not that humor generally is a problem in co-op game, practice presentation, or study session. Instead, it's that some humor tells specifically are seen as unfitted.

Since humor tells express intensity of humor, humor tells are a way of thinking about the fittedness of intensity:

The restaurant is having a slow Tuesday, so Jessica is having some fun back-and-forth with the staff as she goes in and out of the kitchen. They’re laughing about elephants—the manager overhears this but doesn’t care. Jessica approaches each table smiling and cheerful. (←the manager thinks that humor tells like optimism, smiling, and raised spirits is fitted in these circumstances)

- OR -

The restaurant is having a slow Tuesday, so Jessica has some fun back-and-forth with the staff as she goes in and out of the kitchen. Over time, their jokes get more complicated, and soon they start putting their arms to their faces like elephant trunks, making elephant trumpets, doubling over with laugher, and they miss someone waiting to be seated.

The manager raps on the door. “Ladies!” she says. (←the manager decides that humor tells like loss of situational awareness, non-normative behavior, raised voices, and laughter is unfitted in the situation)

In the examples above, the manager thinks low-intensity humor tells are fitted—and thinks high-intensity humor is unfitted.

Consider:

The point here isn't to decide whether the manager is right or wrong. Instead, the point is to see how our thinking becomes more tangible when we shift away from something abstract, like “humor,” and toward something more specific, like the intensity or combination of individual humor tells.

Content. Humor tells can help us avoid over-emphasizing the importance of content to fittedness. If the manager was watching employees doubling over with laughter through a window, it wouldn't matter if she didn't know the content of the humor—the intensity and combination of humor tells themselves can be seen as unfitted.

This is often the case. If we're in a lecture or restaurant, we have a clear idea of what physical behavior would be seen as unfitted (play wrestling, shrieking with laughter, leaping and shouting, and so on)—and we have this idea independent of any content.

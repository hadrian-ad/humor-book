Humor bushes are mutual or asymmetric

Emma and Zoe feel equally frequent and intense humor around each other, so their humor bushes aren’t asymmetric, but mutual:

By contrast, the humor bushes are asymmetric when people don't equally feel humor around each other:

Sam and Jasper have asymmetric humor bushes. Since Jasper is a stand-up comic, Sam lights up every time he thinks about Jasper. But since Jasper has never formally met Sam, Jasper never thinks about Sam—let alone feels humor from him.

Humor bushes can be mutual or asymmetric, but whether this is fitted or unfitted depends on the situation.

Sometimes an asymmetric humor bush is part of how we do our job. For instance, a Heather wants her students to feel more humor than they make her feel in return:

Heather wants to be able to acceptably criticize (students), support her arguments (to the students), (have more) control of a situation, deal with taboo topics, and so on—but she doesn’t want to let the students do the same to her in the same proportion.

Just because two sides laugh doesn’t mean there are mutual humor bushes. Heather might make herself laugh, along with her students—but this is Heather growing a humor bush with herself, and not the students growing a humor bush with Heather.

Other times, asymmetric humor bushes aren’t as positive—like how Elias often helps Jessica feel humor, but less often lets Jessica help him feel humor:

Jessica: *to Emma* (happily) “Elias is so funny.”

Emma: (annoyed) “Always cracks himself up, does he?” (←Emma notices Elias laughs because of himself more than he laughs because of Jessica)

When someone isn’t aware of the asymmetry, it can be a sign of manipulation—or a source of inaccurate observations:

The barista flashes Owen a grin.

Barista: “The usual for our best customer?”

Owen: (delighted) *smiling* “You know me too well!”

She goes to make the maple cappuccino.

Owen: (thinking) “That barista and I have chemistry! Wonder if she’s single?” (←Owen mistakes an asymmetric humor bush for a mutual humor bush)

Owen’s error is in thinking that his humor bush is the same size as the barista’s humor bush—but since Owen feels much higher intensity and longer duration humor then the barista does, they actually have asymmetric humor bushes.

Owen and the barista do feel mutual humor together—but their mutual humor bush is capped at the smallest bush of the two.

To use a quantification metaphor, if Owen has a “7” humor bush with the barista, and the barista has a “3” humor bush with Owen, the mutual humor bush is capped at the lowest (3) and isn't the highest (7).

If Owen thinks they mutually share 7, not 3, he can lose track of their actual social relationship.

Webs of humor bushes. We could set people into a web of relations, with one of the general attributes of each connection being a humor bush.

Humor and humor bushes are an important quality in the relations between people—a quality we already use in our thinking when we take the time to notice.

Moments of humor have both intensity and fittedness. Sometimes we prioritize fittedness ("charm") and sometimes we prioritize intensity ("comedy")—and these priorities shape how we judge humor.

But sometimes we prioritize another measure: status. When we prioritize status ("promotion"), we start judging humor as good or bad in sometimes strange ways:

Owen sees Zoe smile as she reads a paperback romance she got at the public library. (←Zoe feels humor)

Owen looks at the cover. There’s a dark-haired duke on a castle balcony with a sunset behind him.

Owen: *thinking* “Lame.” (←Owen prioritizes a measure of status, even over his observation of Zoe’s humor)

Owen flips through the book and finds himself amused at the dialogue between the haughty Duke and the spirited new horse trainer. (←Owen feels humor)

Embarrassed that he’s enjoying himself, Owen puts it back. (←Owen prioritizes a measure of status, even over his own humor)

Above, Owen felt humor himself, and he saw Zoe feel humor—which means the novel had decent intensity, fittedness, or both. It’s not until Owen gives priority to his own ideas about status that he decides that it’s “bad” humor.

There are a few problems with prioritizing status. One is that status is often tangential to the experience—a bit like judging someone’s cooking by how much we like their kitchen decor.

Another problem is that status is often entirely in our own heads—Owen is probably the only one of his social circle who worries that tolerating or briefly enjoying a romance novel is status-decreasing.

This means Owen is prioritizing his status for an audience of one. He’s essentially trying to impress himself!

If we get into a habit of prioritizing personal ideas about status, even when no one cares, we can develop a strange relation to humor.

We tend to see people who prioritize status over fittedness or intensity to be dull, excruciating, narrow, self-serving, socially insecure, uncurious, uninteresting, and so on.

In the same way we feel this about others, others feel this about us!

The point isn’t that Owen should read books he doesn’t want to—the point is that he doesn’t need to have a backlash against himself for simply enjoying paging through a book. And he doesn’t need to look down on Zoe for enjoying what she enjoys.

If Owen looked down on Zoe, he would be judging her based on his own ideas of status—which doesn’t really accomplish anything.

Owen doesn’t gain anything by prioritizing status when around Zoe—but he and Zoe do gain something when he doesn’t prioritize status. If Owen feels humor when Zoe feels humor, they can grow a mutual humor bush:

Owen: (sincerely) *smiling* “Looks like a fun book!”

Zoe: *smiles* “It is fun.” (←Zoe and Owen grow their mutual humor bush)

- OR -

Owen: *smiling* “Good book?”

Zoe: *smiles* “Shameless.” (←Zoe and Owen grow their mutual humor bush)

- OR -

Owen: *smiling* “The dialogue in this book is outrageous!”

Zoe: *smiles* “Isn’t it?!” (←Zoe and Owen grow their mutual humor bush)

When Owen and Zoe grow a mutual humor bush through mutual humor, they grow their ability to mutually characterize their relationship with mutual humor family words—like acceptance, connection, belonging, delight, generosity, welcome, and so on.

This mutual humor allows Owen to accrue greater respect from the people around him. By not prioritizing status, he opens the possibility of this mutual humor.

Owen gains respect by treating Zoe the way he would want to be treated himself.

Owen wouldn’t want Zoe to feel shame for briefly enjoying something he enjoyed, or for her to feel condescension toward him for enjoying what he enjoyed

This equal-ranking is a foundational condition for mutual humor bushes.

If Owen doesn’t treat others as equals, and prioritizes his personal ideas of status over everything else, he’ll have to live with an internal censor in his head—one that’s constantly screening his humor for status, fighting against moments of humor, prompting feelings of anger or guilt, and so on.

This can pointlessly curtail the range and vividness of his experiences and relationships—without any benefit.

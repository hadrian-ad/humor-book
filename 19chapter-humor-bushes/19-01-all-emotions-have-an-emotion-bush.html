The Humor Book

13. Evaluatory information

All emotions have emotion bushes:

contributing to humor bush

Jennifer sees Riley tugging on an old fence post.

Jennifer: "Oh, don’t do that, Riley—if it falls over... SPLAT!!" *tapping Riley’s head*

Riley: *smiles*

Jennifer: "So don't do it any more, okay?"

Riley: “Okay.” (←Jennifer grows the humor bush in her relationship with Riley)

- OR -

contributing to anger bush:

Jennifer sees Riley tugging on a post.

Jennifer: *snapping* “Don’t do that!”

Riley stops. (←Jennifer grows the anger bush in her relationship with Riley)

In both examples, Jennifer reaches her short-term goal of stopping Riley from fiddling with the post—but she does so by growing different emotion bushes.

The size of an emotion bush is the average amount of that emotion over time. To find the humor bush or anger bush, we’d take this one moment of humor (or anger) with Riley—along with every other moment of humor (or anger). This lets us find the average size of the emotion bush.

Consider:

While “emotion bush” a new phrase, the idea is familiar—because we already track emotion bushes! We can probably make an ordered list of who irritates us the most at work, just like we can intuitively order extended relatives by the size of our humor bush with them.

We can do this is because we always have a comparative sense of how much humor—or any emotion—we’ve felt toward someone overall.

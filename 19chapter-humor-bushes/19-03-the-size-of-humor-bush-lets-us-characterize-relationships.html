The Humor Book

13. Evaluatory information

When we look at a single instance of humor, we can use humor outcomes to characterize that moment. (“In this moment we symbolize close ties”).

When we look at many instances of humor, we can use humor outcomes to characterize a relationship. (“In this relationship we symbolize close ties”)

For instance:

If we only create rapport once with our neighbor, we can’t characterize our overall relationship as having strong rapport—one moment doesn't mean we have rapport (in general), just that we felt rapport once! It’s not until we have many moments of fitted humor that we’d start saying we have strong rapport.

The larger our humor bush, the more strongly we can use a humor outcome to characterize our relationship:

  • if we never have humor with someone, we have no rapport with them
  • if we sometimes have humor with someone, we have some rapport with them
  • if we often have humor with someone, we have strong rapport with them
  • if we always have humor with someone, we have complete rapport with them

So when Jennifer frequently generates humor with Riley, it grows a larger humor bush between them—which lets us use humor outcomes as a general characterization of their relationship.

Riley and Jennifer don’t just:

  • create rapport (once)—they have rapport
  • ease tension (once)—they quickly discharge tension
  • acceptably criticize (once)—they are able to criticize all the time
  • reduce defensiveness (once)—they continually have low defensiveness

(and so on)

By contrast, if Jennifer uses anger frequently on Riley, it grows a larger anger bush. Over time, this makes anger outcomes a general characterization of their relationship: they’d raise tensions quickly (between them), be unable to criticize (each other), have high defensiveness, and so on.

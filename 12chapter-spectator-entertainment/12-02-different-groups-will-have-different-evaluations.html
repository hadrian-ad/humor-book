Evaluations are different for different groups

We can use mistakes and OKs to think about why the characters and spectators have different emotional outcomes:

Sam walks into the kitchen and puts hand on Jennifer’s shoulder. He sees her bagel without cream cheese.

Sam: (surprised) “Oh, it’s the thing.” (←medium mistake)

Jennifer: *laughs* “Well, I wouldn’t just... you know…”

Sam: *laughs* *sits down* “Of course not!”

Jennifer: *amused* “One of these days…” *gestures*

Sam: *smiles* (ironically wistful) “One day…” (←Sam and Jennifer feel medium mistake and medium OK↔︎humor)

Audience: (bored) (←audience feels medium mistake and large OK↔︎no humor)

Owen watches a clip from a show.

The farmers give the annoying jocks a barrage of insults. (←large mistake)

The jocks are stunned. (←jocks have small OK↔︎no humor)

Owen laughs. (←Owen has large OK↔︎humor)

We can use the same method to think about why some surreal content makes us laugh, but similar non-surreal content doesn’t:

Sam watches Fired Fireman 3 on his laptop. Chief is trying to get the household ready for school, but he’s comically inept at it. As he runs to get the doorbell, he slips on a toy car left on the stairs. Sam loves the slapstick in these things. (←Sam feels humor)

- OR -

Sam watches Jennifer slip on a toy car left on the stairs. His heart wrenches in fear. (←Sam does not feel humor)

While these diverging outcomes happen everywhere in our lives, in spectator entertainment the priority is on the spectator. This priority shapes how spectator entertainment is put together.

Consider:

Not all diverging outcomes are spectator entertainment—it's only spectator entertainment when the priority is on spectators.

Most plays are designed so that the audience will laugh, not the actors—the intention to prioritize others makes it spectator entertainment.

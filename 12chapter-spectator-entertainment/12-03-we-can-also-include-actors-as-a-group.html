The Humor Book

Actors are an evaluatory group

We’ve made flowcharts that include the characters and the audience, but we've left one group out—the actors themselves!

Sam and Jennifer are acting out a scene where Sam realizes he forget to buy the cream cheese with green onion, and tries to charm his way out of it.

Jennifer and Sam concentrate on acting, as their characters act stand-offish. As Sam gets up on the table, the audience laughs.

Above, we’ve added a third evaluation column: Sam and Jennifer— as the real people under their characters.

Consider:

Sam and Jennifer themselves don’t feel humor, since they’ve rehearsed their scene so much—everything is completely expected (small mistake) and they're so experienced and grounded that everything feels without consequence (large OK).

In order for the actors to feel humor, either the mistake needs to grow or the OK needs to shrink. If their evaluations balance, they’ll feel humor and potentially break character:

This is Jennifer’s first performance—ever. She feels sick to her stomach. (←Jennifer has small OK)

She and Sam are onstage. The curtain rises. Jennifer pauses. Sam looks at her meaningfully—she has the first line. She opens her mouth and pauses. (←Jennifer has small mistake)

She giggles. (←small mistake and small OK match↔︎humor↔︎Jennifer breaks character)

Jennifer’s been acting since third grade. She’s seen and dealt with it all. (←Jennifer has a large OK)

She and Sam are onstage. The curtain rises. Sam takes a step forward and stumbles over a prop. As he twists, his costume snags and gives a loud rip. (←Jennifer has a large mistake)

He stands there, half-shirtless. The audience is shocked, but Sam rolls with it and leans suavely on the table, giving Jennifer a composed look. Jennifer gives a loud laugh before she recovers and continues the scene. (←large mistake and large OK match↔︎humor↔︎Jennifer breaks character)

Consider:

We often love when actors break characters—which is why we like bloopers, improv skits, or live comedians. This interplay between the actors' evaluations and the evaluations the actors are pretending to have is something we find enjoyable.

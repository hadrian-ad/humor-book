Humor can be part of a complicated emotional state

Since humor can be a part of a mixed emotional state, we often feel humor at the same time we feel a mistake-predominant emotion—like shame:

Minh spends fifteen minutes explaining to Amaya how the mobile game Fired Fireman: First-Person Hooter had abandoned the things that made the franchise great. He talks and talks and talks and Amaya gets more and more bored.

Amaya: “Minh—you’re being just like that fan boy character in Fired Fireman 2!”

Minh: (aghast) “Squire Dorkulous Knows-a-Lot?” (ruefully) *laughs* “Ouch!” (←Minh experiences both humor and shame)

Minh reaches humor outcomes, like being given new perspectives, being acceptably criticized, an argument being supported, and so on—he feels humor. At the same time, the unflattering comparison also makes him feel mistake-predominant emotions:

In this case, we can still describe Minh’s humor in terms of good outcomes—but that doesn’t mean his humor can’t coexist with other things we’d describe as less straightforwardly positive, like shame.

Other times, we might first feel humor, only for the humor to evaporate quickly, leaving us with just negative outcomes:

Elias: *leaving* “See ya!” (mocking imitation) “I’m off to the big city to be an ahctress.” *laughs*

A. Jessica: *laughs* (←humor↔︎Jessica feels Elias symbolized close ties and created rapport) “I do not sound like that!”

B. Jessica: *closes door* *feels sad* (←sadness↔︎Jessica feel Elias symbolized weak ties and created division)

Even though humor outcomes are describable as positive for those who feel humor, that’s different than saying the overall consequences of humor to a situation are always positive or remain positive—or that people can’t generate humor as part of a wider intention to cause harm.

That's the end of this chapter! Next, we'll look at the "appropriateness" of humor.

The only way to concordantly control a situation is with humor.

There are many ways someone can control a situation besides humor—they can make us feel fear by threatening us with a lawsuit, drawing a knife, calling the police, or so on.

But if they influence us through fear—or physically control us—the influence doesn't happens because of agreement (concord), but happens despite our disagreement (discord). Humor is the only way to control a situation concordantly.

Consider:

Most of us are more familiar with the word discord than concord—but concord is a useful word that implies mutual agreement of purpose. It etymologically means "hearts together."

Each of our emotional zones corresponds to a different kind of influence:

  • Mistake-predominant influence (coercive influence)—when we obey mean-spirited police officers; no entry signs; nitpicking professors; confusing tax forms; grasping managers; domineering parents; and so on—we're being influenced through non-concordant means.
  • Humor influence (concordant influence)—if someone smiles and asks us nicely if we'd move our bags, we may smile too and want to move our bags—this creates compliance by merging individual outlooks together.
  • OK-predominant influence (inertial influence)—when we choose the familiar route over another; wear what we have in our closet; use one calendar system and not another; and so on—we're being influenced by habit or roteness. This influence is often invisibe to us unless it's pointed out.

In this book, the the emotion of humor is the mechanism of concordant influence. This lets us clarify our humor outcomes with a critical addition—they're outcomes achieved through concord, not inertia or coercion.

Consider:

Note that in the same way we can have mixed emotional states (anger and humor), there's nothing odd about various combinations of influences—like an experience that mingles concord with coercion.

That means humor is how we gain a new perspective (through concord), the way we reveal a personal review (through concord), the way we acceptably criticize (through concord), and so on—and humor is the only way these things happen concordantly.

For instance:

If we want someone to talk about something with us, we could grab a megaphone and follow them around badgering them until they feel they have to talk about it. We'll have opened a topic of conversation, but in a way that's a bit like pulling teeth from an unwilling patient. (Coercive, or unilateral, influence.)

On the other hand, if we open a conversation in a way that they show show humor tells (social tightening, increased agency, loosening, smiling, and so on) they may talk more openly about the topic, without feeling unilaterally compelled. In this case, we'll have opened a topic of conversation that will lead to more of a mutual conversation, where the motivation comes from shared alignment. (Concordant, or multilateral, influence.).

We can explain why someone feels humor using humor outcomes.

  • Humor outcomes describe actions that happen within an emotion situation. By focusing on these actions, we can track where and why humor happens in a way that sidesteps outcome-nonspecific terms of genre or tone.

  • By framing intentions as intended humor outcomes, we understand why people make the comments or actions they did.
  • Humor is a positive emotion. If we feel shame, it's because we're feeling shame—not because we're feeling humor. If we're feeling humor and shame, the positive component is from the humor, the negative component is from the shame.

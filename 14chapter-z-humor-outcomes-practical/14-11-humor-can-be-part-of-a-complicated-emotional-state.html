Humor outcomes don't always describe the entire emotional state

Since humor can be a part of a mixed emotional state, we often feel humor at the same time we feel a mistake-predominant emotion—like shame:

Minh spends fifteen minutes explaining to Amaya how the mobile game Fired Fireman: First-Person Hooter had abandoned the things that made the franchise great. He talks and talks and talks and Amaya gets more and more bored.

Amaya: “Minh—you’re being just like that fan boy character in Fired Fireman 2!”

Minh: (aghast) “Squire Dorkulous Knows-a-Lot?” (ruefully) *laughs* “Ouch!” (←Minh experiences both humor and shame)

Minh reaches humor outcomes, like being given new perspectives, being acceptably criticized, an argument being supported, and so on—he feels humor. At the same time, the unflattering comparison also makes him feel mistake-predominant emotions:

In this case, we can still describe Minh’s humor in terms of good outcomes—but that doesn’t mean his humor can’t coexist with other things we’d describe as less straightforwardly positive, like shame.

Other times, we might first feel humor, only for the humor to evaporate quickly, leaving us with just negative outcomes:

Elias: *leaving* “See ya!” (mocking imitation) “I’m off to the big city to be an ahctress.” *laughs*

A. Jessica: *laughs* (←humor↔︎Jessica feels Elias symbolized close ties and created rapport) “I do not sound like that!”

B. Jessica: *closes door* *feels sad* (←sadness↔︎Jessica feel Elias symbolized weak ties and created division)

Even though humor outcomes are describable as positive for those who feel humor, that’s different than saying the overall consequences of humor to a situation are always positive or remain positive—or that people can’t generate humor as part of a wider intention to cause harm.

In this book, control a situtation is a humor outcome—and the only way to control a situation is with humor.

This doesn't make sense without an important clarification, since obviously someone can control a situation without humor—they can make us feel fear by threatening us with a lawsuit, drawing a knife, calling the police, or so on.

The clarification is this: humor is the only way to concordantly control a situation.

Consider:

We're often more familiar with the word "discord" than we are with it's opposite, "concord." But concord is a useful word that implies mutual agreement of purpose. It etymologically means "hearts together."

If someone influences us through fear, the influence doesn't happens because of agreement (concord), but happens despite disagreement (discord). The only way to concordantly control a situation is through humor.

Each of our emotional zones corresponds to a different kind of motivating influence:

  • Mistake-predominant influence (coercive influence)—when we obey mean-spirited police officers; no entry signs; nitpicking professors; confusing tax forms; grasping managers; domineering parents; and so on—we're being influenced through non-concordant means.
  • Humor influence (concordant influence)—when we take a willing action on behalf of a friend; street performer; mentor; stranger; and so on—we're being influenced by concordant methods.
  • OK-predominant influence (inertial influence)—when we choose the familiar route over another; take a door because it was built here and not there; wear what we have in our closet; use one calendar system and not another; and so on—we're being influenced by habit or roteness. This influence is often invisibe to us unless it's pointed out.

In this book, "evaluatory outcomes" can happen by any mechanism of influence. Humor outcomes are the subset of evaluatory outcomes that happen concordantly. The mechanism that concordantly transitions us from no rapport to rapport is humor.

That means humor is how we gain a new perspective (through concord), the way we reveal a personal review (through concord), the way we acceptably criticize (through concord), and so on.

Consider:

Note that in the same way we can have mixed emotional states (anger and humor), there's nothing odd about various combinations of influences—like an experience that mingles concord with coercion.

The role of humor as the mechanism of concordant influence is so foundational to our experiences, it's hard to even notice, like breathing.

It's easiest to notice when we think about situations that require concord—like making friends, creating romantic relationships, asking someone to do something for us, motivating volunteers, organizing strangers, and so on.

Consider:

If two friends want to transition to a romantic friendship, they won't get what they want by ignoring each other or threatening each other with lawsuits. That's because what makes those relationships meaningful is the fact that two people want the same thing—they want a relationship of mutually aligned goals and actions.

It's concord, not coercion or roteness, that would create what they want. So their only option is humor—to concordantly avoid conflict, create rapport, deal with taboo topics, ease tensions, help each other feel accepted, open up new topics of conversations, reduce defensiveness, reveal personal views, symbolize close ties, and so on.

We want the same thing everywhere. All things being equal, we'd rather there be concord than not—with our children, coworkers, friends, municiple government, teams, and so on. Without concord, we often only have the picture of what we want only.

We could pull a gun or pay money to make someone do something—but we can't make someone want to do those things. If we pull a gun to make someone be our friend, they'll want to not get shot more than they want to be our friend. A similar twist often happens when we offer people money, where they want money and not what the money makes them do.

Instead, it's concordant influence that makes someone want to be our friend, want to be at our birthday party, want to volunteer, want to care for mutual space, want to do us a favor, and so on. It's the same for what motivates us—a genuine, shared smile with someone and we want to do things with each other.

This gives some context for what we've been talking about throughout this book: humor creates and marks concordant internal changes or motivation. When multiple people feel humor for the same reason, the alignment of evaluations is an expression of concord.

Humor outcomes can describe motivation, while sidestepping descriptive terms. (Compare "rude" to trying to reveal a personal view.) This lets us parse situations that include negative outcomes and humor.

  • People intend humor outcomes, which helps us understand why someone made the comments or actions they did.
  • Humor is a positive emotion. If we feel shame, it's because we're feeling shame—not because we're feeling humor. If we're feeling humor and shame, the positive component is from the humor, the negative component is from the shame.
  • Humor is the mechanism of concordant influence. What differentiates a humor outcome from a mistake-predominant outcome is concordancy. (Compare concordantly controlling a situation and unilaterally controlling a situation)

The Humor Book

15. Explaining humor

When using humor outcomes to explain humor's relation to something like a title, we start by picking the humor outcome that seems most relevant:

Zoe is browsing the bookstore, and finds a book called Wherever You Go, There You Are. She smiles. (←Zoe gained a new perspective)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called The Big Book of Masturbation: From Angst to Zeal. She smiles. (←Zoe dealt with taboo topics)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Why Be Happy When You Could Be Normal? She smiles. (←Zoe eased tension)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called In a Cold Crater: Intellectual Life in Berlin, 1945-1948. She smiles. (←Zoe felt an argument was supported)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called A Queer and Pleasant Danger. She smiles. (←Zoe felt rapport was created)

In each case, Zoe feels humor because she reached a humor outcome, like taboo topics were dealt with, and so on. This lets us work backward—what was taboo, and how was it dealt with?

Consider:

As before, humor outcomes are all different ways of describing the same thing ("humor")—so we can use as many humor outcomes as we think would be useful:

We can use the same process, just repeated multiple times: since Zoe gained a new perspective, what was that perspective, and how was it delivered? Since Zoe eased tension, why was there tension to begin with, and what eased it?

This gives us a handhold into thinking a bit more concretely about why someone feels humor when looking at something like a book title.

Push. Zoe may not feel humor when she reads the title In a Cold Crater: Intellectual Life in Berlin, 1945-1948. But she may still recognize something humorous about the title.

We can use humor outcomes to think about why we think some things are "humorous"—we think the author or publishers were intending readers to feel an argument was supported, new perspectives were given, something was acceptably criticized, and so on.

When Zoe thinks she sees these intentions, she describes the vehicle for those intentions as "humorous." That doesn't mean she necessarily feels humor—it just means she sees the push toward humor outcomes. This is why we can describe a variety of things as humorous, even if we find them appalling or unfunny.

Finally, there's nothing strange about someone intending us to feel both mistake-predominant emotions and humor simultaneously. An editor may title a book hoping that a reader finds that the title supports an argument even at the same time hoping we find it grim and severe.

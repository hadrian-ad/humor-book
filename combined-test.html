Chapter 2

Humor is an Emotion

Humor is an emotion. We can add this into our model:

Humor is an emotion that occurs when a mistake is evaluated as OK.

We usually think of things like anger, happiness, sadness, and so on as emotions—but we don’t always think of humor explicitly as an emotion.

But humor behaves just like other emotions:

  • We can feel humor grow over time, like over the course of a funny story—in the same way we can feel anger grow over time, like over the course of an infuriating story.
  • We have physical reactions when we feel humor, like smiles or laughs—in the same way we have physical reactions when we feel sadness, like sighing or tears.
  • We sometimes have bursts of unexpected humor, which seem to come out of nowhere—in the same way we sometimes have bursts of unexpected grief or irritation, which seem to come out of nowhere.
  • Humor has dimensions of intensity and duration to it—in the same way sadness or anger has dimensions of intensity and duration to it.
  • Babies go through periods of humor—in the same way they go through periods of boredom or irritation.
  • There are videos oriented around making us feel humor—in the same way there are videos oriented around making us feel anger, “aww,” amazement, empowerment, fear, frustration, mystery, grief, sadness, and so on.

(and so on)

Since humor behaves similarly to things like anger and sadness, we’ll be calling humor an emotion.

Humor is an emotion that occurs when a mistake is evaluated as OK.

By plugging humor into our existing understanding of our emotional lives, we’ll have a greater intuitive understanding of humor.

Our model requires a person

Since humor is an emotion, a specific person feels humor. Different people may feel different things—so one person may feel humor, while someone else may feel something else:

Xiaowei is dropping his friend Lina off at their high school for the opening day of her play.

Xiaowei: "Break a leg! Also: You look nervous as hell!"

Lina gives him the finger on the way out. (←Lina does not feel humor) Xiaowei laughs and gives the finger back. (←Xiaowei feels humor)

Elias stops by to give Jessica and Emma a ride, but Jessica is running a bit behind. When she finally comes out of the bathroom, Elias makes a belittling comment about the slogan on Jessica’s shirt. (←Elias feels humor)

It makes Jessica laugh. (←Jessica feels humor) It makes Emma furious. (←Emma does not feel humor)

Bleary-eyed from staying up late, Zoe starts giggling. (←Zoe feels humor) Emma puts her head in her hands.

Emma: (despairing) “We’ll never finish,” (←Emma does not feel humor)

Amber visits Jennifer, and laughs at the cat wedged in a cardboard box. (←Amber feels humor)

But Jennifer has seen the cat there so often, she no longer notices anything. (←Jennifer does not feel humor)

Since these outcomes are different for each person, we’ll need to be thoughtful about which perspective we’re looking at:

  • Humor is an emotion Xiaowei feels when he evaluates a mistake as OK.
  • Humor is an emotion Yasmin feels when she evaluates a mistake as OK.
  • Humor is an emotion Jessica feels when she evaluates a mistake as OK.
  • Humor is an emotion Zoe feels when she evaluates a mistake as OK.
  • Humor is an emotion Amber feels when she evaluates a mistake as OK.

Because of this, we can update our model:

Humor is an emotion that someone feels when they evaluate a mistake as OK.

Humor is just one possible emotional outcome

Last chapter, we looked at evaluations as leading to either humor or no humor:

Sara laughs. (←humor)

- OR -

Sara doesn't laugh. (←no humor)

But when we think of humor as an emotion, “no humor” could mean any other emotion:

That’s because humor is one possible emotional outcome of many:

Sara is moving in three days. She can barely sleep.

Sara: *tossing and turning* *thinking* “I’m so nervous. I’m not ready for this.” (brightly) “I know: I’ll run away!”

She gives a complex laugh. (←humor)

- OR -

Sara: *tossing and turning* *thinking* “It’s going to be different!” (←excitement)

- OR -

Sara: *tossing and turning* *thinking* “What if I don’t make any friends there. What if I live there for the rest of my life and nothing good ever happens ever. I really fucked things up here.”

The future feels hopeless. (←dread)

Professor Justin reads a poem out of the class’s assigned anthology.

Justin: “Of darkling mortals famished for one ray / Of thy so-hoarded luxury of light…”

Emma tries to listen, but this is the worst Gen Ed ever. “So-hoarded?” she thinks. “More like so-boreded!”

She’s briefly amused. (←humor)

- OR -

Zoe thinks Robert Browning is hit or miss. Like, “darkling mortals famished?” He's like a spoof of a poet. Zoe draws lines on her paper. (←boredom)

- OR -

Trevor is irritated. This is a waste of time. (←irritation)

Professor Justin hands back everyone’s literary analysis essay.

Owen: (amused) "A B! Wow. I totally wrote this literally in the half-hour before it was due.” *laughs*(←humor)

- OR -

Emma: (proud) “A B! I followed the rubric exactly! I had the professor read it early! First time I’ve put effort into a literature essay. I feel weirdly proud!” (←pride)

- OR -

Zoe: (shocked) “A B! But I followed the rubric exactly! I had the professor read it early! He should be fired!” (←outrage)

Last chapter, we said we’ll be as interested in when humor doesn’t occur as when it does. Here, we’re clearer about what it means for humor to not occur—it means another emotion happens instead.

When we don't react with humor, we react with another emotion instead.

Reviewing mistakes and OKs We feel humor when we evaluate a mistake as OK:

Owen: *shows Emma and Zoe paper* *smiling* “Look guys! Justin totally just gave my paper a B, even though I wrote it in half an hour, sitting in the hallway before class!” (←the B is a mistake, since Owen had expected a worse grade, but it's OK↔︎humor)

Emma: (aghast) “You got a B for half an hour…! I spent all Thanksgiving on that paper! Arg!” *hits Owen* (←Emma sees several mistakes, but none of them are OK↔︎no humor)

Zoe: (snippy) “See? That confirms it. Justin’s grades are practically random. He should be fired!” (←the B isn’t a mistake, since Zoe already expects unfair grading↔︎no humor)

Owen: (pleased with himself) “See? A proper work ethic oft goes wrong, my friends.” (←Owen thinks the situation has several mistakes that are each OK↔︎humor)

Humor and jokes aren't the same thing

Sometimes we think of jokes and humor as the same thing. But they're not—for the same reason insults and anger are different.

“Insult” makes us think of a comment, while “anger” makes us think of an emotion:

Insults and anger sometimes coincide, but they're distinct things. There can be insults without anger, just like there can be anger without insults:

It’s the same thing for jokes and humor. They sometimes coincide—but they’re distinct things. There can be jokes without humor, and humor without jokes:

The complication is that we sometimes call jokes “humor.” In this case, we have the same-sounding word for both comments and emotions. There's humor and humor:

This can make us think of these two things as more similar than they are. But humor (comment) and humor (emotion) aren’t the same thing—for the same reason insults and anger aren’t the same thing!

"Humor" refers to an emotion

Humor is a broad word in our day-to-day conversations. At different times, we use the word “humor” to refer to a kind of:

  • comment (compliment, insult, humor, question, and so on)
  • genre (drama, horror, humor, thriller, and so on)
  • personality trait (gentleness, humor, temper, thoughtfulness, and so on)
  • quality (aggressive, beauty, humor, meanness, persuasiveness, and so on)
  • emotion (anger, humor, listlessness, sadness, and so on)

(and so on)

We see this fluidity of meaning all the time:

Zoe and Emma do a double take on a joke scribbled on a white board.

Zoe: (disapprovingly) “Distasteful humor.” (←“humor” as a comment or joke)

Amber: *to clerk* “Hey, excuse me, I’m looking for the new Gram Bridgette book—is that in the memoir or humor section?” (←“humor” as a genre)

Owen fills out his dating profile.

Owen: “The most important quality in a partner? Humor. For sure.” (←“humor” as a trait) *checks box*

Timothy takes his residents on rounds.

Timothy: “Having a humorous name like Dr. Ailes is usually a good icebreaker.” (←“humor” as a quality)

The residents chuckle loudly.

Emma goes to Zoe’s house for Thanksgiving. Everyone laughs a lot.

Emma: (pleased) “There’s a lot of humor at your house!” (←“humor” as an emotion)

Most of the time, this fluidity of meaning is a source of utility and rich meaning. But at the same time, if we're not careful, this fluidity can also cause us unnecessary confusion.

We'll avoid this confusion by only using "humor" to refer to an emotion in this book.

Consider:

There’s nothing wrong with other ways we define or use the word humor. But it can be wrong to be inconsistent in how we're using the word "humor." We don't want to mean one thing by "humor" in one sentence, and another thing the next.

So this book will stick consistently to one definition of humor, which will prevent us from flipping back and forth between meanings.

Sleight of hand. We often jump fluidly between different meanings of humor, even without noticing that we do:

Emma: (curiously) “Jessica, what do you think about humor?” (←Emma doesn’t clarify whether she’s asking about comments, a genre, a personality trait, a quality, or an emotion)

Jessica: “Ohmigosh, I am so bad at jokes!” (←Jessica decides that Emma is asking about jokes) *laughs* (←Jessica feels humor) “I once tried to tell Elias that joke the waitress told Chief in Fired Fireman…” *stops to laugh* (←Jessica feels humor) “… the one about the difference between boobs and an apron…?” *laughs harder* (←Jessica feels humor) “… and I mangled it. God! I’m just not that good at humor. Never been funny. So there you go.” (←conversation about humor turns into a conversation about jokes)

Emma: *laughs* (←Jessica helps Emma feel humor) “I’ll never forget that time you messed up that knock, knock joke.”

Jessica: *laughs* (←Jessica feels humor) (recalling) “Knock, knock!”

Even though Jessica laughs (Jessica feels humor), and makes Emma laughs with her story (Jessica helps Emma feel humor)—Jessica still says she’s bad at humor because she’s bad at delivering prepared jokes.

Jessica doesn't notice that she might have flipped between meanings of the word "humor." We sometimes do something similar when we aren't careful—which can sometimes make our thinking incoherent or vague.

We'll focus on humor, not genres

Instead of talking about humor, we sometimes drift into talking about "farces," "jokes," "irony," and so on instead. But this can distract us from understanding why we sometimes laugh, and why we sometimes don’t. 

Like anything else, comedies, farces, jokes, irony, satires, witty remarks, and so on can generate humor—but they don’t always. Someone may or may not see mistakes in them, which they may or may not evaluate as OK:

So while our evaluations of jokes, comedies, wittiness, satire, and so on can lead to humor—our evaluations can also lead to any other emotion as well:

Jokes can have any emotional outcome

Tucker: (delightedly) “Hey, son, you know what one of the most remarkable things in the world is?”

Elias: (warily) “What?”

Tucker: *chuckles* *chuckles more* “Whiteboards!”

Elias: *pause* “Re-markable?” *laughs* (←humor)

- OR -

Elias: *pause* “Remarkable?” (confused) (←confusion)

- OR -

Elias: *pause* “Re-markable?” *purses lips* (←annoyance)

Comedies can have any emotional outcome

Sam and Riley are on the couch watching Fired Fireman.

Chief and the Arsonist are wrestling over the stolen wallet when the police run in.

Police: *draws gun* "Freeze, thief!"

Chief and the Arsonist shoot their hands up. But Chief somehow ended up holding the stolen wallet. He does a shocked double take at it.

Sam: *laughs*

Riley: *laughs too* (←humor)

- OR -

Riley: (bewildered) “What’s so funny?” (←bewildered)

- OR -

Riley: *eyes droop* (←tired)

Wittiness can have any emotional outcome

Zoe is reading Gone with the Wind.

Scarlett: (indignant) (suspicious) “Are you lying to try to soothe me?”

Rhett: (amused) “My dear, when did I ever try to soothe you?"

The book just lights up whenever Rhett’s in the scene. Zoe smiles as she reads. (←humor)

- OR -

The whole relationship between Rhett and Scarlett bothers her. Zoe frowns. (←annoyance)

- OR -

Zoe checks to see how many pages she has left in the chapter. She sighs. (←bored)

Satire can have any emotion outcome

Professor Justin is reading a satire mocking how academics write.

This is too good! He chuckles continuously as he reads. (←humor)

- OR -

Justin has a sinking feeling as he reads it. It's just like how he writes. (←dismay)

- OR -

Justin feels the satire is unfair and off-base. It's like it's purposefully misrepresenting something he thinks is important. It makes him feel alone and morose. (←sadness)

Since we’re focusing on humor in this book, we won’t focus on jokes or satire—since those things aren’t humor. Humor is a particular emotional outcome—and we'll stay focused on why this outcome is or isn't reached.

Consider:

We'll be learning why anything does or doesn't lead to an outcome of humor. This is inclusive to jokes and satire—but inclusive to everything else besides. We won't need to focus on jokes to understand humor.

Jokes and tentpoles. We sometimes idiomatically use “joke” as a tentpole word for humor—where everything that causes us to laugh is called a joke. But just because we have idiomatic ways of talking doesn't mean we should take those expressions at face value. ("Everything we laugh at is a joke.")

It's easy to see why we shouldn't take this literally. The reasoning would go like this: "we sometimes laugh at jokes. Therefore everything we laugh at is some kind of joke." To see this as bizarre, we can create new variations: "we sometimes buy coffee. Therefore, everything we buy is some kind of coffee.”

For the same reason it would be strange to frame all buying around coffee—it would be strange to frame all laughing around jokes.

Small amounts of humor matter

Sometimes, we only notice humor when it’s unambiguous and most of our emotional state—like when we’re laughing at a movie:

Emma and Owen are watching Fired Fireman.

Chief is hired to work at a warehouse, but his attempt to move crates of lobsters goes horribly, horribly wrong. Emma and Owen laugh. (←only humor)

Even though we sometimes seem to feel “one” emotion, most emotions happen in a mix—like when Jennifer feels humor and anger in her emotional state: 

Jennifer looks up from her tablet. Where’s Riley? Her heart sinks in fear. She checks the street, then runs around the house. She sees Riley squatting quietly next to an anthill, and Jennifer puts a hand over her heart and laughs. (←humor)

Jennifer: (relieved) (annoyed) “Riley! Thank god. I thought I told you to stay the side yard.” (←Jennifer feels humor and anger)

Above, Jennifer isn’t feeling humor and anger in equal proportions—instead, she feels a lot of humor and a small amount of anger in her emotional state. 

We can see this in other examples as well:

Trevor walks into class and trips, and the cereal he’s carrying falls down his shirt.

At first shocked, his classmates see that he’s not hurt and start to laugh with relief.

Trevor stands there, embarrassed, and laughs sheepishly. Red-faced, he goes to heads to the bathroom.

Riley is bored at the dinner table, and Sam makes a funny face at her. Riley smiles and squirms with amusement and boredom.

In complicated emotional states, small amounts of humor mingle with more prominent feelings of bitterness, disappointment, sadness, and so on:

Emma sadly shuts the door to her apartment.

Emma: “I can’t believe Sara didn’t tell me until today that she decided to move!”

She looks around. She feels angry, resentful, regretful, sad. A cloud of bitter humor passes through her. (←Emma’s complicated emotional state includes some humor)  “It sucks.” 

She lays across her bed, wishing she could turn off her mind.

Timothy: *calling Jennifer* “Jennifer, I know Sam said otherwise, but I checked with the insurance company. They said the treatment is still experimental. They won’t cover it.”

Jennifer: *pause* (exhausted) *laughs* (←Jennifer’s complicated emotional state includes some humor, and other emotions besides) “That’s what I thought. If we hold a fundraiser, I think we should still be able to cover it.”

Jennifer is helping her father pack up his house in preparation for his move into a nursing home. When they come across some mementos in an old box, he gives a funny smile as he touches them, and he gives an unusual laugh. He wipes his eyes. (←Jennifer’s father has complicated emotional state that includes some humor, and other emotions besides)

Most of the humor we feel in our lives isn’t unadulterated humor. Instead, most of the humor we feel is just small twinges of humor, often as part of more complicated emotional moments.

Consider:

Small amounts of humor matter to how we describe our emotional state. This is like how an all-blue panel is very different than a similar panel with an orange dot in the middle.

We'd be suspicious of someone who insisted the orange wasn't relevant, simply because both are mostly blue. The overall composition matters, not just the majority color.

It's the same for our emotional state. For similar reasons, we can't just ignore small amounts of humor and still be accurate to our experiences.

Brownies and importance. Sometimes we assume that the more intense an emotion is, the more that emotion matters—like that a twinge of humor wouldn't matter much if it happens alongside much more intense anger. We may even think we don’t even need to account for that humor at all.

But we can use a comparison to food to see that that isn’t quite right. If we eat an orange-zest brownie, we wouldn’t say the orange-zest is unimportant just because chocolate is by far the most prominent flavor. And we definitely wouldn't say we only taste chocolate.

It’s the same for our emotions. We can't ignore small amounts of humor, since that flavors the entire experience. That’s why this book will be as inclusive of these small twinges of humor as it will be to intense moments of humor.

That's it for this chapter!

We categorized humor as an emotion. By putting humor alongside similar things, like anger, boredom, or sadness, we can place humor within broader system of emotions.

  • Our evaluations have range of emotional outcomes. Humor is one potential outcome.
  • Jokes (comments) aren't the same as humor (emotion), for the same reason insults (comments) aren't the same thing as anger (emotion). To avoid equivocating, we'll only use "humor" to refer to an emotion. In this book, humor is something we feel, not say.
  • We have an emotional state, which can be complicated. Because of this, it doesn't really make sense to only study the humor that occurs in simple emotional states—we want to study humor in complicated emotion states too. This sets a broad boundary for our study of "humor."

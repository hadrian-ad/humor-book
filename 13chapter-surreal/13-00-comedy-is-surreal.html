Chapter 13

Surreal

In this chapter, we'll look at how spectator entertainment is surreal.

Consider:

The things depicted in spectator entertainment can't happen without effort and the intention to prioritize others—what we're calling surreal.

We can't expect this effort and intention to happen without effort and intention—these surreal things can't straightforwardly happen spontaneously in our life.

It's obvious that fight scenes are surreal:

Owen and Emma are watching the trailer for Blood Soul Revenge.

The hero, Rage Man, guns down dozens of henchmen while they ineffectively return fire. Then a missile explodes near him, throwing Rage Man against a concrete wall—but he gets up in time to behead a charging henchmen with a keyboard. (←action is surreal)

Emma: “You know, I hear your body just liquidates if you’re close enough to be blasted off your feet by an explosion.” (←action is surreal)

Owen: *laughs* “Yeah, but the dude’s not even wearing earplugs, so he’s clearly made of sterner stuff!” (←action is surreal)

Emma: (thoughtfully) “Do you think you could really decapitate someone with a keyboard?” *laughs* (←action is surreal)

Emma and Owen know this is surreal—they don't go around thinking that they could elegantly decapitate henchmen with keyboards.

Consider:

It doesn't matter to Emma and Owen that Blood Soul Revenge is surreal—because they aren't looking for realism for realism’s sake.

They want the movie to entertain them—not to teach them how to fight with keyboards.

But while it’s often obvious to us that action scenes are surreal, we don’t always think of comedy scenes as being equally surreal—but they are!

Sarah is trying to make a video of a funny rant. She's been scripting in her head on the bus ride home, and when she gets home she hops in front of the mirror for some aesthetic adjustments.

She opens the blinds for light, and then records herself. She's not satisfied with the take.

She repositioning herself, takes a few breaths, and records again.

Satisfied, she trims the ending of the clip a bit, and hits upload. (←rant video is surreal)

Zoe watches a great rant by Sarah about her daycare kids. Sarah's so funny!

Above, Sarah's video is surreal. In the same way Owen can't be like Rage Man (use grit to survive missiles), Zoe can't be like Sarah (give fluid, funny rants).

In fact, Rage Man's actor can't be like Rage Man, and Sarah can't spontaneously be like her performance character.

When we can seperate what is and isn't possible to experience or do spontaneously, we can relate better to spectator entertainment.

We'll be talking about this surreality throughout this chapter.

Consider:

There's nothing wrong with surreal things—instead, we can be wrong if we don't spot when surreal things require effort and intention to produce.

Partly, it's a matter of apples and oranges. There are two skills: producing amusing rant videos, and actually ranting to someone.

If Zoe confuses the two, she can make inaccurate comparisons or judgements.

Coherent and surreal. When the audience watches Rage Man punch through concrete in the opening scene of Blood Soul Revenge, the audience knows this is surreal—people can't punch through concrete.

Even though it's surreal, Rage Man's strength can still be coherent if the movie is consistent to its surreal premise. Rage Man punches through concrene throughout the movie, making his abilities both surreal and coherent—but we shouldn't take this coherence to be the same thing as realism.

It's the same for emotions too. Rage Man is always angry, so the emotional premise is that he always has a large mistake and small OK. We know this is surreal—people get calm from time to time.

At the same time, if the movie is consistent to these surreal evaluatory premises, the movie will be emotionally surreal while still be emotionally coherent.

As before, we shouldn't take this coherence to be the same thing as realism. If Rage Man is consistently furious, that doesn't mean it's realistic—it just means it's consistent.

This is relevant to comedies, where the character's emotional reactions may be coherent to a surreal emotional premise—and we shouldn't take it for more realistic than it is.

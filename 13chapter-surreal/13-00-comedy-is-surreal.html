Chapter 13

Surreal

In this chapter, we'll look at how spectator entertainment is surreal.

It's obvious that fight scenes are surreal:

Owen and Emma are watching the trailer for Blood Soul Revenge.

The hero, Rage Man, guns down dozens of henchmen while they ineffectively return fire. Then a missile explodes near him, throwing Rage Man against a concrete wall—but he gets up in time to behead a charging henchmen with a keyboard. (←action is surreal)

Emma: “You know, I hear your body just liquidates if you’re close enough to be blasted off your feet by an explosion.” (←action is surreal)

Owen: *laughs* “Yeah, but the dude’s not even wearing earplugs, so he’s clearly made of sterner stuff!” (←action is surreal)

Emma: (thoughtfully) “Do you think you could really decapitate someone with a keyboard?” *laughs* (←action is surreal)

Emma and Owen know this is surreal—they don't go around thinking that they could elegantly decapitate henchmen with keyboards.

Consider:

It doesn't matter to Emma and Owen that Blood Soul Revenge is surreal—because they aren't looking for realism for realism’s sake.

They want the movie to entertain them—not to teach them how to fight with keyboards.

But while it’s often obvious to us that action scenes are surreal, we don’t always think of comedy scenes as being equally surreal—but they are!

Sam watches Fired Fireman 3 on his laptop.

Chief is trying to get the household ready for school, but he’s comically inept at it. As he runs to get the doorbell, he slips on a toy car left on the stairs.

Chief rolls down three flights of stairs, but gets up and shakes it off. (←character is immune to harm)

- OR -

Sam watches Jennifer slip on a toy car left on the stairs.

Jennifer is bruised, sore, and frightened for the rest of the day. (←scene from movie was surreal, which is why Jennifer was hurt when chief wasn't)

Sam and Jennifer are performing a play. In the audience, Justin and Heather are enjoying themselves.

Sam jumps on the table and smoothly props his head up, just like he's practiced hundreds of times. The audience laughs.

The audience doesn't know how much trouble the prop team had in getting a table that was sturdy and low enough for Sam. This was one of the hardest scenes to do in the entire play! (←Sam has the right props and practice to lay across the table in a play)

- LATER -

While eating breakfast the next morning, Justin decides to get on the table to make Jennifer laugh—but Heather's table shakes ominously as he awkward clambers on. He barely avoids knocking off a plate, and he doesn't fit on the table like he thought.

Heather is shocked, bewildered, and concerned. (←scene from a comedy is surreal, which is why it can't be straightforwardly imitated in real life)

Owen is watching a clip online from a comedy show about a small town in Canada.

Daryl, Dan, Katy, and Wayne are picking up rocks in a field of tall grass.

Wayne: *popping up out of the grass with armful of rocks* (confessing) “There's been an incident, and I'd like to talk about it.”

Daryl: “Get after it.”

Wayne: *hesitates*

Dan: *encouraging* “Well pitter patter, let's get at 'er.”

Katy: (exasperated) "Spit it out big shoots.”

Wayne: (reluctantly) “I seen Stewart's horn.”

Katy: *taken aback* “What?”

Daryl: “You've seen his impaler?”

Dan “His bobbydangler?”

Wayne: “Well...”

Daryl: “You've seen his PhD?”

Dan: “His WMD?”

Wayne: “Well look fellers...”

Daryl: “You've seen his friendly weapon?”

Dan: “His sticky grenade?”

Katy: “His ground squirrel?”

Wayne: (confirming) “Yeah, I've seen his ground squirrel.”

Daryl: “You've seen Stuart's mill stone?”

Dan: “You've seen his custard slinger?”

Wayne: “Well I'm coming up the stairs, he's coming out the bathroom, naked as a jaybird for some fucking reason… I round the corner and, yeah, I seen his, ah...”

Daryl: “His hollow point?”

Dan: “His hard drive?”

Wayne: (correcting) “Well his floppy disk is what I've seen, yeah.”

Daryl: “Well. What's the evaluation?”

Katy: “Yeah.”

Dan: (curiously) “I'd hear an assessment.”

Wayne: *far away look* “You want to know what. It looked like a tall boy can of red bull hanging there.”

Katy: “No.”

Wayne: “Like a tube of tennis balls hanging there, four pack”

Katy: “Shut up”

Wayne: “Looked like a fucking policeman's flashlight from the 1980s hanging there.”

Katy: “You're joking.”

Wayne: *turns to look at Katy* “That is no joke.”

Daryl: (admiringly) “Good job Stewart.”

Wayne: (politely) “That's what I said, I said ‘good for him’.”

Dan: (with mixed feelings, but politely) “Good for you, Stewart.”

Owen has a broad smile as he watching the exchange, and laughs at the flashlight joke.

Like the action scene, this comedic scene is emotionally, practically, and situationally surreal.

  • The scene is emotionally surreal. No one reacts normally. It would be surreal for Emma, Zoe, and Owen to somehow have an impromptu conversation like this in a field and not show any amazement, amusement, reflection, self-reference, or so on.
  • The scene is practically surreal. Emma, Zoe, and Owen can’t have this kind of prolonged, rhythmic, and spontaneous wit, any more than they can have prolonged, rhythmic, and spontaneous sword fights. It’s just not something people can suddenly do.
  • The scene is situationally surreal. They’re collecting rocks from tall grass without gloves, work clothes, wheelbarrows, and so on.

In the same way extended fist fights don’t actually happen in the world, extended witty repartee doesn’t happen either. These things are surreal!

Consider:

In real life, there are witty conversations, and there are fist fights—but they’re not extended, rhythmic, unhesitating, and crystal clear to spectators, like they can be in some spectator entertainment.

Once it’s pointed out, it’s obvious that scripted, practiced, stylized, witty conversations aren't straightforward mirrors to the witty conversations that happen in real life.

We'll be talking about this surreality throughout this chapter.

Coherent and surreal. When the audience watches Rage Man punch through concrete in the opening scene of Blood Soul Revenge, they know this is surreal—people can't punch through concrete.

Even though it's surreal, Rage Man's strength can still be coherent if the movie is consistent to its surreal premise. If he can punch through concrete all throughout the movie, it's surreal and coherent—but we wouldn't take this coherence to be the same thing as realism.

It's the same for emotions too. If James Bond is always calm or focused, the emotional premise is that he always has a large OK and small mistake. We know this is surreal—people aren't calm when gambling, fighting, debating, and so on.

At the same time, if the movie is consistent to these surreal evaluatory premises the movie will be emotionally surreal while still be emotionally coherent.

As before, we shouldn't take this coherence to be the same thing as realism. If James Bond is consistently unflappable, that doesn't mean it's realistic—it just means its consistent.

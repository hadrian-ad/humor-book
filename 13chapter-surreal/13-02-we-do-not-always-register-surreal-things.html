Registering to surreal things

We sometimes get so familiar to surreal things that we don’t even register them as they’re surreal.

This is like how editing is surreal, even though we sometimes stop noticing it's there:

Trevor and Yasmin are watching Fired Fireman 2

The lieutenant and Chief are arguing about the secret identity of the Arsonist King as they walk down a hallway in the police station. The scene cuts to them in the lieutenant's car, but their dialogue continues unbroken from where it was in the hallway.

Yasmin: “I love how the movie just cuts as if they paused their conversation for ten minutes while they got into the car!" (←the scene treats time and space surreally by continuing same conversation in different places)

Trevor: *laughs* “I never noticed that before!" (←Trevor has lost track of the surreal elements of spectator entertainment)

No one has ever had a conversation like Chief and the lieutenant, where causality, space, and time warp to allow a conversation to continue unbroken. It’s surreal!

There’s nothing wrong with this surrealness, since the movie’s goal is to generate humor—not be realistic for realism’s sake.

At the same time, it would be wrong to lose track that these comedies are surreal. Otherwise, we'd have a distorted understanding of how the world actually works around us.

If police took grappling cues from professional wrestling matches, they’d be taking spectator entertainment as a template for how to interpersonally interact with others—a disturbing error.

If we watched a musical and thought people were supposed to be able to make a train car full of strangers spontaneously dance, we’d be comparing ourselves to a surreal standard.

In the same way, if we watch a comedy with amazing extended repartee and think it’s a template for how we should interacting with others—we'd also end up in disharmony with how humor and the world works around us.

Seeing surrealness. With practice, we can see how spectator entertainment is surreal. For instance, we can imagine:

  • sitcoms with the laugh track removed—and notice how the cadence of conversation is surreal without it.
  • transcripts of a hilarious night with family or friends—and notice how the transcription doesn’t at all mimic the script of a comedic scene
  • screenplays that took months to write and tune—and notice that movie characters pretend to do all that extemporaneously during conversation
  • comedies with the editing removed, and being just a single camera following a single person—and notice how that changes the flow of the film
  • scenes where a character doesn’t notice someone is obviously hiding something behind their back—and notice that this isn’t how people would usually behave

(and so on)

The goal here isn't to see this surrealness as "bad"—the goal is simply to see surrealness as surrealness.

We can still find surreal things to be meaningful or related to our lives in meaningful ways—without seeing surreal things as straightforward mirrors of life.

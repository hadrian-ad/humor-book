Witty banter is surreal

We don't always notice that witty banter is surreal—but it is!

Owen is watching a clip online from a comedy show about a small town in Canada.

Daryl, Dan, Katy, and Wayne are picking up rocks in a field of tall grass.

Wayne: *popping up out of the grass with armful of rocks* (confessing) “There's been an incident, and I'd like to talk about it.”

Daryl: “Get after it.”

Wayne: *hesitates*

Dan: *encouraging* “Well pitter patter, let's get at 'er.”

Katy: (exasperated) "Spit it out big shoots.”

Wayne: (reluctantly) “I seen Stewart's horn.”

Katy: *taken aback* “What?”

Daryl: “You've seen his impaler?”

Dan “His bobbydangler?”

Wayne: “Well...”

Daryl: “You've seen his PhD?”

Dan: “His WMD?”

Wayne: “Well look fellers...”

Daryl: “You've seen his friendly weapon?”

Dan: “His sticky grenade?”

Katy: “His ground squirrel?”

Wayne: (confirming) “Yeah, I've seen his ground squirrel.”

Daryl: “You've seen Stuart's mill stone?”

Dan: “You've seen his custard slinger?”

Wayne: “Well I'm coming up the stairs, he's coming out the bathroom, naked as a jaybird for some fucking reason… I round the corner and, yeah, I seen his, ah...”

Daryl: “His hollow point?”

Dan: “His hard drive?”

Wayne: (correcting) “Well his floppy disk is what I've seen, yeah.”

Daryl: “Well. What's the evaluation?”

Katy: “Yeah.”

Dan: (curiously) “I'd hear an assessment.”

Wayne: *far away look* “You want to know what. It looked like a tall boy can of red bull hanging there.”

Katy: “No.”

Wayne: “Like a tube of tennis balls hanging there, four pack”

Katy: “Shut up”

Wayne: “Looked like a fucking policeman's flashlight from the 1980s hanging there.”

Katy: “You're joking.”

Wayne: *turns to look at Katy* “That is no joke.”

Daryl: (admiringly) “Good job Stewart.”

Wayne: (politely) “That's what I said, I said ‘good for him’.”

Dan: (with mixed feelings, but politely) “Good for you, Stewart.”

Owen has a broad smile as he watching the exchange, and laughs at the flashlight joke.

Like an action scene, this comedic scene is emotionally, practically, and situationally surreal.

  • The scene is emotionally surreal. No one reacts normally. It would be surreal for Emma, Zoe, and Owen to somehow have an impromptu conversation like this in a field and not show any amazement, amusement, reflection, self-reference, or so on.
  • The scene is practically surreal. Emma, Zoe, and Owen can’t have this kind of prolonged, rhythmic, and spontaneous wit, any more than they can have prolonged, rhythmic, and spontaneous sword fights. It’s just not something people can suddenly do.
  • The scene is situationally surreal. They’re collecting rocks from tall grass without gloves, work clothes, wheelbarrows, and so on.

We can produce this scene with effort, but it won't happen in our lives like this.

In the same way extended fist fights don’t actually happen in the world, extended witty repartee doesn’t happen either. These things are surreal!

Consider:

In real life, there are witty conversations, and there are fist fights—but they’re not extended, rhythmic, unhesitating, and crystal clear to spectators, like they can be in some spectator entertainment.

Once it’s pointed out, it’s obvious that scripted, practiced, stylized, witty conversations aren't straightforward mirrors to the witty conversations that happen in real life.

Evolution. Since spectator entertainment prioritizes the evaluations of spectators, there's an evolution of everything in it to orient around us—everything from the character design to the camera angles to the sets evolve toward our evaluations.

In some ways, we can think of a lot of spectator entertainment as a kind of yes man—someone who is trying to get into our good graces even at the cost of relevance to reality.

Some things don't evolve for spectators, but for participants—while the design of Disney World evolves for spectators, that's not always the case of residential streets, which evolves for all users. These differing priorities make them evolve differently.

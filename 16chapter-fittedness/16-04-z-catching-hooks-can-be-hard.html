It can be hard to catch hooks

Hooks can sometimes be hard to catch:

Trevor: *mouth suddenly dry* “So… I weighed myself the other day. I’ve lost ten pounds.” (←Trevor shares an achievement↔︎hook to help him feel achievement)

Minh: “Oh?” (←Minh is unsure how to take Trevor’s comment)

Trevor: *embarrassed* “Yeah.” (←Trevor stops offering hook)

Trevor was trying to offer a hook, but didn’t contextualize it enough for Minh—Minh wasn’t sure what Trevor was looking for.

Consider:

There was trouble from both ends—Trevor didn't explicitly offer enough of a hook at the same time Minh didn't have enough practice to spot the implicit hook.

Other times, we're used to prioritizing hooks for ourselves. Sometimes, when someone is uncomfortable, we become uncomfortable—and then we try to make ourselves comfortable instead of them.

Jennifer: (sad) "I keep thinking of things I never told mom." (←Jennifer shares a regret↔︎hook to help her feel acceptance, belonging, connection, reconciliation, and so on)

Sam: (uncomfortable) *trying to think of something to say* "Like your criticisms of her home decor?" (←Sam tries to make himself feel comfort more than he tries to make Jennifer feel acceptance or connection)

Jennifer: (sad) *smiles* "Yeah."

Closeness. Fitted humor attempts can seem frightening if they seem difficult or high-stakes. Sometimes, we're not used to initiating and sustaining emotional connection with others, and we shy away from it—like Sam, who recognized a hook for fitted humor, but pruned away the fitted elements in his rely.

Things can be extra hard too if we think humor like acceptance, belonging, reconciliation, and so on require eloquence and wit—instead of things that sometimes require holding hands or simply being sincere. ("She was so lucky to have you as a daughter.")

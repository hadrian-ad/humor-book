It can be hard to catch hooks

We sometimes don't catch hooks:

Trevor: *mouth suddenly dry* “So… I weighed myself the other day. I’ve lost ten pounds.” (←Trevor shares an achievement↔︎hook to help him feel achievement)

Minh: “Oh?” (←Minh is unsure how to take Trevor’s comment)

Trevor: *embarrassed* “Yeah.” (←Trevor stops offering hook)

Trevor was trying to offer a hook, but didn’t contextualize it enough for Minh—Minh wasn’t sure what Trevor was looking for.

Consider:

There was trouble from both ends—Trevor didn't explicitly offer enough of a hook at the same time Minh didn't have enough practice to spot the implicit hook.

Other times, we're used to prioritizing hooks for ourselves. So if someone else is uncomfortable, we become uncomfortable—and then we try to make ourselves comfortable instead of them:

Jennifer: (sad) "I keep thinking of things I never told mom." (←Jennifer shares a regret↔︎hook to help her feel acceptance, belonging, connection, reconciliation, and so on)

Sam: (uncomfortable) *trying to think of something to say* "Like your criticisms of her home decor?" (←Sam tries to make himself feel comfort more than he tries to make Jennifer feel acceptance or connection)

Jennifer: (sad) *smiles* "Yeah."

Above, Sam doesn't prioritize generating humor in Jennifer. He instead says something that would generate humor for him—which leads to a dividing moment between them.

Closeness. If we're not used to initaiting and sustaining emotional connection with others, it can feel difficult, frightening, or high stakes to try to generate fitted humor.

This can make us shy away from them—like Sam, who recognized a moment for fitted humor (a need for acceptance, connection, closeness, reconciliation, safety, and so on)—but took what he saw was an easier route, which ended up not meeting the moment.

Things can be extra hard too if we think humor like acceptance, belonging, reconciliation, and so on require eloquence and wit—like Sam trying to be witty. Instead, fitted humor can often be generated simply by being sincere or holding hands. ("She was so lucky to have you as a daughter.")

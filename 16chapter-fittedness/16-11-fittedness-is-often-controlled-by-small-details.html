Small details often control fittedness

Small details can make a big difference in how fitted a humor attempt is:

Sam: *to drama team* “I remember when I was cast as the slug in my fifth-grade play about compost…” *crowd chuckles* (←anecdote is mostly irrelevant to drama team)

- OR -

Sam: *to drama team* “You guys are lucky you have so much talent—I remember when I was cast as the slug in our fifth-grade play about compost…” *crowd laughs* (←anecdote is framed in a way that’s more relevant to drama team)

Above, a small prelude (“you guys are so lucky”) sets up a comparison that helps Sam's audience feel humor family words—which gives Sam’s comment greater topical relevance and fittedness.

We can use a quantification metaphor to graph the difference in fittedness:

Small differences in presentation can make a large difference in fittedness.

Differences. A small difference isn't the same thing as an unimportant difference. This is obvious with screws. When we need to be precise, a millimeter of difference is the difference between something working and something not working.

It's the same for fittedness, where a word or small inflection is the difference between something being fitted and something being unfitted—the "millimeter" adjustments that determine whether a humor attempt fits or not.

These small differences are often the controlling factor in what's fitted or not. These small details can seem finicky and hard to manage at first. But they're not uniquely finicky—it's actually no more or less finicky than everything else. We work under the neccessity for small details in screws, music rhythms, proper spelling, pronuciation, matching paint colors, and so on.

Like every skill, the "finickiness" slowly disappears as it increasingly becomes naturalized into our intuitive understanding within complicated systems.

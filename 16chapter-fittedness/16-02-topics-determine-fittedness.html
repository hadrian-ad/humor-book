The Humor Book

Topics determine fittedness

Fittedness increases when humor or humor attempts have relevant topics. When they have irrelevant topics, the fittedness decreases:

Artem is waiting for Alina at her lab. Hana asks if he’s related to Alina, and, shyly, he says yes.

Hana: (appreciatively) “Alina is really good at what she does.”

Artem: (awkwardly) “Yeah.”

There’s a moment of silence while Artem tries to think of something to say.

Artem: “You know why elevators like libraries so much?”

Hana: “Why?”

Artem: “Because libraries have so many stories.” (←joke on irrelevant topics makes humor attempt less fitted)

Hana: *smiles* “Haha. Good one.”

- OR -

Artem: “She is a great DJ!” (←Artem generates humor while continuing a topic (‘Alina’) that Hana expressed interest in)

Hana: *laughs*

- OR -

Artem: “You must be good at what you do too, since you’re here! That’s so cool that you guys do all this cool stuff here!” (←Artem includes other relevant topics like ‘Hana,’ ‘electrical engineering’, ‘lab,’ and so on, and tries to make Hana feel achievement)

Hana: *smiles* “Haha, it’s not that impressive, but thanks.” (←Hana feels achievement)

Attempts. In this book, “humor” is an emotion, while a “humor attempt” is an action that tries to generate humor.

A joke can exist inertly in a book. The humor attempt would be using that joke—inclusive of the delivery, intention, situation, and so on. In this case, the joke would just be a part of the humor attempt:

So if a hundred people tell the same joke, they're using one joke but making a hundred different humor attempts&mbash;since no one would be talking to the same person; using the same delivery; speaking at the same time of day; and so on.

Anything that intends someone to feel humor is a humor attempt. Bringing our parents tea is a humor attempt—because we want them to smile and experience belonging, comfort, delight, and so on. It's not a "joke"—but the tea is involved in a humor attempt.

A humor attempt may or may not actually generate any humor. We may bring our parents tea, hoping to make them smile—but they may ignore us, or cry or shout in anger. It's still a humor attempt (we wanted them to experience belonging, comfort, and so on)—just a humor attempt that didn't generate humor.

We can think about the fittedness of both humor (an emotion) and humor attempts (an action). We use the same process for finding the fittedness of both. For instance, we looked at the fittedess of Artem's humor attempts with Hana using topics—which can be done no matter what Artem or Hana feel in result.

The Humor Book

Topics determine fittedness

Fittedness increases when humor or humor attempts have relevant topics. Fittedness decreases when they have irrelevant topics:

Artem is waiting for Alina at her lab. Hana asks if he’s related to Alina, and, shyly, he says yes.

Hana: (appreciatively) “Alina is really good at what she does.”

Artem: (awkwardly) “Yeah.”

There’s a moment of silence while Artem tries to think of something to say.

Artem: “You know why elevators like libraries so much?”

Hana: “Why?”

Artem: “Because libraries have so many stories.” (←joke on irrelevant topics makes humor attempt less fitted)

Hana: *smiles* “Haha. Good one.”

- OR -

Artem: “She is a great DJ!” (←Artem generates humor while continuing topic ‘Alina’)

Hana: *laughs*

- OR -

Artem: “You must be good at what you do too, since you’re here! That’s so cool that you guys do all this cool stuff here!” (←Artem includes other relevant topics like ‘Hana,’ ‘electrical engineering’, ‘lab,’ and so on, and tries to make Hana feel achievement)

Hana: *smiles* “Haha, it’s not that impressive, but thanks.” (←Hana feels achievement)

Consider:

Above, we're just looking at fittedness of a humor attempts above—not intensity of humor.

Hana feels roughly the same intensity of humor in each example. But even though the intensity stayed the same, the fittedness varied. That's because fittedness and intensity are two different measures, which can change independent of each other.

Attempts. In this book, humor is an emotion, while a humor attempt is an action that tries to generate humor.

A joke can exist inertly in a book. The humor attempt would be using that joke—inclusive of the content, delivery, intention, situation, and so on. In this case, the joke would just be a part of the humor attempt:

So if a hundred people tell the same joke, they're using one joke but making a hundred different humor attempts—since each person using the same joke would be:

  • talking to a different person
  • using a different inflection
  • have a different relation to the joke
  • speaking at different times of day

(and so on)

Anything that intends someone to feel humor is a humor attempt. So bringing our parents tea is a humor attempt—so long as we want them to smile or loosen and ease tension and experience belonging, comfort, delight, and so on. Bringing someone tea isn't a "joke"—but the tea is involved in a humor attempt anyway.

A humor attempt may or may not actually generate any humor. We may bring our parents tea, hoping to make them smile—but they may ignore us, or cry or shout in anger. In this case, it's still a humor attempt (we wanted them to experience belonging, comfort, and so on)—just a humor attempt that didn't generate humor.

Consider:

We can use the same process to find the fittedness of both humor (the emotion) and humor attempts (the action).

We looked at the fittedness of Artem's humor attempts with Hana. We can do this whether or not Artem or Hana actually feel humor.

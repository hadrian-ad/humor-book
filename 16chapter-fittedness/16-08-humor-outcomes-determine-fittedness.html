The Humor Book

Humor outcomes determine fittedness

In addition to topics and humor family words, humor outcomes also contribute to fittedness.

Humor outcomes

acceptably criticize, achieve catharsis, avoid conflict, change negative emotions into positive ones, control a situation, create rapport, deal with taboo topics, ease tensions, give new perspectives, handle complaints, help one become accepted, make a point, open up a new topic of conversation, reduce defensiveness, reveal a personality trait, reveal a personal view, support an argument, survive in high stress situations, symbolize close ties.

Humor outcomes help us frame questions about fittedness with narrative clarity.

Instead of asking “when does humor fit?” we can ask more tangible questions—like “when does avoiding conflict fit?” or handling a complaint, reducing defensiveness, symbolizing close ties, and so on.

Justin’s walking out of the English department when Dave catches him.

Dave: *smiling* “Justin! The department’s going to get some drinks tonight. You should come!”

Justin: *shaking head* *smiling* "Thanks, but I’d better pass. The mountain of sobriety, you know, Tantalus and Sisyphus. Maybe another event!” (←Justin reveals a personality trait)

Dave: *laughs* “Sure, I get it. Maybe another time, another event then!”

In this example, Justin wants to reveal a personality trait that he’s sober. With enough situational relevance, Dave will see this humor attempt as fitted:

But revealing a personal trait isn’t the only humor outcome in this example—Dave also sees Justin’s humor attempt as trying to avoid conflict (while declining an invitation), deal with a taboo topic (alcoholism), support an argument (that he has a valid excuse to decline), and so on:

By comparison, if Justin makes a funny voice and goes “NOOOooooOO… thank you,” Dave wouldn't describe that humor attempt as dealing with taboo topics or avoiding conflict—and so would be less fitted.

Consider:

Dave’s humor in response to Justin’s humor attempt is also fitted. (*laughs* "sure, I get it.") Dave wasn’t laughing at content, like alcoholism—Dave was laughing because he reached humor outcomes.

Dave felt an argument was supported, he gained a new perspective, he feels Justin revealed a personality trait, he feels rapport has been created, and so on.

The more fitted a humor attempt is, the more informationally dense and multipurpose it is. This means fitted humor starts doing things that can't be done as efficiently any other way.

This efficiency is part of what makes others see humor as fitted:

Carl picks up his sister Sara at the bus station for the funeral. They drive in silence. Finally, Carl says profoundly: “Elias says the world is a place we prove real by dying in it.”

Sara thinks about that.

Then Carl grins: “What an asshole!”

Sara laughs. (←Carl and Sara have achieved catharsis, eased tension, reduced defensiveness, avoided conflict, created rapport, opened up a new topic of conversation, dealt with taboo topics)

Trevor: *shopping online* “Should we order it or not?”

Yasmin: “I just can’t decide! I just love StormConQuest, but the reviews on the expansion are just so mixed. They say StormConQuest: Seas of Drake and Myrrh really simplifies the resource management. What if that ruins it?”

Trevor: *out of the blue* “What do you think about having a kid?” (←Trevor wants to use humor to open a new topic of conversation, reduce defensiveness, reveal a personal view, deal with a taboo topic)

Yasmin: *laughs* “Oh god, Trevor.” (←Yasmin’s laugh shows a new topic has been opened, that she’s dealing with a taboo topic, is easing tensions, revealing a personal view)

Since Yasmin sees Trevor's humor attempt here as efficiently doing many relevant things at once, she sees it as fitted.

Humor outcomes can describe the absence of humor

Humor outcomes can be used to describe both the presence and absence of humor in different situations:

Justin and Heather go biking through the cemetery every weekend. They stop to ponder a new sign that says “Cremation? Do we have a place FOR YOU!”

Heather: *smiles* “Why do I want that cremation spot so bad now?” (←Heather feels the sign created rapport, deals with taboo topics, opens a new topic of conversation, and so on)

Justin: *frowns* "What are they going here exactly? Reverse psychology? Or is it just tone deaf?" (←Justin does not feel the sign created rapport, dealt with taboo topics, opened a new topic of conversation, and so on)

Zoe is backpacking in Brazil. She stays at a hostel. As she cooks, she examines the composting sign. She wants to be a good guest, but the instructions are complex. The header clarifies the instructions by saying “think of our compost as vegan.”

It makes Zoe smiles. (←Zoe feels an argument was supported and her tension was eased)

But it makes her backpacking friend huff with annoyance.

Giulia: (annoyed) "Egg shell aren't vegan." (←Guilia does not feel any argument was supported and does not feel her tension was eased)

Minh is super stressed about a graphic design project. He can’t pick the right type for his website. But in the hallway, he comes across a student poster.

Poster: “Take care of yourself and your type will follow.”

It's an odd phrase. Minh laughs, seeing it. (←Minh feels tension has been eased and rapport was created, he’s surviving high stress situation, achieved catharsis, and so on)

Later, Owen is walking down the hall, relaxed and happy. He sees the poster and smiles at the odd sentence and continues on his way. (←Owen does not feel humor the same way Minh does)

Above, Minh and Owen feel humor at the same thing for different reasons. Minh feels the poster eases the tension (he has about decision-making), while Owen feels the poster creates rapport (by being empathetic).

At base, humor outcomes are narrative descriptions of mistakes and OKs balancing. So Owen won't feel any empathetic poster creates rapport, just because it's empathetic—he only feels rapport if his mistakes and OKs balance.

If university hallways are usually sterile and cold, empathetic posters from fellow students will both deviate from his expectations (mistake) at the same time he feels secure from negative personal consequences (OK).

By contrast, if Owen walks through a kindergarten every day, he'll expect empathetic posters (small mistake)—and he'll feel posters for young children have little negative consequence to him (large OK).

In this case, Owen won't feel an empathetic poster in a kindergarten creates rapport, simply because it's empathetic.

Ties. As we evaluate the world around us, we entwine ourselves to it—we become emotionally woven to everything around us.

These emotional relations can be hard to pick apart—like having tangled wires that are constastly flowing into different configurations.

We can use humor outcomes to make narrative sense of some of these configurations—helping us sort through our active, fluid, and always-present evaluatory ties to things.

At this point, we’ve gone over the five interchangeable ways we’ll be describing humor in this book.

These are different scales of looking at humor—from the highest scale (the general word “humor”) to the smallest scale (“mistakes and OKs”):

  • Humor as an overall pattern (“humor”)
  • Humor as experiences (humor family words)
  • Humor as actions, intentions, or consequences (humor outcomes)
  • Humor as expressive behavior (humor tells)
  • Humor as information-assessments (mistakes and OKs)

These are all different scales of talking about the same thing.

We pick a scale or scales to focus on based on what's useful to us. Sometimes we want to pull back for a bigger picture view, and other times we want to zoom in for smaller details.

This is why any of our examples can be annotated with any combination of mistakes and OKs, humor tells, humor outcomes, humor family words, or the word humor:

Zoe is browsing the bookstore when she picks up a book on meditation. She reads the book title: Wherever You Go, There You Are.

She thinks about that for a second, puzzled. Then something clicks, and she gives an amused nose-exhale. (←a mistake is OK↔︎laughs↔︎gain a new perspective↔︎insight, realization↔︎humor)

Assessments (mistakes and OKs)↔︎behavior (humor tells)↔︎actions (humor outcomes)↔︎states (humor family word)↔︎overall pattern (humor)

Santiago and Damien are walking through the halls.

Santiago: “Well. We lost every game but two this season. I feel like that’s some kind of achievement!”

Damien: *laughs* “Uh-huh, sure man.” (←mistakes are OK↔︎laughs, brightens, loosening, smiling, focusing on present, raised spirits, social integration↔︎argument was supported, created rapport, eased tension, gained new perspectives, been acceptably criticized, symbolized close ties↔︎acceptance, connection, self-forgiveness, insight, self-reconciliation, release, relief, success↔︎humor)

Assessments (mistakes and OKs)↔︎behavior (humor tells)↔︎actions (humor outcomes)↔︎states (humor family word)↔︎overall pattern (humor)

These are all different ways of describing humor! Throughout this book, we’ll be choosing among these options based on what we think would be helpful to growing our intuitive understanding of things. And sometimes, we’ll use multiple scales of humor for a greater range of perspective.

Humor can be described as actions or outcomes. This lets us think narratively about humor using more ordinary language.

  • We'll use an arbitrarily limited list of humor outcomes in this book: acceptably criticize, achieve catharsis, avoid conflict, change negative emotions into positive ones, control a situation, create rapport, deal with taboo topics, ease tensions, give new perspectives, handle complaints, help one become accepted, open up a new topic of conversation, reduce defensiveness, reveal a personality trait, reveal a personal view, support an argument, survive in high stress situations, symbolize close ties.
  • Since humor outcomes are humor, humor outcomes also have intensity and duration.
  • Humor outcomes all overlap and redescribe the same thing: humor. That means we can use any humor outcomes, which help us see emotional situations in new light.

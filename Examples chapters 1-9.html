When we see many pictures of the same person, we start acclimating to their humor profile—their overall tendencies around how they express humor, which are particular to them:

One method of remembering someone's face is to remember them feeling humor. Since their humor profile is something that makes them them, it's an effective focal point for recall—someone's experience of belonging, connection, joy, relief, and so on make durable impressions on us.

Sometimes we try to recall a blank, "passport photo" memory of a face. But since we almost never see anyone blank-faced, this is a double task—not just remembering a face, but inventing an expression on it!

We can practice this method. Can we recall the faces of our seventh grade teachers by focusing on memories of them smiling? What about the coworkers at our first job? What about politicians from decades ago? How about our siblings at different ages?

9.4

The above portrait is of some people living in the Russian Empire in 1914. We can use this picture to think about how humor tells are identifiably the same, even though humor tells are shaped by circumstances.

The men in the middle feel relatively intense humor—we can see this from their smile, expansive posture (relative to the others), touching self (by crossing legs or putting hands in lap or on legs), touching others, energy, brightened, loose (legs and arms), and so on.

The one on the right feels the least humor relative to the others—he's not smiling, not touching self, has hunched (not expansive) posture, not brightening, has low energy, and so on.

These are all the same humor tells we use throughout this book. At the same time, these humor tells are also distinctive to a particular time and place—since physical aspects of our environment influence which humor tells we use when.

For instance, the backless bench doesn't allow them to slouch backward, so they can't lean back the way they might in a sofa or an armchair. And since it's a bench, they can sit close enough to link arms—which people are more likely to be doing if benches like this are common.

Similarly, their jackets discourage them from raising their arms—since they'd crumple and lift. If our clothing makes it harder for us to put our arms around someone's shoulders—like if we frequently wear dress frames, epaulets, puffy sleeves, or so on—we'll minimize putting arms around shoulders as a method of touching others.

By contrast, if we're just wearing t-shirts, it'll be easy to put our arms around someone's shoulder or waist—something we often see among sports teams, or how arms sometimes rest on shoulders at casual cookouts with simple clothing.

That means how we touch others depends on common furniture, what we and others frequently wear, and so on. If we grew up with backless benches and people wearing jackets, a certain way of touching others will become normal—like linking arms. If we grew up with individual chairs and simple, sleeveless clothing, we way may not.

So the same humor tells (touching others, social tightening) takes on distinctive characteristics place-by-place, person-by-person, time-by-time—often influenced by clothing, manufacturing, resources, weather, and so on.

The Humor Book

Spectator humor profiles are shaped by constraints

There are a few reason live actors use spectator humor profiles:

We can't act out humor

There's a physical limitation—most actors can’t convincingly act medium-intensity or higher humor on command!

Consider:

If we watch a video of our friend doubled over with laughter, and then are asked to perfectly imitate our friend's laughter—we can't!

This means we'll adjust how we pretend to have humor to what we're physically capable of doing.

Real humor takes too long

For pacing reasons, most movies don't want realistic durations of humor.

Consider:

When we laugh in conversation, the laughter may take up a 25% of the conversation—but most movies don't want 25% of a scene to be taken up by laughing!

This means we'll adjust how we pretend to have humor to fit wider objectives around pacing.

Humor changes how we look

When we give genuine smiles, our skin crinkles, our lips rise toward our nose, our eyes change shape, dimples form, and so on. These can go against a movie's stylistic goals.

Consider:

Some movies rarely lets actors or characters blink on screen, let alone scrunch their eyes when they smile. These movies are making a choice to subordinate realism for a stylistic effect.

This means we'll pretend to have humor in a way that also fits certain aesthetic goals.

What's appealing gets scrambled

In real life, we find genuine, broad smiles appealing. But that doesn't mean we feel the same way about people giving broad smiles in spectator entertainment.

Consider:

We can immediately tell which picture is of the actor's spectator humor profile, and which is the actor's interpersonal humor profile. But what we'd prefer to see in a movie and what we'd prefer to see in real life aren't always aligned:

If we were talking to this man at a bus stop, we'd prefer he give an interpersonal smile—since his smile would show he's feeling connection, comfort, delight, and so on.

That means we'll pretend to have humor in a way that audiences expect and enjoy to see when watching a surreal movie.

All of these factors shape spectator humor profiles—the humor tells we do or don't see in movies.

Faults. It's not a criticism to point out that movies don't mirror emotions perfectly. That's because the priority of a movie is the emotional response of the audience, not emotional realism for its own sake. If we like the scoundrel character, it doesn't matter if their smirk isn't realistic.

This is obvious in other cases, like when people think it's a criticism to point out something unscientific in a movie. ("You can't hear noises in space, so that made it a bad movie.") That's because the goal of most movies is the emotional response of the audience, not realism for realism's sake. ("That space battle was heartpounding!")

In general, we criticize movies when the emotional handling feels off, not when the realism feels off—since the realism is often neither here nor there.

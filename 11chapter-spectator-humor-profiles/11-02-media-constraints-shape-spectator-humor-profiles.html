The Humor Book

Constraints on representation

There are a few reason spectator humor profiles are the way they are for live actors:

We can't act out humor

There's a physical limitation—we can’t convincingly act medium-intensity or higher humor on command!

Consider:

If we watch a video of our friend doubled over with laughter, and then are asked to perfectly imitate our friend's laughter—we can't!

This means actors will adjust how they pretend to have humor to what they're physically capable of doing.

This is supported by directors and screenwriters, who won't ask their actors to do what they can't do well.

Real humor takes too long

For pacing reasons, most movies don't want realistic durations of humor.

Consider:

When we laugh in conversation, the laughter may take up a 25% of the conversation—but most movies don't want 25% of a scene to be taken up by laughing.

This means actors, screenwriters, directors, and editors will adjust how humor is pretended to fit wider objectives around pacing.

Humor changes how we look

When we give genuine smiles, our skin crinkles, our lips rise toward our nose, our eyes change shape, dimples form, and so on. These can go against a variety of goals in a movie.

The style of many movies are for very smooth faces—which often seem standard and correct to us when seen on film.

Consider:

Some movies rarely lets actors or characters blink on screen, let alone scrunch their eyes when they smile. These movies are making a stylistic choice— because the goal isn't realism for realism's sake.

What's appealing gets scrambled

What we'd prefer to see in a movie and what we'd prefer to see in real life aren't always aligned:

If someone smirked as they recieved an award in real life, we'd find it off-putting. If they gave a broad smile instead, we'd be pleased and happy for them.

But this can be inverted in movies—we'd enjoy the characterization of the smirk, and be nonplussed by a normal broad smile.

This is obvious when we take pictures for social media or for professional shots. What is appealing to look at doesn't always feel natural to take.

We are creating spectator entertainment—we are prioritizing how the audience feels watching over how we feel taking the picture.

Of course, watching a movie isn't the same thing as meeting someone at an event—which leads to spectater entertainment having a diverging humor profile.

Movies prioritize their own goals

Movies—or any spectator entertainment—design their presentation of humor to fit their goals.

In Pride and Prejudice, the humor is part of the storytelling. The movie has Jane pretend more realistic humor early on in order to:

  • to make her feel relatable
  • to show her connection to her sister
  • contrast with the formal and awkward Mr. Darcy
  • to create a baseline for the audience to measure later emotional changes against

(and so on)

At the conclusion, Jane and Mr. Darcy share smiles and Jane has an unrealistic smile in order to:

  • make her look conventionally pretty to spectators
  • complete an arc toward maturity
  • settle towards a happily-ever-after tone
  • closer match to Mr. Darcy's emotional cadence

and so on

The creators of this movie made choices for Jane's presentation of humor for reasons beyond realism for realism's sake.

All of these factors shape spectator humor profiles—the humor tells we do or don't see in movies.

Consider:

We looked at movies, which are straightforward examples of spectator entertainment. But we can use movies as a baseline to see how this is something we encounter everywhere.

This includes the profile pictures of our friends, creators of video essays, speakers for motivational events, teachers giving an energetic lecture, celebrities having photo shoots, and so on.

These are often emotionally-mixed things, where spectator humor profiles and personal humor profiles blur or interplay with each other.

Faults. There's nothing wrong with movies for using spectator humor profiles—any more than there's something wrong with portraits using paint.

Instead, we can be wrong if we start confusing spectator entertainment as being a straightforward mirror to real life.

This can lead to odd criticisms of spectator entertainment (like saying it's emotionally unrealistic even though that was never the goal) or odd expectations of the world around us (like assuming Pride and Prejudice accurately depicts how people in love smile at each other).

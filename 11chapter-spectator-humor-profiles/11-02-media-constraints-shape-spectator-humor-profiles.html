Constraints on representation

There are many reasons spectator humor profiles are they way they are in movies. In addition to physicaly limitations (we can't convincingly produce high-intensity humor on demand), it's often that the priorities aren't on realism for realism's sake.

A movie may:

  • Prioritize camera shots. If a movie wants a clear shot of a face, the director will instruct the actor not to put their hands near their heads when pretending to laugh. This prevents a common humor tell from being represented in many movies.
  • Prioritize pacing. When we laugh in conversation, the laughter may take up 25% of the conversation—but for pacing reasons, movies don't want laughter to take up 25% of a scene! This shapes how actors, screenwriters, directors, and editors portray humor.

There's nothing wrong with movies using spectator humor profiles—for the same reason there's nothing wrong with portraits using paint. Without paint, it wouldn't be a painting!

Instead, we can be wrong if we assume that movies are straightforward mirrors of real life. Movies have constraints and goals that aren't simply realism for realism's sake.

Social media. Successful photos for social media or business promotion often look better than they feel to take. We prioritize how the viewers feel looking at our picture over how we feel taking the picture.

A candid picture of us grinning on our futon may be very accurate—but this kind of accuracy isn't the goal of many photographs. Instead, the photographer may tell us to cross our arms and tilt our torso in a way that feels stilted, but that is recieved positively by viewers.

This is a familiar instance of a spectator humor profile—which is similar to how actors feel representing humor.

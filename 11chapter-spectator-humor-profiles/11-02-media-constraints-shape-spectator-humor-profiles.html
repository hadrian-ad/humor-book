Constraints on representation

Why don't live movies always have vivid, realistic humor in movies? There are several reasons, which all lead to a specific style of pretended humor in movies:

We can't act out humor

There's a physical limitation—we can’t convincingly act medium-intensity or higher humor on command!

Consider:

If we watch a video of our friend doubled over with laughter, and then are asked to perfectly imitate our friend's laughter—we can't!

Actors, directors, and screenwriters will produce scenes that actors are physically capable of doing.

Real humor takes too long

For pacing reasons, most movies don't want realistic durations of humor—so actors, screenwriters, directors, and editors will adjust how actors pretend humor.

Consider:

When we laugh in conversation, the laughter may take up a 25% of the conversation—but most movies don't want 25% of a scene to be taken up by laughing.

Humor changes how we look

When we give genuine smiles, our skin crinkles, our lips rise toward our nose, our nose moves, our eyes change shape, dimples form, and so on:

Whether a movie wants this contortion or not is a stylistic choice—and the style for many movies are for smooth faces, which often seem standard and correct to us.

Consider:

Some movies rarely lets actors or characters blink on screen, let alone scrunch their eyes when they smile.

These movies are making a stylistic choice—which all movies do, since the priority is never realism for realism's sake.

What's appealing gets scrambled

What we want to see in a movie and in real life aren't always aligned:

In a movie, it's charming to see the roguish character smirk as they recieve an award. But in real life, it would be off-putting if someone smirked as they recieved an award.

Similarly, we may find the smile below to be appealing (attractive, glamorous, magical, romantic, and so on) in a movie, but would be a bit disturbing in real life:

We can try making this smile in a mirror, and imagine how people would react to us smiling at them like this! Even so, in the context of the spectator entertainment, it's enjoyable.

That's because spectator entertainment often inverts what's enjoyable in real life—a smirk is fun in a movie (and annoying in real life), just like a carefully composed half-smile is charming in a movie (and a bit unsettling in real life).

Consider:

This is obvious when we take pictures for social media or for business websites. What is appealing to look at doesn't always feel natural to take.

However, like in any spectator entertainment, we are goal-oriented. We are prioritizing how the audience feels looking at our picture over how we feel taking the picture.

Movies prioritize their own goals

Movies—or any spectator entertainment—design their presentation of humor for the emotional ends of their audience. We can see this in a movie like Pride and Prejudice:

Early in the movie, the actor pretends more realistic humor in order to:

  • make Jane feel relatable
  • establish Jane's ease and freedom with laughing
  • to show her close connection to her sister
  • contrast with the formal and awkward Mr. Darcy
  • set her emotional baseline for the rest of the story to measure against

(and so on)

At the conclusion, when Jane and Mr. Darcy share smiles, the production team has the actor linger in the shot with an unrealistic smile in order to:

  • let the audience take in the actor's face
  • draw out an emotional note for pacing
  • support the soundtrack
  • complete an arc toward a mature happiness
  • move the story toward a happily-ever-after conclusion
  • better match the character's emotional cadence to show closeness

(and so on)

The creators of this movie didn't just tell the actor to smile and laugh. Instead, they told her to smile and laugh in specific ways that harmonized with overall goals.

All of these factors shape spectator humor profiles in movies—how humor is or isn't pretended in movies.

Consider:

Movies are straightforward examples of spectator entertainment. But we can use movies as a baseline to see how this is something we encounter everywhere:

  • the profile pictures of our friends
  • creators of video essays
  • speakers for motivational events
  • teachers giving an energetic lecture
  • celebrities having photo shoots

    • (and so on)

Spectator humor profiles and personal humor profiles tend to blur or interplay with each other in these cases.

Faults. There's nothing wrong with movies for using spectator humor profiles—for the same reason there's nothing wrong with portraits using paint.

Instead, we can be wrong if we start thinking that spectator entertainment is a supposed to be a straightforward mirror to real life.

If a movie doesn't claim to be emotionally realistic, then criticizing them for being unrealistic can speak worse of the critic than the movie—a bit like criticizing Star Wars for its depiction of technology.

For the same reason, thinking a movie like Pride and Prejudice accurately depicts how people in love smile at each other can lead strange expectations—a bit like expecting warfare in space to look like Star Wars.

We can relate positively or negatively to spectator entertainment while at the same time knowing it's not intended as a straightforward mirror to reality.

Media constraints shape spectator humor profiles

There are a few reason live actors use spectator humor profiles:

We can't act out humor

There's a physical limitation—most actors can’t convincingly act medium-intensity or higher humor on command!

Consider:

If we watch a video of our friend doubled over with laughter, and are asked to do it on command—we can't!

Real humor takes too long

For pacing reasons, most movies don't want realistic durations of humor. In actual conversations with laughter, the laughter may take up a large proportion of the conversation. But most movies don't want 25% of a scene to be taken up by someone laughing!

Humor changes how we look

When we give genuine smiles, our skin crinkles, our lips rise toward our nose, our eyes change shape, dimples form, and so on. These can go against a movie's stylistic goals.

Some movies rarely lets actors or characters blink on screen, let alone scrunch their eyes when they smile. These movies are making a choice to subordinate realism for a stylistic effect.

What's appealing gets scrambled

In real life, we find people giving genuine broad smiles appealing. But that doesn't mean we feel the same way about people giving broad smiles in spectator entertainment.

In the contrasting pictures below, we can immediately tell which one is the actor's spectator humor profile, and which is the actor's interpersonal humor profile. One looks good in a movie, the other would be appealing in real life—but not necessarily vice versa.

If we were talking to the actor at a bus stop, we'd prefer he smile like he is on the right—since his smile would show he's feeling connection, comfort, delight, and so on.

But when we’re simply looking at a still picture, the broad smile looks a bit odd. It's not how we're used to characters expressing humor in movies.

All of these factors shape spectator humor profiles—the humor tells we do or don't see in movies.

Faults. We can point out something scientifically unrealistic in a movie—but just pointing this out doesn't mean it's good criticism of the movie. ("You can't hear noises in space, so any movie that has sound happening in space is a bad movie.") That's because the goal of most movies is the emotional handling of the audience, not realism for realism's sake. ("That space battle was heartpounding!")

It's the same for emotions. It's not a criticism to point out that movies don't mirror emotions perfectly. ("No one smirks like that in real life.") That's because the priority is the emotional handling of the audience, not emotional realism for realism's sake. ("The scoundrel was my favorite character.")

In general, we criticize movies when the emotional handling feels off, not when the "realism" feels off—since the realism is often neither here nor there.

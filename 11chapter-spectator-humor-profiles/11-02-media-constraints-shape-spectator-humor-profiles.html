The Humor Book

Constraints on representation

Spectator humor profiles are shaped by physical limitations (actors can't convincingly produce high-intensity humor on demand)—but there's more shaping how humor is conveyed in movies.

Some humor tells interfere with camera shots

If a movie wants a clear shot of a face, the director will instruct the actor not to put their hands near their heads when pretending to laugh. This prevents a common humor tell from being represented in many movies.

Real humor takes too long

When we laugh in conversation, the laughter may take up 25% of the conversation—but for pacing reasons, movies don't want laughter to take up 25% of a scene! This shapes how actors, screenwriters, directors, and editors portray humor.

That's because what we enjoy is often inverted between spectator entertainment and real life. A smirk is fun in a movie (but infuriating in real life), just like an open-eyed, tight smile is nice in a movie (but unsettling in real life).

This is obvious when we take pictures that are successful on social media or for business websites. What's an appealing photo for others to look at doesn't always feel natural to take.

But because we're spectator-oriented, we prioritize how the audience feels looking at our picture over how we feel taking the picture. This is essentially how actors feel when representing humor.

These are examples of how constraints and goals lead to a distinctive "movie" humor profile.

Faults. There's nothing wrong with movies using spectator humor profiles—for the same reason there's nothing wrong with portraits using paint.

If we think movies are "supposed" to be emotionally realistic, we can invent a goalpost for criticism—or else draw off-base conclusions about how people behave.

Pride and Prejudice isn't trying to document how people in love look at each other, any more than Star Wars is trying to predict what space warfare will be like.

That's okay, because Pride and Prejudice isn't trying to be emotionally realistic any more than Star Wars is trying to be scientifically accurate.

As movies, they are engaging their audience—and aren't straightforward mirrors of life.

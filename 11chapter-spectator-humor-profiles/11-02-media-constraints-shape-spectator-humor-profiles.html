Constraints on representation

Sometimes we think movies are supposed to show realistic humor—but movies aren't trying to be realistic for realism's sake.

For instance:

It's obvious that Star Wars isn't trying to present realistic space battles for realism's sake—realism isn't the priority!

For exactly the same reason, movies aren't trying to present realistic emotions for realism's sake—realism also isn't the priority.

When a movie is made, a variety of goals will have to be balenced—shaping how actors, screenwriters, directors, and editors portray humor. Instead of prioritizing realism, they may:

  • Prioritize pacing. When we laugh in conversation, the laughter may take up a susbtantial proportion of the conversation. But for pacing reasons, movies don't want laughter to take up the same proportion of a scene.
  • Prioritize camera shots. If a movie wants a clear shot of a face, the director will instruct the actor not to put their hands near their heads when pretending to laugh. This prevents a common humor tell from being present in many scenes:
  • Prorioritze characterization. In a movie, it can be charming to see the roguish character smirk as they recieve an award—but it can be off-putting in real life. In reverse, it can be charming to see someone beam at an award ceremony in real life, but might fee;sl like weak characterization in a movie:
  • Prioritize what's flattering on camera. Genuine smiles contort our faces. Some movies rarely let actors or characters blink on screen, let alone scrunch their eyes when they smile. Movies often avoid normal humor tells as a stylistic choice:
  • Prioritize storytelling. As we watch a movie, we may register a smile as fitting the themes, character arcs, music, romance, and so on. But when we imagine many movie smiles happening in real life, they can seem odd:

All these priorities shape how humor is presented in spectator entertainment. Spectator entertainment will selectively use or modify humor tells in a way that creates spectator humor profiles.

Consider:

There's nothing wrong with movies using spectator humor profiles—for the same reason there's nothing wrong with portraits using paint. Without paint, it wouldn't be a painting!

Instead, we can be wrong if we assume that movies are straightforward mirrors of real life. Movies have constraints and goals that aren't simply realism for realism's sake.

Limitations. One reason spectator humor profiles are so distinct is that we simply can't convincingly act out medium-intensity humor or higher on command!

We can't convincingly act out our own laugh on command—let alone imitate someone else's laughter. While we can sometimes caricature, we can't convincingly replicate intense laughter.

Directors and actors will shape presentations of humor to what is physically possible to do. If a lead actor can't act out laughter, it won't be scripted. We don't always notice this about movies, once it's pointed out it's hard to unsee!

The Humor Book

Spectator humor profiles vary by medium

Different kinds of spectator entertainment have different constraints in how humor can be presented. So they develop spectator humor profiles to make the best of those constraints:

  • If we’re producing a play, we want someone in the back row to clearly perceive an actor’s humor—so we'll avoid subtle physical humor tells.
  • If we’re making a graphic novel, we need to show the intensity of humor in a single panel without sound—so we'll use fonts and marks as humor tells.
  • If we’re writing a book, we need to use quick and generic cues to keep a brisk pace of written conversation—so we'll use shorthands like "he laughed."
  • If we’re filming a fast-paced movie, we'll want the audience to be able to be able to read emotion states in a split second—and so we'll use musical cues and exaggerated facial expressions as humor tells.

(and so on)

Once we get used to spectator humor profiles, they don't seems surreal—like how the humor in a novel seems completely normal to us:

Samantha laughed. "No, m'lord," she says, eyes dancing, "I think you a fool!" (←Samantha has a spectator humor profile)

A light smile plays upon the duke's lips as he turns to look at her. (←the Duke has a spectator humor profile)

Samantha is briefly speechless from surprise. How could he have known? The duke laughs at her. (←the Duke has a spectator humor profile)

Zoe devours the novel. (←Spectator humor profiles in novels seem unmentionably normal to Zoe)

We often get so familiar to these spectator humor profiles that they seem normal—but being familiar isn't the same thing as being realistic.

It's often not until we have to acclimate to new genres that we realize how artificial spectator humor profiles are.

For a while, emotions in manga would strike us as annoying, distracting, simplistic, unrelatable, weird, and so on. But this is how all spectator entertainment feels at first—from novels to plays to radio performances.

Once we acclimate to different spectator humor profiles, they become unnoticeably normal—and the spectator humor profile does what it’s supposed to do, which is to efficiently convey information about the character’s evaluations.

At that point, we roll everything together as part of the experience, and nothing in particular stands out to us as unusual—we read a novel, watch a movie, enjoy a graphic novel, and don't register that the way they depict humor is an abstract representation with just loose accuracy to actual human behavior. (*eyes dancing*)

We probably didn't bat an eye when Rhett was "laughing softly" in the passage from Gone With the Wind—it's so normal and familiar it's hard to notice!

But just because it's familiar doesn't mean it's realistic. In this case, "laughing softly" is so vague as to not really describe anything at all.

Consider:

If a book describes a city street as "bustling," we wouldn't declare this the same thing as actually being on the street ourselves. It's a pretty vague description. Descriptions of humor are similar.

"Bustling" can describe thousands of very different streets, just like "laughing softly" can describe millions of different laughs.

At first glance, we may not notice that the actor is using a spectator humor profile. But he is!

It's not a coincidence that the actor is supporting the needs of the medium—he walks to stand close enough to the other actor so that both are in frame, and speaks in measured, complete sentences. These are done to support the conventions and limitations of films from this time period—not to mirror reality.

And as a broader point, if we had two clips—one of Rhett laughing and another someone laughing spontaneously—no one would confuse Rhett's spectator humor profile as being the more realistic one.

The point of all this isn't to take away from spectator entertainment. Instead, the point is just to recognize how depictions of humor in spectator entertainment are different than how humor is experienced and expressed in our own interpersonal lives.

"No one can remain a lady after saying and doing what have just overheard. However, ladies have seldom held any charms for me. I fail to understand what charms the elegant Mr. Wilkes can hold for a girl of your tempestuous nature. "You aren't fit to wipe his boots!" she shouted in rage.

"And you were going to hate him all your life!" He sank down on the sofa and she heard him laughing.

Spectator humor profiles vary by medium

Different kinds of spectator entertainment have different constraints in how humor can be presented. So they develop medium-specific spectator humor profiles to make the best of the constraints:

  • If we’re producing a play, we want someone in the back row to clearly perceive an actor’s humor—so we'll avoid subtle physical humor tells.
  • If we’re making a graphic novel, we need to show the intensity of humor in a single panel without sound—so we'll use fonts and marks as humor tells.
  • If we’re writing a book, we need to use quick and generic cues to keep a brisk pace of written conversation—so we'll use shorthands like "he laughed."
  • If we’re filming a fast-paced movie, we'll want the audience to be able to be able to read emotion states in a split second—and so we'll use musical cues and exaggerated facial expressions as humor tells.

There are a few reason spectator humor profiles exist:

  • Most actors can’t convincingly act medium-intensity or higher humor on command. We can watch a video of ourselves doubled over with laughter, and then try to do this perfectly on command—we can't, and actors can't either!
  • Laughing takes a long time! Having a realistic amount of it in a movie would mess with pacing—unless spectator entertainment has a good reason for 10% of the runtime be characetrs laughing, they probably won't.
  • Our face changes when we give genuine smiles—our skin crinkles, our lips rise toward our nose, our eyes change shape, dimples form, and so on. Some movies make a choice to subordinate realism for a stylistic effect—like how many movies have a style of cinematography and editing that rarely lets actors or characters blink, let along squint their eyes when they smile.
  • But some kinds of spectator entertainment, an intense smile can strike us as unusual or come off as unappealing. In this case, what feels good in our lives doesn't feel as good on a screen.

All of these can lead to spectator humor profiles—like how movie characters won't smile or laugh intensely for various reasons, supported both by the script and the directing.

For most movies, emotional realism isn't the goal. Instead, the goal is the emotional handling of the audience—not realism for realism's sake. This includes how actors use humor tells.

We don't usually notice conventions in novels as artificial or stilted, because we're so used to them.

The point of all this isn't to take away from spectator entertainment. Instead, the point is just to recognize how depictions of humor in spectator entertainment are different than how humor is experienced and expressed in our own interpersonal lives.

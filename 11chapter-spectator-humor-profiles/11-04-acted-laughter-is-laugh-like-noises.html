Acted laughter is a laugh-like noise

Everyone has a distinctive, memorable laugh—if we recorded a hundred people laughing, we’d have a hundred different laughs. Despite this, we almost never hear distinctive laughs in movies, unless they’re part of a gag.

In fact, we rarely hear high-intensity laughs at all in movies—because it’s hard to act or fake laughter!

Consider:

It’s hard to do our laugh on command—and it’s exponentially harder to do someone else’s laugh on command. While we can often caricature someone else’s laugh, we can’t reliably mimic them as easily as some people can mimic speaking voices.

This is why most characters in movies have forgettable, unremarkable laughs. These laughs aren’t particularly intense, memorable, or realistic (“hah!” or “hahahaha!”). These are just the imitative laughter that’s possible to act out.

The exception to generic acted laughs are noises that are used for emotional shortcuts. These are surreal, laugh-like noises that are self-referential, like:

  • an evil man laugh ("Mwah-haha!")
  • an evil woman cackle (“EEheeheehee!”)
  • a good old man chuckle (“ho ho ho”)
  • deranged laughter (“ha ha ha ha ha!”)
  • mocking laughter (“haw haw haw”)
  • an around-the-dinner-table laugh (“ah hah haha”)
  • an end-of-the-movie silent laugh (*head shakes in happiness as they open their mouth and laugh in slow silent motion*)

(and so on)

These laugh-like noises are just part of common spectator humor profiles that we’ve all gotten used to. Audiences register these noises as laughter by convention—not because they’re particularly realistic.

We do the same thing in our day-to-day lives: if we go “mwah-haha!”, we aren’t really laughing—we’re just making a noise as an emotional or narrative shortcut:

Emma and Owen are playing a racing game. Owen cuts her off.

Emma: (pissed) “Bastard!”

Owen: (amused) “Mwah-haha!” (←Emma registers this as a humor tell, but not as a laugh)

Emma doesn’t think “mwah-haha” is a real laugh—in the same way she doesn't think of “mwah-haha” as a real laugh when done in movies.

Consider:

"mwah-haha" is like a verbal emoji. An upside down smiley face 🙃 isn't a "real" expression, but still conveys something about our evaluations. Noises like "mwah-haha" or "heh heh heh" function similarly—they convey something specific about our evaluations, without being literal laughs.

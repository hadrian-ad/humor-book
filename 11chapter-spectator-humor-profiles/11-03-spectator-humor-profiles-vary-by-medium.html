The Humor Book

Spectator humor profiles vary by medium

"Movie humor profiles" are suited to the practical and stylistic constraints in movies.

In the same way, other humor profiles develop to the practical and stylistic constraints of other mediums:

  • If we’re producing a play, we want someone in the back row to clearly perceive an actor’s humor—so we'll avoid subtle physical humor tells.
  • If we’re making a graphic novel, we need to show the intensity of humor in a single panel without sound—so we'll use fonts and marks as humor tells.
  • If we’re writing a book, we need to use quick and generic cues to keep a brisk pace of written conversation—so we'll use shorthands like "he laughed."
  • If we’re making a fast-paced narrative game, we'll want players to be able to be able to read emotion states in a split second—and so we'll use musical cues and exaggerated facial expressions as humor tells.

(and so on)

We often get so familiar to these spectator humor profiles that they seem normal—but being familiar isn't the same thing as being realistic.

For instance:

If a novel says "the city was bustling," it's not not the same thing as being there. For the same reason, the way novels present humor ("she laughed") is something that's familiar, but not "realistic."

Once we acclimate to different spectator humor profiles, they do what they're supposed to do—which is to efficiently convey information about the character’s evaluations to the spectator.

At that point, we roll everything together as part of the experience—and nothing in particular stands out to us as unusual. We read a novel, watch a movie, enjoy a graphic novel, and don't register that the way they depict humor is abstract and not always particularly accurate to actual human behavior.

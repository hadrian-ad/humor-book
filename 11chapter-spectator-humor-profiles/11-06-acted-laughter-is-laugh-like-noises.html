Shorthands for laughter

One distinctive element of spectator humor profiles are self-referential, shorthand laughing noises, like:

  • an evil man laugh ("Mwah-haha!")
  • an evil woman cackle (“EEheeheehee!”)
  • a good old man chuckle (“ho ho ho”)
  • deranged laughter (“ha ha ha ha ha!”)
  • mocking laughter (“haw haw haw”)
  • an around-the-dinner-table laugh (“ah hah haha”)
  • an end-of-the-movie silent laugh (*head shakes in happiness as they open their mouth and laugh in slow silent motion*)

(and so on)

Audiences register these noises as laughter by convention—not because they’re particularly realistic.

We do the same thing in our day-to-day lives: if we go “mwah-haha!”, we aren’t really laughing—we’re just making a noise as an emotional or narrative shortcut:

Emma and Owen are playing a racing game. Owen cuts her off.

Emma: (pissed) “Bastard!”

Owen: (amused) “Mwah-haha!” (←Emma registers this as a humor tell, but not as a laugh)

Emma thinks Owen is feeling humor, but doesn’t think “mwah-haha” is a real laugh—in the same way she doesn't think of “mwah-haha” as a real laugh when done in movies.

Consider:

"mwah-haha" is like a verbal emoji. An upside down smiley face 🙃 isn't a "real" expression, but still conveys something about our evaluations.

Noises like "mwah-haha" or "heh heh heh" function similarly—just because they're not literal laughs doesn't mean they don't convey something specific about our evaluations.

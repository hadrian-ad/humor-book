The Humor Book

Acted laughter is a laugh-like noise

Everyone has a distinctive, memorable laugh—if we recorded a hundred people laughing, we’d have a hundred different laughs. Despite this, we almost never hear distinctive laughs in movies, unless they’re part of a gag.

It's not surprising that laughs are often used to generate humor. They're places where mistakes and OKs can easily be balanced—with the goal of making the spectators laugh, not straightforwardly mimic reality for its own sake.

In fact, we rarely hear high-intensity laughs at all in movies—because it’s hard to act or fake laughter! This is why most characters in movies have forgettable, unremarkable laughs that aren’t particularly intense, memorable, or realistic (“hah!” or “hahahaha!”)—these are just the imitative laughter that’s possible to act out.

Consider:

It’s hard to do our laugh on command—and it’s exponentially harder to do someone else’s laugh on command. We can often caricature someone else’s laugh, but we can’t reliably mimic them as easily as some people can mimic speaking voices.

We can vividly see the difference between a personal humor profile and an acted humor profile when we see actors actually laugh:

When the actor breaks character, she gives a bright, distinctive peal of laughter—a kind of laugh we never hear her character make, or hear in scripted spectator entertainment in general.

This actor has a rolling, blooming giggle—a kind of laugh we never hear his characters make, or hear in scripted spcetator entertainment in general.

The exception to generic acted laughs are noises that are used for emotional shortcuts. These are surreal, laugh-like noises that are self-referential, like:

  • an evil man laugh ("Mwah-haha!")
  • an evil woman cackle (“EEheeheehee!”)
  • a good old man chuckle (“ho ho ho”)
  • deranged laughter (“ha ha ha ha ha!”)
  • mocking laughter (“haw haw haw”)
  • an around-the-dinner-table laugh (“ah hah haha”)
  • an end-of-the-movie silent laugh (*head shakes in happiness as they open their mouth and laugh in slow silent motion*)

(and so on)

The audience registers these noises as laughter by convention—not because they’re particularly realistic. These laugh-like noises are just part of common spectator humor profiles that we’ve all gotten used to.

We do the same thing in our day-to-day lives: if we go “mwah-haha!”, we aren’t really laughing—we’re just making a noise as an emotional or narrative shortcut:

Emma and Owen are playing a racing game. Owen cuts her off.

Emma: (pissed) “Bastard!”

Owen: (amused) “Mwah-haha!” (←Emma registers this as a humor tell, but not as a laugh)

Emma doesn’t think “mwah-haha” is a real laugh—in the same way she doesn't think of “mwah-haha” as a real laugh when done in movies.

Consider:

"mwah-haha" is like a verbal emoji. An upside down smiley face 🙃 isn't a "real" expression, but still conveys something about our evaluations. Something like "mwah-haha" "heh heh heh" function similarly—they convey something specific about our evaluations, without being literal laughs.

Ho ho ho. Do people really go "ha ha ha"? Not really—people go "ha ha ha" in the same way cats go "meow." Of course, German speakers say cats go "miau miau" and Japanese speakers say they go "nya", but no one is trying to be "right"—these are just conventions to refer to all kinds of nontranscribable noises that cats make.

It's the same for laughter. Many people in the United States write "ha ha ha.", but many people in Europe write "ah ah ah" and many people in Brazil write "kkkkkkk."

Like with the sounds that cats make, there's no exact way to transcribe even a single instance of laughing—let alone one way to transcribe all the infinite variations of a laugh. Things like "ha ha ha" are just conventions some people use for some writing.

Sometimes people take these conventions too seriously, like when people expect cows to literally "moo" over and over—or when actors go "ha ha ha!" because that's how it's written. This is like how Santa actors will literally say "ho ho ho"

"Ho ho ho" was a written convention for Santa, since it conveyed more of a good-natured, full-body laugh than other vowels like "ha ha ha" or "hee hee hee". But actually saying "ho ho ho" aloud instead of laughing is a bit odd—like an actor literally saying "zzz" when pretending to sleep, or a video editor adding the spoken word "splat!" to a scene of a pancake falling on the floor.

The point isn't that there's something wrong with saying "ho ho ho"—since, like any spectator humor profile, it serves various purposes. Instead, the point is that these spectator profiles are surreal—and if we lose sight of this, we can make observational errors.

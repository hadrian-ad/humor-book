11. Spectator humor profiles

Last chapter we looked at spectator entertainment. In this chapter, we’ll look at how characters in spectator entertainment express humor—their spectator humor profile.

Consider:

We said before that each person has a humor profile. Our humor profile is how we express humor tells, specific to ourselves.

But if we act in a play, we won't transplant our actual humor profile to the stage wholesale. Instead, we'll use a spectator humor profile.

We can usually tell the difference between the actor acting humor tells (spectator humor profile) and the actor themselves feeling humor (personal humor profile)—in part because the humor we feel is exponentionally more vivid than the humor we can act out:

Often, we don’t notice how artificial acted humor is until someone points it out—but then it's something that's hard to unsee.

We'll be talking more about spectator humor profiles throughout this chapter.

There are a few reason spectator humor profiles exist:

  • Many actors can’t convincingly act medium-intensity or higher humor on command. ("This shot needs to look identical to videos of you on the best day of your life!") To accommodate this, movies will avoid scripting this intensity of humor.
  • Laughing takes a long time! Having a realistic amount of it in a movie would mess with pacing. To accomodate this, spectator humor profiles often show intense humor in ways other than long moments of laughter.
  • When we give genuine smiles, our faces crinkle, our lips rise toward our nose, our eyes change shape, dimples form, and so on. If filmmakers don’t want their actors to look like this, they can simply script and direct for acted half-smiles instead instead of humor that's more realistic or intense.
  • Movies often have a style of cinematography and editing that rarely let actors or characters blink during a shot—let alone let them realistically close their eyes when they smile. This is a stylistic choice to subordinate realism for a visual effect.
  • In interpersonal interactions, everything feels better when someone is expressing intense humor tells. But in spectator entertainment, an intense smile can strike us as unusual or come off as unappealing.

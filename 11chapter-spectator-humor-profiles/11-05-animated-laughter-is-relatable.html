Laughter makes things emotionally vivid

It's not just harder to pretend laughter in live action spectator entertainment—it's harder to have characters laugh together!

Consider:

On top of having to pretend to laugh, and do so on set—actors also have to do it with other actors!

This creates additional challenges to creating scenes involving group laughter—the scenes we often find most emotionally resonant.

Animated shows not only have more nuanced and varied laugh-like noises—characters laugh together more often:

Owen is watching Rick & Morty:

Testy rivals have been forced to collaborate and develop mutual respect for each other.

Zeep: (with sincerity) "If this works, drinks are on me."

Rick: "If drinks are on you, you're going to need a second mortgage." *leans in* *whispers* "I'm an alcoholic."

Zeep: *leans in* (shared disclosure) "Opium addict!"

Zeep & Rick: *laugh*

Owen laughs. For how aggressive and random this show can be, it's also sometimes surprisingly emotionally substantive.

In the show above, characters briefly have a large OK which allows them to share large mistakes to generate humor. As they do so, they feel mutual acceptance, comfort, connection, forgiveness, generosity, insight, reconciliation, release, revelation, relief, safety, and so on.

Consider:

Animated laughter also can have an easier time conveying humor's intensity, duration, and lean (toward mistake- or OK-predominance)—further driving emotional nuance to these scenes.

When characters laugh together, we often experience these scenes as having relatability, emotional depth, and greater coherence.

Translation. One reason live-action characters don't always translate well to animation and vice versa is laughing.

Real laughing doesn't superimpose well over many styles of animations—just like a lot of noise-laughter doesn't look convincing when done on camera.

This ends up making many live adaptions of animation things feel flat—since its the laughing that provides a lot of the emotional connection to the character.

The Humor Book

Animation allows for more vivid humor

Since animated shows can have more nuanced and varied laugh-like noises, these shows can have shows a feeling of emotional finesse and relatability that's often lacking in live-action scenes:

Owen is watching Rick & Morty:

Testy rivals have been forced to collaborate and develop mutual respect for each other.

Zeep: (with sincerity) "If this works, drinks are on me."

Rick: "If drinks are on you, you're going to need a second mortgage." *leans in* *whispers* "I'm an alcoholic."

Zeep: *leans in* (shared disclosure) "Opium addict!"

Zeep & Rick: *laugh*

Owen laughs. For how aggressive and random this show can be, it's also sometimes surprisingly emotionally substantive.

In the show above, characters briefly have a large OK which allows them to share large mistakes to generate humor. As they do so, they feel acceptance, comfort, connection, forgiveness, generosity, insight, reconciliation, release, revelation, relief, safety, and so on.

Since animated shows have an easier time conveying intensity, duration, and lean toward mistake- or OK-predominance, we experience animated shows as having emotional depth.

By comparison, live-action shows struggle to convincingly present nuanced, intense humor—making the emotional activity in live-action scenes feel flat, and often centered around mistake-predominant emotions instead of humor.

Resonance. It's harder to do scenes with shared laughter in live-action scenes than in animated scenes—not only do live-action actors have less leeway to establish memorable noises for their characters' laugh, but then have to do so convincingly on set and on camera!

Shared laughter tends to be an emotional focal point that makes for vivid scenes and characters. Since this is hard to do in live-action, but easier through voice acting, shows like Rick & Morty or The Simpsions are frequently experienced as having emotionally resonant characters—in part because they laugh together.

The Humor Book

We're allowed to censure unfitted humor

We have a specific way of censuring someone: we don't synchronize fully with them.

We experience desynchronization as censure. That's because we want others to synchronize or be synchronized with us.

Practically, this often simply means not expressing an equal amount of humor tells:

Emma sees Owen selling candy in the hallway.

Emma: (surprised) “You’re selling candy?” *laughs* “What are you, a girl scout?”

Owen: (annoyed) “No.” (←Owen withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Owen censures Emma by not feeling humor↔︎Owen thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Owen, Zoe, and Emma are studying in the library. Emma keeps opening and closing her mouth.

Owen: “What are you doing?”

Emma: “I’m pretending I’m a penguin. Do you think penguins have teeth?”

Zoe: (not in the mood) “I don’t know.” (←Zoe withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe censures Emma by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Jennifer: *checks phone* They do a daily check-in on my dad at his home. He always texts me after, but he’s late today.”

Amber: “Check-in every day at the nursing home?” *smiles* “Is it like a Black Death cart that goes door-to-door?”

Jennifer: “Um. It’s just staff knocking on the door.” (←Jennifer withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Jennifer censures Amber by not feeling humor↔︎Jennifer thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Emma pulls out her phone, but is stupefied to see she accidentally grabbed Owen’s phone. Zoe doubles over with laughter at Emma’s expression.

Emma: (stupefied) “It’s not funny! Where’s my phone?”

Zoe laughs harder, and Emma gets more and more annoyed.

Emma: “I wish you would take this seriously.” (←Emma withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Emma censures Zoe by not feeling humor↔︎Emma feels the humor attempt was below dress code)

Zoe: *smiling* “Okay, okay, sorry.”

Tucker pokes his head out of his office to talk to Amber.

Tucker: “Okay. Pun-time. Here we go: I’m a big fan of whiteboards. I find them totally re-markable!”

Amber: *exasperated* “Ha ha Tucker, ;snot bad, but you know I’m pretty busy.” (←Amber withholds synchronization by expressing less humor↔︎Amber censures Tucker by not feeling humor↔︎Amber thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Emma and Owen are watching videos of a prankster tricking people into walking into poles. Emma and Owen guffaw at each successful prank. But Zoe isn’t so amused by them.

She just tilts her head and twists her mouth instead of laughing. (←Zoe withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe censures Emma and Owen by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Essentially, someone shares an evaluatory perspective. By showing disagreement with that perspective, we censure them. It's this evaluatory disagreement that's censure.

We're using a lighter definition of "censure" than how we often use it in day-to-day conversations. Our censure here is almost never room-stopping conflict—more often it's just a half-smile contrasting with someone's full smile. ("Censure")

Most of the censure we receive in our day-to-day lives aren't a big deal to us—it's just something done by everyone, including us and our friends or family, in ways we think of as neither good nor bad, but just how things work.

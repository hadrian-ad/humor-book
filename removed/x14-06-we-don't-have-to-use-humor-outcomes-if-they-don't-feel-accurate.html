The Humor Book

Humor outcomes help us characterize humor

Sometimes it's harder to "match" something to a humor outcome:

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Ferdydurke. She smiles at the title. (←it feels hard to match Zoe's humor to a humor outcome)

To use humor outcomes to think about random words like Ferdydurke, we have to see humor outcomes as linking someone to something.

A title like Ferdydurke doesn't support arguments (generally)—instead, someone feels the title "Ferdydurke" supports an argument (about what the book would be like).

This lets us use humor outcomes even with things that at first seem nonsensical or unrelated to humor outcomes:

We can do this for other book titles:

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Cryptonomicon. She smiles at the title. (←it feels hard to match Zoe's humor to a humor outcome)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Handy Andy. She smiles. (←it feels hard to match Zoe's humor to a humor outcome)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called If On a Winter’s Night a Traveler. She smiles at the title. (←it feels hard to match Zoe's humor to a humor outcome)

In all of these titles, Zoe's humor doesn't seem to intuitively match with any humor outcomes. To use humor outcomes, we become more attentive to what humor outcomes are linking:

  • instead of saying a title created rapport (generally), we'd say Zoe felt a title created rapport (with her)
  • instead of saying a title controlled the situation (generally), we'd say Zoe felt a title controlled a situation (by influencing how she spent her attention)
  • instead of saying a title supported arguments (generally), we'd say Zoe felt a title supported an argument (about what the book will be like)
  • instead of saying a title gave her new perspectives (generally), we'd say Zoe felt a title gave her new perspective (about how books can be titled)
  • instead of saying a title acceptably criticized (everything), we'd say Zoe felt (she) acceptably criticized (the title) or that Zoe felt that (the title) acceptably criticized (other titles)
  • instead of saying a title eased tensions (generally), we'd say Zoe felt (she) eased tension (with herself after encountering something weird)
  • instead of saying a title handled complaints (about itself), we'd say Zoe felt (she) handled (her own) complaint (about the title)

(and so on)

This lets us unpack Zoe's humor in more specific ways, and explore some of what may be happening in the emotional situation.

We're used to describing physical actions in a situation. (She moves across the room.) But we're less used to describing evaluatory actions in a situation. (She creates rapport with the people in the room.)

Humor outcomes are like "evaluatory verbs." They describe actions, changes, or outcomes in someone's evaluatory state.

We can use these evaluatory verbs ("humor outcomes")—even when they involve something that seems otherwise random, like a nonsense book title ("Ferdydurke").

-->

We can assign humor outcomes by working backwards. We can pick a humor outcome and ask: in what way does this humor outcome apply? ("In what way does Zoe reveal a personal view when she smiles at Handy Andy?")

-->

Evaluations are ties from people to the world around them. Humor is a relational expression of these ties. Like wires that are wound together, these relations can be hard to pick apart and make sense of—but humor outcomes let us pull part the tangle to see how someone's evaluatory system is currently composed.

-->

We often feel that a lot happens in moments of humor—and that this complexity is hard for us to describe.

We can use humor outcomes to pull apart this complexity. By thinking about how they relate to moments of humor, Humor outcomes help us describe the texture and dimensionality to humor, and see more how people are relating to things around them.

-->

Explaining. If we laugh at a book title, we sometimes want to explain our laugh by calling the title a joke or satirical. ("We laughed because the title was a joke.") But this isn’t usually the best way to go about explaining our humor.

For one, there are many possible ways of describing a title like Ferdydurke or Wherever You Go, There You Are—"joke" being just one of many possible descriptions. For another, we don't always laugh at jokes—so calling something a joke doesn't yet explain why we did or didn't laugh when we saw it.

But while Zoe doesn't always feel humor at every joke every time, Zoe does always feels humor when she gains a new perspective—otherwise, she wouldn’t have gained a new perspective. This is why explaining Zoe's new perspective directly explains her humor.

15. Explaining humor

Last chapter, we looked at humor outcomes—ways of describing humor as an outcome.

Humor outcomes

acceptably criticize, achieve catharsis, avoid conflict, change negative emotions into positive ones, control a situation, create rapport, deal with taboo topics, ease tensions, give new perspectives, handle complaints, help one become accepted, open up a new topic of conversation, reduce defensiveness, reveal a personality trait, reveal a personal view, support an argument, survive in high stress situations, symbolize close ties.

In this chapter, we'll look at how these descriptions double as explanations for humor:

Zoe is browsing the bookstore, and finds a book called Wherever You Go, There You Are. She smiles. (←Zoe gained a new perspective)

Why did Zoe feel humor? We can say she encountered a mistake that balanced with an OK—but we can also use humor outcomes to answer this question: since she gained a new perspective, we can look for what that new perspective might be.

We'll be talking about this in the rest of this chapter.

Tether. When explaining Zoe’s humor, we first start with a humor outcome. (“Zoe gained a new perspective.”) We then think about why something did that for Zoe specifically. (“What about this title was a new perspective for her?”)

A title can't itself have a new perspective—it’s only a new perspective to someone. This is why we have to tether our humor outcomes to Zoe, not just to the title by itself.

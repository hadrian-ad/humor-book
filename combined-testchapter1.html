Humor in Various Moods and Meters

In this book, you'll learn when and why there's humor—and much more besides:

Yasmin puts on her glasses for the first time. She can see leaves! Clouds have texture! She beams and laughs. (←humor)

Riley sees a squirrel on a pumpkin and laughs. (←humor)

Sam: (baffled) “What’s so funny?” (←no humor)

Jennifer sees the squirrel and laughs too. (←humor)

Emma: *red-eyed*

Zoe: “What’s wrong, Em?”

Emma: “Nothing. I’ve just realized I’m older now than my brother was when h-he died…” (←no humor)

Zoe: “Aw. Emma.” *hugs*

Emma: *hugs* *cries and laughs* (←humor)

Alina is talking on the phone with her younger brother, Artem.

Alina: “I’m sorry things are rough right now. Lots of people have troubles after high school. There’s no shame in being depressed. Listen, I have to go, but I’ll call you soon.”

She hangs up. Artem sits back.

Artem: (thunderstruck) “I have depression!?” *laughs* (←humor) “That makes so much sense. Why didn’t I think of that before?”

Why is there humor in the examples above? We'll learn to think fluently about this, step-by-step.

Our starting step will be to learn a universal pattern for humor.

Chapter 1

Humor Pattern

Humor happens in a universal pattern. We can put this pattern most simply as:

Humor is a mistake that’s evaluated as OK.

We'll explain this in detail soon. But first we'll get a general sense of how this pattern works:

Yasmin puts on her glasses for the first time. She can see leaves! Clouds have texture! She beams and laughs. (←Yasmin evaluates the mistake of clarity as OK↔︎humor)

Riley sees a squirrel on a pumpkin and laughs. (←Riley evaluates the mistake of a squirrel on a pumpkin as OK↔︎humor)

Sam: (baffled) “What’s so funny?” (←Sam evaluates his confusion as mistake that’s not OK↔︎no humor)

Jennifer sees the squirrel and laughs too. (←Jennifer evaluates the mistake of a squirrel on a pumpkin as OK↔︎humor)

Minh is playing with his baby nephew, and covers his face with his hands.

Baby: (confused) *mouth drops* (←no humor)

Minh: *pulls hands aside* (enthusiastically) "Peek-a-boo!"

Baby: (delighted) *laughs* (←baby evaluates the mistake of disappearing and reappearing as OK↔︎︎humor)

Trevor walks into class and trips, and the cereal he’s carrying falls down his shirt.

At first his classmates are shocked. But when they see that he’s not hurt, they smile and laugh with relief. (←Trevor’s classmates evaluate the mistake of falling as OK↔︎humor)

Trevor, on the other hand, is embarrassed. His shirt is dirty, and he stalks to the bathroom, decidedly unamused. (←Trevor evaluates the mistake of falling as not OK↔︎no humor)

In each example, someone found something humorous or not based on their evaluation of a mistake as OK or not OK.

Humor is a mistake that’s evaluated as OK.

We're using words like "mistake" and "OK" in new ways. We'll be looking more precisely at what these words mean over the next few sections.

What do we mean by "mistake"?

The first part of our pattern of humor is a mistake.

Humor is a mistake that’s evaluated as OK.

In this book, mistakes aren't something “bad”—they're just mistakes in our expectations:

Justin and Heather are mountain biking when Justin flips on a root and tumbles down a small hill. (←mistake)

“Oh my god! Justin! Are you okay?” Heather calls, jumping off her bike and scrambling down to the body and bike below.

Timothy calls Jennifer.

Timothy: “Jennifer, I know Sam said otherwise, but I checked with the insurance company. They said the treatment is still experimental. They won’t cover it.” (←mistake)

Damien checks his pocket.

Damien: “Where’s my wallet? Maybe I left it at the bus stop!” (←mistake)

He gets off at the nearest stop and runs back, panicking.

Sara sits down disconsolately.

Sarah: (dispirited) *to self* “I can’t believe she’d cheat on me.” (←mistake)

Elias sees Jessica come in carrying a big box.

Elias: “Jess, be careful, don’t knock the table…!”

The table is bumped, and a clay statue falls over. One of the arms shatters. (←mistake)

Yasmin realizes her boyfriend has been quiet for an hour. Didn’t he say he was just going to the kitchen for some leftovers? (←mistake)

She suddenly has a bad feeling.

Xiaowei pulls up to his high school. He's dropping his friend Lina off for the opening day of her play.

Xiaowei: “Break a leg!" (teasing) “Also. You look nervous!”

Lina gives him the finger on the way out. (←mistake)

Sam walks into the bedroom, tying his tie.

Sam: “Jen, the kids were eaten by wolves.” (←mistake)

Sabrina opens her email from her supervisor.

Email: “You’re being let go. Don’t come in on Monday.” (←mistake)

The examples above aren’t examples of humor—we're just looking at examples of what we're calling mistakes. Mistakes are just one part of our pattern of humor.

Consider:

We labeled mistakes above based on the perspective of the people in the examples. The characters thought they were mistakes—which may or may not overlap with what we, as readers, feel are mistakes. We’ll do this throughout this book.

Expectations. A more formal term for a mistake would be a deviation from our expectations. We have expectations for how things should be, and sometimes things deviate from those expectations.

This isn't about whether something is good or bad—instead, it's about whether something conforms to our expectations.

For instance:

Since we expect an egg to have one yolk, it’s a deviation from our expectations to find an egg with two yolks—or an egg with no yolk.

Similarly, if we thought we knew the billing, it’s a deviation from our expectations to get an unexpected fee—or get an unexpected discount.

It doesn’t matter whether we feel these deviations from our expectations are good or bad. A mistake is when our expectations don't match what we actually encounter.

For now, we’ll be using these terms interchangeably (mistake = deviation from our expectations). We’ll mostly use “mistake,” since it’s shorter and less cumbersome.

What do we mean by "OK"?

The next part of the pattern we'll look at is OK.

Humor is a mistake that’s evaluated as OK.

In this book, OK doesn't refer to something "good"—instead, it refers to someone's personal relation to consequences:

Yasmin realizes her boyfriend has been quiet for an hour. Didn’t he say he was just going to the kitchen for some leftovers? (←mistake) She suddenly gets a bad feeling.

She goes to check on him in the kitchen. Trevor’s at the table with look of extreme concentration, trying to stack grapes. Many are on the floor. Yasmin guffaws. (←mistake is OK)

Yasmin: (surprised) (amused) “Trevor! What on earth?”

Timothy calls Jennifer.

Timothy: “Jennifer, I know Sam said otherwise, but I checked with the insurance company. They said the treatment is still experimental. They won’t cover it.” (←mistake)

Jennifer is silent for a second.

Jennifer: *laughs* (←mistake is OK) (exhausted) “That’s what I thought. If we hold a fundraiser, I think we should still be able to cover it.”

Elias sees Jessica come in carrying a big box.

Elias: “Jess, be careful, I just bought that! Don’t knock the table…”

The table is bumped, and a clay statue falls over. One of the arms shatter. (←mistake)

Elias rushes over and examines the broken statue.

Elias: “You know… I think I like it this way!” *laughs* (←mistake is OK)

Justin and Heather are mountain biking when Justin flips on a root and tumbles down a small hill. (←mistake)

“Oh my god! Justin! Are you okay?” Heather calls, jumping off her bike and scrambling down to the body and bike below.

Justin stirs and sits up. “Oof,” he says, adjusting his helmet shakily. “I’m fine!” They look at each other and start giggling with relief. (←mistake is OK)

Sam walks into the bedroom, tying his tie. “Jen, the kids were eaten by wolves.” (←mistake)

Jennifer smiles while getting dressed. “Har har, I wish.” She laughs. (←mistake is OK)

Damien checks his pocket. “Where’s my wallet? Shit. Maybe I left it at the bus stop!” (←mistake) He gets off at the nearest stop and runs back, panicking.

The wallet is still on the bench, and Damien picks it up and laughs. (←mistake is OK)

Damien: “Phew! It IS here! How careless could I be?”

Xiaowei pulls up to his high school. He's dropping his friend Lina off for the opening day of her play.

Xiaowei: “Break a leg!" (teasing) “Also. You look nervous!”

Lina gives him the finger on the way out. (←mistake)

Xiaowei laughs and gives the finger back as the door slams. (←mistake is OK) Teasing followed by the bird was practically their secret handshake.

In all these examples, people felt that a mistake was OK.

Consequences. A more formal term for OK would be secure from negative personal consequences. As we observe things, we pay attention to how those observations will personally affect us.

This isn’t about whether something is good or bad—it's just about our sense of security to negative personal consequences.

For instance:

If we hear a building collapses two thousand miles away from us, we may feel it’s OK—because we feel we are personally secure from negative consequences. This doesn’t mean we’re “okay” that it happened! All our OK here means is that the possibility of personal consequences seems remote.

By contrast, if the building next door to us collapsed, or a building collapsed in the city our best friend lived in, we won’t feel personally secure from negative consequences—and it would be not OK.

We’ll be using both terms interchangeably (OK = secure from negative personal consequences). We’ll mostly use “OK,” since it’s shorter and less cumbersome.

What do we mean by “evaluated”?

The last part of our pattern is the word evaluated.

Humor is a mistake that’s evaluated as OK.

The word evaluation is like a hinge, where mistakes can swivel to OK or to not-OK. This lets our pattern describe when humor does and doesn’t occur:

Timothy calls Jennifer.

Timothy: “Jennifer, I know Sam said otherwise, but I checked with the insurance company. They said the treatment is still experimental. They won’t cover it.” (←mistake)

Jennifer is silent for a second.

Jennifer: *laughs* (←mistake is evaluated as OK) (exhausted) “That’s what I thought. If we hold a fundraiser, I think we should still be able to cover it.”

- OR -

Jennifer: *cries* (←mistake is evaluated as not OK) (exhausted) “Oh my god. What are we going to do?”

Elias sees Jessica come in carrying a big box.

Elias: “Jess, be careful, I just bought that! Don’t knock the table…”

The table is bumped, and a clay statue falls over. One of the arms shatter. (←mistake) Elias rushes over and examines the broken statue.

Elias: “You know… I think I like it this way!” *laughs* (←mistake is evaluated as OK)

- OR -

Elias: “You know… I was so proud of finding this thing and now you ruined it.” (←mistake is evaluated as not OK)

Sam walks into the bedroom, tying his tie. “Jen, the kids were eaten by wolves.” (←mistake)

Jennifer smiles while getting dressed. “Har har, I wish.” She laughs. (←mistake is evaluated as OK)

- OR -

Jennifer’s face turns pale. “WOLVES?!” (←mistake is evaluated as not OK)

She stares at Sam, flabbergasted. (←mistake is still evaluated as not OK)

Something clicks, and she covers her mouth and laughs. Sam laughs too. (←mistake is finally evaluated as OK)

Xiaowei pulls up to his high school. He's dropping his friend Lina off for the opening day of her play.

Xiaowei: “Break a leg!" (teasing) “Also. You look nervous!”

Lina gives him the finger on the way out. (←mistake)

Xiaowei laughs and gives the finger back as the door slams. (←mistake is evaluated as OK) Teasing followed by the bird was practically their secret handshake.

- OR -

Xiaowei shakes his head as the door slams. “Hmph. Asshole.” (←mistake is evaluated as not OK)

Damien checks his pocket. “Where’s my wallet? Shit. Maybe I left it at the bus stop!” (←mistake) He gets off at the nearest stop and runs back, panicking.

The wallet is still on the bench, and Damien picks it up and laughs. (←mistake is evaluated as OK)

Damien: “Phew! It IS here! How careless could I be?”

- OR -

The wallet is still on the bench, and Damien kicks a trash can in anger. (←mistake is evaluated as not OK)

Damien: “It IS here! Jesus Christ, how could I be so fucking careless!”

Justin and Heather are mountain biking when Justin flips on a root and tumbles down a small hill. (←mistake)

“Oh my god! Justin! Are you okay?” Heather calls, jumping off her bike and scrambling down to the body and bike below.

Justin stirs and sits up. “Oof,” he says, adjusting his helmet shakily. “I’m fine!” They look at each other and start giggling with relief. (←mistake is OK)

- OR -

Justin stirs and tries worriedly to sit up. His legs are tangled in the frame of the bike. “I hope I didn’t break something,” he says.

Heather tugs on her helmet with worry. “Oh gosh oh gosh.” (←mistake is evaluated as not OK)

Sara sits down disconsolately. “I can’t believe she’d cheat on me.” (←mistake)

Something clicks in her perspective. “Well. Maybe I’d cheat on me too.” (←mistake is OK) She hiccup-laughs sadly.

- OR -

She puts her hand on her head. “Oh my God.” (←mistake is evaluated as not OK) A black despair creeps over her.

These examples help us see how an evaluation is something that someone does that pivots them toward humor or not-humor.

Consider:

This word "evaluated" adds a person to our pattern: someone has to do the evaluating.

Does a book of jokes sitting on a shelf have any humor in it? Not according to our pattern of humor—since no one is currently evaluating the jokes. Humor is only possible if someone is evaluating the jokes.

Absence. Humor doesn't only happen—humor also doesn't happen. We’ll be learning as much about the humor that doesn’t happen as we do about the humor that does.

That's because we won't be able to fully understand why we sometimes laugh at jokes, hugs, failure, and so on without also have to understand why we sometimes don’t laugh at them.

That's why we’ll be as comfortable explaining the presence of laughter as we are explaining the absence of laughter by the end of this book.

That's it for this chapter!

Chapter 7

Humor Family Words

In this chapter, we’ll look at humor family words. These are contextual variations of the word humor: 

Humor family words

  • acceptance
  • accomplishment
  • achievement
  • appreciation
  • belonging
  • comfort
  • connection
  • delight
  • forgiveness
  • generosity
  • insight
  • joy
  • realization
  • reconciliation
  • release
  • revelation
  • relief
  • safety
  • success
  • welcome

Humor family words are moments where we feel humor:

Justin and Heather are mountain biking when Justin flips on a root and tumbles down a small hill. “Oh my god! Justin! Are you okay?” Heather calls, jumping off her bike and scrambling down to the body and bike below.

Justin stirs and sits up. “Oof,” he says, adjusting his helmet shakily. “I’m fine!”

They look at each other and start giggling with relief. (←mistake is OK↔︎realization, relief, release, safety↔︎humor)

We can describe Heather and Justin’s humor as humor—but also as humor family words, like relief, realization, safety, and so on.

The generic word "humor" by itself doesn't imply any context:

When we want to give more situational context, we replace humor with a humor family words—like relief:

Realization as humor

Humor family words

acceptance, accomplishment, achievement, appreciation, belonging, comfort, connection, delight, forgiveness, generosity, insight, joy, realization, reconciliation, release, revelation, relief, safety, success, welcome

Since at first it can seem odd to think of humor family words as humor, we'll go through some of our humor family words one-by-one.

We'll see that these words are all humor—all moments where we evaluate mistakes as OK:

Zoe is browsing the bookstore when she picks up a book on meditation. She reads the book title. “Wherever You Go, There You Are.” She thinks about that for a second, puzzled.

Then something clicks. She laughs. (←realization↔︎mistake is OK↔︎humor) 

Zoe laughs after reading a book title: her mistakes and OKs shift in a way that generates humor. In context, we can describe this specific instance of humor as insight, realization, revelation, and so on. These are all different words that describe the same thing: humor.

When mistakes are balanced with an OK, we often describe this humor in context as realization:

Carol passes a window display.

Carol: (realizing)  “Oh, it was a bong!” *laughs* (←realization↔︎humor)

Jessica: *reading trivia on her bottle of sweet tea* “Emma! Did you know sharks can lose 100 teeth a day!” *laughs* (←realization↔︎humor)

Emma: *not really listening* “Really?”

Zoe: “Ms. Gardener was pretty cool, looking back.”

Emma: “Uh, yeah. Hey, yeah! She was always going to those concerts… EDM concerts, I guess…” (realizing) “…to do drugs!” *laughs* (←realization↔︎humor) “Of course. Duh.”

Jennifer: *texts Emma* “sry for the misunderstanding!! Sam was going to take them to their grandparents but he’s delayed till 3. he was supposed to tell you. Can you watch for 30-45 min? we’ll pay $$”

Emma: *reads text* “Phew!” *laughs with relief* (←realization↔︎humor) *texts Jennifer* “No problem! Enjoy Mexico!”

Alina is talking on the phone with her younger brother, Artem.

Alina: “I’m sorry things are rough right now. Lots of people have troubles after high school. There’s no shame in being depressed. Listen, I have to go, but I’ll call you soon.”

She hangs up. Artem sits back.

Artem: (thunderstruck) “I have depression!?” *laughs* (←realization↔︎humor) “That makes so much sense. Why didn’t I think of that before?”

Yasmin: *getting ready* “I can’t believe I have to blow off Trevor just because Stephen and Tucker wants me to come into work this weekend. Tucker’s an entitled asshole, and Stephen thinks I’m like some kind of slave. God I hate this job. But I have to stick with it, because this is the only way I’ll ever be able to one day have a nice life. I have no choice!”

She stops to think about that, and then laughs. (←realization↔︎humor) “Wow,” she says. “Maybe I should find a new job.” A weight falls off her, and she laughs again. 

We don’t always notice the humor we feel during moments of realization—but once it’s pointed out, we see it everywhere.

Consider:

We used the word realization in the examples above, but we could have used other humor family words too—like insight or revelation.

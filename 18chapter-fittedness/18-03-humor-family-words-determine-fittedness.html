Humor family words determine fittedness

Topics are just one component of fittedness. We can also use humor family words to think about the fittededness of humor attempts.

Humor family words

acceptance, accomplishment, apology, appreciation, belonging, comfort, connection, delight, forgiveness, generosity, humor, insight, joy, reconciliation, realization, release, revelation, relief, safety, success, surprise, welcome

Humor family words let us break questions about fittedness into more managable chunks.

For instance:

Instead of the very broad question of “when does humor fit”, we can decompose it into more answerable questions—like “when would acceptance fit?” or delight, relief, or so on.

Below, Emma feels sadness—and after Zoe hugs her she feels humor and sadness at the same time.

Emma: *red-eyed*

Zoe: “What’s wrong, Em?”

Emma: “Nothing. I’ve just realized I’m older now than my brother was when h-he died…”

Zoe: “Aw. Emma.” *hugs*

Emma: *cries* *laughs* (←Emma has mixed emotional states with both sadness and humor)

We can use topics and humor family words to estimate the fittedness:

  • Relevancy of topics. The humor is generated on relevant or recently active topics—since Emma had been thinking about her brother, and Zoe gave Emma a hug in response.
  • Relevance of humor family words. We can cleanly describe Emma’s humor as related to experiences of acceptance, belonging, comfort, insight, release, realization, reconciliation, and so on.

Emma's humor fit the situation—so did Zoe's humor attempt.

Consider:

We don’t usually think of hugs as humor attempts. But since Zoe was intending Emma to feel humor (acceptance, belonging, comfort, release, and so on), her hug was part of a humor attempt.

Not only was it a successful humor attempt, it was a fitted one too too—since it involved relevant topics and was intending Emma to feel humor family words.

If Zoe just randomly hugs Emma out of the blue, her humor attempt would be less fitted. Even though Zoe would still be intending Emma to feel acceptance, belonging, comfort, release, and so on—it wouldn't involve the same relevant or recently active topics.

If Zoe randomly hugs a stranger, her humor attempt would be even less fitted. The topics involved would have even less relevance—and there would be less context to describe the intention as acceptance, belonging, comfort, and so on.

Coexistence. Sometimes we think of humor as the opposite of sadness, in the sense that they can’t exist together—but humor isn’t really the opposite of sadness. And even if they were, there's no reason why they can't be present simultaneously.

For the same reason there's nothing preventing us from having a black and white painting, there's nothing preventing sadness and humor from being present simultaneously.

The Humor Book

Humor is one possible emotional outcome of many

Last chapter, we looked at evaluations as leading to either humor or no humor:

Sara laughs. (←humor)

- OR -

Sara doesn't laugh. (←no humor)

But when we think of humor as an emotion, “no humor” could mean any other emotion:

That’s because humor is one possible emotional outcome of many:

Sara is moving in three days. She can barely sleep.

Sara: *tossing and turning* *thinking* “I’m so nervous. I’m not ready for this.” (brightly) “I know: I’ll run away!”

She gives a complex laugh. (←humor)

- OR -

Sara: *tossing and turning* *thinking* “It’s going to be different!” (←excitement)

- OR -

Sara: *tossing and turning* *thinking* “What if I don’t make any friends there. What if I live there for the rest of my life and nothing good ever happens ever. I really fucked things up here.”

The future feels hopeless. (←dread)

Professor Justin reads a poem out of the class’s assigned anthology.

Justin: “Of darkling mortals famished for one ray / Of thy so-hoarded luxury of light…”

Emma tries to listen, but this is the worst Gen Ed ever. “So-hoarded?” she thinks. “More like so-boreded!” She’s briefly amused. (←humor)

- OR -

Zoe thinks Rebert Browning is hit or miss. Like, “darkling mortals famished?” He's like a spoof of a poet. Zoe draws lines on her paper. (←boredom)

- OR -

Trevor is irritated. This is a waste of time. (←irritation)

Professor Justin hands back everyone’s literary analysis essay.

Owen: (amused) "A B! Wow. I totally wrote this literally in the half-hour before it was due.” *laughs*(←humor)

- OR -

Emma: (proud) “A B! I followed the rubric exactly! I had the professor read it early! First time I’ve put effort into a literature essay. I feel weirdly proud!” (←pride)

- OR -

Zoe: (shocked) “A B! But I followed the rubric exactly! I had the professor read it early! He should be fired!” (←outrage)

Last chapter, we said we’ll be as interested in when humor doesn’t occur as when it does. Here, we’re clearer about what it means for humor to not occur—it means another emotion happens instead.

We feel humor when we evaluate a mistake as OK:

Owen: *shows Emma and Zoe paper* *smiling* “Look guys! Justin totally just gave my paper a B, even though I wrote it in half an hour, sitting in the hallway before class!” (←the B is a mistake, since Owen had expected a worse grade, but it's OK↔humor)

Emma: (aghast) “You got a B for half an hour…! I spent all Thanksgiving on that paper! Arg!” *hits Owen* (←Emma sees several mistakes, but none of them are OK↔no humor)

Zoe: (snippy) “See? That confirms it. Justin’s grades are practically random. He should be fired!” (←the B isn’t a mistake, since Zoe already expects unfair grading↔no humor)

Owen: (pleased with himself) “See? A proper work ethic oft goes wrong, my friends.” (←Owen thinks the situation has several mistakes that are each OK↔humor)

More formally, a “mistake” is a deviation from the expected—and an "OK" is personally secure from negative consequences.

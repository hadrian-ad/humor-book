The Humor Book

humor and jokes are different things

Instead of talking about humor, we sometimes drift into talking about "farces," "jokes," "irony," and so on instead. But this can distract us from understanding why we sometimes laugh, and why we sometimes don’t. 

Like anything else, comedies, farces, jokes, irony, satires, witty remarks, and so on can generate humor—but they don’t always. Someone may or may not see mistakes in them, which they may or may not evaluate as OK:

So while jokes, comedies, wittiness, satire, and so on can lead to humor—our evaluations can also lead to any other emotion as well:

Jokes can have any emotional outcome

Tucker: (delightedly) “Hey, son, you know what one of the most remarkable things in the world is?”

Elias: (warily) “What?”

Tucker: *chuckles* *chuckles more* “Whiteboards!”

Elias: *pause* “Re-markable?” *laughs* (←humor)

- OR -

Elias: *pause* “Remarkable?” (confused) (←confusion)

- OR -

Elias: *pause* “Re-markable?” *purses lips* (←annoyance)

Comedies can have any emotional outcome

Sam and Riley are on the couch watching Fired Fireman.

Chief and the Arsonist are wrestling over the stolen wallet when the police run in.

Police: *draws gun* "Freeze, thief!"

Chief and the Arsonist shoot their hands up. But Chief somehow ended up holding the stolen wallet. He does a shocked double take at it.

Sam: *laughs*

Riley: *laughs too* (←humor)

- OR -

Riley: (bewildered) “What’s so funny?” (←bewildered)

- OR -

Riley: *eyes droop* (←tired)

Wittiness can have any emotional outcome

Zoe is reading Gone with the Wind.

Scarlett: (indignant) (suspicious) “Are you lying to try to soothe me?”

Rhett: (amused) “My dear, when did I ever try to soothe you?"

The book just lights up whenever Rhett’s in the scene. Zoe smiles as she reads. (←humor)

- OR -

The whole relationship between Rhett and Scarlett bothers her. Zoe frowns. (←annoyance)

- OR -

Zoe checks to see how many pages she has left in the chapter. She sighs. (←bored)

Satire can have any emotion outcome

Professor Justin is reading a satire mocking how academics write.

This is too good! He chuckles continuously as he reads. (←humor)

- OR -

Justin has a sinking feeling as he reads it. It's just like how he writes. (←dismay)

- OR -

Justin feels the satire is unfair and off-base. It's like it's purposefully misrepresenting something he thinks is important. It makes him feel alone and morose. (←sadness)

Since we’re focusing on humor, we won’t focus on jokes or satire—since those things aren’t humor. Humor is a particular emotional outcome, and we'll want to stay focused on why this outcome is or isn't reached.

Consider:

We'll learn why anything does or doesn't lead to an outcome of humor. This is inclusive to jokes and satire—and inclusive to everything else besides. We won't need to focus on jokes or satire to understand how they work.

Jokes and tentpoles. We sometimes use “joke” as a tentpole word for humor—where everything that causes us to laugh is called a joke. The reasoning goes like this: "we sometimes laugh at jokes. Therefore, everything we laugh at is some kind of joke."

This is pretty strange when we think about it! We can see how strange it is by making new variations: "we sometimes buy coffee. Therefore, everything we buy is some kind of coffee.” It would be strange to frame all buying around coffee—just like it's strange to frame all laughing around jokes.

What's happening is that we sometimes idiomatically call everything jokes. But we shouldn't take idiomatic language straightforwardly ("everything we laugh at is a joke"), for the same reason anything that becomes slang or an idiom shouldn't be taken literally.

The Humor Book

What do we mean by “evaluated”?

Fitted humor shows that we’re incorporating new framings or perspectives, which is true whether or not the humor is associated with formal learning or schools.

Consider:

Sometimes we think something is “educational” (Clouds over the Atlantic), and other things aren’t (Pulp Fiction). But when there’s fitted humor, it’s all the same process of learning—whether or not it’s associated with schools or universities.

We can see people learning throughout their lives as they feel humor:

Minh tries out Amaya’s VR game, AlienWaterCraft. It takes him a while to get used to the controls. His watercraft awkwardly backs up and rams into the dock, and Minh grimaces and smiles. (←Minh is incorporating new framings↔︎humor)

Trevor: *playing StormConQuest* (frustrated) “I can’t beat the Ogre Time Magistrate. I’ve been trying for hours. It’s just not possible.”

Yasmin: “Did you buy armor potions?”

Trevor: *pause* (confused) “Do magic arrows do physical damage?”

Yasmin: *smiles* “Half physical, half magical.”

Trevor: *laughs* “Fuuuck me.” (←Trevor incorporates a new framing↔︎humor)

Jessica: *reading trivia on her bottle of sweet tea* “Emma! Did you know sharks can lose 100 teeth a day!” *beams* (←Jessica incorporates a new framing something↔︎humor)

Emma: *not really listening* “Really?” (←Emma doesn’t incorporate a new framing↔︎no humor)

Zoe picks up a book called The Human Use of Human Beings. She smiles. (←Zoe incorporates a new framing↔︎humor)

Sam and Jennifer are shopping for a new chair, and Riley is walking behind them, bored.

Jennifer: *pointing* “How about this one?”

Sam: “I dunno, honey It’s kind of unsightly, isn’t it?”

He tries sitting into it, and his face cracks into a smile at how comfortable it feels. He leans back.

Sam: (in pleasure) “Wow. Haha. Gosh.” (←Sam incorporates a new framing↔︎humor)

Lynette is walking through the grocery store, when she sees the tabloid headlines.

Tabloid: “Movie Star Gregory Barre Cheats on Annie with European Swimsuit Model! (I’m Heartbroken, Says Tearful Annie)"

Lynette examines the pictures and smiles. (←Lynette incorporates a new framing↔︎humor) She puts one into her cart. She loves this stuff. Poor Annie.

Alina is talking on the phone with her younger brother, Artem. “I’m sorry things are rough right now,” she tells him. “Lots of people have troubles after high school. There’s no shame in being depressed. Listen, I have to go, but I’ll call you soon.”

She hangs up and Artem sits back. “I have depression!?” he says, thunderstruck. He laughs. (←Artem incorporates a new framing↔︎humor) “That makes so much sense. Why didn’t I think of that before?”

Zoe is reading a book about Aesopian fables.

A trumpeter marches into battle in the vanguard of an army, and uses his music to instill his comrades with courage. But in the course of battle, the trumpeter is captured and taken prisoner. Later, a council is assembled to decide what to do with the prisoners.

Trumpeter: *begging* "Do not put me to death. Not only have I not killed anyone, I don't even have any weapons! I carry with me only my trumpet."

Council: "That is only the more reason why we should take your life. For though you did not fight yourself, you stirred up others to do so."

Zoe smiles, thinking about it. (←Zoe incorporates a new framing↔︎humor)

Sam is examining his balding head in the mirror. He rubs it thoughtfully. Jennifer sees him do this, and she grabs him by the arm. “You look so handsome,” she says, squeezing him. Sam gives a releasing series of small laughs, and leans against her affectionately. (←Sam incorporates a new framing↔︎humor)

In all these examples, people feel tension when they encounter new framings or perspectives. Humor shows that this tension isn’t ongoing, but has been resolved. These new perspectives or framings can happen anywhere—and collectively contribute to our overall opinion of something.

Since humor has a communicative function, humor tells are the expression of learning.

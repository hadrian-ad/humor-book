The Humor Book

We have a universal learning preference for humor

Since we learn from feeling humor, when we have a choice, we have a universal preference for learning materials that make us feel humor:

Trevor is idly scrolling through a think tank report on the future of rotary-wing airframes. There’s a lot of expense projections. He scrolls to the conclusion to skim it, before closing out of it. (←Trevor doesn’t incorporate new framings↔︎Trevor doesn't learn↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Trevor is listening to his favorite podcast, MilitaryHardware.

Host: “Helicopter pilots are expensive to maintain for militaries, because it’s a demanding job! It’s hard. I mean, think about video games. Video games use very, very simplified controls for vehicles. But even in these games, the vehicle you’re most likely to crash is a helicopter!”

Trevor: *laughs* *thinks* “He’s right.” (←Trevor incorporates new framings↔︎Trevor learns↔︎humor)

Host: “Point being, rotary-wing assets are expensive to maintain, both from maintaining the equipment and skills needed to use them. So why don’t countries get rid of them? Well, until you have something that can do their task better, you’re kind of stuck with them. If you’re deploying soldiers mountain top to mountain top, your choice is either donkeys or helicopter—and until that changes, most militaries will stick with helicopters, no matter their expense.”

Trevor: *smiles* (←Trevor incorporates new framings↔︎Trevor learns↔︎humor)

We always trend toward the documentaries, non-fiction books, podcasts, and so on that we hope will make us feel connection, delight, insight, realization, and so on.

If we’re using something to learn that doesn’t let us feel delight, insight, realization, success, and so on—we give them up or resent them.

This is why we have a universal preference for the option of learning that produces more fitted humor.

Trevor will avoid the think tank report (no humor), books for children (no humor), and choose something that makes him feel humor on relevant topics—something that helps him learn.

Consider:

We learn on the specific topic that generates humor. That means we don't have a preference for the most humor generally, but the most humor specifically (on the topic we want to learn about).

So if Trevor is interested in military helicopters, he wants to feel appreciation, insight, realization, and so on—specifically on topics related to ‘military helicopters,’ and not off-topic on things like ‘apples’ or ‘fantasy novels.’

This is why we have a preference for fitted humor. If a podcast makes Trevor feel humor by making irrelevant asides on apples or fantasy novels, the humor will be unfitted—since his humor isn't on relevant topics. He'll be incorporating new framings—just not on what he’s trying to learns

Quality. Sometimes we think the amount we learn isn’t tied to our frequency and intensity of fitted humor, but instead to the quality of information that's presented. But that's obviously not quite right when we think about it.

If a kid can't read, it would help them learn much to give them a 500 page report—no matter how immaculate the report's anaylis is. Similarly, giving a 500 page report to an unmotivated adult also won't help them learn much on that topic.

Since it's not the quality of information alone that determines learning, it's instead the relation of the person to the content that determines learning—not content by itself. This is why how much someone gets a technical report depends on the user.

Trevor may feel more humor from the podcast—but a defense economist may feel more humor from the think tank report. That’s because a defense economist would have the context to see a graph showing a 4.3% increase in global expenditure on rotary-wing aircraft as a mistake that's OK, and feel insight or realization—while Trevor won't.

The Humor Book

What do we mean by “evaluated”?

We can look at examples where the presence of humor shows the presence of learning, and the absence of humor shows an absence of learning:

Zoe and Emma enter the library, and glance at an illuminated manuscript from the 15th century on display.

Emma: “Hold up. Does that dude have a violin on his head?” (←Emma notices a mistake)

They gather around the case. (←humor tells like focus on present, social tightening, loss of situational awareness, and so on)

Zoe: *squinting* “I guess it depends on when it’s from. When were violins invented anyway?”

Emma: “What’s happening here? Why are there so many flowers?” *half-smiles*

Zoe: *Examining* “Two people are fighting, one’s bloody and... running away?”

Emma: “The guy on the left looks sad, and the guy on the right’s like, dude, what the fuck?”

Zoe: *smiling* “Treachery? Regret?”

Emma: *looking closer* “The guy on the left is holding the bottom of his sword. Do you think they ever held their blade like that, or is that just bad drawing?”

Zoe: “I guess if you had armored gloves, you could do it.” (thoughtfully) “It’s probably hard to do an strong upward swing otherwise, now that I’m thinking about it.”

Emma: *satisfaction* “Right under Violin-Man’s neck-thing! Blurgh!” *mimes blood gushing* *smiles*

They move on in a better mood. (←Zoe and Emma incorporate new perspectives↔︎Zoe and Emma learn↔︎humor)

There was fitted, low-intensity humor throughout the example, with an instance of more intense, less-fitted humor at the end. (“Blurgh!”)

To see how the humor and the interpretation of the manuscript went hand-in-hand, we can use contrasting examples of them not feeling humor:

Zoe and Emma enter the library, and glance at an illuminated manuscript on display. They keep walking. (←Zoe and Emma don’t incorporate new perspectives↔︎Zoe and Emma don't learn↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Zoe and Emma enter the library, and glance at an illuminated manuscript on display. Emma becomes enraged.

Emma: (seething) “I hate these things.”

She can’t settle for an hour. (←Emma doesn’t incorporate new perspectives↔︎Emma doesn't learn↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Zoe and Emma enter the library, and glance at an illuminated manuscript on display. Zoe starts examining it.

Zoe: (interested) “What do you think’s happening here?”

Emma taps her foot impatiently, hanging back, trying to telegraph that they should move on. (←Emma doesn’t incorporate new perspectives↔︎Emma doesn't learn↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Zoe and Emma enter the library, and glance at an illuminate manuscript on display. They start joking about Violin-Head, and make up a stupid backstory for him. They double over laughing. (←since most humor isn’t on topic ‘manuscript,’ Emma and Zoe aren’t incorporating new perspectives about the manuscript↔︎Zoe and Emma don't learn about manuscript)

We can see more examples where humor marks learning:

Zoe and Emma are doing Justin’s assigned reading: a 1915 booklet of pro-suffrage satirical poetry called Are Women People? A Book of Rhymes for Suffrage Times, which mocks inadequate arguments against women’s suffrage.

;sOne satire presents the anti-suffragette arguments as absurd by using the same arguments to say women shouldn’t ride trains:

Why We Oppose Women Travelling in Railway Trains

By Alice Duer Miller

1. Because travelling in trains is not a natural right.

2. Because our great-grandmothers never asked to travel in trains.

3. Because woman’s place is the home, not the train.

4. Because it is unnecessary; there is no point reached by a train that cannot be reached on foot.

5. Because it will double the work of conductors, engineers and brakemen who are already overburdened.

6. Because men smoke and play cards in trains. Is there any reason to believe that women will behave better?

Zoe: *smiling* (←Zoe is incorporating new framings↔︎Zoe learns↔︎humor) “I like this one!”

Emma: (skeptical) “I get it, but train travel just doesn’t really do it for me.” (←Emma hasn’t comfortably incorporated new framings↔︎Emma doesn't learn↔︎no humor)

Zoe: “I guess the today we’d have to imagine they’re talking about opposing women riding busses or cars instead.”

Emma: “Oh.” *smiles* (←Emma incorporates new framings↔︎Emma learns↔︎humor) “Well, that’s even more ridiculous.”

Zoe is doing homework and is reading about cells.

“Cells are small, but not unimaginably so. Cells are about one thousand times smaller in length than objects in our everyday world. Typical cells in our bodies are about ten micrometers in length—roughly one thousand times smaller than the last joint in your finger. A thousand-fold difference in length is not difficult to visualize: a grain of rice is about one thousand times smaller in length than the room you are sitting in. Imagine your room filled with rice grains, and this will give you an idea of the billion or so cells that make up your fingertip.”

Zoe smiles at the analogy. This is really interesting! (←Zoe incorporates new framings↔︎Zoe learns↔︎humor)

- OR -

Zoe is bored by this remedial biology reading. She can’t believe she has to take Ecological Perspectives to graduate. It’s a class for freshmen! (←Zoe doesn’t incorporate new framings↔︎Zoe doesn't learn↔︎humor)

Trevor is idly scrolling through a think tank report on the future of rotary-wing airframes. There’s a lot of expense projections. He scrolls to the conclusion to skim it, before closing out of it. (←Trevor doesn’t incorporate new framings↔︎Trevor doesn't learn↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Trevor is listening to his favorite podcast, MilitaryHardware.

Host: “Helicopter pilots are expensive to maintain for militaries, because it’s a demanding job! It’s hard. I mean, think about video games. Video games use very, very simplified controls for vehicles. But even in these games, the vehicle you’re most likely to crash is a helicopter!”

Trevor: *laughs* *thinks* “He’s right.” (←Trevor incorporates new framings↔︎Trevor learns↔︎humor)

Host: “Point being, rotary-wing assets are expensive to maintain, both from maintaining the equipment and skills needed to use them. So why don’t countries get rid of them? Well, until you have something that can do their task better, you’re kind of stuck with them. If you’re deploying soldiers mountain top to mountain top, your choice is either donkeys or helicopter—and until that changes, most militaries will stick with helicopters, no matter their expense.”

Trevor: *smiles* (←Trevor incorporates new framings↔︎Trevor learns↔︎humor)

We have words like insight, learning, perspective-incorporation, teaching, understanding, and so on—and the connection between them all is fitted humor.

Consider:

We learn on the specific topic that generates humor. So if Trevor is interested in military helicopters, he wants to feel appreciation, insight, realization, and so on—specifically on topics related to ‘military helicopters,’ and not off-topic on things like ‘apples’ or ‘fantasy novels.’

If a podcast makes Trevor feel humor by making irrelevant asides on apples or fantasy novels, the humor will be unfitted—since his humor isn't on relevant topics. The irrelevent topics means he wouldn't be incorporating new framings about what he’s trying to learns.

When we have a choice, we always choose documentaries, non-fiction books, podcasts, and so on that we hope will make us feel connection, delight, insight, realization, and so on.

If we’re using something to learn that doesn’t let us feel delight, insight, realization, success, and so on—we give them up or resent them.

This is why we have a universal preference for the option of learning that produces more fitted humor. Trevor will avoid the think tank report (no humor), books for children (no humor), and choose something that makes him feel humor on relevant topics—something that helps him learn.

Quality. Sometimes we think the amount we learn isn’t tied to our frequency and intensity of fitted humor, but instead to the quality of information. But that's obviously not quite right when we think about it.

Giving a kid who can't read a 500 page report wouldn't help them learn much on that topic—no matter how immaculate the anaylis is in that report. For the same reason, giving a 500 page report to an unmotivated adult also won't help them learn much on that topic.

Instead, it's the relation of the person to the content that determines learning—not content by itself. This is why how much someone learns from a podcast compared to a technical report depends on the user.

Trevor may feel more humor from the podcast—but a defense economist may feel more humor from the think tank report. That’s because a defense economist would have the context to see a graph showing a 4.3% increase in global expenditure on rotary-wing aircraft as a mistake that's OK, and feel insight or realization—while Trevor won't.

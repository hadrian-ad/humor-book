A long mistake doesn't mean long humor

A long mistake doesn't mean long humor.

For instance:

If we lost our cat, that would be a long mistake—but that doesn't mean we would feel long humor.

That's because humor only happens if mistakes and OKs are balanced. Otherwise, if our mistake is larger than our OK, we'll feel a long duration of mistake-predominant emotions instead:

Hana gets a position at her dream lab. Then her offer is unexpectedly rescinded. She cries for a week. (←a long mistake only leads to humor if it balances with an OK)

- OR -

Hana is crushed to not get a position at her dream lab. But she gets an unexpected offer a week later. She laughs and smiles for a week. (←a long mistake only leads to humor if it balances with an OK)

Trevor is about to ask Yasmin out for the first time. He sweats and his hands shake. Finally he hits send, and sits on his couch, ashen faced.

She never responds. Trevor feels sick to his stomach for days.

- OR -

Yasmin: *texting* “Sure! I was hoping to hear from you!”

Trevor: *fist pumps* *beams* *laughs* *dances* *takes long ecstatic walk* (←long duration of humor)

In our broader system of evaluations, the duration of our mistakes helps us understand why any emotion lasts as long as it does.

The wooded lot near their home was cleared. It causes Jennifer grief that year. (←Jennifer feels long duration of grief) 

We experience long durations of mistake-predominant emotions all the time, like when: 

  • a friend dies
  • a limb is lost
  • a partner leaves us
  • a nearby forest is developed
  • we do something we think is shameful
  • we become displaced

(and so on)

The same process that prolongs our humor (things that go against integrated expectations) is what can prolong our anger or sadness as well.

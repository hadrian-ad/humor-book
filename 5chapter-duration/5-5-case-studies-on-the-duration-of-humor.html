Case studies on the duration of humor

Putting everything together, we can think about why a specific instance of humor was as short or long as it was:

Short duration of humor

Trevor is talking the dog out at night. As General Zod squats to pee, Trevor looks into the nearby patch of dark trees, and a deep fear comes over him.

He starts to jog back to the house with the dog until he feels possessed by terror and sprints the final way. He races inside and locks the door, and breathes a sigh of relief.

“Phew. We made it, Zod. Safe!” He laughs as the dog wags its tail. (←two seconds of humor) Trevor goes about his business.

Trevor's mistake became small fast, since nothing really went contrary to any of his integrated expectations.

His humor was a short duration, as there wasn't much time for his evaluations to balance.

Medium duration of humor

Lina gets an email that her college decision notification is ready. Her blood freezes. Heart pounding, she logs on to her account and clicks the ‘view decision update letter.’ Her mouth is dry while it loads.

“Congratulations!” the letter says. Lina leaps up and pumps her fist.

“Mom, mom!” she yells, thumping down the stairs. “I got into Pitt!” he jumps up and down and dances, glowing with happiness.

As her mom congratulates her, she puts on a celebratory plastic tiara, feeling invincibly happy. They head out to get pizza, and she catches her reflection in the doors and laughs, and spends the rest of the evening joking with her mom. (←two hours of humor)

Lina's mistake took a while to shrink, since it went contrary to a year of thought and worry. This large mistake matched her large OK for a medium duration.

Long duration of humor

Dr. Timothy Ailes participates in Doctors Without Borders, and spends six months assisting people displaced by violence. While he has comfortable lodging not far from the capital, seeing the degraded infrastructure, services, and environment continually shocks him.

When he returns home to Washington, he’s shocked in reverse. As he lands in Tacoma and takes the train into Seattle, he’s speechless at the orderly housing and surplus of urban trees and efficient infrastructure with clean boundaries.

For weeks, he finds himself laughing almost at random. It’s complex humor, giving a strange sense of unreality as he goes about his old routines in Seattle. (←two weeks of humor)

Since Timothy had habituated to living in Seattle, he had developed integrated expectations to those urban conditions. When he moved somewhere else, the different conditions went against his integrated expectations. 

This created a long mistake, since it took him a long time to acclimate. But this long mistake didn’t generate long humor, since this mistake didn't constantly match with an OK. 

The same process happens in reverse when Timothy moves back to Seattle. He had by then developed new expectations around Bangui, which meant that it was now Seattle that deviates from those expectations.

This long mistake does generate long duration of humor, since the long mistake is seen as mostly OK. 

Awareness. It's usually second nature to note the intensity of humor. ("How much humor are they feeling?") But once it's pointed out, it quickly becomes second nature to note the duration of humor too. ("How long are they feeling humor for?")

Both dimensions of humor are important to notice—intensity and duration—since both give us important information about their evaluations.

We usually think that our senses keep us aware of what is around us. An interesting alternative perspective is that our senses let us be aware of what’s changing around us. Our senses are “difference noticers.”

This is why our eyes glide over familiar things—while immediately latching onto very small differences, like a:

  • new grey hair
  • new flower in our yard
  • new behavior in the dog
  • frame that’s no longer level
  • small scratch on our partner’s face
  • fork that’s out of place on the counter
  • single typo in a four-hundred-page book

(and so on)

Our senses de-emphasize whatever accords with our expectations, while emphasizing things that deviate from those expectations. That means our senses prioritize our awareness of mistakes.

The things that accord with what we expect accrue perceptual camouflage—our attention slides off them. The more perceptual camouflage something has, the more effort it takes to evaluate something and to generate emotions from it.

By screening out the things that have perceptual camouflage, we create a flat backdrop (“expectations”) that mistakes (“deviations from expectations”) pop out on.

Consider:

Perceptual camouflage is a bit like laying out a white tablecloth on the grass. This flat background makes it easy to see insects crawling across it. If we weren’t able to use a tablecloth, it would be much harder to notice insects on the grass.

By turning the expected things around us into a "tablecloth," anything that moves onto that tablecloth pops to attention.

This is why the view from our new office may be intensely interesting to us at first—but stop generating emotions in us at all after a few weeks. We no longer notice or evaluate the view once it's fully accrued perceptual camouflage.

Perceptual camouflage can get stripped, like when a guest exclaims at the view from our office, or when smoke from a distant fire catches our eye. These bring the view back to our attention, making it noticeable again.

Similarly, we'll notice every creak and groan in our new house for a few weeks. But eventually we'll stop noticing any noises from our house at all—because those noises accrue perceptual camouflage.

By de-emphasizing regular sounds, we create a flat backdrop that lets even small noises to jump out at us—like how unexpected soft noises will demand our attention, even at the same time we no longer register the much louder cars, creaks, pipes, wind, and so on.

Sometimes, something happens that strips away all the perceptual camouflage on something, like when we:

  • get high (and everything becomes jewel-like)
  • visit a new city (and everything seems interesting)
  • have a first snowfall (and the landscape seem new)
  • attend a street protest (and the world seems different)
  • use glasses for the first time (and everything seems clear)
  • celebrate a festival (and decorations reinvigorated an old space)
  • watch high winds (and we’re mesmerized by how the trees move)

(and so on)

In these cases, since everything deviates from our expectations, everything is interesting to us!

Consider:

Normally, it takes effort to notice the branch outside our window. But after the first snowfall, it's hard not to look at the snowy branch! It attracts our attention.

We experience moments vividly when something strips perceptual camouflage.

Sometimes, this vividness is part of mistake-predominant experiences—like how driving through high winds can cause us attentive worry, and not humor.

But other times, these camouflage-stripping moments generate broad feelings of humor, like when Yasmin gets glasses for the first time. We often describe these positive experiences as being alive, interesting, intense, joyful, or so on.

While all this happens with our external senses (what we hear or see)—it also happens with our internal senses too. (What we think or feel.)

In the same way we get used to a view, we also get used to a framing. Those framings accrue perceptual camouflage just like anything else, and eventually we stop giving evaluatory attention to that framing:

Frank: “How’s your commute?”

Yasmin: “Oh, long. Twenty-five minutes.” (←Yasmin is used to a framing of “long”)

Frank: “Oh, that’s not so long. Mine takes me three hours each day!”

Yasmin: (shocked) “What! Oh my god!” *laughs*

- LATER -

Yasmin gets into her car and zips home. Three hours! (←a reframing from “long” to “short” strips perceptual camouflage)

She feels lucky beyond belief.

Zoe is used to feeling shame about her conversation with Ryan. How could she have been so stupid! (←Zoe is used to a framing of “big deal”)

- LATER -

After school one day, the text conversation crosses Zoe’s mind. It wasn’t such a big deal after all, was it? She gives a loud laugh, suddenly feeling cleansed. (←a reframing from “big deal” to “not a big deal” strips perceptual camouflage)

Yasmin: *getting ready* *thinking* “I can’t believe I have to blow off Trevor just because Stephen and Tucker wants me to come into work this weekend. Tucker’s an entitled asshole, and Stephen thinks I’m like some kind of slave. God I hate this job, but I have to stick with it, because this is the only way I’ll ever be able to one day have a nice life. I have no choice!” (←Yasmin is used to a framing of “trapped”)

She stops to think about that.

Yasmin: *laughs* “Wow. Maybe I should look for a new job.”

A weight falls off her, and she feels a clarity to things, and she laughs again. (←a reframing from “trapped” to “not trapped” strips perceptual camouflage)

As we become used to framing something a particular way, that framing becomes expected and invisible. Those framings start to seem like just how the world is—and it becomes hard to give these framings attention.

But alternative framings strip perceptual camouflage, making things seem new.

So in the same way looking at something from a new angle (our house, our face, a tourist attraction) generates mistakes, looking at something from a new framing also generates mistakes.

All this helps us think about how emotions and senses connect to mistakes. Mistakes are nubs of non-regular information. Our senses highlight those nubs for us, which fast-tracks them for evaluation. These evaluations of mistakes make up our evaluatory state—our emotional state. Sometimes, these evaluations are expressed as humor.

This gives us a brief initial look at the connection between expectations, senses, and humor—and the connection between humor and the positive removal of perceptual camouflage.

Humor doesn't just have a magnitude, but a duration as well. 

  • The duration of humor is the duration that evaluations are near to a 1 ratio.
  • Our evaluations have a varying resistance to change. This resistance is the durability and broader integration of either our expectations or our perception of security.
  • In the same way durable expectations can lead to long durations of humor, they can also lead to long durations of any emotion.

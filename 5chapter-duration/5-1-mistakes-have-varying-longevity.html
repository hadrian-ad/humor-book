Mistakes have varying longevity

All mistakes shrink over time. At first, something deviates from our expectations—but we immediately start acclimating to that deviation. 

Eventually, what was a deviation from our expectations turns into our expectations:

Heather gets a haircut. All week, she feels strange whenever she looks into the mirror or washes her hair. (←mistake lasts for a week)

Eventually, she starts to look normal to herself when she looks in the mirror. (←Heather acclimates to mistake)

A month later, Heather looks at an old post of her with her old hairstyle. She looked so different back then! (←Heather’s old mistake has become her expectations, and her old expectations have become a mistake)

But while we always acclimate to mistakes, sometimes this happens quickly, and other times slowly: 

The rate of acclimation depends on the durability of our expectations. Sometimes our expectations are mallable, and adjust quickly. Other times, our expectations are durable, and it takes us longer to adjust:

Amaya usually wears bright colors, so when Minh sees her wearing all black she briefly looks like a new person to him. (←mistake deviates from Minh's more malleable expectations↔︎relatively short mistake↔︎Minh quickly acclimates)

- OR -

Amaya only wears bright colors, and so when Minh sees her wearing all black he can’t get over how much she looks like a new person to him. (←mistake deviates from Minh's more durable expectations↔︎relatively long mistake↔︎Minh slowly acclimates)  

Owen decides to wear a shirt he doesn't usually wear. At first he feels self-concious about it, but soon he doesn't give it any thought.(←mistake deviates from Minh's more malleable expectations↔︎relatively short mistake↔︎Minh quickly acclimates)

- OR -

Owen decides to wear an accessory he has never worn before. It's supposed to be fashionable, but that doesn't stop him from feeling self-concious and noticing it the entire day. (←mistake deviates from Minh's more durable expectations↔︎relatively long mistake↔︎Minh slowly acclimates)

Zoe and Emma marvel at how much better the university's administrative building looks with the new coat of paint that was applied over the weekend. They notice it's new paint for a while. But after a while, it just seems like how the building has always been. (←mistake deviates from Zoe and Emma's more malleable expectations↔︎relatively short mistake↔︎Zoe and Emma quickly acclimate)

- OR -

Zoe and Emma marvel at how different the street looks now that the university tore down the old administrative building over the weekend. It’s just… gone. The site seems weird for weeks. (←mistake deviates from Zoe and Emma's more durable expectations↔︎relatively long mistake↔︎Zoe and Emma slowly acclimate)

Hourglasses. We can use an hourglass as a metaphor for the durability of our expectations. When we start to form expectations, we flip over the hourglass and sand pours into the bottom.

The longer something behaves in ways we expect, the more sand we accumulate at the bottom of our hourglass. The more sand on the bottom, the longer it takes to empty the bottom of our expectations when we flip the hourglass over again.

For instance, when Trevor makes a 69 joke, it’s like briefly flipping the hourglass over, and then flipping it over again—the mistake empties quickly:

By contrast, if Zoe and Emma look at a building every day for four years, they have a lot of time for sand to be added to their expectations. Once the building is torn down, it then takes a while for the sand to drain out: 

Once the sand has drained out, they’ve acclimated to a new normal, and they start adding sand to new expectations.

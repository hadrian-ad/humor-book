Our mistakes become expectations

All mistakes shrink over time: 

That's because when something deviates from our expectations, we immediately start adjusting our expectations. Eventually, our expectations completely readjust:

Heather gets a haircut. All week, she feels strange whenever she looks into the mirror or washes her hair. (←mistake lasts for a week)

Eventually, she starts to look normal to herself when she looks in the mirror. (←Heather readjust to mistake)

A month later, Heather looks at an old post of her with her old hairstyle. She looked so different back then! (←Heather’s old mistake has become her expectations, and her old expectations have become a mistake)

While our expectations always adjust, sometimes our expectations readjust quickly, and other times slowly: 

Minh and Trevor check their co-op score. 469.

Trevor: “69. Nice.”

Minh: "Heh" (←Minh's expectations quickly adjust)

- OR -

Minh and his mom check their co-op score. 469.

Mom: "69. Nice."

Minh: (surprised) "Mom!" *laughs* (←Minh's expectations slowly adjust)  

Zoe wears an accessory she's never worn before. It's a bit unusual at first, but she gets used to it quickly. (←Zoe's expectations quickly readjust)

- OR -

Owen wears an accessory he's never worn before. He's never worn anything like that before, and he feels self-conscious about it the entire day. (←Owen's expectations slowly readjust)

The university's administrative building had a new coat of paint applied over the weekend. Zoe and Emma notice the refreshed colors for a while, but after a week it just seems like how the building has always been. (←Zoe and Emma's expectations quickly readjust)

- OR -

The university's administrative building was torn down over the weekend. Zoe and Emma marvel at how different the street looks now. The building is just… gone. The site seems weird for weeks. (←Zoe and Emma expectations slowly readjusts)

The more integrated our expectations are, the longer it takes us to readjust them.

Consider:

It's easy to move a piece of furniture in an otherwise empty room. We just move it!

But if our room has shelving, tables, chairs, storage, and so on, moving furniture interrupts an integrated arrangement. Moving one thing has knock-on effects on everything else.

Hourglasses. One component of "integrated" expectations is their durability. We can use an hourglass as a metaphor for this. When we start to form expectations, we flip over the hourglass and sand pours into the bottom.

The longer something behaves in ways we expect, the more sand we accumulate at the bottom of our hourglass. And the more sand on the bottom, the longer it takes to empty the bottom of our expectations when we flip the hourglass over again.

For instance, when Trevor makes a 69 joke, it’s like briefly flipping the hourglass over, and then flipping it over again—the mistake empties quickly:

By contrast, if Zoe and Emma look at a building every day for four years, they have a lot of time for sand to be added to their expectations. Once the building is torn down, it then takes a while for the sand to drain out: 

Once the sand has drained out, they’ve readjusted to a new normal, and they start adding sand to new expectations.

Some long mistakes lead to long humor

Sometimes we feel humor not just for a few seconds—but for hours or days. This happens when something deviates from particularly durable expectations: 

Jennifer’s had chronic pain for months. She had forgotten what it felt like to be without pain—and when she gets new pain medication, she smiles all day. (←a durable mistake leads to long humor)

When Trevor is discharged from the army, he barely knows what to do with his time. No longer having to obey a controlling schedule, he settled into a new routine. It felt good—he smiled and laughed a lot for weeks. (←a durable mistake leads to long humor)

Yasmin moves from her crowded rental house into a one-bedroom apartment with a nice window. She hums and smiles for weeks as she gets used to the privacy and new surroundings. (←a durable mistake leads to long humor)

Amber sells her sedan and gets a truck with a much higher ride. She feels like she’s driving from the top of a skyscraper, and it causes her no end of amusement. (←a durable mistake leads to long humor)

Yasmin gets glasses for the first time and blinks in amazement. She can see the individual leaves on trees! She laughs and smiles all day, and can’t stop exclaiming to her coworkers. (←a durable mistake leads to long humor)

Eventually, we acclimate to even very durable expectations—and then the old expectations becomes our new mistakes. This is like how eventually not wearing glasses will be a mistake for Yasmin. 

At that point, Yasmin will be more likely to feel humor if she doesn't put on her glasses than if she does.

Fuel. Sometimes, it seems like one mistake generates a long duration of humor. When Yasmin puts on glasses for the first time, she’ll think of getting glasses as a single mistake that causes a lot of humor.

Other times, it seems like many mistakes generate a long duration of humor. When Yasmin watches a comedian, she’ll think of the comedian's jokes and stories as many different mistakes that together cause a lot of humor. 

We can use a metaphor with wood fuels to think about this. Durable expectations are like logs that burn for a long time (getting glasses). If we don't have a method or opportunity to burn a big log for humor, we can use many smaller sticks instead (hearing a comedic routine).

Even though most comedians can't do one thing and make the audience chuckle for an hour afterward, this often does happen in our own lives—like when we get glasses, hear really good news, have good luck, and so on.

In these cases, there's nothing surprising about laughing and smiling for ten minutes, an hour, or more—they're just things that deviate from our durable expectations.

We synchronize to form groups

We always network emotionally with everyone around us. When we walk into a coffee shop, we’ll be part of the overall emotion network:

The overall network has the most shared topics its synchronizing around, like ‘public manners,’ ‘physical safety,’ ‘noise decorum,’ and so on.

Within the overall network are subnetworks. These are emotion networks set within larger emotion networks—they synchronize around different topics than are shared by everyone:

For instance, Zoe and Emma are constantly synchronizing around many topics specific to them—which constitutes their emotion network within the emotion network.

They’ll notice small desynchronizations on topics only they are closely monitoring:

Emma: "Thank god Justin gave us all extensions."

Zoe: “Yeah.” *tilts head back slightly* (←Zoe doesn’t feel their evaluations are synchronized)

Emma: (curious) “Yeah? But what?" (←Emma picks up their desynchronization, and wants to resynchronize)

Zoe and Emma are on their laptops, when Zoe gives a small laugh.

Emma: *looks up* *smiling* “What?” (←Emma picks up their desynchronization, and wants to synchronize)

While head tilts or small laughs will desynchronize Emma and Zoe’s emotion network, it doesn't desynchronize the overall emotion network. No one outside the subnetwork cares that much, since Zoe’s head tilt doesn't involve any of the network-wide topics.

By contrast, if someone starts yelling, the overall network may feel desynchronized:

Someone start yelling and the entire cafe goes quiet. (←yelling desynchronizes the overall network)

Zoe and Emma start looking around. Zoe makes eye contact with someone sitting near the window, and Emma examines a couple who’s looking at the person yelling. The barista scans everyone in the room. (←cafe tries to synchronize evaluations and reach an evaluatory consensus)

When the cafe becomes desynchronized, everyone there will try to resynchronize their evaluations. This establishes an emotional consensus, which helps them decide on what to do—like whether to intervene with the yeller or not.

If the cafe can’t resynchronize, there's no emotional consensus. The emotion network fragments, and people will feel uncomfortable or unsafe and leave:

Efficiency. Our emotion network lets us to have greater evaluatory awareness than we would on our own. That's because emotion networks give efficiencies of scale. In the same way many hands make light manual work, many evaluators make light evaluatory work.

For instance, when a dish breaks in the kitchen of a cafe, all twenty customers don't need to walk to the kitchen to personally decide whether or not everything's okay. Instead, one or two people can make a evaluation on it, and then everyone else can join that conclusion.

So if a dish breaks and the staff are calm, the customers will be calm—even without actually knowing what specifically happened. By pooling our evaluations, the cafe limits the number of redundant evaluations that had to happen for everyone to feel aware and calm.

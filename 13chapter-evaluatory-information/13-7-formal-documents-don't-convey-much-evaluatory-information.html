Formal documents don't convey much evaluatory information

Sometimes we think formal writing probably has more information in it than informal writing—but it often has less:

Yasmin: *texting* "did you get groceries lmaoo" (←informal writing)

Trevor looks at the text and laughs.

- OR -

Yasmin: *texting* "Did you get groceries?" (←formal writing)

Trevor looks at the text and wonders: Is she blaming him, laughing at him, happy that he got her groceries, upset, or...?(←the formal writing was less clear to Trevor than the informal writing)

Emma: (excited) *texting* “I got into the CONFERENCE!!!!!!! AAAAAAAAA” (←informal writing)

Zoe: (excited) *texting* “Yasssss” *series of emojis* (←informal elements increase the information density of the exchange)

- OR -

Emma: (excited) *texting* “My paper was accepted into the conference.” (←formal writing)

Zoe: (excited) *texting* “Well done.” (←formality decreases the information density of the exchange)

Even though the content information was the same in both (Emma got into the conference), the amount of evaluatory information was different. (How Emma felt about getting into the conference).

The informal writing fit more information in the same amount of space than the formal writing.

Consider:

Formal writing tends to minimize evaluatory information. This doesn’t mean readers don’t create their own evaluatory information in response to formal writing—it just means the documents themselves don't convey much evaluatory information.

We use evaluatory information to interpret sentences. This is why grammatically straightforward sentences can still be ambiguous when texted. ("Did you get groceries?") Evaluatory cues can be used to increase the information in a sentence and decrease ambiguity. ("lmaoo")

Density. Formal writing struggles to convey evaluatory information. This is one reason descriptions of humor are so stilted in formal documents—they avoid oral words like “funny”; transliterations of laughter (“hurhh!”); exclamation marks; and so on.

Instead, we get relatively lifeless phrases like “laughter was heard,” “the comment was met with derision,” the speaker entertained the audience for an hour,” and so on. This formal language doesn’t record much evaluatory information about duration, intensity, frequency, or whether the humor tilted toward mistake-predominance or OK-predominance.

By contrast, informal writing wants to increase evaluatory information—since we use informal writing to group and efficiently share information over our emotion network. This is why informal writing constantly incorporates abbreviations, acronyms, colors, emoticons, emojis, filters, font, formatting, meme formats, repeat letters, timing standards, and so on.

This is often most noticeable when it comes to humor. Informal writing uses words like lol, lolz, lololololol, lmaoo, rofl, roflmao, and so on—which add precision and nuance to the evaluatory information in a way that we can’t do as quickly or efficiently in formal writing.

We can look at an example of how informal English increases the amount of evaluatory information.

Since they were watching a livestream, their goal in the chat was to share as much evaluatory information as quickly as possible—content information was neither here nor there, just like when a crowd boos, cheers, claps, stomps their feet, and so on.

By using informal English, they made the most of limited space, time, and attention—the informal English increased the density of their evaluatory information. That's because the difference between “I'm doing this” and “IM SO DOING THIS” is greater information density:

The informal English has more evaluatory information. Often, formal English tries to make up for its low density of evaluatory information by becoming much longer. So instead of the dense and short "IM SO DOING THIS," they go for something light and long. ("I have to say, everyone, that I feel engaged and ready. I think I'll be taking action this time around, and I'll do so with vigor. I feel like yelling, to be honest.")

Above, what's the benefit of the formal English over the informal English ? Not much. It's not sharing much more content information, and is only taking more time to share the same amount of evaluatory information. In a livestream, this time isn't available—by the time someone types out a few sentences, what they're reacting to may be passed.

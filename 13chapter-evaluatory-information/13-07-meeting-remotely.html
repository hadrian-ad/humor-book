Just like when a crowd boos, cheers, claps, stomps their feet, and so on (shares evaluatory information), content information was neither here nor there for the chat. Their goal was to share as much evaluatory information as quickly as possible.

They did this by using informal English, which makes the most of limited attention, space, time, and so on. That's because the difference between something like “I'm doing this” and “IM SO DOING THIS” is greater information density:

Often, formal English tries to make up for its low density of evaluatory information by becoming much longer. So instead of the informationally dense and short "IM SO DOING THIS," they go for something informationally light and long. ("I have to say, everyone, that I feel engaged and ready. I think I'll be taking action this time around, and I'll do so with vigor. I feel like yelling, to be honest.")

There isn't much benefit to formal English in cases like these. Formal English only takes more time to share the same amount of evaluatory information—and not even sharing much more content information.

In a livestream, this time isn't available. By the time someone types out a few formal sentences, what they're reacting to may be passed.

Consider:

One definition of "formal" is that evaluatory information is stylistically reduced—a document or building is de-personalized.

The amount evaluatory information is reduced creates degrees of formality.

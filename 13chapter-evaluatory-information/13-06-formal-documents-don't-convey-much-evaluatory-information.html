Formal writing conveys less evaluatory information

Between writing formally and informally, writing informally usually conveys more information:

Yasmin: *texting* "did you get groceries lmaoo" (←informal writing)

Trevor looks at the text and laughs.

- OR -

Yasmin: *texting* "Did you get groceries?" (←formal writing)

Trevor looks at the text and wonders: Is she blaming him, laughing at him, happy that he got her groceries, upset, or...? (←the formal writing was less clear to Trevor than the informal writing)

Without evaluatory information, even grammatically straightforward sentences can be ambiguous. ("Did you get groceries?")

That's because we interpret sentences with evaluatory information. When this evaluatory information is stripped, it's harder to interpret sentences—we find them ambiguous.

It's not until we add evaluatory information to sentences that ambiguity decreases. ("lmaoo")

Zoe recieves a text from Emma that says: “I got into the CONFERENCE!!!!!!! AAAAAAAAA” (←informal writing)

Zoe: (excited) *texting* “Yasssss” *series of emojis* (←informal elements increase the information density of the exchange)

- OR -

Zoe recieves a text from Emma that says: “My paper was accepted into the conference.” (←formal writing)

Zoe: (excited) *texting* “Well done.” (←formality decreases the information density of the exchange)

Even though the content information was the same in both (Emma got into the conference), the amount of evaluatory information was less. (How Emma felt about getting into the conference).

Emma's formal writing ("My paper was accepted into the conference") doesn't specify whether she's amused, bored, thrilled, horrified, and so on. It's the informal writing fit more information in the same amount of space than the formal writing.

Consider:

Formal writing tends to minimize evaluatory information. This doesn’t mean readers don’t create their own evaluatory information in response to formal writing—it just means the documents themselves don't convey much evaluatory information.

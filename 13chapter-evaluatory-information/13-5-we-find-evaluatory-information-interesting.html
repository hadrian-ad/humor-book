We find evaluatory information interesting

We find evaluatory information to be inherently interesting and important generally. By contrast, we only care about content information circumstantially.

In the following picture, there's nothing really interesting happening. Even so, we still make a split-second glance at the people—because evaluatory information is inherently interesting to us:

But even though we find their emotion tells briefly interesting (evaluatory information), we probably aren't tormented by unfulfilled curiosity about what they're thinking about (content information)—because content information is only of circumstantial interest to us.

Even if the people in the picture above were deep in conversation, we probably still wouldn't care much about the content information. We often enjoy spending a second people watching to see how people are reacting to things (evaluatory information) without giving much thought to what they're talking about (content information).

Raw data. We probably have sets of data that do interest us, but many more sets of data that don't interest us. That's because, unlike evaluatory information, content inforamtion isn't inherently interesting.

If a random person walked up holding up the data below, we'd first check their face. And unknown tables of data hold little interest to us:

Raw data doesn't communicate evaluatory information. ("That table of raw data is feeling upset right now.") The data itself isn't expected or not expecting anything, nor does it have a sense of consequences—instead, it's people who express evaluatory information at the data.

Of course, people can present data in a way that conveys their reactions—like by using an exclamation mark (!) or red font. But these are actions taken by people in order to entwine evaluatory information to content information.

Often, the data we find most interesting is already entwined with evaluatory information, or when we have the background to express evaluatory information on the data ourselves—since we usually find evaluatory information more important than content information.

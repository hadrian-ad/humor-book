The Humor Book

Words and emotions fill different roles

At this point, we can think about what words do, and what emotions do. They work together, but fill different roles:

What emotions do

We use emotion tells to share evaluatory information—and emotion tells share this with much greater information density than words do alone. This is obvious whenever we look at a transcription of a typical conversation:

Zoe: “Emma… Emma… is the… uh. Is the…”

Emma: “The… the thing…”

Zoe: “No, the other…, uh…”

Emma: “Um. Mm. I think it’s…”

Zoe: “Later…”

Emma: “Yeah. February, or something. I, uh, can check.”

Zoe: “No, it’s—it’s good.”

When we transcribe day-to-day conversation into just words, we strip the conversation of most of the emotion tells—which also strips the conversation of most of its information!

To see how much information has been stripped, we can notice that we don’t even know whether Zoe was angry, amused, confused, crushed, bored, eager, excited, happy, hopeful, indifferent, nervous, sad, thoughtful, wistful, and so on—or in what combination, at which moments.

What words do

We use words (or indications, pictures, or so on) to activate specific topics in combination or sequences—even if those topics are distant, fictional, future, past, surreal, and so on. This ensures we're synchronized on active topics with someone.

Once we're synchronized on active topics, we know what our emotion tells are communicating about:

If there were dinosaurs around, Emma and Zoe could simply point to them to synchronize their active topics. But words allow them to synchronize around these topics even when dinosaurs aren't around!

Consider:

Words let them stay on the same page when exchanging evaluatory information. So if Emma mentions a dinosaur and Zoe grins, Emma can sort Zoe's evaluatory information into the 'dinosaur' bin.

But if Zoe just grins out of the blue, Emma won't know which bin to sort this evaluatory information into—at least, not without words.

The topic 'dinosaur' isn't that complicated to convey without words—since we can act them out, draw them, point at a picture, and so on. But this gets harder as soon as we start chaining topics together, like 'keeping dinosaurs as pets.'

Without words, it's hard for Emma to get Zoe to synchronize over the topic 'keeping dinosaurs as pets.' But it's easy with words—Emma just says: "How awesome would it be to have a dinosaur as a pet?" and then takes in Zoe's reaction.

What words and emotions do together

In this sense, emotions are like a fire hydrant of information. Emotions have a lot of informational volume—but making use of that volume depends on on words to direct that information somewhere specific.

We can see Emma do this below: she uses words to activate specific combinations of topics, which lets her and Zoe share evaluatory information on those topics specifically:

Emma and Zoe are watching cars from a bench.

Emma: “Remember Mr. Myrtle?” (←Emma uses speech to activate an old topic)

Zoe: (angry) “Yes.” (←Zoe uses their emotional network to share evaluatory information on that topic)

Emma: “Remember Mrs. Goldberg? (←Emma uses speech to activate another topic)

Zoe: (happy) “Yeah!” (←Zoe uses their emotional network to share evaluatory information on that topic)

Emma: “They got married!” (←Emma uses speech to combine and introduce topics)

Zoe: (confused) “No!” (←Zoe uses their emotional network to share evaluatory information on that topic)

Emma used words to synchronize their active topics, which lets them share evaluatory information on the same thing. But even though the words were necessary for this kind of control over topics, the majority of the information that was communicated was actually done over the emotion network.

This is obvious when we count the words: Emma spoke nine words, and Zoe spoke three. That's not a lot of words! Emma's words were necessary to topicalize the evaluatory information—but at the same time, the words themselves were an insignificant amount of the total information communicated with each other.

This helps us see how words and emotion tells work together to be exponentially more useful together than when apart. It also helps us avoid giving too much weight to the role that words play by themselves in our day-to-day lives.

This is important when it comes to thinking about humor. Humor isn't something about words per se—humor is an emotion. It’s based on evaluations of content, not the content itself. In interactions, it’s this sharing and synchronizing of evaluations that makes interpersonal humor possible.

This gives us context for the rest of this book. Getting better at humor isn’t really about getting better at words—it means getting better at emotions.

Sophistication. Even though humor is an emotion, we sometimes think of humor as a skill of rhetorical sophistication. But it’s not—humor is better thought of as a skill of emotional sophistication.

This is obvious whenever we look at what makes people laugh outside of spectator entertainment. Most of the time, it’s not beautiful or clever speech. Often, the speech has no special qualities at all—in fact, the coherence of our speech usually declines when we’re feeling intense. But since humor isn’t rhetorical sophistication, the coherence of our speech is often neither here nor there to how much humor two friends can make each other feel.

Our verbal communication activates certain topics, which partially constructs someone’s evaluatory state. From there, we shift mistakes and OKs into humor—an activity that’s much broader and subtler than “good speech.” It’s instead something much more intricately linked to evaluations and synchronization, and requires emotional sophistication to do well.

That's the end of this chapter!

All social animals feel humor. At first, it can seem weird to say this outright, without any hedging—but it's mainly weird if we only think that humor is about jokes, satire, wit, resolving existential tension through art, and so on.

Other animals don’t appreciate puns in comedic movies like we do—but just because animals don't appreciate comedic movies doesn't mean they don't feel humor.

Babies can’t appreciate comedic movies either—but we still say they feel humor (they smile and laugh), even though no five-month-old has ever written a script or made a witty comment. That’s because we intuitively know that humor is something separate from verbal language.

In the same vein, it wouldn’t make sense to say animals don’t feel humor either—just because they don’t laugh at comedic dialogue or make witty comments.

Our model of humor applies fairly well to non-human animals:

Mistakes and OKs

Animals feel humor when the size of their mistakes and OKs balance. When those don’t balance, they feel mistake-predominant or OK-predominant emotions instead:

mistake-predominant emotions

Trevor shouts as he loses an online match. General Zod looks up, startled. (←mistake)

Zod is worried. (←mistake is not OK) He puts his head on his paws and he glances around, concerned. (←mistake is larger than OK↔mistake-predominant emotions)

balanced emotions

Trevor shouts as he loses a match. General Zod looks up, startled. (←mistake) Zod sees everything’s fine. (←mistake is OK) He relaxes and wags his tail. (←mistake is OK↔humor)

OK-predominant emotions

Trevor shouts as he loses a match. General Zod doesn’t even register it. (←no mistake) Trevor does this every night. (←OK is larger than mistake↔OK-predominant emotions)

We see similar things with any social animal:

  • apes investigate things
  • bear cubs wrestle with each other
  • cats play with mice
  • crows try to pull animal tails
  • dogs see people return early
  • dolphins explore novelty
  • elephants perform pranks
  • fawns explore fields
  • monkeys observe behavior
  • otters push things around
  • parrots are mischievous

(and so on)

In all these cases, other animals have the same forking outcomes we do: sometimes the mistake is too large, and they become defensive—other times the OK is too large, and they get bored. When the mistake and OK balance, they feel humor.

Generating humor

Animals intentionally try to generate humor—we already have words for this, like “play.” An animal that’s playing is intentionally enlarging mistakes or shrinking an OK to generate humor.

For instance:

When fawns have a large OK, they’ll prance to create large mistake and generate humor. Cats will intentionally generate humor by playing with mice or string. “Playing” is a context-specific word for trying to generate humor.

Like in humans, humor in other animals indicates the Goldilocks experiential zone—not too much mistake, not too much OK. When intentionally pursued ("play"), it’s the sweet spot where we’re pushing something while also mitigating risk. Humor is part of how we communicate our relation to this sweet spot to others.

For instance:

When bear cubs, dog puppies, or human kids wrestle, they’ll use humor to calibrate themselves into this Goldilocks zone. Too much mistake, and they’ll slow up. Too much OK, they’ll go a bit harder. Their emotion network helps them arrive at the place between too consequential and not consequential enough—which is how we play.

Other times, animals don't intentionally generate humor, but their humor "rings" like a convenience store bell, signaling their transition between mistake-predominant and OK-predominant emotions—like how General Zod is first startled by Trevor (mistake-predominance), and then transitions back to being placid (OK-predominance). Zod has to "ring" through humor to make this transition, maybe with a brief tail wag.

Humor family words

We can use humor family words to think about where animals feel humor. For instance, dogs and cats feel humor when they feel:

  • achievement (successful hunting)
  • appreciation (petting)
  • comfort (warmth)
  • connection (attention)
  • generosity (treats)
  • relief (removal of something stressful)
  • welcome (inviting a dog up on furniture)

(and so on)

Humor tells

Social animals have humor tells too. They'll consistently show humor tells when mistakes are OK, like by:

  • seeking out other animals
  • touching each other
  • making contented noises
  • making loud noises
  • losing awareness of what’s around them
  • moving non-normatively
  • becoming bolder
  • relaxing

(and so on)

Humor profiles

Each species has their own species humor profile. Within that template, each member of that species has their own individual humor profile.

For instance:

This is like how all dogs wag their tails, but each dog wags their tail a bit differently.

Animal humor profiles don’t usually look much like a human humor profile. While dogs can emote in ways that have nuance to us, cats are less readable to us—and crows even less so.

Of course, our inability to read the emotional state of a crow doesn't mean other crows have the same inability. And the more we watch or interact with crows (or cats or dogs), the more evaluatory information we can pull from them.

Emotion networks

Social animals have emotion networks so they can form groups and share evaluatory information with each other.

For instance:

Crows will spread out across a field to eat—and if one crow notices a mistake, the others pick up on this too through their emotional network.

If many crows are anxious, but one becomes calm, the others will calm down too. If they can’t synchronize, they squabble.

In this case, humor isn’t some exceptional side-development that exists apart from “normal” function—humor is just part and parcel of an emotion network. Humor is like one frequency of how animals communicate evaluatory information.

Even though our model applies relatively well to all social animals, it’s sometimes still uncomfortable to say outright that non-human animals feel humor. We might worry that it’s anthropomorphizing them in ways that aren’t useful.

It’s worth noticing that, whatever the merits of that concern, it puts a double standard on humor that we don’t for other emotions. Since humor is just a normal, regular emotion, it's no more or less weird to say animals feel humor as it is to say they feel fear.

In our day-to-day lives, we acceptably describe animals as feeling afraid, anxious, bored, curious, playful, lonely, sad, social, stressed, and so on. We should also see humor as as acceptable a day-to-day way of describing animal emotions—whatever uncertainty we have in precise contexts about what animals are "really" feeling. (“How similar is cat stress to human stress?”)

The wider point to all this isn’t actually about animals, but about us—and how we think about humor. When we understand how humor can sensibly apply to animals—even though they can’t tell jokes—we’re ready to more deeply understand how humor applies to us in our lives.

Consider:

Trevor would struggle to understand humor if he can’t escape a learned connection between humor and jokes:

Yasmin: *scratching General Zod* “I think animals feel humor.”

Trevor: “No they don’t! If they did, we’d market comedies to them!” (←Trevor thinks humor means laughing at comedic movies)

Trevor’s essentially making a non sequitur—a bit like saying animals must not feel hunger because they never made pizzerias!

Humor is part of a broader system, where communication, evaluations, groups, and senses all relate together. It allows us to make assessments about the world around us, and quickly communalize those assessments—something that allows groups to form and to function well.

In that light, it’s not surprising that social animals would have similar ways of sharing information together, whether those animals are humans or crows—including through humor.

To a greater degree than other animals, humans combine their emotion network with a complementing system of language, which allows us to topicalize our evaluatory information. Even though this gives a distinctive layer to our interactions relative to other animals, the foundation is still a similar system that exists for similar reasons among all social animals.

There are two kinds of information: content information (what) and evaluatory information (how we feel about something). Humor is a kind of evaluatory information—an expression of the ratio of our mistakes and OKs.

  • We can know someone's evaluations without knowing what they're evaluating.
  • We give a higher priority to evaluatory information than we do to content information. This ensures that everything we do always happens in the context of an emotion network.
  • Evaluatory information makes up the bulk of information we process.

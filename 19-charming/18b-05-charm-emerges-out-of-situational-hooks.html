Charm emerges out of situational hooks

Another way we improve our charm is by improving our ability to notice hooks for fitted humor—since charm unfolds easily out of situational hooks.

For instance:

If someone feels nervous, this is a hook to try to make them feel comfort or safety—if someone mentions an achievement, this is a hook to try to help them feel achievement—and so on.

Similarly, if we mention someone's accomplishment, we're creating our own hook to make them feel accomplishment—or if we run into a moment where reconciliation is needed, we'd apologize.

We can see Zoe do this when an old friend comes to visit her. Zoe will keep an eye out for hooks throughout the conversation. She’ll then try to press toward the most relevant humor family word. So Zoe might:

  • welcome Sara when she arrives
  • appreciate that she’s come
  • ask about what she’s done and react to it as an accomplishment
  • reframe what she learns from Sara as insight or realization
  • help her get comfortable
  • show acceptance or forgiveness to anything she brings up

(and so on)

When Zoe does this consistently and successfully, their interactions will be filled with humor, even if Zoe doesn’t use a single joke—and Sara will think of Zoe as charming:

Zoe gets a text from Sara and runs down to the apartment lobby to meet her.

Zoe: “Sara! You’re here!”

Sara: (awkwardly) “I am here!”

Zoe and Sara have pre-hug confusion.

Zoe: (pushing forward) “Hugs!” *hugs Sara*

Sara: (awkwardly) *small smile* “Yes!” (←Sara feels closeness↔︎humor)

Zoe: *leading her to elevator* “Come on up, my apartment is your apartment—I want you to be as comfortable as possible while you’re here.” (←Zoe tries to make Sara feel welcome)

Sara: “Thanks, I really appreciate it. Thanks for letting me spend the night.” (←Sara doesn’t feel welcome↔︎no humor)

Zoe: “No, it’s my pleasure. It’s just so good to see you again!”

Sara: *smiles* (←Sara feels welcome↔︎humor) “It’s good to see you too!”

Zoe: “I see your videos all the time, but it’s just not the same! Even though your comedy’s as perfect as ever.” (←Zoe tries to make Sara feel appreciation and accomplishment)

Sara: *smiles* “Well. Thanks.” (←Sara feels appreciated↔︎humor)

Zoe: “So what’s Maryland like? You settling in there?”

Sara: “Yeah, I think so. I’m running afterschool programs there.”

Zoe: *smiles* “Oh! I didn’t know that!” (←Zoe feels realization↔︎humor)

Sara: “Yeah. It’s… less bad than I thought it would be.” *laughs*

Zoe: *laughs* (←Zoe feels insight↔︎humor) “Hence that bit about the playground antics of the bread-eaters!”

Sara: *laughs* “Oh yeah!”

Consider:

Zoe’s goal is for Sara to feel welcome—not just feel that she’s “technically” welcome. This is where small adjustments come in to help Sara feel genuine welcome.

Zoe will have to keep an eye out for humor tells, which will let her know if her welcomes are actually helping Sara feel welcome.

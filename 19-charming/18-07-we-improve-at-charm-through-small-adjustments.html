We improve at charm through small adjustments

We improve our charm attempts through small adjustments here and there—not by any sort of big, sweeping change to our approach:

Below, small changes make a big difference:

Sam: *to drama team* “I remember when I was cast as the slug in my fifth grade play about compost…” *crowd chuckles*

- OR -

Sam: *to drama team* “You guys are lucky you have so much talent—I remember when I was cast as the slug in our fifth grade play about compost…” *crowd laughs*

Sam improved his charm attempt through small adjustments toward efficiency, cohesion, relevance, humor family words, humor outcomes, and so on—without substantially changing what he was saying, or imitating someone he wasn't like.

This is why becoming more charming isn’t an impossible matter of changing our personality ("I must get from gruff to bubbly")—where one big change somehow makes us charming all at once.

Instead, how charming we are or aren't depends on our overall ability to observe emotional situations and then consistently make small adjustments accordingly.

Consider:

We may know someone who is both gregarious and charming. We may then link them: to be good at charm, we have to be gregarious—or at least pretend to be. But this often confuses a means (gregariousness) with the ends (fitted humor).

It’s obvious that being gregarious isn’t the same thing as being charming—since we know gregarious people who aren’t charming, but annoying! The gregariousness is neither here nor there—what matters is whether or not they generate fitted humor.

We described charm as an outcome, not a method—with there bing many paths to the same outcome. This is why we know people who are charming even while being any combination of awkward, direct, brusque, careful, flamboyant, focused, forward, generous, impulsive, gruff, kind, loud, premeditative, quiet, sincere, shy, thoughtful, and so on.

We don't need to confuse a means (gruffness) to an ends (fitted humor)—since we also know gruff people who aren’t charming. What matters is reaching fitted humor—which ultimately is about small nuance and not macro elements of personality..

Small things. We’re used to small adjustments having outsized important all across our lives. A millimeter here and there isn’t trivial to screw sizes. A half-an-inch isn't trivial to whether a 6x6 windowpane fits or not. That's because we all know that screws and window panes fit or don’t fit on this kind of precision.

It's the same for our interactions with others. Sam’s comment will be fitted or not based on small things—like eye contact or a brief prelude. We improve our charm attempts through attention to these "interaction millimeters," not by dramatic changes.

This can feel unbearably finicky at the beginning, but that's not unique to humor. Every skill is based on the importance of small things—whether baking, building, drumming, gaming, painting, and so on. But much of the initial feeling of finickiness goes away as we become more familiar with it.

Eventually, the importance of small things becomes something normal and even empowering—the skill revolves around mastery and exploration of these small things. This is as true in humor as in anything else.

We improve at charm through small adjustments

We improve our charm attempts through small adjustments here and there—not by any sort of big, sweeping change to our approach.

Below, small changes make a big difference:

Sam: *to drama team* “I remember when I was cast as the slug in my fifth grade play about compost…” *crowd chuckles*

- OR -

Sam: *to drama team* “You guys are lucky you have so much talent (←prelude)—I remember when I was cast as the slug in our fifth grade play about compost…” *crowd laughs*

Sam improved his charm attempt by making small adjustments toward efficiency, cohesion, relevance, humor family words, humor outcomes, and so on—and didn't substantially change what he was saying, or imitate someone he wasn't like.

This is why becoming more charming isn’t an impossible matter of changing our personality ("I must get from gruff to bubbly")—where one big change somehow makes us charming all at once.

Instead, how charming we are or aren't just depends on our overall ability to observe emotional situations and then consistently make small adjustments accordingly.

Consider:

We may know someone who is both gregarious and charming. We may then link them: to be good at charm, we have to be gregarious—or at least pretend to be. But this often confuses a means (gregariousness) with the ends (fitted humor).

It’s obvious that being gregarious isn’t the same thing as being charming—since we know gregarious people who aren’t charming, but annoying! The gregariousness is neither here nor there—what matters is whether or not they generate fitted humor.

We described charm as an outcome, not a method—with there being many paths to the same outcome. This is why we know people who are charming even while also being any combination of awkward, direct, brusque, careful, flamboyant, focused, forward, generous, gruff, impulsive, kind, loud, premeditative, quiet, sincere, shy, thoughtful, and so on.

What matters is reaching fitted humor—not so much how we get there. This is why consistent charm is ultimately about small nuance, and not macro elements of personality.

Comparisons. If we have a charming friend, it can be exhausting to imagine trying to be charming like they are. But we don't have to try to be charming like they are—since there are many ways to be charming.

When a thousand people become better at fitted humor, they’re not all converging toward the same style of being charming. Instead, they’d each be refining their own style in a way that feels very comfortable to each of them.

We already know this feeling. When we’re in an unusually good mood, we’re better at generating fitted humor in ourselves or others. This experience usually feels less like becoming more like someone else, and more like ourselves—and feels good, not stressful.

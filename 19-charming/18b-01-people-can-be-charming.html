We come to bring not peace but joy

An advanced course on humor that starts at the beginning and goes through everything

We’ve known people in our lives who we would describe as charming:

  • a basketball coach who was indefatigable and made practice fun
  • a friend from college that we’d go exploring with
  • a grandparent that delighted us whenever we’d visit
  • an internet poster who improved an entire forum
  • a pastor who always said the right things
  • a pen pal who always remembered our birthday
  • a romantic partner who was generous and kind
  • a local politician we liked despite ourselves
  • a train conductor who was the best of all train conductors

(and so on)

We often feel charming people have amazing social abilities. Often, we describe these abilities with humor outcomes, just by other names. These charming people could:

  • direct conversation (open new topics of conversation)
  • fit in new places quickly (create rapport)
  • gather people with ease (ease tension)
  • make friends easily (mutually symbolize close ties)
  • make people feel listened to (help them achieve catharsis)
  • make someone smile after something went wrong (turn negative emotions into positive ones)
  • make their problems shrink (survive stressful situations)
  • project optimism (give new perspectives)
  • tell people the things they needed to hear (acceptably criticize)
  • transmute problems into something else entirely (handle complaints)

(and so on)

In other words, the people who come to mind as “charming” are charming because they could frequently generate well-fitted humor. They could reach humor outcomes in a wide range of situations.

Consider:

We've previously talked about humor as the mechanism of concordant influence. People who are fluent with this mechanism are described as charming.

Dress codes. In our chapter on humor dress codes, we saw how more fittedness corresponded to more efficiency and cohesion. So successful moments of charm promote social efficiency and cohesion—which is why charming people seem socially effective and connective to us.

We describe these charming people as charismatic or influential for this reason. We don't always think of charisma or influence as a humor skill, but it is! To see this, we can observe the presence of humor tells around charming people—or notice the absence of humor tells around uncharming people.

If someone can frequently generate fitted humor in many kinds situations, they'll constantly seem socially effective—and as an agent of social cohesion, we'll describe them as charming.

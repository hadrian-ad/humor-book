There are degrees of charming

In our day-to-day conversations, we often use the word "charm" in a specific and narrow sense—something often connected to flirting or seducing. But that's not how we're using charm here.

Our word "charm" is just a way of looking at humor. This is why we can meet two cats: a “charming” cat and an “uncharming” cat.

  • The charming cat would quickly become accepted, ease tension, change our negative emotions into positive ones, and so on.
  • The uncharming cat would resist becoming accepted, raise tension, change positive emotions into negative ones, and so on.

The cats would be charming or not depending on the ease and frequency that they reach humor outcomes. This doesn’t have anything to do with the cats being flirtatious and seductive!

We can make this comparison between anything. We can imagine:

  • two classmates, one more charming and the other less charming
  • two middle school basketball coaches, one more charming and the other less charming
  • two guest speakers, one more charming and the other less charming
  • two houses, one more charming and the other less charming
  • two walks, one more charming and the other less charming

(and so on)

Since people are charming by degrees, we can intuitively compare people or things by how "charming" they are. Over time, we can sense which more easily promote humor outcomes or humor family words.

Consider:

Our perceptions of charm are subjective. We may find a baseball game charming, but our friend may not.

Someone isn't so much "charming (everywhere to everyone)" but "charming (to different degrees to different people)"

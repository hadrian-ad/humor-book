Emotion networks are why we can communicate without words

Emotion networks can help us look past words and speakings to get at a deeper fabric of how people interact with each other.

For instance

If people synchronize mistakes and OKs over an emotional network—then we generate humor in others by getting them to synchronize their mistake or OK with us in a way that brings that topic's evaluations into balance.

This helps us think with greater depth and breadth about how humor is often intentionally or unintentionally brought about—with or without words.

To help us think beyond words, it's worth thinking about all the things our emotion network allows us to do—all wordlessly, like:

  • assemble things together (without speaking)
  • assign tasks with a specified degree of urgency (without speaking)
  • claim a section of a beach (without speaking)
  • express affection (without speaking)
  • fan out to search for something (without speaking)
  • flirt with someone (without speaking)
  • indicate something with a specified degree of permissiveness (without speaking)
  • organize ourselves on a bus (without speaking)
  • show that we’re unbothered (without speaking)
  • show support (without speaking)
  • walk around busy streets (without speaking)
  • welcome someone (without speaking)

(and so on)

Humor plays a role in all of those things above—we smile to share evaluations, which allows others to reach evaluatory consensus with us.

Consider:

This helps us see how humor is more than something that happens in reaction to jokes. Instead, jokes are using on a broader system of grouping around evaluations. Focusing on this system helps us see a fuller scope to something like humor.

This all applies to any emotion, not just humor. All this is often easiest to see in dramatic circumstances, like when:

  • awareness of a shocking event trickles from one end of the bar to everyone else (without spoken explanation)
  • belligerent attitudes harden people into groups (without spoken declaration)
  • fear travels throughout a city (without spoken reasoning)
  • festive attitudes draw people together (without spoken invitation)
  • good news ripples through a classroom (without spoken announcement)
  • a restaurant suddenly falls silent (without spoken declaration)
  • uncertainty sweeps through a train (without spoken announcement)
  • worried crowds soften into release and celebration (without spoken announcement)

(and so on)

What's worth noticing above is that we usually think evaluations are more important than the context.

Consider:

If we're about to go into a bar, we don't care so much what people are talking about. We instead care about how they're talking about things—whether they're afraid, belligerent, laughing, relaxed, and so on.

Similarly, if a bar we're in suddenly goes silent, we'll go silent too—since our first concern isn't what happened, it's how people feel about what happened.

This all is important to humor. Humor often happens as smiles exchanged while walking or after a greeting, or laughter after hugging or recieving good news, from someone rolling their eyes, and other interactions.

These can be wordless or not—but despite how important these instances of humor are to our sense of being around others, we don't always find it easy to talk about how or why this happens. This is especially true humor happens wordlessly.

We'll use "emotion networks" throughout this book as a way of talking about how evaluations are shared between people. Essentially, emotion networks are the social or evaluatory medium that everything happens in.

Consider:

This isn't specific to humor—it's the same for any emotion. Like humor, mistake-predominate emotions can happen simply by exchanging looks or listening to angry people. Words are sometimes involved, but not always.

Medium. Emotion networks are important! We can't ignore them—trying to learn about humor without mentioning emotion networks would be like trying to learn about fish without mentioning water.

When we think about the people in our lives who have been good at making others laugh, we'll notice that they often have a field-like quality of influence on people around them—part of their good understanding of the overall emotional situation.

In other words, they're skilled at being able to see, interpret, and act within these emotion networks! This skill is inseparable from their broader skill at making others laugh. It's a skill we can improve ourselves through attention and practice.

As we increase our attention to each other’s emotional state, we increase the informational tension between them—forming a connection between them.

This human surface tension is what makes groups groups:

We can see the degrees of human surface tension any time we look at any group of people:

Zoe and Emma are walking down the campus quad, arguing about which dinosaur would make the best pet. (←high surface tension)

They pass someone relaxing on a bench. They register some basic things about that person’s emotional state. (←low surface tension)

We increase or decrease this informational tension by how much mutual attention we give each other:

Damien sees Xiaowei and Lina come over, and dreads having to talk to people.

Xiaowei: “Want to come sit with us?”

Damien gathers his lunch bag and sits with them. They include him in their conversation. As they explain some of their inside jokes to him, he starts to join the discussion more fully. (←Xiaowei and Lina increase surface tension with Damien)

Owen goes to office hours to speak to Justin. Owen pokes his head in.

Justin: “Come in!” *typing* “Hang on, I’ll be with you in just a second… Have a seat!”

Owen: *sits* (←lack of attention decreases surface tension)

Justin: *typing* *finishing email* *swivels* *looks at Owen* *puts hands together* “Okay. Now, what can I do for you?” (←attention increases surface tension)

Owen hates talking to professors. He always feels tongue-tied.

Owen goes up to the register. The barista is busy at the espresso machine. (←low surface tension)

Barista: *glancing over* (recognizing him) *smiles* “Hey! I’ll be right with you.”

Owen: *smiles* “Okay!” (←mutual attention increases surface tension)

The barista goes back to making her drink. (←reduction of mutual attention decreases surface tension)

A teacher corners Sam to complain. Sam nods and listens carefully, but Heather keeps going on and on. (←attention increases surface tension)

After about ten minutes, Sam briefly checks his phone, trying to signal to her that it’s time to wrap up the conversation. (←wandering attention decreases surface tension)

Heather keeps talking, and Sam listens patiently. (←attention increases surface tension)

Zoe is reading on the bus. Someone sits next to her. (←low human surface tension) She can feel him look over at her book. (←increased human surface tension)

Stranger: “Watcha reading?” (←stranger tries to increase surface tension)

Zoe: *not looking up* “Just an assignment.” (←Zoe tries to decrease surface tension)

Stranger: “Oh so you’re a student!” (←stranger tries to increase surface tension)

Zoe says something noncommittal and shifts to try to show she’s focusing on the book. (←Zoe tries to decrease surface tension)

"Grouping" is so normal that it's sometimes hard to even think about. Human surface tension is one way of drawing attention to the connection that groups us, how that connection happens, what it is, and how it changes over time.

It can also more elegantly describe emotion networks and subnetworks:

These groupings are fluid and can quickly change over time—since we’re constantly changing which people we put effort into sharing and receiving information with.

Our ability to handle human surface tension is what enables us to form or disband groups. And while this skill is broader than just "humor," it's also essential to any skill around humor.

We can improve our awareness and control of human surface tension—which in turn enables connective and precise humor with others. That's because it gives us the nuanced information needed to shift mistakes and OKs on various topics to generate mutual humor.

Evaluators radiate their evaluations to other evaluators. This creates a reactive network of evaluations. These emotion networks are the medium where humor happens.

  • We automatically join emotion networks whenever we're around others.
  • Emotion networks try to synchronize evaluations. People on emotion networks feel uncomfortable when they can't arrive at a shared evaluatory perspective.
  • Emotion networks can be nested within each other. These subnetworks try to stay synchronized on the sets of topics specific to that subnetwork.

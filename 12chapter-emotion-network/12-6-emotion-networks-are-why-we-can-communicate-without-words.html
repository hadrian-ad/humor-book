Emotion networks are why we can communicate without words

Our emotion network lets us think about how we communicate without speaking. Our emotion network is how we can:

  • assemble things together (without speaking)
  • assign tasks with a specified degree of urgency (without speaking)
  • claim a section of a beach (without speaking)
  • express affection (without speaking)
  • fan out to search for something (without speaking)
  • flirt with someone (without speaking)
  • indicate something with a specified degree of permissiveness (without speaking)
  • organize ourselves on a bus (without speaking)
  • show that we’re unbothered (without speaking)
  • show support (without speaking)
  • walk around busy streets (without speaking)
  • welcome someone (without speaking)

(and so on)

Often, this process is even more obvious in dramatic circumstances, like when:

  • awareness of a shocking event trickles from one end of the bar to everyone else (without spoken explanation)
  • belligerent attitudes harden people into groups (without spoken declaration)
  • fear travels throughout a city (without spoken reasoning)
  • festive attitudes draw people together (without spoken invitation)
  • good news ripples through a classroom (without spoken announcement)
  • a restaurant suddenly falls silent (without spoken declaration)
  • uncertainty sweeps through a train (without spoken announcement)
  • worried crowds soften into release and celebration (without spoken announcement)

(and so on)

We carefully notice these evaluative changes—since we usually think evaluations are more important than the context.

Consider:

If we're about to go into a bar, we don't care so much what people are talking about. We instead care about how they're talking about things—whether they're afraid, belligerent, laughing, relaxed, and so on.

Similarly, if a bar we're in suddenly goes silent, we'll go silent too—since our first concern isn't what happened, it's how people feel about what happened.

This all is important to humor. A lot of humor happens simply by exchanging smiles—or watching happy people celebrate—or through wordless interactions.

Consider:

This isn't specific to humor—it's the same for any emotion. A lot of anger happens simply by exchanging looks, or by listening to angry people.

We don't always have words for how this happens. But being able to talk about it is necessary to broad understanding of humor.

We'll use emotion networks throughout this book as a way of talking about how evaluations are transferred between people.

Medium. Emotion networks are important! We can't ignore them—trying to learn about humor without mentioning emotion networks would be like trying to learn about fish without mentioning water.

When we think about the people in our lives who have been good at making others laugh, we'll notice that they often have a field-like quality of influence on people around them, along with an acute understanding of the situational zeitgeist.

In other words, they're skilled at being able to see, interpret, and act within these emotion networks! This skill is inseparable from their broader skill at making others laugh. As we improve our ability to see and think about emotion networks, our broader skill at making others laugh improves as well.

That's the end of this chapter! Next, we'll see what kind of information is communicated over emotion networks.

People have informational tension between them. As people increase their attention to each other’s emotional state, they increase this informational tension—pulling them together. This human surface tension is what makes groups groups:

We can see the degrees of human surface tension any time we look at any groups of people:

Zoe and Emma are walking down the campus quad, arguing about which dinosaur would make the best pet. (←high surface tension)

They pass someone relaxing on a bench. They register basic things about that person’s emotional state, but don’t put in any more effort to register his evaluatory state. (←low surface tension)

We increase or decrease this informational tension by how much mutual attention we give each other:

Owen goes to office hours to speak to Justin. Owen pokes his head in.

Justin: “Come in!” *typing* “Hang on, I’ll be with you in just a second… Have a seat!”

Owen: *sits* (←lack of attention decreases human surface tension)

Justin: *typing* *finishing email* *swivels* *looks at Owen* *puts hands together* “Okay. Now, what can I do for you?” (←attention increases human surface tension)

Owen hates talking to professors. He always feels tongue-tied.

Owen goes up to the register. The barista is busy at the espresso machine. (←low human surface tension)

Barista: *glancing over* (recognizing him) *smiles* “Hey! I’ll be right with you.”

Owen: *smiles* “Okay!” (←mutual attention increases human surface tension)

The barista goes back to making her drink. (←reduction of mutual attention decreases human surface tension)

A teacher corners Sam to complain. Sam nods and listens carefully, but Heather keeps going on and on. After about ten minutes, Sam briefly checks his phone, trying to signal to her that it’s time to wrap up the conversation.

Zoe is reading on the bus. Someone sits next to her. (←low human surface tension) She can feel him look over at her book. (←increased human surface tension)

Stranger: “Watcha reading?” (←stranger tries to increase surface tension)

Zoe: *not looking up* “Just an assignment.” (←Zoe tries to decrease surface tension)

Stranger: “Oh so you’re a student!” (←stranger tries to increase surface tension)

Zoe says something noncommittal and shifts to try to show she’s focusing on the book. (←Zoe tries to decrease surface tension)

Damien sees Xiaowei and Lina come over, and dreads having to talk to people.

Xiaowei: “Want to come sit with us?”

Damien gathers his lunch bag and sits with them. They include him in their conversation, and explain some of their inside jokes to him, and he starts to join the discussion more fully. (←Xiaowei and Lina increase surface tension with Damien)

These groupings are fluid and can quickly change over time—since we’re constantly changing which people we put effort into sharing and receiving information with.

One of the ways we improve at generating humor is by improving our awareness and control around human surface tension. We enable connective and precise humor by increasing our informational tension with someone—otherwise, we simply have no way to reactively shift mistakes and OKs to generate humor.

Our ability to handle human surface tension is what enables us to form or disband groups. While this skill is broader than just "humor," it's also essential to any skill around humor.

Each evaluator constantly radiates evaluations to other evaluators. This creates a potential or actual network of reactive evaluation. These emotion networks are foundational parts of communication, and are also the medium where humor happens.

  • We automatically join emotion networks whenever we're around others.
  • We synchronize evaluations over emotion networks. When we don't feel synchronized on an emotion network, we feel uncomfortable.
  • There can be emotion networks within emotion networks. These subnetworks try to stay synchronized on more minute topics.

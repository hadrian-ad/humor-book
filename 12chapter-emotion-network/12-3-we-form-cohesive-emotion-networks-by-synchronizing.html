We form cohesive emotion networks by synchronizing

We always network emotionally with everyone around us—it’s why we always feel the mood of a room whenever we walk into one.

For instance:

We can walk into a room and immediately tell if the people there are belligerent, bored, cowering, delighted, focused, posturing, relaxed, scared, studious, worried, and so on—and in what combination.

We can do this even if we don't know the context of those feelings, and even if they're not aware of us!

When we walk into a coffee shop, we’ll be part of the overall emotion network:

To join the overall network, we synchronize on just a few high-level topics, like ‘public manners,’ ‘physical safety,’ ‘noise decorum,’ and so on.

Within the overall network are subnetworks. These are emotion networks set within larger emotion networks. Generally, these smaller groups of people synchronize on many more topics together than they do with the big group:

For instance, Zoe and Emma will form a subnetwork. They'll be closely synchronizing and resynchronizing on many topics all the time—which is why they’ll notice small differences in evaluations:

Emma: "Thank god Justin gave us all extensions."

Zoe: “Yeah.” *tilts head back slightly* (←Zoe doesn’t feel their evaluations are synchronized)

Emma: (curious) “Yeah? But what?" (←Emma picks up their desynchronization, and wants to resynchronize)

Zoe and Emma are on their laptops, when Zoe gives a small laugh.

Emma: *looks up* *smiling* “What?” (←Emma picks up their desynchronization, and wants to synchronize)

While head tilts or small laughs will desynchronize Emma and Zoe’s subnetwork, they won’t desynchronize the overall emotion network of the cafe—since Zoe’s head tilt didn’t involve any of the network-wide topics.

By contrast, if someone starts yelling, the overall network may feel desynchronized:

Someone start yelling and the entire cafe goes quiet. (←yelling desynchronizes the overall network)

Zoe and Emma start looking around. Zoe makes eye contact with someone sitting near the window, and Emma examines a couple who’s looking at the person yelling. The barista scans everyone in the room. (←cafe tries to synchronize evaluations and reach a consensus assessment)

When the cafe becomes desynchronized, everyone there will try to resynchronize their evaluations. This establishes an emotional consensus—which helps them decide on what to do, like whether to intervene with the yeller or not.

Consider:

If the cafe can’t resynchronize, there's no emotional consensus. The emotion network fragments, and people will feel uncomfortable or unsafe and leave:

Efficiency. Our emotion network lets us to have greater evaluatory awareness than we would on our own. That's because emotion networks give us an efficiency of scale. In the same way many hands make light work for everyone, many evaluators shrink our individual evaluatory responsibilities.

For instance, when a dish breaks in the kitchen of a cafe, all twenty customers don't need to personally walk to the kitchen to decide whether or not everything's okay. Instead, all twenty can quickly come to an evaluatory conclusion using the evaluations of just one or two other people.

If a dish breaks and the staff are calm, the customers will be calm—even without actually knowing what specifically happened. By pooling our evaluations, the cafe limited the number of redundant evaluations that had to happen.

This book focuses on humor, not jokes

Instead of talking about humor (emotions), we sometimes drift into talking about humor (genres) instead—like "farces," "jokes," "irony," and so on. The problem with this is that we don't always laugh or smile at these things!

Like anything else, comedies, farces, jokes, irony, satires, witty remarks, and so on can lead to humor—but they don’t always.

Instead, jokes, comedies, wittiness, satire, and so on can lead to any emotion outcome:

Jokes can have any emotional outcome

Tucker: (delightedly) “Hey, son, you know what one of the most remarkable things in the world is?”

Elias: (warily) “What?”

Tucker: *chuckles* *chuckles more* “Whiteboards!”

Elias: *pause* “Re-markable?” *laughs* (←humor)

- OR -

Elias: *pause* “Remarkable?” (confused) (←confusion)

- OR -

Elias: *pause* “Re-markable?” *purses lips* (←annoyance)

Comedies can have any emotional outcome

Sam and Riley are on the couch watching Fired Fireman.

Chief and the Arsonist are wrestling over the stolen wallet when the police run in.

Police: *draws gun* "Freeze, thief!"

Chief and the Arsonist shoot their hands up. But Chief somehow ended up holding the stolen wallet. He does a shocked double take at it.

Sam: *laughs*

Riley: *laughs too* (←humor)

- OR -

Riley: (bewildered) “What’s so funny?” (←bewildered)

- OR -

Riley: *eyes droop* (←tired)

Wittiness can have any emotional outcome

Zoe is reading Gone with the Wind.

Scarlett: (indignant) (suspicious) “Are you lying to try to soothe me?”

Rhett: (amused) “My dear, when did I ever try to soothe you?"

The book just lights up whenever Rhett’s in the scene. Zoe smiles as she reads. (←humor)

- OR -

The whole relationship between Rhett and Scarlett bothers her. Zoe frowns. (←annoyance)

- OR -

Zoe checks to see how many pages she has left in the chapter. She sighs. (←bored)

Satire can have any emotion outcome

Professor Justin is reading a satire mocking academics language.

This is too good! He chuckles continuously as he reads. (←humor)

- OR -

Justin has a sinking feeling as he reads it. It's just like how he writes. (←dismay)

- OR -

Justin feels the satire is unfair and off-base. It's like it's purposefully misrepresenting something he thinks is important. It makes him feel alone and morose. (←sadness)

Since we’re focusing on humor in this book, we won’t focus on jokes or satire—since those things aren’t humor.

Humor is an emotion—and we'll stay focused on when and why this emotion happens.

Consider:

Sometimes we get preoccupied with categories. (Jokes, comedies, satires, and so on.) But thinking a joke will explain laughing is a bit like thinking a salad will explain eating.

Why can't a salad explain eating? For one, we can have a salad and not eat it—since a salad isn't the same thing as eating.

For another, if our goal is to explain "eating," we can distort or narrow things by explaining "eating a salad" instead.

Jokes and tentpoles. We sometimes call everything we laugh at a joke: "We sometimes laugh at jokes. Therefore everything we laugh at is some kind of joke."

It's easy to see this as idiomatic. We can make a similar analogy: "we sometimes buy coffee. Therefore, everything we buy is some kind of coffee.” We would never take this expression too literally!

For the same reason it would be strange to frame all buying around coffee, it would be strange to frame all laughing around jokes.

That's it for this chapter!

We often notice humor mainly when it’s unambiguous—like when we’re laughing at a movie:

Emma and Owen are watching Fired Fireman.

Chief is hired to work at a warehouse, but his attempt to move crates of lobsters goes horribly, horribly wrong. Emma and Owen laugh. (←just humor)

But even though we sometimes seem to feel “one” emotion, most emotions happen in a mix—like when Jennifer feels both humor and anger: 

Jennifer looks up from her tablet. Where’s Riley? Her heart sinks in fear. She checks the street, then runs around the house. She sees Riley squatting quietly next to an anthill, and Jennifer puts a hand over her heart and laughs. (←humor)

Jennifer: (relieved) (annoyed) “Riley! Thank god. I thought I told you to stay the side yard.” (←Jennifer feels humor and anger)

We can see this in other examples as well:

Trevor walks into class and trips, and the cereal he’s carrying falls down his shirt.

At first shocked, his classmates see that he’s not hurt and start to laugh with relief.

Trevor stands there, embarrassed, and laughs sheepishly. Red-faced, he goes to heads to the bathroom. (←Trevor feels humor and embarassment)

Riley is bored at the dinner table, and Sam makes a funny face at her.

Riley smiles and squirms with amusement and boredom.(←Riley feels humor and boredom)

In complicated emotional states, a small amount of humor may mingle with feelings of anger, bitterness, disappointment, sadness, and so on:

Emma sadly shuts the door to her apartment.

Emma: “I can’t believe Sara didn’t tell me until today that she decided to move!”

She looks around. She feels angry, resentful, regretful, sad. A cloud of bitter humor passes through her. (←Emma’s complicated emotional state includes some humor)

She lays across her bed, wishing she could turn off her mind.

Timothy: *calling Jennifer* “Jennifer, I know Sam said otherwise, but I checked with the insurance company. They said the treatment is still experimental. They won’t cover it.”

Jennifer: *pause* (exhausted) *laughs* (←Jennifer’s complicated emotional state includes some humor, and other emotions besides) “That’s what I thought. If we hold a fundraiser, I think we should still be able to cover it.”

Jennifer is helping her father pack up his house in preparation for his move into a nursing home.

When they come across some mementos in an old box, he gives a funny smile as he touches them, and he gives an unusual laugh. He wipes his eyes. (←Jennifer’s father has complicated emotional state that includes some humor, and other emotions besides)

In this book, we'll be learning about all these moments of humor—whether laughing at a movie, or feeling a twinge of humor during a complicated emotional experience.

We categorized humor as an emotion. By putting humor alongside similar things, like anger, boredom, or sadness, we can place humor within broader system of emotions.

  • Our evaluations have range of emotional outcomes. Humor is one potential outcome.
  • Jokes (comments) aren't the same as humor (emotion), for the same reason insults (comments) aren't the same thing as anger (emotion).
  • To avoid equivocating between different focuses of the word "humor", we'll always be referring to an emotion when we use the word "humor". In this book, humor is something we feel, not say.

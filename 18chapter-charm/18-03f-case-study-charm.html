The Humor Book

Case study of charm

We can put everything together to see how we can describe a scene that includes humor and a humor attempt:

Zoe is watching a comedy show.

Fleabag is stressed from a family reunion. She steps outside the restaurant to smoke. Hot Priest, one of the dinner guests, steps outside as well.

Hot Priest: *smiles* "Fellow smoker. Have a spare one?"

Fleabag: "Sure." *smiles* *hands him cigarette and lights it*

Hot Priest: *takes a drag* (conversationally) "So does your family get together much, or—?"

To destroy the possibility of small talk, Fleabag brusquely heads back inside.

Hot Priest: "Fuck you, then."

Fleabag: (shocked) *turns to stare at him*

Hot Priest: *smiles*

Fleabag: *smiles broadly* (←humor that seems to defy social physics) *heads inside*

Zoe smiles as she watches. They have good chemistry, and she can't wait to see what horrible things befall them this season.

We can assemble a description of Hot Priest's comment. ("Fuck you, then.")

First, was it a humor attempt, or a mistake-predominant attempt? (Did he want her to smile or become angry?) It was a humor attempt.

Once we identify it as a humor attempt, we decide whether it was a charm attempt or a comedy attempt. (Was he prioritizing fittedness, or intensity?) It was prioritizing fittedness.

Consider:

We know the priest's goal was to prioritize fittedness, not intensity. He foremost wanted to reach humor outcomes, like for her to think he supported an argument (that her action wasn't worthwhile), created rapport (with her), became accepted (by her), reduced (both of their) defensiveness, revealed a personality trait, revealed a personal view, handled (her) complaints, acceptably criticized (her actions), symbolized ties (between them), and so on.

Whether she laughs or just smiles isn't as relevant as whether or not she reaches these humor outcomes on relevant topics.

Finally, we ask if this was a low- or high-velocity charm attempt. (Was the priest ambling toward humor outcomes, or rushing for them?) It was high-velocity.

Consider:

The woman was guarding against humor outcomes. It took something with high velocity to break through these guards. ("Fuck you, then.")

This lets us assemble a description of the priest's action in this scene: it was a successful high-velocity charm attempt:

This describes the comment's relation to the emotional situation, without getting distracted by surface descriptions of content.

Consider:

We often describe high-velocity charm attempts as insults, rudeness, teasing, and so on—words that don't always specificy an intention. ("Is a ha-ha insult or a mean insult?")

These words aren't always as necessary as they seem at first. We can ground comments to both their intention and to what actually happens in an emotional situation. ("No matter what else we call it, it was a high-velocity charm attempt—and it was successful.")

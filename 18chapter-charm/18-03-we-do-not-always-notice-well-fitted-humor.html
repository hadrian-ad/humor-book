The Humor Book

We don't always notice well-fitted humor

In the same way we don't notice humor on a walk, we don't always notice the fitted humor we feel all across our life, like when we:

  • get really great hugs
  • step into someone’s house to visit them
  • are complimented on our outfit
  • hear good news
  • greet a friend
  • step out from the cold
  • get a text from a good friend
  • are offered a glass of water
  • open a perfect gift
  • reconcile with someone after a disagreement
  • are served delicious-looking food

(and so on)

These are all moments where we evaluate mistakes as OK. Even though it's obvious these are all moments where we laugh, smile, social seek, loosen, increase energy, have active eyes, become optimistic, move non-normatively, and so on it's often not until it's pointed out that we actually notice this humor as humor:

Zoe comes in from the rain, and has to push against the door to close it against the wind. Inside, her apartment is calm and quiet, with the rain against the windows.

Zoe: *laughs* “Phew!” (←Zoe has survived a stressful situation, her tensions have eased, she has a new perspective, her negative emotions have changed into positive ones)

Zoe won’t necessarily register this as humor—or even that she laughed. That’s because the humor she feels when she comes home is so well-fitted that she just feels it as one moment unfolding easily into the next—a bit like how it feels to use stairs and not really notice them:

Often, we don't have a name for well-fitted humor, which makes it hard to talk about. We see that people smile when complimented or laugh at good news.

Zoe is waiting for the bus. It’s drizzly and grey. Then, the bus pulls around the corner, the headlights shining through the rain. Zoe steps out of the shelter to the edge of the curb in anticipation. (←Zoe experiences low-intensity humor tells like brightening, energy, loosening, smiling, focusing on present, raised spirits↔︎Zoe feels comfort, release, relief, safety, welcome)

Zoe lines up with everyone else to file into the bus. Zoe taps her phone at the farebox when it’s her turn. This always makes her a tiny bit anxious. Then it beeps and gives a green light, the driver waves her in, and she takes her seat. (←Zoe feels low-intensity relief, welcome, and so on)

Despite this humor being decent charm—since it’s relatively fitted—Zoe doesn’t really think to notice this humor until it’s pointed out.

Low-intensity humor can simultaneously be both there and not noticed—but once this humor is pointed out, we can't stop noticing this it.

Consider:

Sometimes we think humor is comedy, and so we try to understand all humor in terms of being “comedic.” But that would be odd—a bit like thinking eating is restaurants, and then trying to understand all eating in terms of being “restaurantic.”

That would be taking something niche that came second (comedy/restaurants), and then trying to use it to back-explain the much wider thing that came first (humor/eating).

Jessica: *to a customer* *setting down condiments* “Oh, I love your style!” (←Jessica’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm attempt)

Yasmin: *turns red* *brightens* *beams* “Oh. Thanks!”

Tucker pokes his head into Amber’s office unannounced.

Tucker: “Amber, great work with setting up that meeting today. It was flawless. I don’t know what we’d do without you.” (←Tucker’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Amber: *smiles* “Thank you!”

Riley: *upset* “Mom? I’m sorry. I didn’t mean to break it. Maybe I can make a new one.”

Jennifer: “Aw Riley. It’s okay.” *hugs her* (←Riley’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm) “Let’s just be more careful next time, okay?”

Sam is examining his balding head in the mirror. He rubs it thoughtfully. Jennifer sees him do this, and she grabs him by the arm.

Jennifer: *squeezing him* “You look so handsome.” (←Sam’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Sam gives a releasing series of small laughs, and leans against her affectionately.

Emma: *red-eyed*

Zoe: “What’s wrong, Em?”

Emma: “Nothing. I’ve just realized I’m older now than my brother was when h-he died…”

Zoe: “Aw. Emma.” *hugs* (←Zoe’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Emma: *cries* *laughs*

Trevor: *shopping online* “Should we order it or not?”

Yasmin: “I just can’t decide! I just love StormConQuest, but the reviews on the expansion are just so mixed. They say StormConQuest: Seas of Drake and Myrrh really simplifies the resource management. What if that ruins it?”

Trevor: *out of the blue* “What do you think about having a kid?” (←Trevor’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Yasmin: *puts hand on face* *laughs* “Oh god, Trevor. That’s not StormConQuest!”

Trevor: *laughs* “Not now obviously. Just spitballing here, random talk.”

Yasmin’s mother: *sets down a glass of water* “Here, I got you some water!” (←Yasmin’s mother’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Trevor: *smiles* “Thank you.”

If we're prioritizing fittedness, the humor above is good—and the intensity of humor is neither here nor there.

We're often used to only having one measure for humor (intensity). Having only one measure can make discussions of humor seem dimensionless and flat.

When we add in a second measure (fittedness) we create some depth perception to how we think about humor.

Importance. We often think of money as having only quantity and no quality. So if we think a pennies and nickles are always "bad" compared to dollars, we'd simply screen out pennies and nickles—they wouldn't be worth noticing, let alone bending down to pick up.

We sometimes do something similar with humor. We assume a light smile is "bad" compared to a loud laugh—and so at some point, we stop paying attention to them. Sometimes, we even stop pursuing them, too.

But humor isn't like money. When we pay attention to the fittedness of our humor, we can track our emotional relation to the world around us—our changing sense of acceptance, belonging, generosity, insight, and so on. Those experiences and variations are worth noticing and keeping track of, at any intensity.

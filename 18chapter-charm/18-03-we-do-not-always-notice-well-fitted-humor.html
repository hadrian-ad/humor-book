The Humor Book

We don't always notice well-fitted humor

In the same way we don't notice humor on a walk, we don't always notice the fitted humor we feel all across our life, like when we:

  • get really great hugs
  • step into someone’s house to visit them
  • are complimented on our outfit
  • hear good news
  • greet a friend
  • step out from the cold
  • get a text from a good friend
  • are offered a glass of water
  • open a perfect gift
  • reconcile with someone after a disagreement
  • are served delicious-looking food

(and so on)

These are all moments where we evaluate mistakes as OK. Even though it's obvious these are all moments where we laugh, smile, social seek, loosen, increase energy, have active eyes, become optimistic, move non-normatively, and so on it's often not until it's pointed out that we actually notice this humor as humor:

Zoe comes in from the rain, and has to push against the door to close it against the wind. Inside, her apartment is calm and quiet, with the rain against the windows.

Zoe: *laughs* “Phew!” (←Zoe has survived a stressful situation, her tensions have eased, she has a new perspective, her negative emotions have changed into positive ones)

Zoe won’t necessarily register this as humor—or even that she laughed. That’s because the humor she feels when she comes home is so well-fitted that she just feels it as one moment unfolding easily into the next—a bit like how it feels to use stairs and not really notice them:

Low-intensity humor shows up all across our lives—and much more frequently than intense humor. It even shows up where it isn't "supposed" to, like when waiting for a bus:

Zoe is waiting for the bus. It’s drizzly and grey. Then, the bus pulls around the corner, the headlights shining through the rain.

Zoe steps out of the shelter to the edge of the curb in anticipation.(←Zoe experiences low-intensity humor tells like brightening, energy, loosening, smiling, focusing on present, raised spirits↔︎Zoe feels comfort, release, relief, safety, welcome)

Zoe lines up with everyone else to file into the bus. Zoe taps her phone at the farebox when it’s her turn. This always makes her a tiny bit anxious. Then it beeps and gives a green light, the driver waves her in, and she takes her seat. (←Zoe feels low-intensity relief, welcome, and so on)

Zoe's low-intensity humor can simultaneously be both there and not noticed—but once this humor is pointed out, it's hard to not notice it.

Consider:

When thinking about charm, it's important to be able to notice low-intensity humor by noticing low-intensity humor tells. Otherwise, we can miss "good" moments of humor when they happen.

Importance. Sometimes we see stray pennies and nickles as "low-intensity" forms of money—and so don't really think they're worth noticing or bending down to pick up.

We sometimes implicitly do something similar with humor. Like a penny, we assume a light smile isn't that important—and so maybe not worth paying attention to the light smiles of people boarding busses.

But low-intensity humor isn't like pennies or nickles. When we pay attention to the fittedness of our humor, we track our emotional relation to the world around us—our changing sense of acceptance, belonging, generosity, insight, and so on.

Those experiences and variations are worth noticing and keeping track of, at any intensity.

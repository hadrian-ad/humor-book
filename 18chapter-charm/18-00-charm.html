18. Charm

What makes humor “good?” When we watch a funny scene, we usually think that the harder we laugh, the better the humor. In these cases, we're using intensity to judge humor.

Owen and Emma are watching Fired Fireman.

Chief starts hiccuping as he goes up to the witness stand for cross-examination. He can't stop, even as he's cross-examined by the high-powered lawyer.

The timing is perfect. Emma and Owen are howling with laughter.

Emma: *breathless from laughing* "This scene is so good!" (←Emma judges scene by the intensity of her humor)

But we don't always judge by intensity of humor. Sometimes, the humor is neither here nor there:

Yasmin’s mother: *sets down a glass of water* “Here, I got you some water!”

Trevor: *smiles* (←humor) *takes glass* “Thank you.”

-LATER-

Trevor: *to Yasmin* "Your mom is pretty nice." (←Trevor judges interaction by the fittedness of his humor)

Above, Trevor wasn't going "hm, I didn't guffaw and chortle, so this humor was bad." Instead, he thought it was good humor—because Trevor was judging by fittedness. ("Hm, I felt belonging and welcome, so this humor was good.")

Consider:

Yasmin's mother would prefer fitted humor over intense humor. If we paused time right before Yasmin's mother handed Trevor some water, and said: "choose one: either Trevor laughs long and loudly, or he feels belonging and welcome"—she'd choose belonging and welcomee

When judging humor, we can judge mostly by intensity or mostly by fittedness. We'll call judging by intensity comedy—and judging by fittedness charm.

We'll be talking about this throughout this chapter.

Intensity. We sometimes assume that more intensity of an emotion is always better. But when we think about it, we'll see that more intensity is usually worse.

This is obvious with anger or sadness—we think someone with an explosive temper has "bad" anger, despite that anger being intense. That's because we usually judge anger, boredom sadness, and so on by fittedness, not intensity. We consider anger and sadness to be "good" when their justified—not simply by being intense.

It's the same for humor. If our coworker had giggling fits for minutes whenever we said hi or asked them a question—or a classmate laughed so hard at his own jokes that snot came out of his nose every time—we'd become exhausted and avoid them.

That's because the general rule of thumb, with plenty of exceptions, is that we prefer fittedness to intensity for all emotions—including humor.

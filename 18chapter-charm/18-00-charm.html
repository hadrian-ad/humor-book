18. Charm

In previous chapters, we’ve looked at both the intensity and the fittedness of humor or humor attempts.

In this chapter, we’ll look at prioritizing fittedness over intensity. We’ll call this charm:

Jessica: *to a customer* *setting down condiments* “Oh, I love your style!”

Yasmin: *turns red* *brightens* *beams* “Oh. Thanks!” (←Jessica’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Tucker pokes his head into Amber’s office unannounced.

Tucker: “Amber, great work with setting up that meeting today. It was flawless. I don’t know what we’d do without you.”

Amber: *smiles* “Thank you!” (←Tucker’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Riley: *upset* “Mom? I’m sorry. I didn’t mean to break it. Maybe I can make a new one.”

Jennifer: “Aw Riley. It’s okay.” *hugs her* (←Riley’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm) “Let’s just be more careful next time, okay?”

Sam is examining his balding head in the mirror. He rubs it thoughtfully. Jennifer sees him do this, and she grabs him by the arm.

Jennifer: *squeezing him* “You look so handsome.”

Sam gives a releasing series of small laughs, and leans against her affectionately. (←Sam’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Emma: *red-eyed*

Zoe: “What’s wrong, Em?”

Emma: “Nothing. I’ve just realized I’m older now than my brother was when h-he died…”

Zoe: “Aw. Emma.” *hugs* (←Zoe’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Emma: *cries* *laughs*

Trevor: *shopping online* “Should we order it or not?”

Yasmin: “I just can’t decide! I just love StormConQuest, but the reviews on the expansion are just so mixed. They say StormConQuest: Seas of Drake and Myrrh really simplifies the resource management. What if that ruins it?”

Trevor: *out of the blue* “What do you think about having a kid?”

Yasmin: *puts hand on face* *laughs* “Oh god, Trevor. That’s not StormConQuest!” (←Trevor’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

Trevor: *laughs* “Not now obviously. Just spitballing here, random talk.”

Yasmin’s mother: *sets down a glass of water* “Here, I got you some water!”

Trevor: *smiles* “Thank you.” (←Yasmin’s mother’s humor attempt prioritizes fittedness↔︎charm)

In these examples, people were prioritizing the fittedness of their humor attempt—the intensity was neither here nor there.

For instance:

When Yasmin’s mother gives Trevor water, she foremost wants him to feel acceptance, connection, welcome, and so on. Whether he laughs hard or just smiles isn't much of a concern. She cares more about the fittedness of Trevor's humor than the intensity.

Having only one measure for humor can make things seem dimensionless and flat. But when we add in a second measure (fittedness) we create some helpful depth perception to how we think about humor.

We'll be going over this in the rest of the chapter.

Intensity. When we watch comedies, we often think more intensity is better. And so we sometimes think more intense humor is always better. But in most of our lives, we don't usually think this. This is obvious with other emotions besides humor—we don’t think anger or sadness is better the more intense it is.

We often think an explosive temper involves “bad” anger, despite the high intensity of it. Similarly, we think someone who weeps at minor slights has “bad” sadness, despite the high intensity of it.

That’s because we usually use fittedness, not intensity, to judge whether anger or sadness is good or not. Anger is seen as good when justified (fitted), just like sadness is seen as good when justified (fitted). The intensity is usually much less important.

It’s the same for humor. If we imagine a coworker who laughs long and loudly all the time, they'd get on our nerves. Similarly, if we laughed long and loud at everything—minor jokes, unlocking the car, getting a pat on the back, and so on—we'd exhaust ourselves and other people.

That's because we generally prefer more fitted humor than more intense humor. It's only in relatively niche things—like comedies—that we tend to greater weight to the intensity of humor.

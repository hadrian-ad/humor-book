22. Charm

In this chapter, we'll look at what we'll call charm—which is judging humor by fittedness.

We often judge humor by intensity, not fittedness. When Owen and Emma watch a funny Scene, they judge how good it is by how hard they laugh:

Owen and Emma are watching Fired Fireman.

Chief starts hiccuping as he goes up to the witness stand for cross-examination. He can't stop, even as he's cross-examined by the high-powered lawyer.

The timing is perfect. Emma and Owen are howling with laughter.

Emma: *breathless from laughing* "This scene is so good!" (←Emma judges scene by the intensity of her humor)

But we don't always judge by intensity of humor. Sometimes, the intensity of humor is neither here nor there:

Yasmin’s mother: *sets down a glass of water* “Here, I got you some water!”

Trevor: *takes glass* *smiles* (←humor) “Thank you.”

-LATER-

Trevor thinks meeting Yasmin's family went pretty well. Her mom made him feel right at home. (←Trevor judges interaction by the fittedness of his humor)

Above, it was more important to Trevor that he felt belonging and welcome than that he laugh hard. So he thought his smile was good humor—because he was judging by fittedness, not intensity.

Consider:

Yasmin's mother would prefer fitted humor over intense humor too. Given the choice of making him laugh hard or making him feel belonging and welcome—she'd choose belonging and welcome

We'll be talking about this throughout this chapter.

Intensity. Without thinking about it, we sometimes think that more intensity of an emotion is always better. But when we give it some thought, we'll notice that more intensity is usually worse.

This is obvious with anger or sadness. We consider anger and sadness to be "good" when they're justified—not simply by being intense. Someone with an explosive temper has "bad" anger, despite that anger being intense—since we usually judge anger, boredom sadness, and so on by fittedness, not intensity.

It's the same for humor. If our coworker had long giggling fits whenever we said hi—or a classmate always laughed so hard at his own jokes that snot came out of his nose—we'd become exhausted and avoid them.

That's because the general rule of thumb is that we usually prefer fittedness foremost for all emotions—including humor.

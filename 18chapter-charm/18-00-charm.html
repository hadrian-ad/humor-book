18. Charm

What makes humor “good?” This depends on whether we’re prioritizing intensity or fittedness:

Jessica: *staring out the window at the moon* "Is the full moon always on a Tuesday?"

Elias: (surprised) "What?" *laughs* "No!" *Laughs more* "What, are you braindead?" (←low fittedness, high intensity)

He continues to chuckle. Jessica is red-faced, but laughs along.

Yasmin’s mother: *sets down a glass of water* “Here, I got you some water!”

Trevor: *smiles* “Thank you.” (←high fittedness, low intensity)

Jessica: *to a customer* *setting down condiments* “Oh, I love your style!”

Yasmin: *turns red* *brightens* *beams* *laughs* “Oh. Thanks!” (←high fittedness, high intensity)

When judging humor, we choose which measure of humor to prioritize. We'll call prioritizing intensity comedy—we'll call prioritizing fittedness charm.

For instance:

When Yasmin’s mother gives Trevor water, she cares more about the fittedness of Trevor's humor than she does the intensity.

That's because she foremost wants him to feel acceptance, connection, welcome, and so on. Whether he laughs hard or just smiles neither here nor there.

We're often used to only having one measure for humor (intensity). Having only one measure can make discussions of humor seem dimensionless and flat.

When we add in a second measure (fittedness) we create some depth perception to how we think about humor.

We'll be talking about charm throughout this chapter.

Intensity. For emotions, more intensity isn't always better—in fact, more intensity is usually worse. We don't always think anger or sadness always gets "better" as they get more intense. Despite its intensity, we think someone with an explosive temper has "bad" anger.

That's because we usually judge anger—or boredom or sadness—by fittedness, not intensity. We consider anger from others to be "good" when it's justified, not simply by being intense.

It's the same for humor. If our coworker fell over anytime we talked to them, even if we were just saying hi or asking a work question, they'd exhaust us. That's because for all emotions, the general rule is that we prefer fittedness to intensity.

It's only in rare cases we see emotional intensity as good for its own sake. If we work out against a heavy bag because we're pissed and have to vent, we'll judge our anger by intensity more so than fittedness. If we hang out with friends and get drunk, we'll judge our humor by intensity more so than fittedness. But these are exceptions to standards of judgement, not the standard itself.

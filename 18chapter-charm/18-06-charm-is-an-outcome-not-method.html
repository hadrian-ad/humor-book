Charm is an outcome not method

Charm (fitted humor) is an specific outcome, not a specific method. There are many methods to reach the same outcome.

For instance:

If people from across the city come to a park for a concert, people will arrive at the park by different routes and different methods of travel. What's shared isn't the route or method, by the destination (the park).

In the same way, if different people prioritize fittedness, they'll generate fitted humor by different approaches and methods. What's shared isn't a single approach or method, but the destination (fitted humor).

This is why charm attempts aren’t all the same. Since there are many ways to reach a particular humor outcome, charm attempts can be abrasive, brusque, conventional, generous, kind, rude, teasing, withholding, and so on.

For instance, Emma tends to be direct and Zoe tends to be tactful—but both are prioritizing fittedness (making charm attempts):

Emma knocks on the door of Zoe’s apartment, a coffee cup in either hand.

Emma: “Hey Zoe! I got you that disgusting tea you like.” *offers cup* (←charm attempt↔︎Emma wants Zoe to feel fitted humor)

Zoe: *eyes light up* *takes it* “That’s so nice of you!” *smiles* (←Zoe feels Emma reveals a personal view, acceptably criticizes, symbolizes close ties, creates rapport, controls a situation, and so on↔︎Zoe feels acceptance, appreciation, connection, comfort, delight, generosity, joy, release, relief, safety, welcome, and so on)

Emma: *smiles* “I know!”

- LATER -

Zoe knocks on the door of Emma’s apartment, a coffee cup in either hand.

Zoe: “Hey Emma! I got you that, uh, very interesting iced drink you like.” *offers cup* (←charm attempt↔︎Zoe wants Emma to feel fitted humor)

Emma: *eyes light up* *hand snakes out* *takes it carefully* “I hate you.” (←Emma feels Zoe reveals a personal view, acceptably criticizes, symbolizes close ties, creates rapport, controls a situation, and so on↔︎Emma feels acceptance, appreciation, connection, comfort, delight, generosity, joy, release, relief, safety, welcome, and so on)

Zoe: *smiles* “I know!”

Even though Emma and Zoe have different tonal methods, they both experience each other’s humor attempt similarly—as charm.

Comparisons. If we have a charming friend, it can be exhausting to imagine trying to be charming like they are. But we don't have to try to be charming like they are—since there are many ways to be charming.

When a thousand people become better at fitted humor, they’re not all converging toward the same style of being charming. Instead, they’d each be refining their own styles in a way that feels very comfortable to each them.

For instance:

If Zoe and Emma both get better at charm, they won't be converging toward the same methods—just the same outcomes.

Instead of becoming more like each other, they'd both end up seeming more like themselves. This process would make them feel more comfortable, not more stressed.

We already know this feeling. When we’re in an unusually good mood, we’re better at generating fitted humor in ourselves or others. This experience usually feels less like becoming more like someone else, and more like ourselves—and feels good, not stressful.

We don't always notice charm

When we're used to paying attention to our intense humor, we don't always notice our fitted humor.

To think about this, we can imagine walking on the road in the photo below:

This photo is almost gloomy—not what we conventionally associate with humor. But we can imagine feeling humor while we walk here. To catch this humor, we can imagine we're:

  • feeling the briskness of late fall air
  • smelling wet leaves and pines
  • enjoying the use of our legs
  • exploring somewhere new
  • feeling light mist on our face
  • hearing forest birds
  • knowing we’ll be able to return to comfort

(and so on)

As all that's happening, can we imagine ourselves:

  • showing humor tells? (loosening, reducing physical tension, smiling, focusing on the present, increasing agency, feeling optimism, raising our spirits, reducing stress, and so on)
  • reaching humor outcomes? (easing tension, reducing defensiveness, controling (our own) situation, creating rapport (with ourselves or the world), changing negative emotions into positive ones, and so on)
  • feeling humor family words? (acceptance, appreciation, connection, delight, insight, release, relief, welcome, and so on)

Once it's pointed out, it's obvious we could feel humor on this walk!

Consider:

We'll feel humor from mistakes and OKs balancing, which happens from a walk just like it does from watching a show. Our evaluations can balance, even if nothing about the place, photo, or walk, strikes us as stereotypically comedic.

As we feel humor as we walk, we might focus on the present, feel optimistic with increased agency, have reduced stress, and so on—and might ease tension and feel release and relief.

If we feel low-intensity, highly-fitted humor on a walk, is that humor good or bad? The answer changes depending on what we're prioritizing:

In this case, even if it’s an unremarkable experience of comedy, the walk would be a good experience of charm.

Consider

All our lives, we we value highly-fitted humor for its own sake—like on a wonderful walk.

Even so, these moments are often nameless. A term like "charm" lets us talk about these familiar experiences that happens all across our lives.

Limits. We sometimes only notice the things we expect to find. So if we only expect to feel humor while watching funny videos, we won't notice the humor we feel while taking nice walks. Of course, once the humor on walks is pointed out, it's obvious!

We don't notice all the humor we feel in part because we're so used to looking for and valuing intense humor—just like we don't always even think of low-intensity humor as humor.

We can prioritize different measuers of humor

Having two measures for humor lets us compare humor or humor attempts in different ways:

  1. Since comedy prioritizes a measure of intensity, the more intense the humor, the better the comedy. So if we’re comparing two instances of humor, the one with more intensity is better comedy.
  2. Since charm prioritizes a measure fittedness, the more fitted the humor, the better the charm. So if we’re comparing two instances of humor, the one with more fittedness is better charm.

This lets us take two different humor attempts and compare them:

Zoe gets a few paintings in a student gallery. Her parents come by to see.

Brad: “So you finally got your oil shop up and running, hey?”

Zoe: *laughs* “Dad!” (←higher intensity, lower fittedness compared to next example)

- OR -

Zoe gets a few oil paintings in a student gallery. Her parents come by to see.

Brad: “I’m so proud of you, Zo!”

Zoe: *smiles* “Thanks Dad!” (←lower intensity, higher fittedness compared to previous example)

Zoe felt humor in both examples above—so which humor attempt was “better?” There’s no single answer! It depends on whether we’re prioritizing fittedness or intensity.

If we’re focusing on the intensity of Zoe’s humor, Brad’s pun was better. If we’re instead focusing on the fittedness of Zoe’s humor, Brad’s affirmation was better.

Consider:

Since Brad values being able to make his daughter feel humor, it's helpful for him to recognize that prioritizing either intensity or fittedness can lead to humor.

By realizing that an affirmation can lead to humor, just like a pun, he has more options to make Zoe smile or laugh.

Better. Sometimes, humor attempts are better at both measures:

Sam: *to drama team* “I remember when I was cast as the slug in my fifth-grade play about compost…” *crowd chuckles* (←lower fittedness, lower intensity)

- OR -

Sam: *to drama team* “You guys are lucky you have so much talent—I remember when I was cast as the slug in our fifth-grade play about compost…” *crowd laughs* (←higher fittedness, higher intensity)

Sam's small adjustment had higher fittedness and higher intensity of humor:

In this case, whether we were prioritizing fittedness or intensity, one option was better. One was better comedy and better charm.

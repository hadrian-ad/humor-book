The Humor Book

We can prioritize different measuers of humor

What makes humor “good?” This depends on whether we’re prioritizing intensity or fittedness:

  • If we prioritize intensity, humor is better the more intense it is.
  • If we prioritize fittedness, humor is better the more it fits the emotional situation.

In this book, when we prioritize intensity, we'll call it comedy—when we prioritizing fittedness we'll call it charm:

  1. Comedy prioritizes a measure of intensity—the more intense the humor, the better the comedy. If we’re comparing two instances of humor, the one with more intensity is better comedy.
  2. Charm prioritizes a measure fittedness—the more fitted the humor, the better the charm. If we’re comparing two instances of humor, the one with more fittedness is better charm.

Having two measures for humor lets us think about instances of humor in different ways:

Zoe gets a few paintings in a student gallery. Her parents come by to see.

Brad: “So you finally got your oil shop up and running, hey?” (←Brad’s goal is to make Zoe feel intense humor↔︎comedy)

Zoe: *laughs* “Dad!” (←humor)

- OR -

Zoe gets a few oil paintings in a student gallery. Her parents come by to see.

Brad: “I’m so proud of you, Zo!” (←Brad’s goal is to make Zoe feel accomplishment↔︎charm)

Zoe: *smiles* “Thanks Dad!” (←humor)

Zoe felt humor in both examples above—so which humor attempt was “better?” There’s no single answer! It depends on whether we’re prioritizing fittedness or intensity.

If we’re focusing on the intensity of Zoe’s humor, Brad’s pun was better. If we’re instead focusing on the fittedness of Zoe’s humor, Brad’s affirmation was better.

We often feel humor at Aesop fables, but not usually intense humor. In that sense, these fables are "bad" at generating humor if we're prioritizing intensity (comedy) but "good" at generating humor if we're prioritizing fittedness (charm):

The Wolf and the Lamb

A wolf came upon a lamb and wanted to eat it, but felt he should give an excuse first.

Wolf: "Why, last year you grossly insulted me."

Lamb: "That's impossible, sir, for I wasn't born then."

Wolf: "Well, you continually feed in my pastures and steel my fodder."

Lamb: "That can't be, sir, for I haven't yet drunk anything but my mother's milk."

Wolf: (annoyed) "Look, I'm not going without my dinner."

And so the wolf sprang upon the lamb and devoured it without more ado.

The Wolf and the Lion

A wolf stole a lamb from the flock, and carried it off to devour it at his leisure. But along the way, the wolf met a lion. "I'll take that lamb," the lion said. The wolf didn't dare to resist, but as the lion was walking away, the wolf spoke his frustration.

Wolf: (frustrated) "It's most unjust of you to take what's mine away from me like that."

Lion: .*laughing* "It was justly yours, no doubt! The gift of a friend, perhaps, eh?"

If we feel the fables supported an argument, gave new perspectives, dealt with taboo topics, acceptably criticized, and so on—we'll feel acceptance, appreciation, delight, insight, realization, release, revelation, and so on—and so we'll smile, loosen, feel optimism, have active eyes, want to seek others, and so on.

To decide on whether these fables are "good," we could focus on how much we laugh (comedy). This is how we might judge puns, burping contests, knock, knock jokes, and so on.

But instead we'll focus on how fitted our humor is (charm). That's because what makes fables good are how fitted we find them—our favorite fabel is the one that helps us reach humor outcomes and feel humor family words.

People can be charming

In our lives, we’ve known people who we would describe as charming, like a:

  • basketball coach who was indefatigable and made practice fun
  • friend from college that we’d go exploring with
  • grandparent that delighted us whenever we’d visit
  • internet poster who improved an entire forum
  • pastor who always said the right things
  • pen pal who always remembered our birthday
  • romantic partner who was generous and kind
  • a local politician we liked despite ourselves
  • train conductor who was the best of all train conductors

(and so on)

These charming people had amazing abilities. They could:

  • direct conversation
  • fit in new places quickly
  • gather people with ease
  • make friends easily
  • make people feel listened to
  • make someone smile after something went wrong
  • make their problems shrink
  • project optimism
  • tell people the things they needed to hear
  • transmute problems into something else entirely

(and so on)

These abilities should look familiar—they’re humor outcomes by other names! The people who come to mind as “charming” could frequently generate well-fitted humor and reach humor outcomes in a wide range of situations:

Minh watches Amaya strike up a conversation with a lady on the bus. Amaya has her laughing and they start chatting.

Minh scratches his head. How does Amaya do that? It’s something he really admires about her. (←charm is involved in easily making friends↔︎creating rapport, easing tension, symbolizing ties, revealing personal views, opening new topics of conversation, and so on)

Minh’s soup is cold. Amaya calls over the server.

Minh: (nervous) “It’s fine. Let’s just leave it.”

The server comes over and Amaya smiles and explains that the soup is cold. Amaya and the server both end up smiling and amused, and she takes the plate away to bring back warm soup. (←charm is involved in criticizing things without angering people↔︎acceptably criticizing, easing tension, opening new topics of conversation, giving new perspectives, supporting an argument, and so on)

Minh is worrying about how his sculpture will be critiqued, and he mentions it to Amaya. What if the panel hates it?

Amaya: “Minh, I’ve seen it! You want my genuine opinion? It’s a totally wonderful piece of art.”

Minh: “You really think so?"

Amaya: “You think so too!”

Minh: “Yeah. Yeah I guess I do!” *laughs* (←charm is involved in Amaya projecting optimism and changing views↔︎insight, realization, release, relief, safety, and so on)

Trevor is talking to Minh about Yasmin. She has her job offer in another city and he was accepted to grad school, and he doesn’t know how to balance any of that with their relationship. Minh takes a sip of beer, listening.

Trevor continues, talking about his lab, and how he’s suddenly less interested in research and has been thinking of going into industry, which is confusing to him. Minh continues to listen as Trevor talks. Trevor finally sighs and smiles with a feeling of relief.

Trevor: “Thanks for listening, man.”

Minh: “Yeah, no problem. You have an interesting set of stuff going on. Let me know whatever I can do to help.”

Trevor: *smiles* “Well. Thanks.” (←charm is involved in making someone feel listened to↔︎achieve catharsis, ease tension, create rapport, survive stress, and so on)

Trevor watches everyone enter class with a burrito. Zoe enters and hands him one.

Zoe: “I don’t think you’re in any of the group chats, so I didn’t want you to miss out on the free burritos down the hall!”

Trevor: *face lights up* “Well… thanks! That’s really nice.” *smiles* *unwraps burrito* (←charm is involved in making someone feel like part of a group↔︎acceptance, delight, welcome)

Justin is reading the Anabasis, by Xenophon—a war account from the 4th century BCE.

Cyrus is leading an army to overthrow the king of Persia, and he maintains the loyalties of his mercenaries and retainers through compliments, flattery, and gifts.

Some soldiers confront him, since they're worried he’s promising more than he can give them. Cyrus assures the soldiers that they’re wrong—there’s actually too much to give to people!

Cyrus: “Consequently I am more afraid of not having enough friends for what I can give—than of not having what to give, if things go well, to all my friends.”

The soldiers, satisfied, leave. (←charm is involved in making people feel acceptance, appreciation, connection, delight, generosity, insight, reconciliation, relief, safety, and so on)

Justin smiles and stretches. He wishes he was able to reform the Persian Empire for eternal glory!

Sam: *gets up* “You know what. I’ll come with you!”

Jennifer: “Oh, you don’t have to.”

Sam: *smiling* “Well I want to.”

Jennifer: *smiles* (←charm is involved in helping someone feel connection, delight, generosity, relief, safety, and so on) *puts on coat*

If we’re judging by intensity, then these humor attempts would be bad to okay instances of comedy. But if we instead judge by fittedness, these humor attempts are okay to good instances of charm.

Consider:

Charming people often seem socially effective and connective to us. That’s because charming people are good at generating fitted humor in a wide range of situations—and fittedness is something that’s both effective and builds cohesion.

This is why we describe these people as charismatic and influential—they're skilled at generating fitted humor! Their skill at humor is what makes them skilled at interpersonal effectiveness and cohesion.

Things can be charming. We can find anything charming, not just people—like events, festivals, flowers, metaphors, packaging, places, sound effects, typography, user interfaces, walks, and so on. These things can have a charm-quality, since they tilt us toward humor outcomes and we often express humor tells toward them.

Things are charming for the exact same reason jokes or people can be charming: they make us feel delight, insight, welcome, and so on; they help us avoid conflict, ease tension, feel an argument has support, and so on.

Consider:

Since these things are static, they’re not always charming—since we don’t always feel humor from them. We may find a new metaphor charming, but with enough exposure find it cliche instead.

These charming things are in contrast with comedic things—like funny sound effects (“sproooinng-ng-ng-ng!!”), trampolines, oversized bouncy balls, penguins, and so on. These things can have an association with prioritizing intensity of humor, not fittedness.

The Humor Book

We have a preference for charm over comedy

We have a long-term preference for charm. Comedy tends to be only transiently important to us.

Consider:

We saw this in miniature with Brad and Zoe. When Zoe thinks back to her gallery opening years later, she probably won’t remember any puns that Brad made there (“got your oil shop up and running!”)—but she probably will remember him helping her feel accomplishment and achievement (“I’m so proud of you, Zo!”).

This long-term preference for charm is true for both our interpersonal lives and in spectator entertainment.

We have a long-term preference for interpersonal charm.

When we think about the people we felt the most connection with throughout our life, or wish we had gotten to know better, it’s usually the people who have made us feel the most fitted humor.

By contrast, if we think back to the various social eras of our lives and try to remember the funny one of the group, we may realize we haven’t thought of them in a while:

Emma: “Hey. Remember Josh B.?”

Zoe: *puts down fork* *puts head in hand* “Oh my god. Josh B.! I haven’t thought of him in ages.”

Emma: *laughs* “Do you think he really drank that bottle of pee, or was he just joking?”

Zoe: “I have no idea. I just remember him singing hundred bottles of beer in AP Lit. Mrs. Gardiner was pissed.”

At the time, Zoe and Emma may have giggled at Josh B. along with everyone else—but when they remember him years later, they forget the intensity and mainly remember the unfittedness.

Consider:

Josh B. single-mindedly focused on intensity of humor, without any concern for fittedness. He made funny remarks at the serious parts of movies; undercut sincere moments with meta-commentary; only wanted to watch outrageous comedies; and so on.

He fixated on using intensity as a measure of good humor, which was at odds with how people around him actually determine good humor.

Josh may have noticed that even though people laughed, he didn't really form lasting connection with them. That's because fittedness is involved with connection and cohesion—and so class clowns often only feel tenuously integrated with the people they make laugh, and tend to be forgotten relatively quickly.

By contrast, memories of achievement, belonging, generosity, realization, safety, and so on tend to persist, since we recall those experiences easily. Years later, Emma and Zoe probably fondly remember moments of kind smiles or unsolicited moments of help.

When Zoe was a freshmen, she dropped her notebook and someone swooped down and picked it up, handing it to her with a smile. Zoe smiles back. For some reason, it sticks with her, even now as a senior. (←memories of connection, generosity, relief, welcome, and so on are durable and vivid)

Zoe: “Remember when you got ‘Best Foam Rollercoaster’ in sixth grade?”

Emma: *smiled* “Proudest day of my life!” (←memories of accomplishment, achievement, appreciation, success, and so on are durable and vivid)

Emma wishes she had gotten to know her grandparents better. She remembers enjoying visiting them when she was very young. (←memories of generosity, safety, welcome, and so on are durable and vivid)

Emma: “Remember Sandra?”

Zoe: “Of course! She was so nice.”

Emma: *smiling* “She drew me a picture for my birthday in fifth grade! It was a chicken, I think.” (←memories of appreciation, connection, generosity, and so on are durable and vivid)

Sandra’s charm attempts weren’t as flashy as Josh B.’s humor attempts. But Josh B.’s comedy attempts faded in importance over time, while Sandra’s charm attempts persisted as positive. Since humor family words and humor outcomes persist in our memories, in a way that intensity usually doesn't, we have a long-term preference for charm.

We have a long-term preference for spectator charm.

When we think back to our favorite entertainment over the years, the things that come to mind are usually things with lots of charm to them. It wasn’t that they weren’t comedic—it was instead that they could occasionally stop prioritizing intensity and take time to prioritize fittedness instead.

This entertainment struck a meaningful chord with us because they gave us feelings of acceptance, comfort, insight, and so on, and helped us reach humor outcomes. These might be media about:

  • sponges and squids that deal with work and boredom in different ways
  • office workers who are dysfunctional but endearing
  • families in a small town with a nuclear power plant
  • doctors during a war
  • a space crew of mercenaries and passengers
  • washed up and depressed animals past the peak of their careers
  • friends travelling between elemental nations
  • a sad cop who lost his memory and talks to himself a lot
  • puppets and people that live on the same block

(and so on)

We remember charm fondly in spectator entertainment years later for the same reason we remember charm from friends or strangers years later—it was the charm mixed into the comedy that kept these things meaningful to us over longer periods of time!

By contrast, the things that were primarily comedic might take more effort to remember. We might have watched a show about talking cat butts years ago, and felt like we were about to die of laughter—but as we change, or the cultural milieu changes, we might forget about it, and our memory of it isn’t straightforwardly positive.

Consider:

This is why it can be odd to show our romantic partner our favorite internet videos from when we were in high school—we may suddenly see them as mean, niche, nostalgic, old, random, pointless, weird, or so on. They don’t always hold up well as we change. But old videos that prioritize fittedness often hold up better than videos that don’t.

Owen is watching an animated show.

Three monster hunters have a testy alliance.

Belmont: "I can't read or understand magic. But my family stored everything they found, including books of magic and whatever other weird stuff they came across. I just can't do anything with it—but you two can."

Alucard: *smiling* "Fortunate indeed, then, that I chose not to kill and eat you, Belmont."

Belmont: (exasperated) "And that I decided against gutting you, flaying you, and turning you into shoes, Alucard."

Sypha: (annoyed) "Such a merry band we are. I will find us a covered wagon and horses if you two can manage not to kill each other while I'm gone."

Alucard: "Oh please. We're not children."

Sypha: (annoyed) *leaves*

Belmont: *watches her leave* *leans to Alucard* (quietly) "Eat shit and die."

Alucard: "Yes, fuck you."

Belmont and Alucard laugh.

Owen smiles. Belmont and Alucard's budding bromance is fun to watch. (←Owen feels appreciation, belonging, connection, release)

Emma: "Did you ever watch Castlevania?"

Owen: *lights up* "That was so unexpectedly good. I miss those characters!"

Owen has seen many shows that have monsters and violence. But the ones that stand out to him over time are of those that moments of charm in them.

Emma: “Hey. Remember lil' bits?”

Zoe: *puts fork down* *puts hands on her head* “Oh my god." (←Zoe doesn't often remember humor that had low fittedness, and when she does it's not always a straightforwardly positive memory)

Emma: "Those skits were so weeeiiirrd.”

Zoe: "Remember how we couldn't stop laughing?"

Emma and Zoe found this memorable in part because of the humor family words they felt having watched it together—they have positive memories of connection (with each other) with each other.

But their relation to the comedy attempt itself is more ambiguous. The show itself is relatively unimportant compared to their experience with each other—and had they watched it by themselves they may not have found it memorable at all.

This long-term preference for charm isn’t surprising. We saw last chapter how fittedness was involved with group efficiency and cohesion—which means charm often marks constructive moments in our lives.

Beyond this, we can think of humor family words as the emotional orientation of our lives. We emotionally orient toward acceptance, achievement, appreciation, comfort, connection, delight, generosity, insight, joy, revelation, relief, safety, welcome, and so on.

And by extension, we orient toward the people, moments, things, and so on that we find charming—that help us feel humor family words.

Intensity. Between two equally intense moments of humor, we’ll usually remember the one with greater fittedness more fondly. That’s because—all things being equal—we’d rather feel intense humor we describe as closeness, joy, welcome, and so on than intense humor we don’t.

In places and times that low-ranked women, it was generally agreed that most women were inferior to most men in things like civic awareness, courage, intelligence, industry, morality, leadership, rationality—and to boot, women weren’t very funny either.

Of course, it wasn’t just that women weren’t funny—women were the opposite of funny: they were killjoys, nagging, shrewish, shrill, thin-skinned, vain, and so on. But to cover all the bases, women were also seen as the opposite opposite of funny too: too caring, delicate, empathetic, moral, nurturing, observant, sensitive, sentimental, thoughtful, virtuous, and so on.

In other words, those societies saw women as bad at humor from both ends, even in incoherent and contradictory ways.

Through a variety of mechanisms, women in these societies were excluded from major roles in for-profit comedy, including as actors, directors, columnists, comedians, comics, screenwriters, radio jockeys, and so on—which created feedback loops of superficial confirmation of prejudices about women and humor.

In addition, the spectator entertainment that was popular with women-majority demographics was belittled and trivialized, so that the period dramas, romantic comedies, romances, sitcoms, soap operas, and so on that were almost exclusively watched by women weren’t seen as having good or worthwhile humor attempts.

This was in contrast to the spectator entertainment that was also popular with men, which was seen as good—as having greater authenticity, civic-worth, cultural value, daring, status, vivacity, and so on.

These prejudices—excluding and belittling women—have had a long life. We can see the stereotypes that pretended to justify this exclusion being both mocked and winked at in a in a 2001 episode of Futurama:

A group of men and women crash land on a planet inhabitant only by giant cavewomen. The cavewomen shackle the men and lead them through their all-women village.

Woman: *looking around* “Where are all the men in your society?”

Cavewoman: *gruffly* “Men die out many year ago!”

Masculine robot: “With all these women around, they were probably nagged to death! Are you with me fellas?”

Men: *laugh uproariously*

Cavewoman: *glares at men, quieting them* *pointing* "Here stadium, where women basketball teams play. We no can dunk, but good fundamentals—that more fun to watch!”

Men: *laugh uproariously*

Cavewoman: “This our comedy club. Humor here funny in different way. It not reinforce stereotypes! Comedy comes from character. Real situations! Not abstract craziness.”

Masculine robot: “Translation! Boring.”

Men: *laugh uproariously*

In the scene, the men discuss what they use to assess how good humor attempts are. For them, good humor was not boring and not primarily based on character or realistic situations. In this book, this is loosely similar to a preference for intensity, instead of fittedness.

Without really ever thinking about it, many of us have absorbed the idea that intensity is the only important—or even the only possible—measure of humor. This is why we sometimes think that “good” humor is either gloriously stupid (“ice to see you”), or something that pushes the envelope. (“I know I’m not supposed to say it, but…”) We sometimes don’t think there can be any other kind of good humor.

There’s obviously nothing wrong with enjoying and celebrating things that prioritize intensity of humor. Instead, the problem comes when we lose the ability to see that other measures of humor exist concurrently. We can maybe feel this ourselves in this chapter, where “charm” is something that’s both incredibly obvious and also hard to wrap our heads around.

All throughout our day-to-day lives, charm has been common, effective, enjoyable, familiar, memorable, normal, nice, powerful, versatile, and so on. Even so, we’ve often never really had a name for it—and it’s frequently been invisible because of it!

But as we start seeing fittedness as existing concurrently with intensity, our ability to use humor will become more capable and in accord to the world around us.

Our model of humor has two quantitative values: fittedness and intensity. We can weight these values in different ways when qualifying humor. When we give more weight to intensity than fittedness, we'll call it comedy. When we give more weight to fittedness that intensity, we'll call it charm.

  • For people who don't think a measure of humor exists other than intensity, intensity is then given highest weight by default. But when we know we can give more weight to fittedness, we can see a different set humor experiences become "good." This includes low-intensity humor that we don't always notice as humor until it's pointed out.
  • Fitted humor is an outcome, not a method. There are many methods to create fitted humor.
  • We emotionally orient toward fitted humor. For instance, we'll be drawn toward things that make us feel belonging, connection, delight, and other humor family words. Over time, this orientation gives us a long-term preference for charm over comedy.

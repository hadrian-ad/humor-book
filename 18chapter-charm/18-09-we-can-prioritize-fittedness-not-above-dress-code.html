Prioritize fittedness doesn't mean we're above dress code

Just because we prioritize fittedness doesn't mean our humor attempts will be above the humor dress code:

Elias is ordering coffee.

Elias: *to barista* *smiling* “If I could see the clearest skies or your eyes—damn! I’d choose your eyes every time.” (←charm attempt that’s below dress code)

Barista: *pursing lips* (annoyed) “Here’s your coffee.”

Elias: *to stranger* “Wow, you’re beautiful. Message me, okay?” (←charm attempt that’s below dress code)

Stranger: *laughs* “You know I won’t.”

Elias: *laughs* “I know. But message me.”

Elias' humor attempt isn’t focused on intensity—it’s not a comedy attempt. Instead, his goal is to reach humor outcomes—it’s a charm attempt.

Consider:

Elias wants to help himself become accepted, open a new topic of conversation, reduce her defensiveness, reveal a personal view, deal with taboo topics, control a situation, acceptably criticize himself, and so on.

But even though Elias was prioritizing fittedness, his humor attempt was still below the situation’s humor dress code—since the topics were irrelevant and there’s a high dress code with strangers:

Being unfitted doesn’t mean someone will censure Elias. It just means that if someone does, people will see the justification for the censure as greater than the justification for the charm.

Consider:

Someone can feel humor and also justifiably censure us—since intensity and fittedness are two different things.

A laugh doesn’t mean the humor is fitted—it just means the humor was relatively intense. We can’t use the intensity of humor tells by themselves to decide if their humor was fitted.

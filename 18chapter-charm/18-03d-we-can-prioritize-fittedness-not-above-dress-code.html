The Humor Book

We can prioritize fittedness while still not being above dress code

Even if someone is prioritizing fittedness, that doesn’t mean their humor attempt will be above the humor dress code:

Elias is ordering coffee.

Elias: *to barista* *smiling* “If I could see the clearest skies or your eyes—damn! I’d choose your eyes every time.” (←charm attempt that’s below dress code)

Barista: *pursing lips* (annoyed) “Here’s your coffee.”

Elias: *to stranger* “Wow, you’re beautiful. Message me, okay?” (←charm attempt that’s below dress code)

Stranger: *laughs* “You know I won’t.”

Elias: *laughs* “I know. But message me.”

Elias isn’t focused on intensity—it’s not comedy. Instead, his goal is to reach humor outcomes—it’s charm. Elias wants to help himself become accepted, open a new topic of conversation, reduce her defensiveness, reveal a personal view, deal with taboo topics, control a situation, acceptably criticize himself, and so on.

But even though Elias was prioritizing fittedness, his humor attempt was still below the situation’s humor dress code—since the topics were irrelevant and there’s a high dress code with strangers:

Being unfitted doesn’t mean someone will censure Elias. It just means that if someone does, people will see the justification for the censure as greater than the justification for the charm.

Consider:

Someone can feel humor and also justifiably censure us—since intensity and fittedness are two different things. A laugh doesn’t mean the humor is fitted—it just means the humor was relatively intense. We can’t use the intensity of humor tells to straightforwardly think “it must have been fitted.”

We've seen high-velocity humor attempts before, like in the clip from a comedy show:

Zoe is watching a comedy show.

Fleabag is stressed from a family reunion. She steps outside the restaurant to smoke. Hot Priest, one of the dinner guests, steps outside as well.

Hot Priest: *smiles* "Fellow smoker. Have a spare one?"

Fleabag: "Sure." *smiles* *hands him cigarette and lights it*

Hot Priest: *takes a drag* (conversationally) "So does your family get together much, or—?"

To destroy the possibility of small talk, Fleabag brusquely heads back inside.

Hot Priest: "Fuck you, then."

Fleabag: (shocked) *turns to stare at him*

Hot Priest: *smiles*

Fleabag: *smiles broadly* (←humor that seems to defy social physics) *heads inside*

Zoe smiles as she watches. They have good chemistry, and she can't wait to see what horrible things befall them this season.

We can assemble a description of Hot Priest's comment. ("Fuck you, then.")

First, was it a humor attempt, or a mistake-predominant attempt? (Did he want her to smile or become angry?) It was a humor attempt.

Once we identify it as a humor attempt, we decide whether it was a charm attempt or a comedy attempt. (Was he prioritizing fittedness, or intensity?) It was prioritizing fittedness.

Consider:

Hot Priest's goal wasn't intensity, since how hard Fleabag laughed was less important to him than that she thought he supported an argument (that her action wasn't worthwhile to her), created rapport (with her), became accepted (by her), reduced (both of their) defensiveness, revealed a personality trait, revealed a personal view, handled (her) complaints, acceptably criticized (her actions), symbolized ties (between them), and so on.

Because of how clear these descriptions apply to the situation, we can call this humor attempt more specifically a charm attempt.

Finally, we ask if this was a low- or high-velocity charm attempt. (Was it ambling toward humor outcomes, or rushing for them?) It was high-velocity.

Consider:

The woman was guarding against humor outcomes, and it took something with high velocity to break through these guards. ("Fuck you, then")

These high-velocity comments don't always strike us as humor attempts, since we're used to describing them as something else—as insults, rudeness, teasing, or so on. But we can clarify those descriptions by grounding them to what's actually happening in the emotional situation. ("No matter what else we call it, it was a high-velocity charm attempt.")

A "high-velocity charm attempt" is the comment's relation to the emotional situation. This is an improvement over descriptions like "joke" or "insult" which are words that don't always relate comments to the emotional situation very well. ("Is a ha-ha insult or a mean insult?")

We said before that high-velocity can get past defenses to reach humor tripwires. But that the same thing that makes high-velocity comments effective makes the consequences of not reaching humor tripwires worse.

For instance:

If Hot Priest had just said "sorry, just trying to make conversation", he probably wouldn't have made the woman smile—but he probably wouldn't create irreparable social damage either. By contrast, "fuck you" may make the woman smile—but may cause social damage if it doesn't.

How risky was this comment by the priest? Not particularly—the priest already has a good idea of what the emotional outcome will be: he has the competence to emotionally forecast that the woman will eventually feel he handled her criticism and acceptably criticized her—and not that he was disgruntled by her criticism and unacceptably criticized her. The priest had gained this competence through experience and practice.

Since this is an example from spectator entertainment (a romantic comedy), we can't take it too straightforwardly as a template for our real-life interactions.

But at the same time, the clip does capture something that we see all across our lives once we know what to look for—moments in our day-to-day life that aren't always easy to record or convey, but are often important to being socially comfortable and effective.

For instance, we can look at a story someone posted in a forum. This story was in response to a call on the forum for strongly sexual memories of people they had never had any physical contact with. He wrote:

I (male) was taking a scuba diving class in my university. There was a stunning redhead with a amazing body. We never spoke in class. Fast forward to the end of the school year. We are diving for 2 days at a lake doing the checkout dives. One day 1 as I am exiting the water she is entering the water and said something to like “hurry up you are holding me up” I have no idea why but my response was “Fuck you” in a playful tone. She smiles and responds “OK”. My mind is blown. Class ends and she said. I’ll see you tomorrow. The next day she did not make it to class because she was involved in a car accident. She had injuries that kept her from diving. So yeah. Never saw her again. It’s been 23 years and I still remember that sexy redhead in the white two piece.

We see something similar in this story as in the show. His "fuck you" was a high-velocity charm attempt in response to a "you are holding me up" sentiment. Like the priest, the diver was trying to reach humor outcomes: create rapport, handle her complaint, reveal a personal view, and so on.

Consider:

The man said fuck you in a "playful tone." In this book, playful is another word for a humor attempt. When we play, we're trying to intentionally shift mistakes and OKs into balance.

In this case, the man doesn't sound like he had practice with this kind of comment, but felt a momentary, intuitive understanding of what the outcome would be.

Like with the scene between Fleabag and Hot Priest, we can use humor outcomes and humor family words to make sense of how the man saw the situation. He felt her smile showed connection, closeness, delight, and so on—and that their mutual humor created rapport, revealed a personal view, symbolized ties, and so on.

Consider:

Note that this is how the writer of this anecdote recalls and interpreted the experience, which may or may not be accurate or similar to how the woman remembers it.

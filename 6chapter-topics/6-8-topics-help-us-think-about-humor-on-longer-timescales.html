Topics help us think about humor on longer timescales

We can also use topics to think about humor on a longer timescale than we usually do—like when we hear jokes, but don't feel humor until later:

A. Elias is watching a recording of a stand-up comic:

Comic: *to crowd* “Every town has the same two malls. The one the white people go to… and the one the white people used to go to.” (←mistake activates new topic)

Audience: *laughs*

Elias: *shakes head* (←no humor)

- LATER -

B. Jessica: “Let’s stop for frozen yoghurt!”

Elias: *driving* “Okay. The Vanilla Fro-Zen on Linden Avenue?”

Jessica: “What? No one goes there anymore! Let’s go to the Razzle Frozen Fresh on Delaware!”

C. Elias: (amused) “Every town has two kinds of FroYo shops…” (←Elias incorporates another topic into the situation) *laughs* (←humor)

Tucker: (delightedly) “Hey, son, you know what one of the most remarkable things in the world is?”

Elias: (warily) “What?”

Tucker: *chuckles* *chuckles more* “Whiteboards!” (←mistake activates new topic)

Elias: *pause* *scoffs* “Re-markable?” (←no humor)

- LATER -

Elias is hanging out at Jessica’s place, and they’re both high. He looks at her whiteboard on her door.

Elias: “That’s… remarkable!” (←Elias incorporates another topic into the situation) *laughs* (←humor)

Jessica: “What?”

Elias doesn't feel much humor when he first hears the jokes. But the jokes were required for a later moment of humor. 

So there's a relevant short timescale (something Elias observes, which is immediately followed by humor) and a relevant long timescale (what laid the groundwork for Elias' present humor).

Consider:

We sometimes think of humor as something that happens primarily on a short timescale, like a few seconds—like someone tells us a joke, and then we laugh. But to fully understand humor, we also need to be able to think about longer timescale too, including days or weeks or even years.

When Elias was spoon-fed wordplay, he didn’t always have the situational flexibility to generate humor—like when just listened to a comedian, and didn't have the time or flexibility to balance his mistakes and OKs.

However, when he was dynamically interacting with the world, Elias did have the flexibility to generate humor. This flexibility allowed him the agency to balance his evaluations, generating humor.

We experience this all the time. Watching a comedy in a movie theater can feel like a brittle experience—it's either funny or its not. If we're not feeling much humor in a movie theater, there's not much we can do about it—we're stuck there.

But when we watch a comedy at home with friends that's not funny, we have more agency—so we can adapt and generate humor no matter what. We do this by:

  • talking during dull parts
  • mocking characters
  • supplementing flat humor with our own commentary
  • exclaiming together
  • ordering pizza 
  • reading about the movie online 

(and so on)

This flexibility allows us to tailor humor out of our experience—and the same way we tailor humor out of a movie, flexibility lets us tailor humor out of any experience.

The Humor Book

Topics help us be precise when generating humor

We don't need to hear a joke to laugh. Sam laughs below, even though Jennifer doesn't do anything "funny:"

Sam is examining his balding head in the mirror. He rubs it thoughtfully. Jennifer sees him do this, and she grabs him by the arm. “You look so handsome,” she says, squeezing him. (←Jennifer identifies that Sam has a medium mistake on a topic related to physical self-esteem)

Sam gives a releasing series of small laughs, and leans against her affectionately. (←Jennifer shifts Sam’s OK to match the medium mistake on this topic)

Jennifer wants Sam to feel humor. But more specifically, she wanted him to feel humor on a specific topic. She wants the topic 'Sam's physical self-esteem' to generate humor.

Consider:

It matters which topic generates humor. Jennifer could make Sam laugh by telling him a joke about a burrito—but even though Sam would laugh at both an affirmation and a joke, the results from each would be different.

That's because it matters which topic generates humor—the outcome is different whether it's a topic 'burrito' or a topic 'physical self-esteem' generating Sam's humor.

To shift Sam's evaluations into balance, Jennifer didn't try to do anything conventionally funny at all. That's because her goal isn't actually to be funny, but to balance mistakes and OKs—and there are many ways to do that.

In this case, Jennifer used an affirmation or compliment to raise Sam's OK—she made him feel more secure from negative personal consequences. A joke would probably have been less effective on this particular topic at an balancing mistakes and OKs.

When we have situational goals, we have to be precise about which topics we activate and focus on. Like Jennifer, we can generate generate humor in a way that's both connected to and emerging out of someone’s life—which can influence people in positive ways.

That's the end of this chapter! Next, we'll talk about how we can talk about humor in context.

We can segment our global evaluation into arbitrary sets of distinct evaluations. We'll call these arbitrary segments topics, which label our evaluation graphs.

  • Topics are either active or inactive, depending on whether we're evaluating mistakes and OKs for them in a particular moment.
  • We can have multiple topics active at once. Each active topic contributes to our emotional state.
  • Topics help us think about evaluatory continuity, even when no two situations are exactly alike. This helps us track how past evaluations are involved in present moments of humor. 
  • Our evaluations can produce humor. But since evaluations are bound within specific topics, it's the specific topics that generate humor. We are precise when we produce humor on specific topics.

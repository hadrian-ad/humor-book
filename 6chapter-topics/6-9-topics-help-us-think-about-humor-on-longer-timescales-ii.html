The Humor Book

Topics help us think about short and long views

To explain humor, we often need to include both a short and long timescale:

Middle school Zoe has a huge crush on Ryan from the boy’s track team. She keeps trying to start a text conversation with him, until he finally gets annoyed.

Middle school Ryan: *texts Zoe* “Please stop texting me.”

Middle school Zoe: *looks at text* *heart freezes* (←evaluations shift substantially on Zoe’s topic ‘Ryan’)

Middle school Zoe’s heart unfreezes and she immediately feels stupid and laughs at herself. (←topic shifts into humor quickly)

- OR -

Middle School Zoe: *tells Emma about it a month later* “… So. That’s it. Crushing.”

Middle School Emma: “So what. Ryan? He’s a douchebag! It’s a miracle he’s ever finished a 100 meter dash, since I don’t think he can count past 10.”

Zoe: “Emma! You’re so mean! He’s not that bad.”

Emma: *returning to laptop* “Yeah. Not that bad. If you ask me, he did you a solid favor of ignoring you, which is a favor you should immediately return. Like the rest of us.”

Zoe: *laughs* (←topic shifts into humor after a month) (feeling better) “Emma! You’re so mean!”

- OR -

College Zoe: *to Emma* “I dunno. I’m embarrassed. I once had this awkward experience in middle school…”

College Emma: (incredulous) “You once had an awkward experience in middle school?”

Zoe: “I guess it is kinda silly.” *laughs nervously*

Emma: “So was it something unforgivably awkward? Did you ask someone’s dad to marry you, or vice versa?” 

Zoe: “What! Jesus, no! It wasn’t that sort of thing.” *laughs* (←topic shifts into humor after a decade)

So what caused Zoe's humor in the variations above: her initial experience with Ryan, which was immediately followed mistake-predominant—or her later experience with Emma, which was immediately followed by humor? 

We have a chicken-or-the-egg scenario—since both required each other for there to be that moment of humor!

On one hand, if Zoe hadn't had her experience with Ryan, she and Emma would never had had the later discussion that ended in humor (long view). On the other hand, if Emma hadn't precipitated a shift in Zoe's evaluations and topics, Zoe wouldn't felt humor (short view).

So both the short view and the long view were necessary for this particular moment of humor! These two timescales together explain an emotion of humor better than one timescale does alone.

Matches and kindling. Both the short view and the long view cause humor, but sometimes we only talk about the short view—since it's easier to talk about. We tell a joke, they laugh, and so the joke seems to "cause" the humor.

But this is a bit like saying a match causes a fire. That can be true—without also changing the fact that without anything to light, there would be no fire!

Throughout this book, we'll want to be able to understand how the short view and long view together explain moments of humor. Focusing on one view is helpful—but focusing on both is most helpful.

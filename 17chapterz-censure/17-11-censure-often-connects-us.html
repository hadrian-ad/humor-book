The Humor Book

Censure is something that connects us more than divides us

We often think of censure as something that divides people. But censure usually connects us instead:

Jasper makes a joke with a tasteless fake accent, and it’s cringey. Sam meets a stranger’s eye. (←Sam and a stranger connect with each other by censuring Jasper)

Justin and Heather are cleaning up after a dinner party.

Justin: *amused* “How about Travis just barging into every conversation?!”

Heather: “I couldn’t believe it!” (←Justin and Heather connect with each other by censuring Travis)

Justin: (mimicking) “Oh you went to Philly? That reminds of the time I went to Seoul!”

Heather: (mimicking) “Fantastic. Fantastic!”

They continue bonding as they clean.

Sam and the rest of the administrators have to watch a new training video. It’s full of tedious puns. It’s both infantilizing and a waste of time. People adjust themselves in their seats, check their phones, and sigh. Everyone is aware of how much they are all hating this. (←Sam and the rest of staff connect with each other by censuring video)

In the examples above, the censure is something people are doing with each other, not against each other:

  • When Sam and a stranger exchange looks at Jasper’s fake accent, they’re checking to confirm that they have synchronized evaluations—which tightens them as a subnetwork. Whether Jasper notices this censure is neither here nor there.

  • Justin and Heather are synchronizing in a way tightens their emotion network—but they intentionally waited until Travis is gone to do this. Their censure is something they do with each other, not something they do against someone.

    Heather and Justin are processing mistakes together. They’re shifting mistakes and OKs into balance and easing tension, sharing personal views, acceptably criticizing, and so on.

  • The school administrators are watching a video, which means they can’t censure the people who made the training video—there’s no one there to censure! Their censure is something they do together. They want to make sure everyone shares their grievances about the video.

Most censure is connective like this—it synchronizes groups more tightly. Censure is usually used to calibrate groups or connect people together—and is only rarely used confrontationally to divide or fragment groups.

Consider:

Censure often redefines groups. This often happens subtly, like over the course of a dinner. Nothing really happens, other than a shift in some of the group dynamics.

Sometimes this happens more aggressively, where enough connective censure builds up that we’re able to exclude or expel the target or source of our censure.

But even though this is sometimes an outcome of this process, it’s not the most common outcome or the main general goal of this process.

Humor dress codes and censure are how we communicate, group, and organize. In our day-to-day lives, humor dress codes allow feedback in ways that allows social groups to function well together.

That's it for this chapter! We'll talk about humor dress codes next.

Sometimes we think that we should be able to rationalize humor or humor dress codes—perhaps through lists of permitted and forbidden content. But this misunderstands humor and emotions more generally.

We know we can’t verbally promise ourselves that we won’t be angry or sad next week—or sign a binding contract that we’ll only feel happiness next week. These promises or contracts wouldn’t do anything!

For similar reasons, we can’t make others sign a contract determining what they'll find fitted or unfitted in the future. We can’t control humor dress codes in this way!

Consider:

This doesn’t stop people from trying. For instance, during the French revolution, the constitutional assembly formally banned laughter at their meetings.

Their goal was to become more effective, deliberative, grave, rational, and so on—but interestingly, an unfollowable rule brought them further away from all of those things. Laughter continued, and there really wasn’t anything formal rules could do about it.

The constitutional assembly was attempting to control humor dress codes through dictate, not through emotional collaboration—but this never works.

Even though we can't control emotional dress codes by dictate, people still try all the time:

Stephen calls an all-staff meeting.

Stephen: “There will be no laughing during all-hands meeting. None. Got it? We’re going to spend the rest of the hour figuring out what’s happening in research. We don’t have time for nonsense.” (←Stephen tries to set a dress code in advance)

Tucker calls an all-staff meeting.

Tucker: “Now I want us to have fun. We have to laugh. No frown-y faces. C’mon everyone. Let’s laugh. Ha ha ha! Amber, why don’t you start us off with a joke? Anything goes!” (←Tucker tries to set a dress code in advance)

Stephen is trying to set the dress code so high that any humor would be seen as unfitted (and so seen as justifiably censurable)—Tucker wants to set it so low that any humor or humor attempt would be fitted (and so not justifiably censurable). The problem is that dress codes aren’t something Stephen or Tucker have centralized control over.

Consider:

This is obvious with something like sadness. We can't just wave our hands to set what things people around us see as justifiably sad or infuriating. The exact same reason we can't control the sadness or anger dress code, we can't controll the humor dress code.

When we think we should be able to set dress codes unilaterally, we don't just treat others as lower in social rank than ourselves—we won’t succeed either!

Consider:

If we want to raise a dress code, everyone around us will need to have a non-theoretical reason for increased efficiency and cohesion.

It’s not enough for Stephen to feel a need for efficiency and cohesion—since the dress code is set by everyone. In a room of twenty people, Stephen is only 1/20th of the opinion on this.

The dress code won't raise until everyone present also agrees on the need for increased efficiency and cohesion. It's the same for Tucker—it’s not until everyone else sees a need for less efficiency or cohesion that the dress code would actually lower like Tucker wants.

At best, Stephen could try to censor—rather than censure—humor. He could try to force people to behave as if the dress code was a certain way, but this is a bit like making people enjoy his birthday party at gunpoint—the picture of what he wants only.

If the staff is cowed, they may censor (not censure) themselves the way Stephen wants—but this is different than Stephen actually setting the dress code for them. He’d just compelling behavior, not change what they internally recognize as fitted or unfitted.

Consider:

Teachers often do this with students. When students don't feel any need for efficiency or cohesion, they lower their dress code and goof off. Instead of addressing the cause of their lower dress code (boredom, lack of perceived need for efficiency or cohesion), the teacher tries to force them to not goof off (censor). The teacher is trying to enforce a dictated dress code in a way that goes against how dress codes are actually set.

Sometimes teachers think classes need to get serious (raise a dress code) before they get started, but this is often backwards—we actually need to get started before we get serious. That's because moving into an activity that requires greater cohesion and efficiency will naturally raise the dress code—while simply yelling at people to knock it off tries to ineffectively force a dress code through censoring.

Sometimes, we think humor is counterproductive—but that's wrong. Fitted humor is by definition productive—it’s doing many things at once in a way that meets the current minimum standard.

If we can't distinguish between fitted and unfitted humor, and so tried to censure all humor, we'd be throwing the baby out with the bathwater. Removing all humor would be a solution that creates problems.

Consider:

Sometimes we think we can get rid of dress codes, like when we go to an anything goes comedy club. But that’s an illusion: any group of people will always form dress codes.

This is why an audience that's hungry for taboo jokes will still censure annoying laughter in the audience, or why members of a boundary-pushing meme forum will still censure people making fun of members a certain way.

That's because humor dress codes haven't gone away, and those audiences haven't been exempted from the normal process that sets them.

The same thing is true for people who try to raise a dress code instead of lowering them. But simply saying a topic is off-limits doesn't turn off our ability to find fitted humor attempts on that topic fitted.

When we understand how dress codes work, we can better influence them or make sense of why people are behaving the way they are.

When we see people having fun when we think they should be serious, or being serious where we think they should be having fun, we don't only need to be frustrated—since their dress code gives us insight into how they’re seeing the world around them.

This in turn lets us make changes to our approach or to our environment in ways that accords with how people work. This is genuine leadership on an emotion network—and not simply using blunt censorship and trying to ban or force changes to humor in ways that conflict with how we work.

A "humor dress code" tells us when humor or a humor attempt is vulnerable to censure.

  • The dress code is the line where two competing measures meet: the justification for censure, and the justification for humor or the humor attempt. The dress code is the line where one justification becomes larger than the other.
  • We can censure humor or humor attempts that fall below the humor dress code. We tolerate degrees of censure proportional to the deficiency of fittedness relative to the humor dress code.
  • We experience desynchronization as censure. This feedback allows for calibration toward harmony on an emotion network. This is a normal, often gentle part of interactions. Censure only occasionally becomes confrontational or high stakes.

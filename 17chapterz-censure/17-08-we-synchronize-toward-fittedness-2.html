The Humor Book

We tolerate what seems justified

We can’t effectively censure something when everyone feels it's well-fitted. Since everyone sees the humor attempt as justified, they'll find our censure unjustified.

We sometimes feel this with fables, proverbs, satire, and so on which are seen as above any justified reproach:

At a department meeting, they discuss using Travis to fill the position instead of searching for someone new.

Justin: (concerned) "He's a familiar face for sure. But he also... can be odd."

Dave: "Well, the devil you know, right?"

Jessica is upset at an implication Emma makes about Elias.

Emma: (cautiously) "It's just like, you know, the boy who cried wolf."

Jessica gets genuinely angry at Emma, and starts shouting at her.

Emma keeps her cool, because she absolutely feels Jessica is wrong to react that way.

Jessica realizes her anger doesn't have any teeth, and so goes back to her room and slams the door.

Satire website headline: “Nation’s 10-Year-Old Boys: ‘If You See Someone Raping Us, Please Call the Police’ (‘Doesn’t Matter Who, Doesn’t Matter Where,’ Children Say)”

Zoe: *sees headline on feed* “Ugh. Is this because that coach was in the news?”

Owen: “Yeah. Covered up for ten years. The athletic director knew all along.”

Emma: “This site always nails it. I can’t believe they even have a news-related reason to write this.”

Above, Zoe, Owen, and Emma implicitly agree that the justification for tolerating the humor attempt is greater than the justification for censuring it. When enough people feel this way, the satire itself won't be condemned.

Consider:

No one felt much humor in any of the examples above, and Jessica actually got angry—but humor attempts can be fitted whether or not anyone actually feels humor.

Protection. When satire is tolerated because of fittedness, it's tolerated because of fittedness—not because it's satire. Similarly, someone censures an unfitted proverb, it's not because it's a proverb—it's because it's unfitted.

We often see something similar when people try to delegitimize censure by saying "It was just a joke!" The issue wasn't confusion over whether it was a joke, which is neither here nor there—the issue was that everyone saw the humor attempt as unfitted.

The Humor Book

17. Censure

Last chapter we talked about humor dress codes. In this chapter, we'll look at the relation between humor dress codes and censure.

When we feel humor or a humor attempt is below dress code, we can censure them by not synchronizing with them:

Emma sees Owen selling candy in the hallway.

Emma: (surprised) “You’re selling candy?” *laughs* “What are you, a girl scout?”

Owen: (annoyed) “No.” (←Owen withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Owen censures Emma by not feeling humor↔︎Owen thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Owen, Zoe, and Emma are studying in the library. Emma keeps opening and closing her mouth.

Owen: “What are you doing?”

Emma: “I’m pretending I’m a penguin. Do you think penguins have teeth?”

Zoe: (not in the mood) “I don’t know.” (←Zoe withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe censures Emma by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Jennifer: *checks phone* They do a daily check-in on my dad at his home. He always texts me after, but he’s late today.”

Amber: “Check-in every day at the nursing home?” *smiles* “Is it like a Black Death cart that goes door-to-door?”

Jennifer: “Um. It’s just staff knocking on the door.” (←Jennifer withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Jennifer censures Amber by not feeling humor↔︎Jennifer thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Emma pulls out her phone, but is stupefied to see she accidentally grabbed Owen’s phone. Zoe doubles over with laughter at Emma’s expression.

Emma: (stupefied) “It’s not funny! Where’s my phone?”

Zoe laughs harder, and Emma gets more and more annoyed.

Emma: “I wish you would take this seriously.” (←Emma withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Emma censures Zoe by not feeling humor↔︎Emma feels the humor attempt was below dress code)

Zoe: *smiling* “Okay, okay, sorry.”

Tucker pokes his head out of his office to talk to Amber.

Tucker: “Okay. Pun-time. Here we go: I’m a big fan of whiteboards. I find them totally re-markable!”

Amber: *exasperated* “Ha ha Tucker, not bad, but you know I’m pretty busy.” (←Amber withholds synchronization by expressing less humor↔︎Amber censures Tucker by not feeling humor↔︎Amber thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Emma and Owen are watching videos of a prankster tricking people into walking into poles. Emma and Owen guffaw at each successful prank. But Zoe isn’t so amused by them.

She just tilts her head and twists her mouth instead of laughing. (←Zoe withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe censures Emma and Owen by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Above, there was evaluatory disagreement—which is registered as censure. We'll be talking about this throughout this chapter

Inclusive definitions. We often think of room-stopping conflict and formal statements when we think of censure. But in this book, more often than not, censure is just a half-smile that contrasts with someone's full smile.

We want others to be synchronized with us. When we share an evaluatory perspective, we feel grouped. When we're desynchronized, we feel this as tension that weakens our grouping—we feel it as censure.

Sometimes censure happens like dynamite—but most of the censure we receive in our day-to-day lives isn't a big deal to us. It's something done by everyone to everyone, including us and our friends and family, in ways we think of as neither good nor bad, but just how things work.

Consider:

We used extreme examples above (abandoning attention to mission control versus not), but the same dynamic happens all across our lives.

We can imagine our reaction to someone bringing donuts in during a test. If we're in a classroom and jump up excitedly as someone brings in donuts, or we're in a conversation and grin and stretch—we can intuitively imagine how censure of our actions would be justified or not based on the dress code.

  • encounter awkward pauses around the dinner table
  • exchange looks with each other when the teacher makes an uncomfortable joke
  • find our social media post quickly censured by dismissive reactions
  • find random pointless stories from our spouse fun, but random pointless stories from the guy in the line in front of us annoying
  • cringe when watching an uncomfortable scene in a comedy
  • make eye contact with others when someone is weird in public
  • share looks with passengers when someone is laughing on the bus too loudly
  • know who in the group everyone finds irritating
  • register who is censuring who in a meeting
  • find our post censured online by immediate consensus

(and so on)

In all these cases, some people have arrived at an shared understanding of the humor dress code—which gives them a shared basis to censure people breaking the dress code.

In other words, the humor dress code tells us when humor is and isn't vulnerable to censure. We'll be talking about this throughout this chapter.

Censure. “Censure” and “censor” are similar sounding words that mean different things. To censure is to condemn, while to censor is to suppress communication.

Censure:

  • if a group of academics publish a letter condemning us, we have been censured.
  • If our parents tell us it was inappropriate to make that humor attempt about Grandma, we have been censured.
  • If people on social media react poorly to the joke we posted, we have been censured.
  • If our friend vaguely smiles at our joke in a way we feel is unenthusiastic, we have been censured.

Censor:

  • if a government commands an organization remove something we wrote, we have been censored.
  • If a militia threatens us with violence if we continue airing a show, we have been censored.
  • If a forum blots out swear words, the forum has censored those words.
  • If the sound engineers cut our mic halfway through our speech, we have been censored.

While censure and censor are overlapping concepts, generally, censure comes from within emotion networks, in the form of desynchronization—while censorship comes from outside an emotion network, as something imposed on it.

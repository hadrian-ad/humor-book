Sword of Joy

A Wielder's Guide to Humor

1. Sword from the Stone

This is a book about pulling a sword from a stone—a sword called joy. And once pulled out, it's about actually wielding that sword.

We'll call wielding the sword of joy humor. This guide will help us gain a greater degree of mastery over transitions, changes, and other elements that make up humor.

Most of us are untrained swordsman when it comes to humor—but since we've been swinging that sword around all our life, we can sometimes become overconfident. We think there's nothing more to learn about swordplay. That's completely wrong!

Consider:

Humor is one of the secret sciences—it's simply not taught. Most of us have had instruction in all kinds of things, ranging from geometry to working out to baking to British literature, and so on—but we've probably never had formal instruction on humor. Which is a bit odd, when we think about it!

Skipping humor as field of study has compounding disadvantages—often in ways we don't even realize until we undergo the study.

So let's pull the sword from the stone, and go on an adventure into a strange and wonderful science—and better learn to wield a fundamental force and power in our lives. Eventually, we can put the world to the sword.

Consider:

Many of us have internalized myths about humor—like that humor can't be taught. This is usually is just a self-reference: "humor can't be taught (by me)."

Other times, we feel helpless in the face of apparent complexity. "Since people laugh at different things, we shouldn't think about it."

These are two common ideas about humor we'll be pushing through in this book.

2. The Underarmament of Joy

For most of us, there's a fundamental problem to our understanding of joy and humor: it's often insufficient for functional tasks.

We can compare two questions:

  • "How could a traffic engineer incorporate safety into an analysis?"
  • "How could a traffic engineer incorporate joy or humor into an analysis?"

It's likely the first question makes sense to us—it's likely the second one doesn't. We might wonder if joy or humor even could be a legitimate topic of analysis.

Of course, when we think about it, this doesn't really make sense. In our day-to-day lives, we can choose between road A or road B based on our expectation of safety. We can also choose between activity A or activity B based on our expectation of joy.

We make these tangible decisions all the time! That's because joy and safety both have analytic possibilities. We're just underequipped when it comes to joy.

This underamament for joy is the reason many of us feel talking about "joy" or "humor" is grating, inappropriate, illegitimate, and so on: we lack the tools (definitions, models, theory, metrics, observational practice) to handle these things to the same standards we're used to elsewhere.

Consider:

By default, we tend to think of safety as tangible, and joy as not tangible. Of course, that's not true—we can tell when a room has more joy than another room.

We can even, with some careful thought, tell which room is more conducive to joy.

The goal of this book is to make humor and joy as tangible and thinkable as anything else.

3. The Subtle War on Joy

There's been subtle conflict over joy. This conflict has played out by treating positive emotions as abstract, but negative emotions as tangible.

For instance:

Originally, the word "bliss" meant something like joy. Overtime, bliss left the world to became something abstract and otherworldly—a positive state only achievable after death.

At that point, it doesn't make sense to suggest that a job or college course or sidewalk should bring people "bliss"—it would cause people to laugh and not take you seriously.

There's a long history of joy being hidden under the bed or held out of reach. Take heaven or hell. Hell has vivid metaphors for negative states, heaven has pretty blank metaphors for positive states.

Hell was described in tangible metaphors (being boiled in a pot, stabbed with poles, tormented by ugly people, wandering wastelands, and so on). Meanwhile, heaven was described in intangible metaphors (bright lights, clouds, a collection of pious saints, a metaphorical throne, and so on).

This sets up an apples to oranges comparison! To be consistent, they could either both be intangible (hell is darkness) or both tangible (heaven is being loved by wonderful people in beautiful forests).

That arrangement makes it easy to focus on hell (we could make things hellish), and slippery to focus on heaven (is it possible to make things heavenly?).

While we could make things hellish, it's much odder to think about making things heavenly. This is so normal to us that it probably flies under our radar.

Consider:

An inversion of hell (worldly torments) would lead to a heaven (worldly pleasures) described like: spending loving time with beautiful people; enjoying feasts; drinking alcohol; winning games; thriving in the absence of rulers; being appreciated; roving gardens; exploring beautiful landscapes, towns, cities; learning; hot baths and nice beds; not working; and so on.

Despite this, these aren't always involved in metaphors for heaven, in part because "heavenly" things like freedom of travel and sexuality; abundance; luxuries; beautiful environments; and so on were actually earthly roads to hell.

So what remains of a metaphor for heaven, if not earthly joys? Well—clouds, light, saints, and so on. Many of us grew up in a cultural context that overdescribed negative things, and underdescribed positive things.

Consider:

If hell is wandering around burnt wastelands, then heaven would probably be wandering around beautiful valleys and forests and shores. Despite this, most of us associate wastelands with hell more than forests with heaven.

And so we have two tracks: hell is imagined in earthly analogies, in a way analagous to misery, while heaven is imagined in otherworldly abstraction, in a way not analagous to joy.

The reason this is relevant is that it helps us see that we do the same thing in actual life—when we think about chronic states of misery and joy.

Like hell, it's probably easy for us to imagine chronic misery. (Garbage, pollution, disease, loud noises, compounding debts, lack of sleep, stress, and so on.) But like heaven, it's probably much harder to imagine chronic joy.

We are often so practiced in thinking about negative states that we don't always have words for positive states. Often, we often use very bland words ("satisfaction").

However, when thinking about chronic joy, we often have a sense that it involves smiling and laughing. People who are chronically joyful would be smiling easily and laugh frequently.

This is where it gets trick, though—we get to a road block with smiling and laughing, because we often donâ€™t have a good idea of them, and there are some pretty powerful blocks stopping us from thinking about them widely.

Consider:

It's easier to make a pitch based on the presence of negative things (students are stressed) than it is to make a pitch on the absence of positive things (students aren't joyful).

There's a lot to unpack there, but for now we can notice two things.

The first is that it's like an algorithm for mediocre outcomes. Imagine a function that adds to any number less than zero. It'll never go higher than zero!

The second is that this also isn't often a choice. Since we can't always think functionally about joy, we're not choosing our approach—we're pushed into a single method. This lack of flexibility is limiting.

"Who would want to live in a world that was not quite fatal?" the environmentalist Rachel Carlson wrote on efforts where the final goal was to avoid worst case scenarios.

We can measure by better case scenarios—so not just reducing isolation, but promoting connection; not just lessoning misery, but building joy.

4. Comedy isn't particularly important

So if our sense of chronic joy is very loosely associated with easy laughing and smiling, we need to think about smiling and laughing. Maybe surprisingly, this is does not mean we need to think about comedy or jokes.

Let's think about this. Have you ever laughed at a joke?

Have you ever not laughed at a joke?

How about other things—have you ever laughed at:

Since we both do and don't laugh at jokes, it's not the joke we're interested—it's something about why we do or don't laugh. And not just for jokes, but for everything. This is the focus of this book.

As we understand this dynamic—of when and why we smile and laugh—we'll get closer to unlocking tangible ideas about joy.

Learning to orient away from comedy is hard, but very important to seeing the big picture. Which came first, laughing, or sitcoms? We want to be careful about trying to work backward—about trying to use sitcoms to figure out laughing.

This is obvious in other cases. Which came first, eating or restaurants? Should we try to understand eating in terms of being restaurantic—or would that be a strange case of working backwards? It's the same for humor and comedy, where it's not always useful to see smiling and laughing as "comedic."

Consider:

Joy isn't always good. Everyone has instances of people smiling or laughing when something horrible was happening. We want to understand this too.

Similarly, joy isn't simple. It's not some kind of happily ever after. It's complicated, and co-exists.

Sword of Joy

A Wielder's Guide to Humor

1. Sword from the Stone

This is a book about pulling a sword from a stone—a sword called joy. And once pulled out, we'll learn how to actually wield that sword.

Wielding the sword of joy—we'll call that humor. This wielder's guide will help us gain a greater mastery over the domains of humor—transitions, changes, merges, separations, and more.

Most of us are untrained swordsman when it comes to humor. That's good enough, much of the time—but sometimes we become overconfident. Since we've been swinging that sword around all our life, we can start to think there's nothing more to learn about swordplay. That's completely wrong!

Consider:

Humor is one of the secret sciences—it's simply not taught!

It's likely you've had formal instruction in all kinds of things, whether that's geometry, working out, British literature, baking, or so on—but it's likely you've never had formal instruction on humor.

Skipping humor as field of study has compounding disadvantages—often in ways we don't even realize until we undergo the study!

So let's pull the sword from the stone—and go on an adventure into a strange and wonderful science. By talking seriously about joy and humor, we'll better learn to wield a fundamental force and power in our lives.

Eventually, who knows?—maybe we can put the world to the sword, and rip it apart through joy.

Consider:

We often don't notice that information about humor is withheld from us—it seems normal that middle schoolers have lessons on Shakespeare but not joy or humor.

In part, that's because we've accepted myths about humor—like that humor can't be taught. But this usually describes the speaker more than anything to do about humor: "humor can't be taught (by me)."

Other times, we see apparent complexity and feel helpless: "since people laugh at different things, we shouldn't think about it."

These are two common but oversized ideas about humor we'll be rightsizing in this book.

2. The Underarmament of Joy

In the fight for joy, most of us are simply underequipped. The main problem is that we simply don't understand joy or humor well enough for applied, functional tasks.

Consider:

It's a bit like how we can do quite a bit with squares and triangles intuitively—but eventually, not knowing any geometry puts a limit on our ability to make descriptions or applied decisions.

Sometimes we don't notice our lack of next-level skills in joy or humor because we all do have our intuitive understanding—it's like how tossing around a baseball doesn't make us think about how we don't have a physics degree.

Of course, when we think about it, we can notice how tossing around a baseball doesn't ensure we can measure, compare, or describe the movement of baseballs—let alone build rollercoasters or planes.

That's obvious with physics—but when it comes to joy, we sometimes don't even think higher skills exist. We can see by comparing two questions:

  • "How could a traffic engineer incorporate safety into an analysis?"
  • "How could a traffic engineer incorporate joy or humor into an analysis?"

It's likely the first question about safety makes sense to us. It's likely the second question on joy does not. We might wonder if joy or humor even could be a legitimate topic of analysis.

Of course, when we actually pause to think about this, it doesn't really make sense. In our day-to-day lives, we can choose between road A or road B based on our expectation of safety. We can also choose between activity A or activity B based on our expectation of joy.

We make these tangible decisions all the time! That's because joy and safety both have analytic possibilities. We're just equipped with tools to think through safety, and underequipped with tools when it comes to thinking through joy.

This underamament for joy is the reason many of us feel talking about "joy" or "humor" is grating, inappropriate, illegitimate, and so on. We lack the tools (definitions, models, theory, metrics, observational practice) to handle these things to the same standards we're used to elsewhere.

This has pretty dire consequences. If people are unable to design for joy, we'll be living grossly. And if you're unable to evaluate joy or humor, you'll find it hard to speak for something we all find important in our lives.

That's why this book is here to give us tangible arms for joy—and make humor and joy as tangible and thinkable as anything else.

Consider:

Since we all have an intuitive understanding of joy and humor, we don't always notice how much we don't know—like how tossing around a baseball doesn't make us think we need a physics degree.

At the same time, when it's pointed out, we readily know that tossing around a baseball doesn't mean we know how to measure, compare, or describe the movement of baseballs—let alone build trebuchets, rollercoasters, or planes.

There's something similar to humor—where simply laughing with friends doesn't always mean we know how to go about thinking through everything about joy, including on larger scales—like gardens, campuses, cities, statewide policy, and so on.

One component of this book will be thinking about "genuine" joy. A traffic engineer will be able to identify things that people think should give safety, but don't. In the same way, a joy engineer will be able to identify the things people think should give joy, but don't.

3. The Subtle War on Joy

There's been long conflict over joy and humor. This has been a subtle war—the method of diminuation has been to keep negative emotions as tangible, while keeping positive emotions as abstract.

Just think about heaven and hell—hell was described in tangible metaphors (being boiled in a pot, stabbed with poles, tormented by ugly people, wandering through wastelands, and so on), while heaven was described in intangible metaphors (bright lights, clouds, a collection of pious saints, a metaphorical throne, and so on).

This sets up an apples to oranges comparison! They're presented as opposites, but are actually two different things.

Consider:

To be consistent, they should either both be intangible (hell is darkness, heaven is lightness) or both tangible (hell is being tormented by horrible people; heaven is being loved by wonderful people).

Instead, we often see them being mixed—hell is being tormented (tangible), heaven is light (abstract)—creating an unworkable comparison.

And so we have two tracks: hell is imagined in earthly analogies, in a way analagous to misery (wastelands), while heaven is imagined in otherworldly abstraction, in a way not analagous to joy (forests and gardens).

The reason this is relevant: it helps us see that we do the same thing in actual life!

When we think about chronic states of misery and joy, it's probably easy for us to picture chronic misery (garbage, pollution, disease, loud noises, compounding debts, lack of sleep, stress, and so on) and harder to picture chronic joy—since we don't think of access to trees as joyful in the same way we think of the presence of garbage is miserable.

Consider:

Similarly to how we relate heaven to abstract things (light), we tend to relate chronic joy to abstract things too (self-actualization).

This can lead to an unwieldy way of thinking and talking about joy, even while we have an extremely accessible way of talking about misery.

We are often so practiced in thinking about negative states that we don't always have words for positive states. Often, we often use very bland words ("satisfaction").

When we try to think tangibly about chronic joy, we often have a sense that it involves smiling and laughing—people who are chronically joyful would smile easily and laugh frequently.

This is where it gets tricky, though. We actually don't always have as good a grasp on smiling and laughing as we think at first—and there are some pretty significant road blocks to getting a handle on them.

Sometimes this has to do with the ambiguities we feel exist in laughter—like when people laugh at other people getting hurt. Other times, it's a lack of understanding of patterns to when and why we laugh. Finally, sometimes it's simply an observation issue, where we actually don't even notice many of the smiles and laughs around us.

One of the goals of this book is to pick up some arms to join in this long war on joy—and not in a way that is abstract and riddled with incoherence, but in a way that is tangible and coherent.

Consider:

It's hard to talk about joy without also talking about the ways in which people don't want you to have it. Medieval ideas of heaven and hell are also instructive here.

For instance:

Originally, the word "bliss" meant something like joy. Bliss was something you felt with your friends.

Of course, overtime, the meaning of bliss became more and more abstract and otherworldly—until it became a positive state only achievable after death.

At that point, bliss can only be used as a kind of surreal metaphor—and therefore nonfunctional when it comes to using bliss as the basis for a critique or goal.

Consider:

It's easier to make a pitch based on the presence of negative things (students are stressed) than it is to make a pitch on the absence of positive things (students aren't joyful).

There's a lot to unpack there, but for now we can notice two things.

The first is that it's like an algorithm for mediocre outcomes. Imagine a function that adds to any number less than zero. It'll never go higher than zero!

The second is that this also isn't often a choice. Since we can't always think functionally about joy, we're not choosing our approach—we're pushed into a single method. This lack of flexibility is limiting.

"Who would want to live in a world that was not quite fatal?" the environmentalist Rachel Carlson wrote on efforts where the final goal was to avoid worst case scenarios.

We can measure by better case scenarios—so not just reducing isolation, but promoting connection; not just lessoning misery, but building joy.

4. Comedy isn't particularly important

So if our sense of chronic joy is very loosely associated with easy laughing and smiling, we need to think about smiling and laughing. Maybe surprisingly, this is does not mean we need to think about comedy or jokes.

Let's think about this. Have you ever laughed at a joke?

Have you ever not laughed at a joke?

Since we both do and don't laugh at jokes, it's not the joke we're interested—it's something about why we do or don't laugh. And not just for jokes, but for everything. This is the focus of this book.

As we understand this dynamic—of when and why we smile and laugh—we'll get closer to unlocking tangible ideas about joy.

Learning to orient away from comedy is hard, but very important to seeing the big picture. Which came first, laughing, or sitcoms? We want to be careful about trying to work backward—about trying to use sitcoms to figure out laughing.

This is obvious in other cases. Which came first, eating or restaurants? Should we try to understand eating in terms of being restaurantic—or would that be a strange case of working backwards? It's the same for humor and comedy, where it's not always useful to see smiling and laughing as "comedic."

Consider:

Joy isn't always good. Everyone has instances of people smiling or laughing when something horrible was happening. We want to understand this too.

Similarly, joy isn't simple. It's not some kind of happily ever after. It's complicated, and co-exists.

More velocity isn't always better

Having more velocity isn’t always better—and, in fact, often isn’t.

In part, that's because what makes a high-velocity push effective at generating humor also makes it more effective at generating mistake-predominant emotions:

Tutor: *finishes reading Zoe’s grad school application essay* *sighs* “This is pretty terrible.”

Zoe: (shocked) *laughs* (←high-velocity comment reaches humor tripwire with Zoe) “Really? Oh no.”

- OR -

Tutor: *finishes reading Emma’s grad school application essay* *sighs* “This is pretty terrible.”

Emma: (icily) “Oh yeah?” (←high-velocity comment reaches mistake-predominant tripwire with Emma)

Xiaowei is driving Lina to the opening day of her play. She's been nervous and withdrawn the entire ride. He drops her off at their high school.

Xiaowei: “Break a leg! Also: You look nervous as hell!”

Lina: *laughs nervously* (←high-velocity comment reaches humor tripwire) "Thanks, asshole."

- OR -

Lina: (frustrated) *gives finger* *slams door* (←high-velocity comment reaches mistake-predominant tripwire)

Xiaowei: *gives finger* *laughs*

Heather: *hands on hips* “Xiaowei, when are you going to start looking for a job?”

Xiaowei: *playing a game* (listlessly) “When my shit turns blue.”

Heather: *throws hands up* (←high-velocity comment generates mistake-predominant tripwire)

Xiaowei: *laughs*

Low-velocity comments are easily deflected, which gives people more choice in how they react to them.

By contrast, high-velocity comments are hard to deflect, and so don't always allow for a choice of outcomes. For the same reason it's hard to resist humor from high-veloctiy comments, it hard to resist negative feelings.

That means low-velocity comments have a buffer to both good and bad outcomes—since what makes it easy to guard against shame also makes it easy to guard against humor.

Consider:

Velocity by itself isn’t good or bad, effective or ineffective. Knowing how much velocity to use requires awareness, experience, discretion, judgment, and so on—something we develop through experience and observation.

Trevor is talking to Minh about Yasmin. She has her job offer in another city and he was accepted to grad school, and he doesn’t know how to balance any of that with their relationship. Minh takes a sip of beer, listening.

Trevor continues, talking about his lab, and how he’s suddenly less interested in research and has been thinking of going into industry, which is confusing to him. Minh continues to listen as Trevor talks.

Trevor finally sighs and smiles with a feeling of relief. “Thanks for listening, man.” (←Minh allows Trevor to walk himself into outcome tripwires of relief, acceptance, insight, changing negative emotions into positive emotions, creating rapport, and so on)

Minh: “Yeah, no problem. You have an interesting set of stuff going on. Let me know whatever I can do to help.”

Trevor: *smiles* “Well. Thanks.”

Minh helps Trevor generates humor by doing nothing. Humor tripwires can help us forecast when this will happen—like when people are clearly leading up to supporting (their own) arguments, handling (their own) complaints, symbolizing close ties (with themselves), easing (their own) tension, and so on.

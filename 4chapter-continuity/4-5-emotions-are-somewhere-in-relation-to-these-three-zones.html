Emotions are in these three zones

Throughout this book, we’ll talk about emotions as being somewhere in relation to these three zones:

Mistake-predominant emotions (afraid, ashamed, bummed, confused, excited, irritated, focused, grieving, jealous, perplexed, sad, stressed, terror, worried, and so on)

Balanced emotions (humor)

OK-predominant emotions (apathetic, bored, calm, certain, content, convinced, detached, leisurely, listless, neutral, relaxed, stagnant, satisfied, tranquil, unexcited, and so on) 

This helps us see humor as part of the same emotional landscape as other emotions. Humor isn’t apart from other emotions—instead, it has a specific place: humor is sandwiched between emotions like anger and calm.

It can seem odd that we sometimes laugh before moments of apathy and also sometimes laugh before moments of terror. But it’s not odd when we think of humor as equally adjacent to both apathy and terror.

When we cross between these two emotional zones, humor “rings”—like how a convenience store bell rings whenever the door is opened, whether someone is going in or out.

Since we grew up with words like "humor" and "emotions," we often take them pretty seriously—like by assuming they're so real as to go back thousands and thousands of years.

But these words are much more strange, nebulous, and inconsistent than they seem at first. In part, that's because they're based on a metaphor that has dropped out of sight.

Let's go over a quick history of both of these words:

Humor

Words like humid, humidify, humidor, humus, and so on all share the same root ("moist, wet")—which is why humor historically means "juice of a plant or animal." So we squeeze the humor from apples to drink it, and squirrels are full of squirrel humors.

Humor didn't have any association with reactions or internal life at all—until a few hundred years ago, when metaphorical human humors (human "juices") were used to label axes on a personality modeling graph.

Eventually, the fluids were seen as an extraneous step, and "humor" started to directly mean something like "the malleable medium of disposition." (“The baby was in bad humor yesterday, but is in good humor today!”)

Over the next two hundred years or so, humor shifted from meaning "mallable medium of disposition" into all the things it also means today—which is a very broad tent.

This includes overlapping meanings of humor, like "laughter," "cause of laughter," "jokes in general," "interpersonal ability to generate laughter," "laughter associated with weirdness," "a feeling associated with laughter," and so on.

These modern meanings are kind of slangy. We can feel this slanginess by imagining that we're saying "juice” instead of humor:

  • “he just has a great sense of juice about him”
  • “that movie has great juice”
  • "it's juice that makes life worth living!"
  • “just juice him, it's not worth the conflict”

We sometimes assume a common word like humor is grounded and formal. But it's actually the opposite! It's an opportunistic, flexible, and vague word. This is only a problem if we think the word is something that it's not.

Consider:

A slang meaning is when the meaning doesn't match the word's etymology—like how many meanings of today's "humor" are independent of moistness and liquids.

This is different than other words, like "anger" and similar words like anguish, anxiety, angina, and so on. These words come from the root that means “painful, painfully constricted.”

These words have all meant something like this for thousands of years. If we say someone is angry or anxious, we could still describe them as pained or constricted.

Our sturdiest emotional words tend to be like this, like grieve, grave, aggravate, and so on, which come from the root of "heavy."

This gives some context words like humor, which seem to exist in a different neighborhood than these more stable words.

Emotion

The word “emotion” was originally just a variation of the word “motion.” Emotion was just motion with an emphatic or expelling e- in front—like erupt and rupt(ure).

This is why early uses of emotion were about motion:

  • an earthquake was emotion in the ground (the ground shakes, has motion)
  • a roving crowd had emotion (they moved together, com-motion)
  • a letter could cause a reader’s emotion (like pacing, from internal feelings)

(and so on)

From there, the word eventually developed into its current sense—“cogitated feelings, especially strong ones (that cause movement).”

Sometimes, we use the word “emotion” expansively, to include all inner life. But in our day-to-day life, the meaning of “emotion” is constantly drifting back to motion.

If a director says “let’s do the scene again, this time with more EMOTION!”—they don’t mean more vivid internal sensations. They mean more emoting, more expressive action in the body and face.

Similarly, we call cats emotionless—not because cats don't have feelings, moods, opinions, personality, preferences, reactions, and so on, because they obviously do—but because their faces don’t move. Cats can’t emote in the same way dogs can, with their moving (emotional) faces.

Consider:

It's the same for people. When a novelist describes someone as "emotionless" they often mean they have a stony poker face— not that there's nothing happening inside them.

Similarly, we think of stormy seas and blowing trees as emotional, where it's the motion that gives power to the analogy to human emotion.

This association with movement is one reason emotion sometimes struggles to encompass everything we want it to—including things like intentions due to mood, or tone of reflections.

To recap, "humor" and "emotion" had changes in meaning that happenend at around the same time—and that were essentially new meanings.

These changes happened together because of an overarching metaphor for human experiences or feelings—of experiences as having a liquid quality to them.

As this metaphor became popular, it caught several words in its wake, giving them new meanings. Since humors (liquids) were particularly emotional (fluid), they both suited metaphorical descriptions of internal human feelings—where something infused us like sap or blood, or mixed and behaved like liquids in a container.

This is why these two words, humor and emotion, changed together from literally describing fluids and movement to metaphorically describing feelings and cause of laughter.

One of the points of all this is to give some distance from the modern sense of these words—which are often confusing and still dominate how we think about ourselves.

But when we know we can step back and see them as metaphors—or even simply as slang—we have some more freedom to think about a variety of ways about ourselves.

Our evaluations (mistakes, OKs) aren't static—our evaluations change over time. This lets us graph the magnitude of mistakes and OKs over time. 

  • We always have a ratio of mistake and OK, which always produces emotions. However, this ratio only sometimes produce humor. By focusing on mistakes and OKs, we can see humor coming from somewhere, rather than nowhere.
  • When the ratio of mistakes and OKs isn't 1, our evaluations are either mistake-predominant (mistake is larger than OK) or OK-predominant (OK is larger than mistake).
  • Our evaluational ratio falls into and move between three emotional zones: OK-predominant evaluations; balanced evaluations (humor); and mistake-predominant evaluations. These zones blend into each other and don't have hard boundaries.

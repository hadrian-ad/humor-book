Emotions fall within these three zones

Throughout this book, we’ll talk about emotions as being somewhere in relation to these three zones:

Mistake-predominant emotions (afraid, ashamed, bummed, confused, excited, irritated, focused, grieving, jealous, perplexed, sad, stressed, terror, worried, and so on)

Balanced emotions (humor)

OK-predominant emotions (apathetic, bored, calm, certain, content, convinced, detached, leisurely, listless, neutral, relaxed, stagnant, satisfied, tranquil, unexcited, and so on) 

This helps us see humor as part of the same emotional landscape as other emotions. Humor isn’t apart from other emotions—instead, it has a specific place: humor is sandwiched between emotions like anger and calm.

It can seem odd that we sometimes laugh before moments of apathy and also sometimes laugh before moments of terror. But it’s not odd when we think of humor as equally adjacent to both apathy and terror.

When we cross between these two emotional zones, humor “rings”—like how a convenience store bell rings whenever the door is opened, whether someone is going in or out.

Since we grew up with words like "humor" and "emotions," we often find it hard to imagine a world without them—a bit like how it would be hard to imagine a world without words like "tree" or "cloud."

But surprisingly, both humor and emotions are relatively new words, whose modern meanings didn't actually exist for most of history.

Let's go over a quick history of both of these words:

Humor

"Humor" originally meant "juice of a plant or animal." So we can squeeze the humors from apples (apple juice), and squirrels are full of squirrel humors. Words like humor, humid, humidify, humidor, humus, and so on all share the same root of "moist, wet."

Humor didn't have any association with laughter at all—and didn't have any association with emotions in general—until just a few hundred years ago. That's when dimensions in a popular personality modeling system were labeled with human humors (human "juices").

Eventually, the combininations of fluids (humors specifically) was seen as an extraneous step, and "humor" started to directly mean something like "malleable medium of disposition" (humor generally), often with a capricious subtext. (“The baby was in bad humor yesterday, but is in good humor today!”).

Over the next two hundred years or so, the meaning of humor shifted from "the malleable medium of disposition" into all the things it also means today—which is a very broad tent that coexist with these older meanings.

The modern meanings tends to related from different angles to laughter—including humor's overlapping meanings like "laughter," "cause of laughter," "jokes in general," "interpersonal ability to generate laughter," "laughter associated with weirdness," "a feeling associated with laughter," and so on.

Emotion

The word “emotion” was originally just a variation of the word “motion.” Like e-rupt and rupt(ure), e-motion was just motion with an emphatic or expelling e- in front.

This is why early uses of emotion were about motion:

  • an earthquake was emotion in the ground (the ground shakes, has motion)
  • a crowd had emotion as they filed out of the stadium (they moved together, com-motion)
  • a letter could cause a reader’s emotion (like pacing, from internal feelings)

(and so on)

From there, the word eventually developed into its current sense—“cogitated feelings, especially strong ones (that cause movement).”

Sometimes, we use the word “emotion” expansively and beyond movement—we use emotion to refer to all inner life. But the meaning of “emotion” constantly drifts back to motion.

If a director says “let’s do the scene again, this time with more EMOTION!”—they don’t mean more vivid internal sensations. The director mean more emoting, more expressive action in the body and face.

Similarly, we call cats emotionless—not because cats don't have feelings, moods, opinions, personality, preferences, reactions, and so on, because they obviously do—but because their faces don’t move. Cats can’t emote in the same way dogs can, with their moving (emotional) faces.

It's the same for people. When a novelist describes someone as "emotionless" they often mean they have a stony poker face ("motionless")— not that there's nothing happening inside them.

"Humor" and "emotion" took on new meanings together because of metaphor that became more and more embedded, which caught several words in its wake, giving them new meanings—the metaphor that experiences were liquid.

Since humors (liquids) were particularly emotional (fluid), they both suited these metaphorical descriptions of internal human feelings. Our evaluatory nature infused us like sap or blood—mixed and behaved like liquids in a container—and could be expelled or drained in some way.

This popular metaphor is why these two words, humor and emotion, changed together from literally describing fluids and movement to metaphorically describing cause of laughter and feelings.

Consider:

We have plenty of remenants of this metaphor cluster, like when we talk about emotions as something we are "filled with" that can be "let out" or "expelled."

These metaphors replaced the Latin word for emotion (affect), which meant "to have done to," "to have been connected in such a way."

The replacement of affect (done to us) with emotions/humor (happens within us) also upturned and outdated many traditional words, like merry (something that causes enjoyement, "merry weather").

One of the points of all this is to give some distance from the modern sense of these words—which are often confusing! Despite this, they still dominate how we think about ourselves.

But when we know we can step back and see their metaphors—and a bit strange, nebulous, and inconsistent—we have more freedom to treat these words as one tool in a bigger toolbox in think about ourselves.

Slang. The modern meaning of "humor" is kind of slangy. We can feel this slanginess by imagining that we're saying "juice” instead of humor:

  • “he just has a great sense of juice about him”
  • “that movie has great juice”
  • "it's juice that makes life worth living!"
  • “just juice him, it's not worth the conflict”

One way of thinking about "slang" is that the meaning doesn't match its etymology. This gives some context to a new word like humor ("moistness"), which often feels categorically different to more durable emotional words.

The etymology of words like anger, anxiety, angina, and so on come from the root for "painful, painfully constricted"—of grief, grave, aggravate, and so on the root of "heavy"—of love from the root for care, love

These words have been stable for thousands of years, which helps us think about why a word like humor can seem to exist in a different neighborhood from them.

Our evaluations (mistakes, OKs) aren't static—our evaluations change over time. This lets us plot the magnitude of mistakes and OKs over time. 

  • We always have a ratio of mistake and OK, which always produces emotions. However, this ratio only sometimes produce humor. By focusing on mistakes and OKs, we can see humor coming from somewhere, rather than nowhere.
  • When the ratio of mistakes and OKs isn't 1, our evaluations are either mistake-predominant (mistake is larger than OK) or OK-predominant (OK is larger than mistake).
  • Our evaluational ratio falls into and move between three emotional zones: OK-predominant evaluations; balanced evaluations (humor); and mistake-predominant evaluations. These zones blend into each other and don't have hard boundaries.

What do people feel when not feeling humor?

When the size of mistakes and OKs are close to balanced, we feel humor. When they're not balanced, we don't feel humor.

But there are two ways these scales can not be balanced. The scales can either be mistake-predominant (mistake is larger than the OK) or OK-predominant (OK is larger than the mistake):

Above, someone starts with mistake-predominant evaluations, moves into humor, and then moves into OK-predominant evaluations. 

This lets us describe different parts of the emotional situation over time—not just when there is humor, but also when there's not humor:

Informal precision. We'll be using these evaluation graphs throughout the rest of this book—but informally, without pretending they offer more precision than they do. Like any sketch, they're just quickly trying to convey the gist of something. 

We've thought about mistakes and OKs with numbers. The goal of the numbers wasn't to imply precision, but to show that these evaluations are fluid. Once we accomplish that goal and see evaluations as fluid and changing, the numbers aren't so important.

That's why in the rest of the book we won't use numbers for mistakes and OKs. Our evaluations will be informally described as "small," "medium," or "large," and our graphs will be informal and sketch-like.

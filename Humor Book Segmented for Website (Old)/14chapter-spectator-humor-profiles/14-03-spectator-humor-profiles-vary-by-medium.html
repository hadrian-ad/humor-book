Humor profiles vary by medium

It's not just movies—each medium has different constraints shaping how humor is presented:

  • A play needs someone in the back row to clearly perceive an actor’s humor—so actors will avoid subtle physical humor tells.
  • A graphic novel needs to show the intensity of humor in a single panel without sound—so creators will use fonts and exclamation marks as humor tells.
  • A novel needs to use quick and generic cues to keep a brisk pace of written conversation—so authors will use shorthands like "he laughed."
  • A game with retro-graphics needs players to be able to be able to understand emotion states, even without animated expressions—and so will use musical cues and exaggerated posture as humor tells.

(and so on)

Each medium creates its own spectator humor profile by selectively using and modifying humor tells.

While these spectator humor profiles often become familiar, being familiar isn't the same thing as being realistic.

For instance

A shirt with a smiley face on it is a familiar instance of presented humor—but it's still surreal.

It's a similar situation when a novel says "he laughed." Like the smiley face, the novel's characterization of humor is familiar—but still surreal.

In both cases, the shirt and the novel are finding ways to express evaluatory information within the constraints of the medium.

Once we acclimate to different spectator humor profiles, they do what they're supposed to do—which is to efficiently convey the character’s evaluations to spectators.

Newness. We don't always realize how distinctive different spectator humor profiles are until we have to acclimate to new genres and mediums.

Chapter 10

Spectator Entertainment

In previous chapters, we looked at emotion networks—everyone's constant exchange of evaluatory information:

The Ultimate team practices on the quad. Everyone has to track everyone.

Sometimes, we can observe an emotion network:

Emma and Zoe are several stories up, looking through the window at the Ultimate team practice on the quad.

This is like a one-way mirror, where Emma and Zoe can take in evaluatory information, but their own evaluatory information is screened out:

Sometimes these screens are real, like if we watch a movie or a game live on TV—there is no way for our evaluations to reach the actors or players we're watching.

Trevor and Yasmin are watching Squeal III.

Rosie explores an old mansion at night, and paused before a small jewelry box ominously on the table. “Don’t do it,” Trevor says, annoyed. Rosie slowly opens the box and a ragged ghost explodes out! 

Trevor and Yasmin leap up. “Fuck!!” Yasmin says, heart thumping. She laughs, hand over her heart. (←Yasmin feels the jump scare is OK ↔︎safety↔︎humor)

Trevor has his hands on his head. “Why can’t they just have the monsters and blood without the freaking jump scares?” he says. (←Trevor feels the jump scare is not OK↔︎no safety↔︎no humor)

Other times, these screens are pretended—like when an audience watches a play. Our evaluatory information does reach the actors, but they pretend it doesn't:

Sam and Jennifer are acting in a play. They pretend the audience isn't there. When the audience chuckles, Sam and Jennifer ignore it.

In this chapter, we’ll look at spectator entertainment—things that prioritize the emotions of spectators.

Consider:

When we perform a play, it doesn't matter how we feel, or how our characters feel—it matters how the spectators feel.

Since the priority is on the spectators, the play is "spectator entertainment."

To become familiar with this, we can make an analogy to wrestling.

Interpersonal wrestling prioritizes the mutual experience between the wrestlers, while spectator wrestling prioritizes the one-sided experience of watching the wrestlers:

  • Interpersonal wrestling is intense and interesting to participants—but not always as interesting to spectators. Casual spectators might see the wrestlers in odd-looking helmets and uniforms locked in a static grapple for a long time before something happens quickly and the match is over.
  • Spectator wrestling is designed to be intense and interesting to the audience—and how the wrestlers feel is less important. In order to better commercialize wrestling, showrunners staged and performed scenes in a way that was easy to follow, exciting, visually stimulating, and so on—for the audience.

When generating humor, sometimes the focus is on a mutual experience—we prioritize the humor we feel with someone. But other times, the focus is on the spectator’s experience—we prioritize the humor others feel.

We'll be talking about this throughout this chapter.

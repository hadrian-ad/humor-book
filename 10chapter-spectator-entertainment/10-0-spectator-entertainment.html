Chapter 10

Spectator Entertainment

In this chapter, we’ll look at spectator entertainment—things that prioritize the emotions of others.

Consider:

When we perform a play, it doesn't matter how we feel, or how our characters feel—it matters how the spectators feel.

Since the priority is on the spectators, the play is "spectator entertainment."

To become familiar with this, we can make an analogy to wrestling.

Interpersonal wrestling prioritizes the mutual experience between the wrestlers, while spectator wrestling prioritizes the one-sided experience of watching the wrestlers:

  • Interpersonal wrestling is intense and interesting to participants—but not always as interesting to spectators. Casual spectators might see the wrestlers in odd-looking helmets and uniforms locked in a static grapple for a long time before something happens quickly and the match is over.
  • Spectator wrestling is designed to be intense and interesting to the audience—and how the wrestlers feel is less important. In order to better commercialize wrestling, showrunners staged and performed scenes in a way that was easy to follow, exciting, visually stimulating, and so on—for the audience.

When generating humor, sometimes the focus is on a mutual experience—we prioritize the humor we feel with someone. But other times, the focus is on the spectator’s experience—we prioritize the humor others feel.

We'll be talking about this throughout this chapter.

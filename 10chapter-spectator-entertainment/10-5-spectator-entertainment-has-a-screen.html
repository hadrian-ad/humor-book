Spectator entertainment has a screen

In spectator templates, there’s a screen between us and what we’re spectating:

This screen is like a one-way emotional mirror: our evaluations change because of them, but their evaluations don't change because of us.

Sometimes this screen is permeable—like if we’re listening to two of our friends argue. Other times, this screen is impermeable—like if we’re reading a graphic novel about two friends arguing.

Try as we might, we'll never influence the characters in a graphic novel—the impermeable screen creates a one-way emotional system where we can be influenced, but we can't influence them.

By contrast, watching our friends argue isn’t a closed emotional system. Just by being present, there's mutual reactivity—and we could become more involved at any moment we choose.

Consider:

Some spectator entertainment has impermeable screens, like static movies—where yelling at a screen does nothing. But when we watch a play, there's a permeable screen—yelling at the stage brings mutual connectivity into play.

We often find spectator entertainment with permeable screens more vivid and interesting—the permeable screen feels dynamic, and allows for subtle kinds of reactive participation.

By contrast, spectator entertainment with impermeable screens often has the feeling of excluding us—like advertisements at gas stations, which relentlessly continue regardless of anything going on around them.

A spectator template doesn't mean something is spectator entertainment.

When we watch both spectator wresting and interpersonal wresting from the sidelines, they're both spectator templates. As a spectator, we're not part of the mutual part of the evaluation system of either.

The diffence is that it's only the spectator wrestling that's prioritizing our emotions. The interpersonal wrestlers don't really care about our emotions—this lack of priority means it's not spectator entertainment.

This is why it's not spectator entertainment when we watch our friends argue or overhear a discussion the table over. We're part of an spectator template—but that doesn't mean it's spectator entertainment.

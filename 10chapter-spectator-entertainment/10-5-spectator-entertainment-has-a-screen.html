Spectator entertainment has a screen

In spectator templates, there’s a screen between us and what we’re spectating:

This screen is like a one-way emotional mirror: our evaluations change because of them, but their evaluations don't change because of us.

For instance:

If we're at a third-story window, watching people walk on the street below, we'll be reacting to evaluations below—but they won't be reacting to us.

Something similar happens when we watch a show. We'll be reacting to the evaluations we see—but they won't be reacting to us.

Sometimes this screen is permeable—like if we’re looking at people down on the street, where at any moment we could yell a greeting or warning to them (permeable screen).

Other times, this screen is impermeable—like if we're watching a show, where no matter how much we yell at the screen we'll never influence them.

Consider:

Some spectator entertainment has impermeable screens, like static movies—where yelling at a screen does nothing. But when we watch a play, there's a permeable screen—since the actors react to the audience, and we could always stand up and yell and bring mutual connectivity more into play.

We often find spectator entertainment with permeable screens gives the feeling of including us, which makes them feel vivid and interesting. The permeable screen feels dynamic, and allows for subtle kinds of reactive participation.

By contrast, spectator entertainment with impermeable screens often has the feeling of excluding us, which makes them feel lifeless and grating outside very specific circumstances.

This is like advertisements at gas stations, which relentlessly continue regardless of anything going on around them—or recorded lectures that are unable to adjust pace for the students.

Emotional templates. While all spectator entertainment uses a spectator template, but not all things that use spectator templates are spectator entertainment:

When we watch both spectator wresting and interpersonal wresting from the sidelines, we're not part of the mutual evaluation system—they're both spectator templates.

But only the spectator wrestling is prioritizing our emotions. The interpersonal wrestlers don't really care about our emotions. In this way, both can have slots for spectators in their emotional system, while only one is spectator entertainment.

This is why it's not spectator entertainment when we watch our friends argue, or when we overhear a discussion the table over. By being removed from the mutual interaction, we're a spectator—but since our emotions aren't the priority, it's not spectator entertainment.

Spectator entertainment has a screen

In spectator templates, there’s a screen between us and what we’re spectating:

This screen is like a one-way emotional mirror: our evaluations change because of them, but their evaluations don't change because of us.

For instance:

If we're watching people walk on the street below from our third-story window, we'll be reacting to evaluations below—but they won't be reacting to us.

Something similar happens when we watch a show. We'll be reacting to the evaluations we see—but they won't be reacting to us.

Sometimes this screen is permeable—like if we’re looking at people down on the street, where at any moment we could yell to them (permeable screen).

Other times, this screen is impermeable—like if we're watching a show, where no matter how much we yell at the screen we'll never influence the characters (impermeable screen).

Consider:

Some spectator entertainment has impermeable screens, like static movies—where yelling at a screen does nothing. But when we watch a play, there's a permeable screen—since the actors react to the audience, and we could always stand up and yell at them and bring about more mutual connectivity.

Spectator entertainment with permeable screens often gives us the feeling of including us, which makes them feel vivid and interesting. Things with permeable screens feel dynamic, and allow for limited amounts of reactive participation.

By contrast, spectator entertainment with impermeable screens often has the feeling of excluding us, which makes them feel lifeless and grating outside very specific circumstances.

These are like recorded training videos or advertisements at gas stations, which trundle on no matter what. The lack of any permeable screen means there can be no tailoring to the emotional situation.

Emotional templates. While all spectator entertainment uses a spectator template, not all things that use a spectator template are spectator entertainment:

We can think about this with wrestling. If we watch a high school wrestling match, we're the spectator—it's a spectator template.

But since our emotional state isn't the priority of the wrestlers, it's not spectator entertainment.

The Humor Book

Spectator entertainment is focused on observers

In the example below, Sam and Jennifer feel more humor than we, the readers, feel:

Sam walks into the kitchen and puts a hand on Jennifer’s shoulder. He sees her bagel without cream cheese.

Sam: (surprised) “Oh, it’s the thing.”

Jennifer: *laughs* “Well, I wouldn’t just... you know…” (←Jennifer feels humor)

Sam: *laughs* *sits down* “Of course not!” (←Sam feels humor)

Jennifer: *amused* “One of these days…” *gestures* (←Jennifer feels low-intensity humor)

Sam: *smiles* (ironically wistful) “One day…” (←Sam feels low-intensity humor)

Sam and Jennifer felt humor participating in the interaction. But that doesn't mean anyone watching a recording of these scene would feel the same amount of humor.

Consider:

Just because Sam and Jennifer laughed doesn't mean it would make spectators laugh if it was put on a sitcom.

By contrast, if Sam and Jennifer were cast in a play, it wouldn't really matter whether they felt humor themselves or not. Their goal as actors would prioritize generating humor in spectators:

Sam and Jennifer are on set, and curtains rise.

Sam: *tries to tiptoe gingerly past Jennifer* *audience laughs* (←audience feels humor)

Jennifer: *not turning around* “Hello dear.”

Sam: *freezes* *audience laughs* (←audience feels humor) (trying to sound normal) “Hello honey.”

Jennifer: (falsely sweet) “I don’t want honey. I want cream cheese. You didn’t forget to buy me the cream cheese with scallions in it for my bagel, did you… dear?”

Sam: *mouth drops* *bites nails with nervousness* *audience laughs* (with fake offense) “Calling me a rapscallion, are you?” *audience laughs* (←audience feels humor)

Jennifer: (with dangerous restraint) “It’s a bad rap for sure. You know my morning depends on those perfect green scallion flecks.” *licks lips*

Sam: *desperately trying to distract her* *lays across the table and roguishly props head up with arm* *audience laughs* “Instead of scallions, how about a stallion instead?” *Sam’s eyebrows wriggle up and down enticingly* *audience laughs harder* (←audience feels humor)

Jennifer: *rolls eyes and pushes him away* “Why, is the mailman coming early? Now where is my damn cream cheese? I’m hungry!”

Jennifer and Sam pause to let the audience react. (←Sam and Jennifer as actors and characters don't feel humor)

Audience: *cheers* (←audience feels humor)

In the example above, it’s only the audience that felt humor. Sam and Jennifer weren't trying to shift mistakes and OKs to generate humor for themselves. They were trying to shift mistakes and OKs into humor for the spectators.

Imitation. Even though some of our favorite experiences of humor happen while watching spectator entertainment, that doesn’t mean what we're watching is a good template for generating humor in our day-to-day lives.

Sometimes we want to imitate whatever made us laugh the most, but this ignores some important distinctions. When we’re spectators, we’re spectators—when we’re participants, we’re participants. What generates the most humor in one role doesn’t always generate the most humor in the other.

If we laugh at something in a TV show, all we know is that it's good at making people watching TV shows laugh. It doesn't straightforwardly mean it would be good at making the people around us laugh.

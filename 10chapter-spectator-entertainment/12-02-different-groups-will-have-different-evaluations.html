The Humor Book

Evaluations are different for different groups

We can use mistakes and OKs to think about why the characters and spectators have different emotional outcomes:

Sam walks into the kitchen and puts hand on Jennifer’s shoulder. He sees her bagel without cream cheese.

Sam: (surprised) “Oh, it’s the thing.” (←medium mistake)

Jennifer: *laughs* “Well, I wouldn’t just... you know…”

Sam: *laughs* *sits down* “Of course not!”

Jennifer: *amused* “One of these days…” *gestures*

Sam: *smiles* (ironically wistful) “One day…” (←Sam and Jennifer feel medium mistake and medium OK↔︎humor)

Audience: (bored) (←audience feels medium mistake and large OK↔︎no humor)

Owen watches a clip from a show.

The farmers give the annoying jocks a barrage of insults. (←large mistake)

The jocks are stunned. (←jocks have small OK↔︎no humor)

Owen laughs. (←Owen has large OK↔︎humor)

We can use the same method to think about why some surreal content makes us laugh, but similar non-surreal content doesn’t:

Sam watches Fired Fireman 3 on his laptop. Chief is trying to get the household ready for school, but he’s comically inept at it. As he runs to get the doorbell, he slips on a toy car left on the stairs. Sam loves the slapstick in these things. (←Sam feels humor)

- OR -

Sam watches Jennifer slip on a toy car left on the stairs. His heart wrenches in fear. (←Sam does not feel humor)

While these diverging outcomes happen everywhere in our lives, in spectator entertainment the the priority is on the spectator. This priority shapes how spectator entertainment is put together.

Coherent and surreal. When the audience watches Rage Man punch through concrete in the opening scene of Blood Soul Revenge, they know this is surreal—people can't punch through concrete.

Even though it's surreal, Rage Man's strength can still be coherent if the movie is consistent to its surreal premise.

In the same way things can be physically surreal while still physically coherent, things can be emotionally surreal while still emotionally coherent.

If James Bond is always calm or focused, the emotional premise is that he always has a large OK. We know this is surreal—people aren't calm when gambling, fighting, debating, and so on.

At the same time, if the movie is consistent to these surreal evaluatory premises (James Bond is so capable that his OKs are large, and so experienced that his mistakes are small), it will be emotionally surreal while still be emotionally coherent.

We don't take physical consistency to be the same thing as physical realism. If Rage Man consistently punches through concrete, it's not realistic—it just means its consistent.

For the same reason, we shouldn't take emotional consistency to be the same thing as physical realism. If James Bond is consistently unflappable, that doesn't mean it's realistic—it just means its consistent.

Audiences tend to dislike inconsistency more than surrealism in spectator entertainment. At the same time, we should be careful of the sleight-of-hand switch that something consistent is something realistic.

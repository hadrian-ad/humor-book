The Humor Book

We feel humor family words from spectator entertainment

We feel humor family words from spectator entertainment:

Trevor is watching Star Battles.

Duke Dirtfarmer is flying through the evil valley, pursued by evil spacecraft. Duke has one chance. The music swells. Trevor tenses. Duke launches his proton lancers—and they hit! The evil base is destroyed.

Duke whoops in celebration. His radio crackles with congratulations and cheers. (←Duke feels achievement and relief↔︎humor)

Trevor smiles with relief. (←Trevor feels achievement and relief↔︎humor)

Above, Trevor and Duke "match"—they're both feeling achievement at the same time. This makes it easy for us to think of our humor as humor family words.

But even when things don't match, we can still describe our humor with humor family words:

Xiaowei pirated Squeal IV, and he and Lina are watching in in the dark.

Father Daniel: *condescendingly* “Listen, Rosie. Those things you saw. They aren’t real. You know that, right?” *smiles*

Rosie: “Yes. I know that now. Thank you, father.”

Daniel: “Anything, my child.”

Rosie: *hesitates*

Daniel: “Yes?”

Rosie: “Father, maybe you can tell me something. The man who came last week.” *camera slowly zooms in on Daniel’s face* “The man in black. I need to know something. Was he...”

Daniel: “BLAHH!” *parasite bursts from head*

Lina and Xiaowei both jump.

Lina: (startled) “Fucker!”

Xiaowei: (startled) “Goddamn it!”

Rosie screams as the parasite chitters around the ground and grabs her jeans with its spiky mouth. She kicks it and runs gets into another room, slamming the door and locking the parasite in, sobbing uncontrollably.

Lina and Xiaowei giggle. (←Lina and Xioawei feel safety↔︎humor)

Since Lina and Xiaowei don't match emotions with Rosie, they may be tempted to describe their humor as a feeling of danger (like Rosie is feeling). But they're actually feeling safety!

Consider:

It's not surprising that Lina and Xiaowei might feel safety watching someone pretend to be chased by a fake alien parasite, even at the same time they'd feel danger if they were watching someone actually be chased by a real alien parasite.

We can see this same outcomes of spectator templates elsewhere:

Emma and Owen are watching Blood Soul Revenge. Guys on horses fight guys on motorcycles; guys with swords fight women with scythes; one old guy uses an abacus as his main weapon, and constantly adds up the number of guys he’s killed on it. There’s just so much great stuff! (←Emma and Owen feel generosity↔︎humor)

Zoe is reading Peace is a Gentle Mountain, and the writing is so calm and relaxing. The characters have a hard life, drawing water from wells and caring for livestock, but everything seems so beautiful and reflective. Zoe smiles lightly as she reads. (←Zoe feels comfort↔︎humor)

Sometimes we read about hard work and wonder why it feels good—but Zoe isn't actually doing hard work. She's instead feeling comfort from the book. ("The writing is so calm and relaxing.")

When we think about it, it's not surprising that reading about something has a different emotional outcome than actually experiencing it. They're completely different!

Consider:

We don't read about a feast and then wonder why we're still hungry. Even so, we sometimes read about something terrible and then wonder why we're laughing!

But in the same way reading about a feast isn't eating, reading about something terrible isn't suffering through it. It's not surprising these have different emotional reactions

Even so, we sometimes see people ask things like "why does this terrible thing make me laugh?"—when the more accurate question is "why does reading about this terrible thing make me laugh?"

To make this question answerable, we can drop the "terrible" and then replace "laugh" with a humor family words: "Why does reading about this thing make me feel safety (comfort, connection, relief, or so on)?"

Wink and a nod. We're sometimes surprised that so many movies advertised as "scary" aren't really scary—but we're only surprised when we're taking advertising at face value.

So even though Squeal IV sells itself as scary, its appeal to teenagers isn't actually scariness—it’s appeal is that it generates humor. Any scariness is just a means to an ends: of feeling safety.

We know this for all kinds of reasons. If scariness alone was appealing, then children scared to their core by adult movies would enjoy the experience—but they don’t. Similarly, if goriness alone was appealing, then adults would love watching gory war footage—but they don't.

That’s because neither scariness nor gore is the appeal of scary movies—the appeal is humor. This is why so many horror movies are campy and cliche. When humor isn't present, scariness and gore isn't appealing.

If someone doesn't feel humor from jump scares, they'd rather do almost anything than watch movies built around them. Of course, even if they'd never watch them alone, they may watch them with friends if they think the film will allow them to together have emotional experiences of connection, belonging, relief, safety, and so on.

That's the end of this chapter! Next, we'll see how spectator entertainment have their own humor profiles.

Spectator entertainment is often commercialized—which means that there's a priority for maximizing money somewhere in the production chain. Commercialization gives things design priorities that shape how they turn out.

We can understand this with an analogy to hotels, since commercialization shapes how hotels are built. For instance, architects are told that the hotel rooms should:

  • be easy to clean
  • be durable
  • be inoffensive
  • have an orderly and efficient arrangement
  • not be more expensively furnished then necessary
  • reduce theft

(and so on)

This helps us understand part of why hotel rooms look like hotel rooms, and not like someone’s bedroom. In our bedroom, we can put whatever art we like on the walls—since we're not worried someone will steal it or give them a bad review, like we might if decorating a hotel.

Something similar happens to when humor is a goal of something commercialized. The commercialization gives design priorities to things like movies or comedians, where the creators may then try to:

  • appeal to a broad audience
  • be a conventional length
  • be accessible to many people
  • be reproducible
  • fit an advertising model
  • not allow disruptive interaction from the audience
  • represent the distributor or host

(or so on)

This explains in part why a funny clip from a comedy and a funny video from our partner aren't similar. In part, that’s because our partner isn’t shaped by any commercial priorities!

Our partner can focus entirely on us alone, without worrying about whether they'll also appeal to a broad audience or if they'll fit with an advertising model. It's similar to how a residential bedroom can look different than a hotel bedroom—the goals and priorities are different.

Our partner can also activate topics specific to just us, in a way a video designed for hundreds of thousands of people can't.

For instance:

If a movie is trying to reach tens of millions of people, it has design priorities that will screen out niche content.

We won’t see a blockbuster movie that makes a joke about a restaurant in our hometown—let alone about a gift we once got during a birthday dinner there.

However, our partner may activate these topics—which is one reason people can provide things in conversation that we can't get from things with more commercialized design priorities.

We’re often aware of the upsides and downsides of scaling, like for rented rooms. We intuitively know this when thinking about staying in a Bed & Breakfast with three rooms or a resort with 1,000 rooms.

For instance:

The resort may have efficiencies of scale, so their 1,000 rooms can support a water park and commercial kitchens—while a three-room bed and breakfast can’t.

At the same time, the resort will be unable to have an entrance that's cozy, delicate, detailed, distinctive, small, textured, unique, and so on—since there's just too many people coming in and out!

But even though we're used to thinking this way for things like hotels, we sometimes aren't used to thinking this way for spectator entertainment—which is often similar.

A comedic movie for ten million people is very different then a small open mic night for ten listeners—which is itself different than a conversation with our partner.

Consider:

We may wonder why people may go to local comedy open mics. There are many reasons—one of which is that we can get local references or characteristics in ways we simply can't from other spectator entertainment!

The point here isn't to say that commercialization or scalability is bad, since it's not—those things can be good or bad. Instead, the point is to notice the reasons that spectater entertainment is the way it is. This lets us be thoughtful about how these things relate to our day-to-day lives.

For instance:

We may enjoy going to a hotel for a vacation. But it wouldn't make sense to think that because we enjoyed it hotel rooms are how all bedrooms everywhere should be.

It's the same for comedic books or movies. We may enjoy them—but at the same time, it's not something that defines how all moments of humor should be everywhere.

By default, our emotional system is mutually reactive to all participants. However, when just a few members of an emotional system are prioritized, it becomes spectator entertainment.

  • Spectator entertainment is surreal. We often think of comedies as mirrors of real life—but they're not.
  • Actors, spectators, and characters can all have different evaluations. In spectator entertainment, the goal is foremost on the spectators.
  • Surreal things often scramble our usual associations between content and humor family words—we can feel comfort by watching others feel hardship. However, we're still feeling comfort.

OKs have a size

We also evaluate the size of the OK, similar to how we measured a mistake.

A more precise word for "OK" is secure from negative personal consequences. The more secure we feel from negative personal consequences, the larger our OK. The less secure we feel from negative personal consequences, the smaller our OK.

Like mistakes, we can intuitively sort comparable situations by the magnitude of their OK: 

Ordering OK by size

Justin flips over his handlebars while mountain biking…

A. … and he’s sixteen years old. (←large OK)

B. … and he’s thirty-two years old. (←less large OK)

C. … and he’s fifty-eight years old. (←smaller OK)

D. … and he’s eighty-seven years old. (←even smaller OK)

Trevor drives on the highway…

… in a video game. (←large OK)

… on a clear day with low traffic. (←less large OK)

… during a heavy thunderstorm. (←smaller OK)

... while there’s gunfire nearby. (←even smaller OK)

Yasmin wants to quit…

… and has a trust fund. (←large OK)

… and has lots of savings. (←smaller OK)

… and has some savings. (←even smaller OK)

… and has no savings. (←smallest OK)

Zoe is painting…

… but she’s wearing her expendable painting shirt. (←large OK)

… but she’s wearing an old tee. (←less large OK)

… but she’s wearing something she likes. (←smaller OK)

… but she’s wearing her favorite white top. (←even smaller OK) 

We can use longer examples to see comparative sizes of OK:

Yasmin takes Trevor to meet her family for the first time.

Trevor’s leg bounces anxiously the entire time. (←small OK from a stressful social setting) 

But Yasmin is very comfortable and relaxed with her mother and sisters in her old house. (←large OK from a comfortable social setting)

Sam and Jennifer are trying to get Riley ready for preschool, but are running behind. As Jennifer runs to get the doorbell, Sam watches her slip on a toy car left on the stairs. Sam’s heart wrenches in fear. (←small OK due to reality)

- OR -

Sam watches Fired Fireman 3 on his laptop. The ex-fire chief is trying to get the household ready for school, but he’s comically inept at it. As Chief runs to get the doorbell, he slips on a toy car left on the stairs. Sam loves the slapstick in these movies. (←large OK due to surrealness)

Middle school Zoe has a huge crush on Ryan from the boy’s track team.  She keeps trying to start a text conversation with him, until he finally gets annoyed. “Please stop texting me,” Ryan finally writes her.

Zoe’s grin fades and she puts down her phone and curls over her legs from shame. (←small OK due to immediacy of event)

- LATER -

Six months later, Zoe thinks back and her ears tinge red. (←moderate OK due to distance from event)

- LATER -

Eight years later, Zoe thinks back to when she kept texting what’s-his-face. That was a long time ago! (←large OK due to distance from event)

By itself, the size of someone’s OK doesn’t tell us much about the intensity of humor.

If we’re very wealthy, we’d have a large OK when pay for parking—but that doesn’t mean we’d feel high-intensity humor every time we spend some money on parking.

The intensity of humor is determined by the comparative size of both the mistake and OK. We’ll be talking about this next. 

Self-reference. We use ourselves as the reference point when thinking about the size of our OK. Our OK isn't a measure of our perception of general consequences—it's a measure of our perception of personal consequences.

When someone describes an accidental injury that happened at their work, we may have a large OK. This doesn't mean we see this injury as good—it just means we personally feel secure from negative consequences. We're the reference point for the OK. 

Sometimes we identify so much with someone else that risk of negative consequences to them feels like risk of negative consequences to us. That's why our OK would become much smaller if we realize the accident at work happened to our sibling, and not to a stranger.

But even in this case, we’d still be using ourselves as the reference point for how secure we feel from negative personal consequences.

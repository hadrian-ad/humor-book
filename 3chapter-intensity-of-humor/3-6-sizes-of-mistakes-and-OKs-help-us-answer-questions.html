Sizes of mistakes and OKs help us answer questions

Once we add sizes of mistakes and OKs to our model of humor, we're ready tangibly answer some questions that otherwise may seem abstract or hard to answer:  

Why does someone stressed not always laugh when everyone else does?

Emma is stressed about her final coding project and is hunched over her laptop. Something happens across the room, which makes Zoe and Owen laugh—but it just makes Emma shake her head.

A group that has different sizes of OK won’t react the same way to similar mistakes—since relaxed people will have a large OK, while a stressed person will have a small OK:

Stressed people may feel humor from things that seem minor to us. That’s because these small mistakes can match their small OK:

Emma: (stressed) *giggles* “I didn’t even button the last button on my shirt!” *laughs* (←small mistake matches small)

Zoe: (concerned) “Is everything okay, Emma?” (←small mistake seems trivial to someone with large OK)

When non-stressed people have a large OK, they often want to pursue large mistake activities—which can be emotionally difficult for people with small OKs. This is one reason why people who are depressed, stressed, worried, and so on don’t always enjoy being around people who aren’t: 

Owen: “Think fast, Zo!” *flicks a sunflower seed at her*

Zoe: “Return fire! Fire the catapults!” *loads spoon with grape* (←large mistakes are fun with people with large OKs)

Emma: (stressed) “Would you guys please STOP it?!” (←large mistakes are stressful with people with low OKs)

Zoe: (earnestly) “Did you hear about the Fail-a-Thon event this evening? You get on stage and people watch you fail at hard things! It sounds like fun! Want to be my partner?” (←Zoe feels this event would have a large mistake, large OK)

Emma: (stressed) *clutches head* "No! God." (←Emma feels this event would have a large mistake, small OK)

Why do kids like breaking the rules?

Sam: “While mom’s at the hospital tonight, I thought we’d break the rules a bit! How about we get pizza and eat it in front of the TV?”

Riley jumps with excitement. As they sit on the floor with pizza and watch one of her shows, she keeps saying “Isn’t this fun?”

 

Kids often feel humor at the things that break rules and routines, like:

  • eating while watching videos
  • going to sleepovers
  • going on field trips
  • helping bake
  • making loud noises
  • skipping school
  • sleeping in a tent
  • wearing costumes

(and so on)

These are large mistakes—they deviate from their expectations. When their guardians also cue that these mistakes are OK, children feel humor. By contrast, it’s very stressful for children when breaking rules doesn’t feel OK. 

Adults have a similar relationship to rules. We laugh and have fun when we feel the mistakes of breaking the rules are OK. This is true for all kinds of things, like:

  • camping
  • clubbing
  • eating at an expensive restaurant
  • trying new sexual activities
  • rioting
  • setting things on fire
  • shooting weapons
  • splurging
  • traveling
  • using drugs
  • watching violence

(and so on)

Like kids, when the mistake is accommodated by our sense of security from negative personal consequences, we enjoy these activies—we feel humor. When the mistake is much larger than our sense of security from negative personal activities, we don't enjoy these activies—we don't feel humor.

Why do we sometimes laugh harder when we try to get serious?

Zoe, Owen, and Emma are working on a group project, but they can’t stop laughing and getting distracted. Owen wants to make progress.

Owen: “Okay for real, we have to get serious.”

Everyone agrees and they all sober up and nod.

Owen flips open his textbook, which causes Emma and Zoe to burst into laughter.

Sometimes, too much effort to “get serious” can cause what it tries to prevent—since it shrinks the OK to the point where small mistakes will match a small OK, generating humor. This is like how the timing or manner of opening a textbook makes Emma, Owen, and Zoe all laugh.

A better way to get serious is to not worry about eliminating humor. A medium OK will let small mistakes go by them without causing them humor. This is the same way we avoid humor while attending a funeral; winning a no-smiling contest; hearing a confession; running a somber tabletop roleplaying scene; and so on.

Why are taboo jokes funny to close friends, but not to strangers?

Amaya tells Minh a taboo joke, which shocks him and makes him laugh. He wonders why it’s funny to him, since he feels most people would be disgusted by it. Like, why could she tell that joke to him, but not someone on the bus?

When we’re with close friends, we might have an OK that’s large enough to absorb big mistakes—like comments that have forbidden content. But when we’re around strangers, we often have a much smaller OK, which makes the mistake of the forbidden content oversize the OK.

We can continue subdividing this table into smaller and smaller increments. But no matter how many increments we use, humor is felt most intensely the closer the sizes of mistakes and OKs are to 1:1. 

We can turn this into a quantification metaphor by using a 1-10 scale for the size of mistakes and OKs. This lets us plot humor: 

We can notate our previous examples and place them on this chart:

A. Medium intensity humor

Emma realizes she has to look after two dogs, instead of one. (←size 4 mistake)

Emma feels this probably isn’t an issue, and even if it is, the consequences aren’t immediately felt to be important. (←size 6 OK)

Size 4 mistake and size 6 OK

B. High intensity humor

Emma watches the scene in Fired Fireman 3 where Chief realizes he has to look after huge household. (←size 8 mistake)

Emma knows this is a comedy series, knows how it ends, knows the rules of the fake world, understands the cues from the editing and acting—there’s no consequences. (←size 8 OK)

Unlike Emma, Zoe feels the scene is a bit embarrassing too. (←size 7 OK)

Size 8 mistake and size 8/7 OK

In the example above, Emma and Zoe both felt high intensity humor. But while Emma felt a 1:1 ratio of mistake to OK, Zoe also felt some embarrassment too—her evaluations were a bit tilted toward the mistake corner at an 8:7 ratio of mistake to OK.

C. Low-intensity humor

Emma watches the first Fired Fireman, where Chief realizes that, instead of just one dog, he has to look after two. (←size 2 mistake)

Emma knows this is a comedy series, knows how it ends, knows the rules of the fake world, including that Chief is ultimately up to it—there’s no consequences. (←size 8 OK)

Size 2 mistake and size 8 OK

D. No humor

 

Emma thinks she has to look after an entire family for three days. (←size 9 mistake)

Emma feels she was tricked or coerced into responsibility, and in way that was destructively burdensome and violatory. (←size 2 OK)

Size 9 mistake and size 2 OK

Consider:

The numbers in these examples are arbitrary—there aren’t actually units of mistakes and OKs. Instead, we're using a quantification metaphor to help us see how humor's intensity is coherent, not random.

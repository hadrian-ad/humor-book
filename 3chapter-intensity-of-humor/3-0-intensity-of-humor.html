3. Intensity of Humor

In this chapter, we’ll be looking at the intensity of humor: why we sometimes feel a large amount of humor, and other times a small amount of humor:

Minh looks at a row of people on the bus, each of who is staring at their phone.

One has a wide smile of delight. (←high-intensity humor)

One has a quiet smile. (←low-intensity humor)

One is not smiling at all. (←no humor)

Owen: *telling joke* “… and that’s why they always called him… Moo-shroom!”

Emma: "Psh." (←low-intensity humor)

Zoe: "Haha!" (←high-intensity humor)

Trevor and Yasmin are playing a board game. Yasmin is about to win until she has a catastrophically bad turn.

Trevor: *shouts* “My halfling runs you down. I win!”

Tears rundown their face as they howl with laughter. (←high-intensity humor)

Then they settle into giggles. (←low-intensity humor)

Finally, they just feel tired. (←no humor)

From the backseat, Riley imitates one of her mom’s reactions to bad drivers: "doosh bag!"

Sam thinks this is really funny and laughs. (←high-intensity humor)

Riley, delighted, says it again. It’s not as funny the second time. (←low-intensity humor)

Riley keeps going, which starts to grate on Sam. "Okay, Riley," he says. (← no humor)

When he was in high school, Brad watched the classic comedy Asian Supercommando and thought it was hilarious. (←high-intensity humor)

Thirty years later, he watches it with his daughters. The whole experience is uncomfortable, and at best moderately funny. (←medium-intensity humor) Zoe gives him a long lecture about how imperialism led to racist exotification of exploited cultures.

Why is humor sometimes high-intensity, and other times low-intensity? To talk about this, we’ll see mistakes and OKs as having a size to them.

The intensity of humor depends on the size of mistakes and OKs. We’ll be talking about how this works throughout the rest of this chapter.

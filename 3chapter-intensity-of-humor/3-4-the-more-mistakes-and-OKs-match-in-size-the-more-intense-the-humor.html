Examples of balanced mistakes and OKs

When the size of mistakes and OKs balance, we feel high-intensity humor. When the sizes don’t balance, we feel low-intensity humor: 

Sizes of mistakes and OKs balance

Emma promised Jennifer she’d look after her dog while she and Amber go to Mexico. Emma opens the door, and a big dog jump up at her.

Emma: “Hey boy!”

Then another dog jumps around the corner, wagging its tail.

Emma: *laughs* (←high-intensity humor) “What the hell, Jennifer? I thought you said there would just be one!"

Emma evaluates the mistake and OK as both relatively small. A balanced size of the mistake and OK generates relatively intense humor.

Sizes of mistakes and OKs don’t balance

Emma and Owen are watching the first Fired Fireman, which has a hectic and fast-paced opening act.

Chief is desperate to impress the senator so his fire station can get more funding. But while trying to deliver the senator’s mysterious box he ruins a state visit from the King of Sweden. As he runs away from the Swedish firefighting team, he runs into Victoria, who promises to deliver the senator’s box if he’ll just look after her dog while she calms the King of Sweden.

But when Chief arrives at Victoria’s house—there are TWO dogs, instead of one! “NoOOOO!” Chief yells as the camera pulls back.

Emma: “You know, this part with the dogs always felt flat to me” (←low-intensity humor)

Owen: “Yeah. Let’s just skip to the lobster delivery scene, where the senator turns out to be the main drug dealer!”

Emma laughed when something similar happened to her in her life (two dogs instead of one), since her mistake and OK balanced. But when it happened in a movie, her OK was too large and her mistake too small for her to feel much humor.

Sizes of mistakes and OK balance

Emma sits down on the couch with Zoe and Owen to watch the movie

Emma: "Is this Fired Fireman 3? I love this one!"

Zoe: “Shhh!”

Mrs. Raleigh: *on TV* “Thanks for agreeing to help watch the kids for a week, Chief! And on such short notice. You are such a darling.” *loads fancy luggage onto the taxi*

Chief: “No problem, ma’am! Don’t you worry about a thing. I’ll take good care of the twins!”

Mrs. Raleigh: “Mm? And everyone else too, I hope!”

Chief: (confused) “Everyone else?”

Mrs. Raleigh: “Bye!” *gets into the taxi and drives off*

Chief: “Uh oh.” *turns around to look at the house.*

The camera focuses on a window with the twins staring at him, before jumping to another window with the twins' younger sister staring at him.

The camera jumps to the second story window with a scowling emo teen, and then jumps again to the attic roof where the triplets are staring and six tiny yipping dogs are bouncing as they bark.

Chief: *falls to his knees in horror* *camera pulls away as he looks at the sky* “OhhH NOooo!"

Zoe: *facepalms* *laughs*

Emma: *laughs* (←high-intensity humor)

Emma sees the mistake and OK as matching, leading to relatively intense humor.

  • Mistake. Emma was led to expect one thing would happen (there would be just twins), but something else happened (there was a huge family)—so what happened deviated from her expectations. The size of this mistake (large) was cued to Emma by the overly-dramatic acting, camera work, editing, music, and so on.
  • OK. Emma saw this as having a large OK because she knows it’s just a movie. Since it’s surreal, there’s no risk of personal consequences to her at all. The movie encouraged the size of this OK (large) through the acting, franchise, genre, zaniness, and so on. 

While Emma’s evaluations were balanced, Zoe’s were slightly unevenly balanced—since Zoe felt some embarrassment as well as humor. (*facepalms*)

Sizes of mistakes and OKs don’t balance

Emma promises Jennifer she’ll look after her dog while she and Amber are gone for the weekend. But when Emma opens the door, there are four dogs and three children.

Riley: *running over* “Emma you’re HERE!”

Maddie: *from the kitchen* "I'm hungry, yet's eat!"

Sebastian: *slouching in* *hands in his pockets* "Hey."

Emma is shocked.

Emma: *calling Zoe* *whispering* (furious) “Zoe, I think Jennifer lost her fucking mind.  She didn’t think she needed to tell me I would be babysitting her extended family for a weekend?! You should see the text she sent me just now. 'oh, I thought you knew, would u mind?’ I mean, what the literal fuck?”

Emma is near tears. (←low-intensity humor)

Emma laughed when something like this happened to Chief in Fired Fireman—but when it happens to her she doesn't feel humor, since she feels insecure from negative personal consequences.

Evolving situations. As the situation changes, Emma’s evaluations also change: 

Jennifer: *texts Emma* “sry for the misunderstanding!! Sam was going to take them to their grandparents but he’s delayed till 3. he was supposed to tell you. Can you watch for 30-45 min? we’ll pay $$”

Emma: *reads text* “Phew!” *laughs with relief* (←Emma feels humor) *texts Jennifer* “No problem! Enjoy Mexico!”

Riley: *tugs on Emma* “Can I use your phone to play games?”

When Emma realizes there was just a miscommunication, her OK grows and her mistake shrinks. As her evaluations come more into balance, the intensity of her humor grows.

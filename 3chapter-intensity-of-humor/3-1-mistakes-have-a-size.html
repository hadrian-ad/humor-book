Mistakes have a size

When we evaluate something as a mistake, we also evaluate the size of that mistake.

Consider:

We talked before about how a more precise word for “mistake” is deviation from the expected. We have baseline expectations—the size of the mistake is how much something deviates from those expectations.

We have an intuitive ability sort comparable mistakes by their magnitude—by their "size:"

Ordering mistakes by size

Base. Someone spills nothing on Jessica’s shirt. (←no mistake↔︎expectations)

A. Someone spills water on Jessica’s shirt. (←mistake)

B. Someone spills coffee on Jessica’s shirt. (←larger mistake)

C. Someone spills paint on Jessica’s shirt. (←even larger mistake)

Stephen runs over nothing. (←no mistake↔︎expectations)

Stehpen runs over a squirrel. (←mistake)

Stephen runs over his neighbor's dog. (←larger mistake)

Stephen runs over his neighbor. (←even larger mistake)

Mistakes can go in any direction of our expectations:

Zoe gets a 150% on her essay. (←even larger mistake) mistake↔︎expectations)

Zoe gets a 120% on her essay. (←larger mistake)

Zoe gets a 105% on her essay. (←mistake)

Zoe gets a 100% on her essay. (←expectaions↔︎no mistake)

Zoe gets a 95% on her essay. (←mistake)

Zoe gets a 80% on her essay. (←larger mistake)

Zoe gets a 50% on her essay. (←even larger mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets three sixes. (←larger mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets two sixes. (← mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets all different numbers. (←expectaions↔︎no mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets two ones. (←mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets three ones. (←larger mistake)

All these expectations and evaluations are subjective. While Zoe may expect to get full points, her expectations are informal—they aren't necessarily accurate to her past experiences or even her thoughtful opinion.

By itself, the size of a mistake doesn't tell us about the intensity of humor.

For instance:

If Stephen accidentally runs over his neighbor, it would be a large mistake—but that doesn't mean he would feel intense humor!

To predict the intensity of humor we'll also need to think about the size of the OK. We'll look at this next!

At first, it might seem that "good" and "bad" matter to the size of a mistake—but they don't. The size of a mistake is just how much something deviates from our expectations.

So if Zoe gets a 50% or a 150% on her essay, it doesn't matter if Zoe thinks one mistake is unexpectedly bad and the other unexpectedly good. The size of mistake is just how unexpected something is.

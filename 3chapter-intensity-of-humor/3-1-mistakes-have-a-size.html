Mistakes have a size

When we evaluate something as a mistake, we also evaluate the size of that mistake.

Consider:

We talked before about how a more precise word for “mistake” is deviation from the expected. We have baseline expectations—and the size of the mistake is how much something deviates from those expectations.

We have an intuitive ability sort comparable mistakes by their magnitude—by their "size:"

Base. Someone spills nothing on Jessica’s shirt. (←no mistake↔︎expectations)

A. Someone spills water on Jessica’s shirt. (←mistake)

B. Someone spills coffee on Jessica’s shirt. (←larger mistake)

C. Someone spills paint on Jessica’s shirt. (←even larger mistake)

Stephen runs over nothing. (←no mistake↔︎expectations)

Stephen runs over a squirrel. (←mistake)

Stephen runs over his neighbor's dog. (←larger mistake)

Stephen runs over his neighbor. (←even larger mistake)

Mistakes can go in any direction of our expectations:

Zoe gets a 150% on her essay. (←even larger mistake)

Zoe gets a 120% on her essay. (←larger mistake)

Zoe gets a 105% on her essay. (←mistake)

Zoe gets a 100% on her essay. (←expectations↔︎no mistake)

Zoe gets a 95% on her essay. (←mistake)

Zoe gets a 80% on her essay. (←larger mistake)

Zoe gets a 50% on her essay. (←even larger mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets three sixes. (←larger mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets two sixes. (← mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets all different numbers. (←expectations↔︎no mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets two ones. (←mistake)

Yasmin rolls three dice and gets three ones. (←larger mistake)

By itself, the size of a mistake doesn't tell us about the intensity of humor. If Stephen accidentally runs over his neighbor, it would be a large mistake—but that doesn't mean he would feel intense humor!

To predict the intensity of humor, we'll also need to think about the size of the OK.

Good and bad. The size of a mistake doesn't have anything to do with "good" or "bad." It just has to do with how much something deviates from our expectations.

If we see a squirrel hop in a way we've never seen before, we don't need to think with words like "good" or "bad"—we just need to think about how much it goes against our expectations.

In the same way, when Zoe gets a grade she isn't expecting, we don't need to think with words like "good" or "bad"—we just need to think about how much it deviated from what she was expecting.

The mistake is only related to unexpectedness. Both a low grade and a high grade can lead can both have the same size mistake—not because they're equally good or equally bad, but because they're equally unexpected.

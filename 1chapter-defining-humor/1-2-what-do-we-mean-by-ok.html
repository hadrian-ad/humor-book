The Humor Book

What do we mean by "OK"?

Humor is a mistake that’s evaluated as OK.

In this book, the word OK doesn't refer to something "good"—instead, it refers to someone's personal relation to consequences.

A more formal term for OK would be "secure from negative personal consequences". As we observe things, we pay attention to how those observations will personally affect us.

This isn’t about whether something is good or bad—it's just about our sense of security to negative personal consequences.

For instance:

If we hear a building collapses two thousand miles away from us, we may feel it’s OK—because we feel we are personally secure from negative consequences. This doesn’t mean we’re “okay” that it happened! All our OK here means is that, based on how we understand the situation, the possibility of personal consequences seems remote.

By contrast, if the building next door to us collapsed, or a building collapsed in the city our best friend lived in, we won’t feel personally secure from negative consequences—and it would be not OK.

We’ll be using both terms interchangeably (OK = secure from negative personal consequences). We’ll mostly use “OK,” since it’s shorter and less cumbersome.

We can look at some examples of mistakes being evaluated as OK:

Yasmin realizes her boyfriend has been quiet for an hour. Didn’t he say he was just going to the kitchen for some leftovers? (←mistake) She suddenly gets a bad feeling.

She goes to check on him in the kitchen. Trevor’s at the table with look of extreme concentration, trying to stack grapes. Many are on the floor. Yasmin guffaws. (←mistake is OK) “Trevor!” she says. “What on earth?”

Timothy calls Jennifer.

Timothy: “Jennifer, I know Sam said otherwise, but I checked with the insurance company. They said the treatment is still experimental. They won’t cover it.” (←mistake)

Jennifer is silent for a second. Then she laughs, exhausted. (←mistake is OK) “That’s what I thought. If we hold a fundraiser, I think we should still be able to cover it.”

Elias sees Jessica come in carrying a big box.

Elias: “Jess, be careful, I just bought that! Don’t knock the table…” The table is bumped, and a clay statue falls over. One of the arms shatter. (←mistake)

Elias rushes over and examines the broken statue. “You know… I think I like it this way!” He laughs. (←mistake is OK)

Justin and Heather are mountain biking when Justin flips on a root and tumbles down a small hill. (←mistake) “Oh my god! Justin! Are you okay?” Heather calls, jumping off her bike and scrambling down to the body and bike below.

Justin stirs and sits up. “Oof,” he says, adjusting his helmet shakily. “I’m fine!” They look at each other and start giggling with relief. (←mistake is OK)

Sam walks into the bedroom, tying his tie. “Jen, the kids were eaten by wolves.” (←mistake)

Jennifer smiles while getting dressed. “Har har, I wish.” She laughs. (←mistake is OK)

Damien checks his pocket. “Where’s my wallet? Shit. Maybe I left it at the bus stop!” (←mistake) He gets off at the nearest stop and runs back, panicking.

The wallet is still on the bench, and Damien picks it up and laughs. (←mistake is OK) “Phew! It IS here! How careless could I be?”

Xiaowei is dropping his friend Lina off at their high school for the opening day of her play. “Break a leg!” Xiaowei says. “Also: You look nervous as hell!” Lina gives him the finger on the way out. (←mistake)

Xiaowei laughs and gives the finger back as the door slams. (←mistake is OK) Teasing followed by the bird was practically their secret handshake.

In all these examples, people felt that a mistake was OK. They encountered something that was a deviation from their expectations. When they felt they were personally secure from negative consequences, they reacted with humor.

Chapter 1

Humor Pattern

In this chapter, we'll be introducing the universal pattern of humor we'll be using throughout this book.

Before looking at this pattern, we'll want to first notice all the places we smile or laugh. There's a bewildering variety! We might laugh when we:

  • are given a compliment
  • are insulted
  • are hugged
  • hear a joke
  • receive a gift
  • are kissed
  • bike downhill
  • come to a scenic overlook
  • put clothes on fresh out of the drier
  • realize we've been foolish
  • are pressured successfully
  • feel a gust of wind
  • finish a workout
  • see a fox
  • win a game
  • lose badly
  • enter a beautiful room
  • see something disgusting
  • recall an embarrassing memory

and so on

To make matters more confusing, we sometimes don't smile or laugh at these things, or laugh at their opposites.

For instance:

If we fail, we may laugh—but we may become angry instead. The same is true of its ostensible opposite: if we succeed, we may laugh—but in some cases we may become angry instead.

What's the pattern to all this, that explains the presence or absence of humor in all cases?

We can find an elegant pattern to when and why there's humor—which can be used to think through any instance of smiling and laughing. We'll introduce the basics of this pattern this chapter.

Scope. We sometimes think about humor in terms of comedy. That's mistaking a cart for a horse—a bit like trying to think about eating in terms of restaurants.

If all restaurants disappeared tomorrow, there would still be eating—just like if all jokes and funny movies disappeared tomorrow, there would still be smiling and laughing.

We're sometimes so focused on comedy that we lose track of all the places we smile and laugh—most of which have no relation to comedy. All this can lead to strange explanations of humor dynamics.

In this book, we'll be explaining why we smile and laugh anywhere—which will be inclusive to comedy, but also to everything else as well.

Evaluatory information is shared quickly

We exchange evaluatory information with lightning speed on our emotion network:

Justin brings Heather a gift. He watches her unwrap it and then open the box. Her face falls slightly, confused. (←Heather shares a lot of evaluatory information in a split second)

Justin: (dispirited) "Don't like it?"

Heather: "No, I do. I'm just surprised."

Jessica is seating customers when she glances up. It's Elias! Her face bursts into a smile. (←Jessica shares a lot of evaluatory information in a split second)

Sam comes home.

Sam: *closing door* "Man, what I day, I..."

He sees Jennifer, red-eyed at the table, and Riley sitting uncomfortably on the couch. (←Sam, Jennifer, and Riley exchange a lot of evaluatory information in a single second)

Zoe and Emma are talking and walking down the hallway when Zoe looks through a window and sees Owen sitting in a classroom. They catch each other’s eye.

Zoe: *raises eyebrow*

Owen: (blearily) *smiles*

Zoe: (sympathetic) *smiles*

The window slides out of sight as Zoe and Emma continue walking, their conversation unbroken. (←Zoe and Owen exchange a lot of evaluatory information in a single second)

Emma, Zoe, Owen, Minh, and Trevor are in the library working on a group project for Justin’s class.

Zoe: "Okay." *reads assignment aloud* "‘Imagine Joseph Conrad and Robert Browning are teenagers in an American university and are debating the merits of 21st century Literary Modernism compared to 19th century Literary Exceptionalism. Work together to write a screenplay that includes their positions. Don’t forget: have fun!’” *pause* “Um.”

No one says anything for few seconds. (←everyone exchanges a lot of evaluatory information in a few seconds)

Emma: “Let’s just run it through EssayFreedom and go get ice cream.”

Everyone reacts to the proposal. (←everyone exchanges a lot of evaluatory information in a few seconds)

Above, multiple people can survey each other's emotional state in just a few seconds. This is a huge amount of information, shared very quickly!

Consider:

To see how efficient and dense this exchange of information is, we can compare sitauations—like when Emma, Zoe, Owen, Minh, and Trevor are:

  • all in the same room and spend two seconds to glance at each other (full emotion network)
  • are all in different rooms and spend two seconds to try to text their reaction to each other (constrained emotion network)

In the first case, everyone could convey their own opinion, while also gathering the nuanced stance of everyone involved, in two seconds

In the second case, everyone could barely type two words in two seconds—let alone also learn much about everyone else's emotional state.

Constraint. An emotion network is accessed with degrees of fullness. So if we're talking to someone in front of us, we have access to all their emotion tells (full emotion network), but if we're just looking at a static picture of them, we don't (constrained emotion network).

The more constrained an emotion network is, the less evaluatory information it shares. So being around an angry man has the most evaluatory information—seeing a picture of him has less—and just reading a snippet of an article ("he was incensed") has the least evaluatory information.

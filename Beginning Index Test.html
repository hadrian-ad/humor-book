Welcome!

This is a treatise on humor—we'll be looking systematically at the when and why of smiles and laughter. It'll be at least somewhat transformative and very interesting—you'll see.

Humor is a puzzle. We often have an intuitive idea of why we laugh—but the more instances of humor we put together, the fuzzier the big picture can get, and the more incoherent everything can seem.

For instance:

We're not usually surprised if our friend smiles when they see us—but we might be surprised if they unexpectedly smile at someone's pain. How can we explain these both together?

This puzzle get harder because of how close to the chest all this is. We've all been smiling and laughing since before we could talk—humor so foundational and familiar that it's hard to see clearly!

It's a bit like looking at our nose—only, in this case, our nose is also a puzzle that becomes more confusing the more pieces of it we consider at once. There's a dual challenge of having to notice what's right in front of our face at the same time we have to discover how it all fits together.

Consider:

We don't always notice how much we don't notice about humor. We may always notice when someone doubles over with laughter at a joke, but we don't always notice when someone gets a hug and smiles, or hears a good point and laughs.

Like our nose, these smiles and laughs are invisibly normal, and sometimes have to be pointed out before we can notice them. This book is inclusive to all this "humor," giving us a life-accurate sample of smiles and laughs to think about.

We'll be going over plenty of "invisible-visible" things about humor, which for most readers is a strange mixture of surprising and obvious at the same time. Once they're pointed out, we won't be able to unsee them.

This book is written for strong learning outcomes. We'll talk about humor with precision and coherance, using carefully sequenced chapters, thousands of examples, and hundreds of figures. Our goal is to unspool our experiences and find an elegant way of thinking about humor.

By the time you finish, you'll be substantially better at observing, describing, understanding, and producing humor than when you started.

If you're ready, we'll get right to it!

In many ways, humor is one of the most important thing we can learn about. Since our smiles and laughs are so tightly laced into everything, the study of humor ends up being the study of just about everything—the study of humor is the study of how people relate to everything, and why. There's personal competence and satisfaction in this study.

Swords. Humor has a situational edge: something happens when people laugh, like stagnant water starting to move. We'll teasing out what is happening explicitly as we go along.

There's an infamous Bible passage where Jesus says "I have come to bring not peace but the sword."

There's an infamous lecture by Jesus where he says he's come not to bring peace but the sword. An Irish copyist in the middle ages simply shrugged and miscopied gladium (sword) as gaudium (joy). "I have not come to bring peace but joy."

Is this funny? We maybe smiled at the contrast, and felt things. There's a lot to be said about all this.

There's also something interesting to be said about the relation to joy and peace.

If we ignore laughter, we leave out something essential. If we're telling someone a story about how our friend insulted us and leave out that it made us laugh—we wouldn't really be sharing a clear story. The presence or absence of the laugh is the essential pivot of the experience.

This is obvious with a story about insults, but we do something similar all the time—like if we tell someone we built a new road, and leave out whether people laughed or not.

Consider:

We leave out something essential if we tell someone that our best friend insulted us—but don't mention that they made us laugh. The laugh or the absence of a laugh is the essential pivot of the experience.

In other words, studying humor is studying the pivotal aspect of experiences—which happens everwhere, from jokes to hugs to failures to chatting with strangers.

We don't usually learn about humor. It's a bit like telling a story and then not caring about the humor. As we'll see, this often happened (and sometimes still happens) with myths. A myth will describe a laughing deity and it's girdle and weapon, and This is actually happens in our lives too. When we describe a building or road, we don't always think to describe whether people are laughing or not there—some kind of overlook.

We'll be defining how this book uses "humor" more carefully soon, but in brief, there's laughs and smiles—humor is inclusive to the when and why of laughs and smiles.

For instance:

Some people might smile and laugh at the signs below. But why only some people, some of the time?

By the end of this course, it won't be challenging to describe who might laugh when, and why.

This course is designed to provide clarity. As we'll see, there are good ways of talking about and observing humor that just about everyone benefits from learning.

When you're ready, we'll dive right in!

Outcomes. This is a serious course on humor—designed for strong learning outcomes in our ability to observe, describe, understand, and produce humor. The ability to do all this leads to greater competence at making social and design decisions.

For instance, understanding why a stranger would or wouldn't smile helps avoid or connect with them—or helps us design events or spaces that are more likely to make strangers smile with each other.

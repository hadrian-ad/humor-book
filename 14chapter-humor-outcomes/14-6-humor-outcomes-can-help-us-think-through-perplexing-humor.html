The Humor Book

Humor outcomes can help us think through perplexing humor

We can use humor outcomes to explain more perplexing attempts to generate humor:

Heather: *hands on hips* “Xiaowei, when are you going to start looking for a job?”

Xiaowei: *playing a game* (listlessly) “When my shit turns blue.”

Heather: *throws hands up* (←Heather feels Xiaowei is being flippant)

Xiaowei: *laughs* (←mistakes and OKs match↔︎humor)

Why did Xiaowei laugh? In the absence of an explanation, we might rely on explanation-substitute words—like that his humor was:

  • destructive
  • discourteous
  • disrespectful
  • counter-productive
  • facetious
  • flippant
  • immature
  • impolite
  • inappropriate
  • inopportune
  • irreverent
  • needling
  • out-of-line
  • senseless
  • rude
  • tastless

(and so on)

Setting aside whether those words have descriptive merit, they don't really have explanatory merit. They characterize tone, not motivation or purpose.

To work through this situation, we follow an order of operations.

First, we sort out who felt humor, and when.

We’ll see that Heather didn’t feel humor at all. But Xiaowei did—after Heather threw up her hands.

Then, at this point—where there’s humor—we can see if any humor outcomes seem to clearly describe the situation.

We might say Xiaowei:

  • avoided conflict (about how he was spending time)
  • handled a complaint (by shutting down a conversation)
  • revealed a personal view (that he doesn’t want to get a job)
  • supported an argument (that this isn’t a good time for a conversation)
  • eased tensions (with himself)
  • controlled a situation (by ending a conversation)

(and so on)

Xiaowei laughed because he reached or stumbled into humor outcomes, like avoiding conflict or controlling a situation. This puts his behavior into an understandable framing.

Consider:

Describing Xiaowei’s humor with humor outcomes doesn’t mean he was effective; did something well; that others shouldn’t describe him as rude; or so on. Instead, we’re trying to avoid what can sometimes be our first impression: that he was being erratic; that his behavior was inscrutable; that humor is fundamentally inexplicable; or so on.

Heather didn’t feel humor, because she felt Xiaowei created conflict, not avoided conflict. Heather and Xiaowei were desynchronized, and she decided to leave instead of trying to resynchronize.

Topics and humor outcomes. We can use topics to describe a situation in an orderly way. That's because humor outcomes aren't experienced abstractly—a specific topic experiences a humor outcome.

Different topics can generate humor. The means that which humor outcome we use depends on the topic. We can see this with our example with Heather and Xiaowei.

Some of the active topics that generate humor in Xiaowei are:

  • ‘Heather’s reaction’
  • ‘this conversation’
  • ‘Xiaowei’s life’

If Xiaowei had avoided conflict, controlled a situation, and handled a complaint, we can loosely correspond each one to their most contextually relevant topic:

  • ‘Heather’s reaction’→Xiaowei avoided conflict (with his mother)
  • ‘this conversation’→Xiaowei controlled the conversation
  • ‘Xiaowei’s overall life’→Xiaowei handled a complaint (about his life)

While this is more detail than we'll use in this book, this attribution of humor outcomes to specific topics helps us think about how we're describing a situation, and why.

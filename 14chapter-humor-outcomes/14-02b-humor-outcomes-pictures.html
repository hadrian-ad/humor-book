The Humor Book

Humor outcomes add context to the word humor

We don't just have to use one humor outcome to describe humor. We can use as many as helps us understand the humor in context:

Justin and Heather go biking through the cemetery every weekend. They often stop to ponder and enjoy a chipper sign that says “Cremation? Do we have a place FOR YOU!”

Heather: *smiles* “Why do I want that cremation spot so bad now?” (←Heather feels the sign created rapport, deals with taboo topics, opens a new topic of conversation, and so on)

Minh is super stressed about a graphic design project. He can’t pick the right type for his project. But in the hallway, he comes across a student poster.

Poster: “In an age of abundant typography designers strive to be the best. Yet we sometimes forget the importance of self. Take care of yourself and your type will follow.”

It's an odd phrase. Minh laughs, seeing it. (←Minh feels tension has been eased, he’s surviving high stress situation, achieved catharsis, and so on)

Zoe is backpacking in Brazil. She stays at a hostel. As she cooks, she examines the composting sign. She wants to be a good guest, but the instructions are complex. The header clarifies the instructions by saying “think of our compost as vegan.”

It makes Zoe smiles. (←Zoe feels an argument was supported and her tension was eased)

But it makes her backpacking friend huff with annoyance.

Giulia: (annoyed) "Egg shell aren't vegan." (←Guilia does not feel any argument was supported and does not feel her tension was eased)

Sam and Jennifer are taking Riley to the kid-friendly farm. A sign at the goat enclosure says “GOATS HAVE NO MANNERS.”

Sam and Jennifer laugh. (←Sam and Jennifer feel the sign supports an argument)

Riley: (demanding) “What’s so funny?” (←Riley does not feel the sign supports an argument)

The sign with the goat doesn’t only support an argument—it also creates rapport, makes Sam and Jennifer feel accepted, deals with stress, pre-emptively handles complaints, opens a new topic of conversation, acceptably criticizes goats and attendees, symbolizes close ties (to goats and customers), controls a situation, avoids conflict (when giving a warning), and so on.

Consider:

We can use as many humor outcomes as we think are useful. We usually pick one or two for concision and conventional accuracy—since some humor outcomes will seem non-standard or poetic when applied to some situations.

Even so, more humor outcomes help us see emotional situations in new light.

Overlap. Our humor outcomes all overlap with each other. Something like creating rapport is similar to easing tension. These humor outcomes all overlap because they’re all re-describing the same thing: humor!

In specific situations, some humor outcomes will feel poetic or loose, while toher humor outcomes will feel like precise descriptions. We'll usually stick with the precise descriptions for concision and intuitive clarity.

Even so, sometimes using more humor outcomes help us see emotional situations in new light and give greater us greater dimensionality of our thinking of specific moments of humor.

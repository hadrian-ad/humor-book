The Humor Book

We can extend humor outcomes

Instead of saying Zoe smiled because she handled criticism, we can say she handled criticism (about something in particular):

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called If On a Winter’s Night a Traveler. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe feels she handled criticism she had about the title)

We can extend humor outcomes like this whenever we think it would be useful:

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Ferdydurke. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe feels the title supported an argument about what the book will be like)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Cryptonomicon. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe feels title controlled a situation by influencing how she spent her attention)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Why Be Happy When You Could Be Normal? She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe feels the title eased tension about how she relates to herself)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called In a Cold Crater: Intellectual Life in Berlin, 1945-1948. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe felt an argument was supported about the intellectual life of post-war Berlin)

As before, we can use as many humor outcomes as we like:

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called The Big Book of Masturbation: From Angst to Zeal. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe gained a new perspective, dealt with taboo topics, eased tension, supported an argument, created rapport)

So Zoe might gain a new perspective on:

  • masturbation
  • how others think of masturbation
  • approaches to discussing masturbation
  • her history to the subject
  • what the book may be like
  • how books can be titled
  • how she's currently spending her time in the book store
  • what she does and doesn't want to read about
  • the frequency or infrequency she's encountered books on this topic
  • her visual focus as she tunes out other titles

(and so on)

We can then do the same for other humor outcomes that feel relevant—an informal process we can use when trying to understand humor.

Consider:

Zoe doesn't always feel humor when she hears a joke. But she does always feel humor when she gains a new perspective—otherwise she wouldn't have gained a new perspective. This means looking for a joke is less reliable at explaining or predicting humor than is looking for a new perspective.

If we laugh at something like Ferdydurke or In a Cold Crater: Intellectual Life in Berlin, 1945-1948, it's not because those titles are "jokes"—since calling something a joke doesn't explain the presence or absence of humor.

Ties. Our evaluations are the ties we have to the world around us—and humor is a relational expression of these ties. Like wires that have been wound together, these relations can be hard to pick apart and make sense of.

We can use humor outcomes as one method of trying to pull part these bundles of evaluations. They can help us get a better sense of how someone's evaluatory system was composed, and why it changed. ("Since they gained a new perspective, we can think about why it was new for them.")

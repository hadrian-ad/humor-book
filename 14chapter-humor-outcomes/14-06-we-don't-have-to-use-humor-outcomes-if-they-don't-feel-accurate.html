Humor outcomes help us characterize humor

Sometimes, it's easy to "match" humor outcomes to humor.

For instance.

When Zoe laughs at The Big Book of Masturbation: From Angst to Zeal, it's obvious to use a humor outcome like deals with taboo topics.

But other times, humor outcomes don't seem to match intuitively:

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called If On a Winter’s Night a Traveler. She smiles at the title. (←we can use humor outcomes to describe this humor)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Cryptonomicon. She smiles at the title. (←we can use humor outcomes to describe this humor)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Handy Andy. She smiles. (←we can use humor outcomes to describe this humor)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Ferdydurke. She smiles at the title. (←it's not obvious how humor outcomes are relevant to this humor)

Humor outcomes can feel imprecise when they happen around something random, like a book titled Ferdydurke.

For instance:

If Zoe smiles, it's not obviously because the title Ferdydurke is dealing with taboo topics.

But we still can use humor outcomes—we just have to pair them with more specific topics and relationships::

  • instead of saying the title created rapport (generally), we'd say Zoe felt the title created rapport (with her)
  • instead of saying the title controlled the situation (generally), we'd say Zoe felt the title controlled a situation (by influencing how she spent her attention)
  • instead of saying the title supported arguments (generally), we'd say Zoe felt the title supported an argument (about what the book will be like)
  • instead of saying the title gave her new perspectives (generally), we'd say Zoe felt the title gave her new perspective (about how books can be titled)
  • instead of saying the title acceptably criticized (everything), we'd say Zoe felt (she) acceptably criticized (the title) or that (the title) acceptably criticized (other titles)
  • instead of saying the title eased tensions (generally), we'd say Zoe felt (she) eased tension (with herself after encountering something weird)
  • instead of saying the title handled complaints (about itself), we'd say Zoe felt (she) handled (her own) complaint (about the title)

(and so on)

Humor shows an evaluatory action happened in the emotional situation. These evaulatory actions can be hard to put into words, especially when it seems to involve something random, like a random title—but humor outcomes help us pin down with words what is happening when someone feels humor.

Explaining. We often want to explain the humor we feel not by an outcome, but by a genre. So if we laugh looking at a book title, we might explain our laugh by calling the title a joke or satirical. ("We laughed because the title was a joke.") But this isn’t usually the best way to go about explaining humor.

For one, a "joke" is just one of many possible ways of describing a title like Ferdydurke or Wherever You Go, There You Are. For another, we don't always laugh at jokes—so calling something a joke doesn't yet explain why we did or didn't laugh when we saw it.

But while Zoe doesn't always feel humor at every joke every time, Zoe always feels humor when she gains a new perspective—otherwise, she wouldn’t have gained a new perspective. This Is why explaining Zoe's new perspectives explains her humor, while simply applying the word "joke" to things doesn't.

Humor outcomes help us characterize humor

To use humor outcomes effectively, we use the humor outcome as a linking phrase between someone and something.

For instance:

Something doesn't create rapport (generally)—instead, someone feels rapport is created (with something specific).

This is how we use humor outcomes when they don't feel intuitive.

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Ferdydurke. She smiles at the title. (←it feels hard to match Zoe's humor to a humor outcome)

Above, it doesn't feel correct to say a strange book title is supporting an argument (generally), dealing with taboo topic (generally), or so on.

Instead, we have to relate the humor outcome to a person (Zoe), and those humor outcomes to specific elements of the situation:

This is true for any book title where the humor outcome alone doesn't intuitively "match" with a humor outcome:

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Cryptonomicon. She smiles at the title. (←it feels hard to match Zoe's humor to a humor outcome)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Handy Andy. She smiles. (←it feels hard to match Zoe's humor to a humor outcome)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called If On a Winter’s Night a Traveler. She smiles at the title. (←it feels hard to match Zoe's humor to a humor outcome)

We can use humor outcomes in these cases by being more attentive to the specific situation. So:

  • instead of saying a title created rapport (generally), we'd say Zoe felt a title created rapport (with her)
  • instead of saying a title controlled the situation (generally), we'd say Zoe felt a title controlled a situation (by influencing how she spent her attention)
  • instead of saying a title supported arguments (generally), we'd say Zoe felt a title supported an argument (about what the book will be like)
  • instead of saying a title gave her new perspectives (generally), we'd say Zoe felt a title gave her new perspective (about how books can be titled)
  • instead of saying a title acceptably criticized (everything), we'd say Zoe felt (she) acceptably criticized (the title) or that (the title) acceptably criticized (other titles)
  • instead of saying a title eased tensions (generally), we'd say Zoe felt (she) eased tension (with herself after encountering something weird)
  • instead of saying a title handled complaints (about itself), we'd say Zoe felt (she) handled (her own) complaint (about the title)

(and so on)

Above, we're treating a humor outcome like a verb. Someone has to do the verb, and sometimes the verb has to include an object. This lets us use "evalutary verbs" to talk about emotional actions.

Consider:

We're used to describing physical actions in a situation. (She moves across the room.) But we're less used to describing evaluatory actions in a situation. (She creates rapport with the people in the room.)

Humor shows that an evaluatory action is happening. To broaden how we describe these actions, we can use humor outcomes—even when it seems related to something otherwise random, like a nonsense book title.

Explaining. We often use genres to explain the humor we feel, not humor outcomes. So if we laugh looking at a book title, we might explain our laugh by calling the title a joke or satirical. ("We laughed because the title was a joke.") But this isn’t usually the best way to go about explaining humor.

For one, a "joke" is just one of many possible ways of describing a title like Ferdydurke or Wherever You Go, There You Are. For another, we don't always laugh at jokes—so calling something a joke doesn't yet explain why we did or didn't laugh when we saw it.

But while Zoe doesn't always feel humor at every joke every time, Zoe does always feels humor when she gains a new perspective—otherwise, she wouldn’t have gained a new perspective. This is why explaining Zoe's new perspectives explains her humor in a way simply applying the word "joke" to things doesn't.

We can see the same thing with Justin and Heather.

Justin and Heather go biking through the cemetery every weekend. They often stop to ponder and enjoy a chipper sign that says “Cremation? Do we have a place FOR YOU!”

Justin: (musing) “Is it a joke? Tongue-in-cheek? Reverse psychology? Tone deaf? Bad taste? Clever? Bold? Stupid? Engaging? Repulsive?” (←Justin gets lost in description)

Heather: *smiles* “Why do I want that cremation spot so bad now?” (←Heather feels the sign created rapport, deals with taboo topics, opens a new topic of conversation, and so on)

Above, Justin wants to describe something with genre words—all of which sometimes generate humor, and sometimes don't. But it's not "tone deaf" that makes Heather laugh—the actual evaluatory action happening is rapport being created, taboo topics being dealt with, new topics of conversation being opened, and so on. This directly explains the humor, instead of just describing an object.

At this point, we’ve gone over the five interchangeable ways we’ll be describing humor. These are different scales of looking at humor—from the highest scale of our general word “humor,” to the smallest scale of “mistakes and OKs”:

  • Humor as an overall pattern (“humor”)
  • Humor as experiences (humor family words)
  • Humor as intentions or consequences (humor outcomes)
  • Humor as expressive behavior (humor tells)
  • Humor as information-assessments (mistakes and OKs)

These different scales of humor are all happening at the same time. We choose between which scale to use based on what’s useful to us.

This is why any of our examples can be annotated with any combination of mistakes and OKs, humor tells, humor outcomes, humor family words, or just the word humor:

Zoe is browsing the bookstore when she picks up a book on meditation. She reads the book title: Wherever You Go, There You Are.

She thinks about that for a second, puzzled. Then something clicks, and she gives an amused nose-exhale. (←a mistake is OK↔︎laughs↔︎gain a new perspective↔︎insight, realization↔︎humor)

Assessments (mistakes and OKs)↔︎behavior (humor tells)↔︎actions (humor outcomes)↔︎states (humor family word)↔︎overall pattern (humor)

Santiago and Damien are walking through the halls.

Santiago: “Well. We lost every game but two this season. I feel like that’s some kind of achievement!”

Damien: *laughs* “Uh-huh, sure man.” (←mistakes are OK↔︎laughs, brightens, loosening, smiling, focusing on present, raised spirits, social integration↔︎argument was supported, created rapport, eased tension, gained new perspectives, been acceptably criticized, symbolized close ties↔︎acceptance, connection, self-forgiveness, insight, self-reconciliation, release, relief, success↔︎humor)

Assessments (mistakes and OKs)↔︎behavior (humor tells)↔︎actions (humor outcomes)↔︎states (humor family word)↔︎overall pattern (humor)

These are all different ways of describing humor! Throughout this book, we’ll be choosing among these options based on what we think would be helpful to our intuitive understanding. And sometimes, we’ll use multiple scales for a greater range of perspective.

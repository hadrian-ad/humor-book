The Humor Book

Humor outcomes add context to the word humor

Sometimes we use one humor outcome to describe humor:

Sam and Jennifer are taking Riley to the kid-friendly farm. A sign at the goat enclosure says “GOATS HAVE NO MANNERS.”

Sam and Jennifer laugh. (←Sam and Jennifer feel the sign supports an argument)

Riley: (demanding) “What’s so funny?” (←Riley does not feel the sign supports an argument)

But other times, we use many humor outcomes:

The sign with the goat didn't only support an argument—it also creates rapport, (makes Sam and Jennifer) feel accepted, deals with stress, pre-emptively handles complaints, opens a new topic of conversation, acceptably criticizes goats and attendees, symbolizes close ties (to goats and customers), controls a situation, avoids conflict (when giving a warning), and so on.

Consider:

We can use as many humor outcomes as we think are useful. Often, considering several humor outcomes help us see emotional situations in new light.

Overlap. Our humor outcomes all overlap with each other, like how creating rapport ovelaps with easing tension. These humor outcomes all overlap because they’re all re-describing the same thing: humor!

In specific situations, some humor outcomes will feel like poetic or loose descriptions, while other humor outcomes will feel conventional or precise. We'll usually stick with the conventional or precise descriptions for concision and intuitive clarity.

Even so, sometimes using more humor outcomes help us see emotional situations in new light and give greater us greater dimensionality of our thinking of specific moments of humor.

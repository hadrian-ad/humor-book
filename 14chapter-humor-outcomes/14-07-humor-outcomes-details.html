We can give details to humor outcomes

We can often clarify humor outcomes by being more specific about what is happening.

For instance:

Even though handle criticism is a humor outcome, it's not always useful to say someone handles general criticisms about everything—we can instead say they handle a specific criticism about something.

We can clarify humor outcomes with details whenever we think it would be useful:

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called If On a Winter’s Night a Traveler. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe feels she handled criticism she had about the title)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Ferdydurke. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe feels the title supported an argument about what the book will be like)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Cryptonomicon. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe feels title controlled a situation by influencing how she spent her attention)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called Why Be Happy When You Could Be Normal? She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe feels the title eased tension about how she relates to herself)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called In a Cold Crater: Intellectual Life in Berlin, 1945-1948. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe felt an argument was supported about the intellectual life of post-war Berlin)

Zoe is browsing through the bookstore, and finds a book called The Big Book of Masturbation: From Angst to Zeal. She pauses and then smiles. (←Zoe gained a new perspective on how one could discuss a specific topic)

We don't have to limit ourselves to just one clarification—when Zoe encounters The Big Book of Masturbation: From Angst to Zeal, she might gain a new perspective on:

  • masturbation
  • how others think of masturbation
  • approaches to discussing masturbation
  • her history to the subject
  • what the book may be like
  • how books can be titled
  • how well "angst and zeal" fit an "A to Z" format
  • how she's currently spending her time in the book store
  • what she does and doesn't want to read about
  • the frequency or infrequency she's encountered books on this topic
  • her visual focus as she tunes out other titles

(and so on)

We can use as many of these as we think would be useful or accurate—each one helps us look for what deviated from her expectations (mistake) in a way that balanced with her sense of personal security from negative consequences (OK).

Consider:

As before, we can use as many humor outcomes as we like. Zoe may not just gain a new perspective, but also feel taboo topics were dealt with:

So in addition to thinking about why and how Zoe gained a new perspective, we can also look at why and how Zoe felt an argument was supported, rapport was created, and so on.

Explaining. When we laugh at a book title, we sometimes want to explain our humor by describing the title as a joke or as satirical. But this isn't a precise or reliable way of explaining humor.

Zoe doesn't always feel humor when she hears a joke. But she does always feel humor when she gains a new perspective—otherwise she wouldn't have gained a new perspective. This means we get closer at explaining humor by looking for Zoe's new perspective, and why she gained it when looking at a title.

Words like "joke" can feel incomplete as explanations for this reason. Someone may smile at both Ferdydurke and In a Cold Crater: Intellectual Life in Berlin, 1945-1948—but "joke" not feeling like a comprehensively accurate description for either. More importantly, it still doesn't explaining the presence or absence of humor in different situations.

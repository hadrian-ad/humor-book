Our opinion isn't the same thing as a comparison

We sometimes confuse a metric comparison with an overall emotional opinion —but they're not the same thing.

Emma: “How was the Tennis for Beginners event? Should I do it?”

Zoe: *smiles* “I didn’t learn much about tennis." (←a metric comparison of instruction) "But it was kind of fun.” (←an overall opinion)

Zoe grew a moderate humor bush with the Tennis for Beginners event. That means her overall opinion was mostly good. ("Kind of fun.") Since she enjoyed being outside, meeting new people and trying new things, she overall felt:

  • accomplishment (at having tried something new)
  • appreciation (of the outdoors or people she met)
  • connection (with new people)
  • generosity (at spending time at something new)
  • insight (at a subculture or game)
  • release (from undergoing and finishing excercises)

(and so on)

Ostensibly, she was there to learn about tennis. But overall, despite not learning much about tennis, she felt enough humor here and there to have a good overall opinion of the experience.

But having a good overall opinion of the event doesn't mean Zoe loses her ability to make comparative judgements about the event.

Emma: *quizzing* "Did you learn a lot about tennis?" (←Emma asks for a metric comparison)

Zoe: "No."

Emma: "Did it prepare you for more engagement with the sport?" (←Emma asks for metric comparison)

Zoe: "No."

Emma: "Was time used efficiently?" (←Emma asks for a metric comparison)

Zoe: "Good god, no."

Emma: "Was it a good use of money?" (←Emma asks for metric assessment)

Zoe: "No, I would have learned more from a free YouTube video." (←Emma asks for a metric comparison)

Emma: "But how was it, overall?" (←Emma asks for overall opinion)

Zoe: *smiles* "Kind of fun." (←Zoe gives overall opinion)

Emma could have asked Zoe endless numbers of comparative questions. These might be metric comparisons of price, effort, outcome, and so on. But these comparisons are different than our overall opinion of something. Our opinion is how we emotionally experience something, not how we set up comparisons after the fact.

That's because our broadest emotional relation to something is our humor bush.

Consider:

Sometimes these overlap. If Zoe was desperately hoping to learn about tennis, it might have been a large mistake and a small OK to realize it wasn't an effective way to learn tennis, and this made everything else miserable.

In this case, one metric assessment ("learn efficiently about how to play tennis?") would overlap with her overall opinion. ("Bad.") But even though in this case one metric assessment would overlap with her overall opinion, they aren't the same thing.

Romance. We can use a humor bush to think about our partner likes us so much, and vice versa—even neither of us has ever cured a disease, modeled underwear, or won an olympic medal. People still like each other, despite everyone being able to compare everyone to literally everyone else in the world. Why is this?

We already know the answer: metric comparisons are neither here nor there to our overall opinion. Instead, our overall opinion is based on our humor bush with them.

People bond when they mutually and frequently feel intense feelings of acceptance, appreciation, belonging, comfort, connection, delight, forgiveness, generosity, insight, joy, reconciliation, release, safety, welcome, and so on. In other words, they have a large mutual humor bush!

We emotionally orient toward things we have a large humor bushes with—including our partner and friends. This is why a large humor bush is the same thing as having a high opinion of someone.

This process is independent of any metric comparisons we could chose to make—which is why people everywhere love people not for their comparative success but for the positive emotional experiences they have with each other.

This is why people tend to say that "humor" is their most important trait in a partner. (“someone I can laugh with,” “someone who makes me laugh,” “someone to be weird with together,” “someone with a great sense of humor,” and so on) We formost want a humor bush!

We can expand how we think about this using humor family words too. If we say we want to be around "someone who accepts me," "someone I feel safe with," "someone I feel comfortable around," this is also humor focus, since feelings of acceptance, comfort, safety, and so onare also humor.

This positive opinion happens this way with romantic partners—but also friends, coworkers, neighborhoods, apartment complexes, online gaming lobbies, yarn stores, and so on. That's because these positive opinions all happen the same way, through cumulative experiences of humor.

Consider:

We looked at comedy (prioritizing intensity), charm (prioritizing fittedness), and promotion (prioritizing status).

We sometimes see the same thing with romantic partners. Instead prioritizing judgement through fittedness or intensity, someone judges a partner by whether they're status-increasing or not.

We may hear or think statements like “we would look so good together,” “I want someone I can introduce to my parents too” “they're someone who will impress my friends,” and so on.

But when we prioritize increasing a status-metric, we fall into metric-comparisons—which by themselves do not predict good experiences.

People who don't prioritize a mutual humor bush often end up with brittle relationships, where their opinions of each other can quickly sour—since those relationships weren't grounded on a large mutual humor bush.

Our overall opinion of something is the average amount of positive emotions we've felt toward it. We can use a humor bush with something as a proxy for our opinion of it.

  • When presented between two things, we'll have a higher overall opinion of the one that we have a larger humor bush with.
  • Humor helps us understand the subjectivity of opinions. A beautiful walk sometimes makes us smile, and sometimes doesn't. We can use humor to understand what builds up our opinion of the walk.
  • An overall opinion of an experience is objective-neutral. We can have a good experience even when we start comparing it negatively toward alternatives, goals, objectives, and so on.

The Humor Book

Fitted humor makes up our opinion of something

Two math teachers may have identical lesson plans. But even so, that doesn't mean high school students will have the same opinion of the two math teachers.

Instead, the students will have a higher opinion of whichever teacher better generated and prioritized fitted humor. This will be the teacher that:

  • presents the content well, and creates insight
  • pushes students the right amount so that they feel accomplishment
  • validates honest efforts, and helps students feel appreciation
  • structures tests well, and allows for feelings of success
  • decorates a room well, and allows students to feel belonging
  • acknowledges students, and can help them feel welcome
  • allows group bonding, and lets students feel connection

(and so on)

When students think back to how good or bad the class was, they'll think about their humor bush—about whether they frequently felt appreciation, belonging, connection, insight, welcome, and so on.

Consider:

Sometimes it doesn’t seem accurate to say that humor makes up our opinion of teachers—in part because we're so used to thinking of comedy. (Prioritizing intensity.) But it makes more sense when we're able to think about charm. (Prioritizing fittedness.)

Sometimes our favorite teachers were the cool, funny, or outrageous ones—but more specifically, we liked best the teachers who best helped us experience humor family words, like achievement, delight, generosity, insight, welcome, and so on. Their ability to do this may or may not have overlapped with our stereopical ideas of being cool, funny, outrageous, and so on.

If a class rarely feels fitted humor, they’ll rarely feel insight, accomplishment, appreciation, success, belonging, welcome, connection, and so on—and the lack of humor family words gives students a negative opinion of the course.

We have a good opinion of a class for the same reason we’d have a good opinion of a city—we have a large humor bush with them.

Orientation. When humor is above the humor dress code, we see humor as efficient and as building cohesion. This means our humor bushes are our overall sense of someone or something's ability to modulate their actions in a way that is effective and builds group cohesion.

Our emotional orientation is toward this fitted humor. That's because all things being equal, we prefer something that makes us feel more accomplishment then less—more acceptance than less—more insight than less—and so on. This orientation toward fitted humor (efficient and cohesive humor) is what drives our personal opinion of something.

If one teacher makes us feel more acceptance, accomplishment, and insight than another teacher, we'll have a better opinion of them. Our overall opinion is how much alignment we feel with this emotional orientation.

The Humor Book

A good opinion does not mean something was helpful toward a goal

Usually, when we give an opinion of something, there are two parts: there's the evaluation of our humor bush, which is our overall opinion. But there's also usually a metric comparison too, which relates an experience to some goal or priority.

Justin: "How was the wine tasting?"

Heather: *smiles* “A bit pricey." (←a metric comparison of cost) "But great!" (←an overall opinion)

Zoe: “You took Cod: The Rise of the International Fish with Professor Schmidt, right? Worth it?”

Owen: *smiles* “I love Professor Schmidt! That course was way more interesting than it had any right to be." (←an overall opinion) "Don’t know when I’ll use that knowledge, but now I know all about Basque fishermen." (←a metric comparison)

Dave: “What did you think of Chairs?”

Justin: “Honestly, it was pretty dry." (←an overall opinion) "Tremendous scholarship though, just tremendous.” (←a metric comparison of utility)

Justin is reading evaluations.

Anonymous: “I liked the course." (←an overall opinion) "but we spent too much on Browning honestly” (←a metric comparison of syllabuses)

Emma: “How was the Tennis for Beginners event? Should I do it?”

Zoe: *smiles* “I didn’t learn much about tennis." (←a metric comparison of instruction) "But it was kind of fun.” (←an overall opinion)

If we feel fitted humor toward something, we’ll think of it positively—but that’s different than seeing it as effective toward a particular goal.

Kids movies. If we loved a movie as kids, we’ll have a good opinion of it as an adult. We'll smile when we think of it—because we still have a large humor bush with it.

If someone tells us it wasn’t very good and that there are better kids movies available today (metric comparisons), we’d probably agree—but our overall opinion is of our humor bush with the movie, not a metric comparison. In that sense, current movies are neither here nor there to our opinion.

Similarly, if Zoe prefers Pittsburgh to Seattle, and Emma points out that Pittsburgh is poorer and smaller that Seattle (metric comparisons), Zoe would probably agree—but her overall opinion of Pittsburgh is her humor bush. Comparative metrics about wealth or size has nothing to do with her overall opinion, which is based on how often and intensely she feels humor family words on the topic of 'Pittsburgh.'

We see this split all the time. Someone will bring up a comparable metric (movie quality, wealth), but these comparisons aren’t always relevant to our overall opinion of something.

Consider:

Sometimes we think our opinion should be based on metric comparisons, like comparative quality or comparative wealth. In this view, we'd think that everyone, everywhere, should prefer Seattle to Pittsburgh because of a few comparative qualities.

Setting aside whether that's aspirational or not, we can start by noticing that, functionally, that's not how we make opinions. Our opinions come from our collective humor with things.

Our opinion isn't the same thing as a comparison

We sometimes confuse a metric comparison with an overall emotional opinion ("humor bush")—but they're not the same thing.

Consider:

This is obvious in many cases. Let's say we're celebrating our birthday with all our favorite people, when someone asks us if we'd be making more money if we were at work than at our party. ("metric comparison")

Whether we say yes or no has no bearing on our overall opinion of our party. Even though metric comparisons of good or bad can sometimes overlap with our overall emotional opinion, they're not the same thing.

Zoe grew a moderate humor bush with the Tennis for Beginners event. That means her overall opinion was mostly good. ("Kind of fun.") Since she enjoyed being outside, meeting new people and trying new things, she overall felt:

  • accomplishment (at having tried something new)
  • appreciation (of the outdoors or people she met)
  • connection (with new people)
  • generosity (at spending time at something new)
  • insight (at a subculture or game)
  • release (from undergoing and finishing excercises)

(and so on)

Consider:

Sometimes, this humor has nothing to do with the ostensible goal what we're doing. Zoe may have found the instruction bad (felt no humor), while at the same time found the instructor so endearingly odd and strange (felt humor) that Zoe has a positive overall opinion of event.

Just because Zoe has an good overall opinion of the event doesn't mean she can't answer yes or no to questions about how it compares to other things, or how it relates to various goals.

Emma: *quizzing* "Did you learn a lot about tennis?" (←Emma asks for a metric comparison)

Zoe: "No."

Emma: "Did it prepare you for more engagement with the sport?" (←Emma asks for metric comparison)

Zoe: "No."

Emma: "Was time used efficiently?" (←Emma asks for a metric comparison)

Zoe: "Good god, no."

Emma: "Was it a good use of money?" (←Emma asks for metric assessment)

Zoe: "I would have learned more from a free YouTube video." (←Emma asks for a metric comparison)

Emma: "But how was it, overall?" (←Emma asks for overall opinion)

Zoe: *smiles* "Kind of fun." (←Zoe gives overall opinion)

There are endless numbers of questions Emma could ask Zoe. These might be metric comparisons of price, effort, outcome, and so on. But these are different than our overall opinion of something—which was how we emotionally experienced something.

It's our humor bush that conveys our broadest emotional relation to something.

Romance. We can use a humor bush to think about why like our partner so much. People bond when they mutually and frequently feel intense feelings of acceptance, appreciation, belonging, comfort, connection, delight, forgiveness, generosity, insight, joy, reconciliation, release, safety, welcome, and so on.

In other words, they have a large mutual humor bush! As we've seen this chapter, this is the same thing as having a very high opinion of each other. We emotionally orient toward things with large humor bushes—including our partner.

This is independent of any metric comparisons. We could ask how much our partner did or didn't inherent from their parents; whether they're as attractive as a statue; if they have a PhD or a bestselling book; won Olympic models; and so on.

These comparative metrics of them aren't the same thing as our overall emotional opinion of them—which is what would actually make up our opinion of our partner.

This is why people tend to say that "humor" is their most important trait in a partner: “someone I can laugh with,” “someone who makes me laugh,” “someone to be weird with together,” “someone with a great sense of humor.” We want a humor bush foremost!

We can use humor family words to expand how we think about this too. If someone says they want "someone who accepts me," "someone I feel safe with," "someone I feel comfortable around," we can also see this as a humor focus, since when they feel acceptance, comfort, safety, and so on they feel humor.

When people can mutual build a humor bush, they emotionally orient toward each other, bond, and have a high opinion of each other—whether or not they've won Olympic models.

Consider:

We looked at comedy (prioritizing intensity) and charm (prioritizing fittedness) and last chapter we looked at promotion (prioritizing status).

We sometimes see the same thing with romantic partners. Instead of judging things as good or bad through fittedness or intensity, they judge by whether it's status-increasing or not.

They may say things like “we would look so good together,” “someone I can introduce to my parents” “someone that will impress my friends,” and so on.

What we want foremost is to build a mutual humor bush with someone. If we're not prioritizing humor, we often end up with brittle relationships where people's opinions of each other can quickly sour—since it wasn't grounded on a large mutual humor bush.

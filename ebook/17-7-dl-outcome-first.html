A deeper look Outcome-first

We can answer the question "how do I generate humor?" by cycling through humor outcomes:

  • “How do I generate humor? By supporting an argument”
  • "How do I generate humor? By revealing personal views"
  • "How do I generate humor? By helping someone become accepted"

(and so on)

This lets us take an abstract question ("how do I generate humor?") and downsize it into something tangible—since we already know how to support arguments and reveal personal views.

Loosely, we have two different approaches when thinking about generating humor:

  • An emotion-first approach, which treats a humor outcome as a consequence of humor. ("If they laugh, they'll reach a humor outcome")
  • An outcome-first approach, which treats humor as a consequence of a humor outcome. ("If they reach a humor outcome, they'll laugh")

These two approaches let us talk about the same thing in different ways:

  • Emotion-first: “If he laughs, I’ll have handled his criticism”
  • Outcome-first: “If I handle his criticism, he’ll laugh”
  • Emotion-first: “If I feel humor, I’ll have dealt with a taboo topic”
  • Outcome-first: “If I deal with a taboo topic, I’ll feel humor”
  • Emotion-first: “If they feel humor, I’ll have eased their tensions.”
  • Outcome-first: “If they ease their tensions, they’ll feel humor.”

Essentially, this is like solving X = Y—or, in this case, X = humor = Y. We can solve the same equation by approaching either end!

If we thought we could only solve for X, we'd limit ourselves! When we realize we can also solve for Y, we'll have more flexibility in how we think about different situations.

For instance:

If we're welcoming a new person to our team, we know we'll want them to smile or laugh. If we frame our question as "how can we make them smile or laugh" (emotion-first), we'll call to mind things like jokes, funny voices, goofy icebreakers, and so on.

Alternatively, if we frame our question as "how can we symbolize close ties" (outcome-first), this calls to mind a different set of things—maybe a thoughtful gift, individualized time, hospitality, displays of equality, and so on.

By reframing the question to center around humor outcomes, we can think beyond our usual associations and think of other things that produce smiles and laughs when done successfully.

The point of all this isn't that there are "two ways" of going about generating humor—the point is to find new perspectives on how we go about taking emotional actions in our lives.

It's also a way of finding synergies from a network of skills. Since all humor outcomes are connected, improving at supporting arguments, revealing personal views, and so on improves our skill at humor—and improving our skill at humor improves our ability to support arguments, reveal personal views, and so on.

In this sense, a rising tide lifts all boats—improving at humor makes us better at all these different humor outcomes! This is especially true as we gain greater awareness to when and where and why humor happens.

That's the end of this chapter!

Chapter summary

Humor has triggering conditions. We can think about these conditions narratively with humor outcomes, including using a spatial metaphor to think about how a situation does or doesn't develop toward these triggering conditions.

  • Our evaluations are shaped by the direct and indirect participation of everyone. That means that everyone has a role in whether or not humor outcomes are reached, and on what topics.
  • We can generate humor by simply focusing on reaching a humor outcomes—whether or not this strikes us as stereotypically "funny."
  • Humor outcomes are something a situation develops toward. This means we sometimes have to take time working toward them. It's sometimes helpful to think of humor as more as a conclusion or destination than as a beginning.

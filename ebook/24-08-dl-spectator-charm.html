But in the same way we can smile at a character getting hit by a bus without that meaning the same thing would make us smile in real life—we can smile at a charming spectator character ("utter confidence") without thinking that same thing would make us smile in real life.

This is why we shouldn't straightforwardly aspire to mimicking spectator entertainment in our day-to-day lives:

Zoe watches Gone with the Wind.

Scarett is upset after a rejection. Rhett, who had been eavesdropping, emerges to mock her. He's dashing and absolutely charming. Scarlett ends the scene in tears.

Zoe smiles at Rhett's easy confidence.

When Rhett talks to Scarlett, the audience feels he's charming—but the character (Scarlett) feels he's repulsive and infuriating. We wouldn't want to be exactly like Rhett in real life—we'd be seen as repulsive and infuriating!

This split happens because we feel Rhett controls the situation, handles criticisms, and so on—but Scarlett doesn't feel this. We take this split in stride because it's spectator entertainment.

So while Rhett may be an example of how characters can be seen as charming—he's not a great example of how we can be seen as charming.

We don't have to be charming like spectator characters—we have to be charming like the real charming people we know. Similarly, we won't find the characters we like in real life—because they're characters, not people!

By broading our idea of the word "charm," we can see how taking walks or welcoming old friends is part of a charming life as well. It's the charming life we can be a part of and have in real life too, and not simply charm that we observe and not have in real life.

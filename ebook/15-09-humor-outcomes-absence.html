Humor outcomes can describe the absence of humor

Humor outcomes can be used to describe both the presence and absence of humor in different situations:

Above, Minh and Owen feel humor at the same thing for different reasons. Minh feels the poster eases the tension (he has about decision-making), while Owen feels the poster creates rapport (by being empathetic).

Consider:

At base, humor outcomes are narrative descriptions of mistakes and OKs balancing. So Owen won't feel any empathetic poster creates rapport, just because it's empathetic—he only feels rapport if his mistakes and OKs balance.

If university hallways are usually sterile and cold, empathetic posters from fellow students will both deviate from his expectations (mistake) and at the same time feel secure from negative personal consequences (OK).

By contrast, if Owen walks through a kindergarten every day, he'll expect empathetic posters (small mistake)—and he'll feel posters for young children have little negative consequence to him (large OK).

In this case, Owen won't feel an empathetic poster in a kindergarten creates rapport, simply because it's empathetic.

Ties. As we evaluate the world around us, we become entwined to those things—a bit like plants taking root. We become embedded in an evaluatory context.

Humor outcomes can help us make some narrative sense of all this—helping us sort through our active, fluid, and always-present evaluatory ties to things.

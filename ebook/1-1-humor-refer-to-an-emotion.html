We'll pick just one meaning of humor

Humor is a broad word in our day-to-day conversations, and we don't always use it to mean an emotion. At different times, we use the word "humor" to refer to a kind of:

  • comment (compliment, insult, humor, question, and so on)
  • genre (drama, horror, humor, thriller, and so on)
  • personality trait (gentleness, humor, temper, thoughtfulness, and so on)
  • quality (aggressive, beauty, humor, meanness, persuasiveness, and so on)
  • emotion (anger, humor, listlessness, sadness, and so on)

(and so on)

We see this fluidity of meaning all the time:

Zoe and Emma do a double take on a joke scribbled on a white board.

Zoe: (disapprovingly) “Distasteful humor.” (←“humor” as a comment or joke)

Amber: *to clerk* “Hey, excuse me, I’m looking for the new Gram Bridgette book—is that in the memoir or the humor section?” (←“humor” as a genre)

Owen fills out his dating profile.

Owen: “The most important quality in a partner? Humor. For sure.” (←“humor” as a trait) *checks box*

Timothy takes his residents on rounds.

Timothy: “Having a humorous name like Dr. Ailes is usually a good icebreaker.” (←“humor” as a quality)

The residents chuckle loudly.

Emma goes to Zoe’s house for Thanksgiving. Everyone laughs a lot.

Emma: (pleased) “There’s a lot of humor at your house!” (←“humor” as an emotion)

Sometimes, the broadness of the word "humor" can be a source of utility and rich meaning. But other times, it can lead to confusion.

In this book, we'll reduce potential confusion by only using "humor" to refer to an emotion in this book.

Consider:

There’s nothing wrong with other ways we use humor. But in a book like this, it can be wrong to be inconsistent in how we use the word humor.

To avoid meaning one thing by "humor" in one sentence, and another thing the next, we'll stick to a single lane for "humor."

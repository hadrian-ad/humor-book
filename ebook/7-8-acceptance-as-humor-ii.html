Humor family words can happen over time

Sometimes, acceptance is an emotion that happens over a long timescale:

Owen: *takes picture of a woman on a bench*

Emma: *catches him* (scornfully) “Taking creep shots now, are we?”

Owen: *turns red* “It’s a nice picture that’s all.”

Emma: (meanly) “Huh. Nice set on her too. Are you sure that didn’t have anything to do with it? Maybe we should take a look at your phone to see!”

Owen: (defensive) “What’s your problem? Like you’re some saint!”

Emma: *eyes flash* “Is that the creep defense these days? Did you learn that line on some gooner forum?” (←Emma makes a negative characterization of Owen)

Owen: (frustrated) “I’m not a creep!”

Emma: “Then delete it and don’t do it again!”

Owen: (angry) (retaliating) “You’re the one that fucking chased Sara off! Let’s ask her what advice you should be giving.” (←Owen makes a negative characterization of Emma)

Emma: *goes back to her laptop* “Fuck you.”

Owen: “Yeah. Fuck you too.” *sulks off* (←Emma and Owen have an interpersonal rupture) 

To track what’s going on, we’ll use two main topics for Owen:

  • [1] ‘Emma’s characterization of Owen’
  • [2] ‘Owen’s characterization of Emma.’

In both these topics, the mistake oversizes the OK, generating mistake-predominant emotions: 

If Owen succeeds in shrinking the mistake and growing the OK in ‘Emma’s characterization,’ he’ll eventually feel humor—which we can describe as acceptance, insight, realization, and so on:

Owen walks around campus after Emma called him out. Her comments about him being a creep bounce around his head. He wasn’t really being a creep, was he?

He pulls out his phone to look at the photo he had taken of the woman. As he scrolls through his camera roll, he sees several photos like this on his phone. He looks at them for a while, and finally he closes out and laughs. (←acceptance↔︎humor) “What a creeper. Ah, fuck me.” 

Above, one active topic is generating humor—he feels acceptance. But the other active topic is still generating mistake-predominant emotions—so Owen is feeling both humor and regret.

Before Owen can fully reconcile with Emma, he’ll need to feel humor on the other topic too.

Consider:

Emma will have to do something similar on her end—to reconcile with Owen, she’ll also need to eventually feel humor from her evaluations on two topics.

Both Emma and Owen will need to feel mutual humor with each other before they have a full, mutual reconciliation.

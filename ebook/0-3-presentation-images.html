Frontispiece Visual Preface

To prefigure some of the things we'll be covering in this book, we can look at some pictures that relate to smiling or laughing.

As you look over them, pay attention to what connections or contradictions you notice between the pictures—without feeling pressure to draw conclusions or resolve anything right now:

This movie parodied dramatic television of the time by exaggerating the genre's lack of smiles and laughs.

The artisan or patron made a choice between representing the philosopher as smiling or not smiling.

In life, Galileo was notable for his humor. It's common to encounter statues of idealized men that are not smiling.

This author was a revolutionary who worked to free enslaved peasants. He described laughter as revolutionary.

The word "clown" originally meant "peasant," or someone without aristocratic manners. Eventually, the word clown evolved from meaning peasant to meaning "someone who drew your laughter, by habits or profession."

We can see this historical meaning of clown in an old nursery rhyme, where a gentleman is contrasted with a peasant ("clown"):

Shall I marry a gentleman?

Shall I marry a clown?

Or shall I marry old Knives-and-Scissors

Shouting through the town?

The splitting of a pomegranate was sometimes a poetic image of smiling or laughing, like in the 13th century poem below:

So the sublime kindnesses

descended again, and the courtyard seemed split apart

like a pomegranate laughing, with all the forms

of the universe opening their tent flaps,

new creations every second.

Above, as the pomegranate splits open ("laughs"), it reveals all the seeds inside—associating a humorful pomegranate with divine, infinite creation.

Most people can intuitively rank places by how much they facilitate smiling and laughing.

What's the big picture here? There's a lot to sort through. We'll be going through everything methodically throughout this book.

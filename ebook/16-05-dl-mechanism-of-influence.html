A deeper look Mechanism of influence

In this book, humor is the only way to reach a humor outcome. For instance, the only way to control a situationis with humor—controlling a situation requires humor.

This doesn't make sense without an important clarification: humor is the only way to concordantly control a situation.

Consider:

We're often more familiar with the word discord than we are with concord. But concord is a useful word that implies mutual agreement of purpose. It etymologically means "hearts together."

So we can discordantly control a situation—like by coercively influencing someone by threatening a lawsuit, drawing a knife, calling the police, or so on.

And we concordantly control a situation through humor—like by making a request (mistake) that seems to have tolerable risk of consequences (OK).

Consider:

If we meet our sibling, we may both smile and feel humor and do what the other asks easily—mutual humor leading to concordant and mutual control of the situation.

Or if we're working as a waiter, we'll be able to concordantly control the situation when we're able to have them smile and feel connection with us.

Each of our emotional zones corresponds to a different kind of motivating influence:

  • Mistake-predominant influence (coercive influence)—when we obey mean-spirited police officers, "no entry" signs, nitpicking professors, confusing tax forms, grasping managers, domineering parents, and so on—we're being influenced through non-concordant (coercive) methods.
  • Humor influence (concordant influence)—when we take a willing action on behalf of a friend, street performer, mentor, stranger, and so on—we're being influenced by concordant methods.
  • OK-predominant influence (inertial influence)—when we choose the familiar route over another, take a door because it was built here and not there, wear what we have in our closet, use one calendar system and not another, and so on—we're being influenced by non-concordant (inertial) methods.

In this book, humor outcomes are the subset of evaluatory outcomes that happen concordantly.

That means humor is how we gain a new perspective (through concord), the way we reveal a personal review (through concord), the way we acceptably criticize (through concord), and so on.

Consider:

Note that in the same way we can have mixed emotional states (anger and humor), there's nothing odd about various combinations of influences—like an experience that mingles concord with coercion.

Humor is the mechanism of concordant influence—something so foundational to our experiences, it's hard to even notice. It's like breathing!

It's easiest to notice when we think about situations that require concord—like making friends, creating romantic relationships, asking someone to help us, motivating volunteers, organizing strangers, and so on.

We could pull a gun or pay money to make someone do something (coercive influence)—but we can't make someone want to do those things (concordant influence).

Consider:

If we pull a gun to make someone be our friend, they'll want to not get shot more than they want to be our friend.

A similar twist often happens when we offer people money—they want money and not what the money influences them do.

If someone wants to transition a relationship to a romantic one, they can't get what they want by sitting around or threatening them with lawsuits. That's because what makes a romantic relationship meaningful is the fact that two people want the same thing (concord).

It's concord, not coercion or inertia, which creates what's wanted from romantic relationships. So the only route to what we really want is through humor. We want to concordantly avoid conflict, create rapport, deal with taboo topics, ease tensions, help each other feel accepted, open up new topics of conversations, reduce defensiveness, reveal personal views, symbolize close ties, and so on.

Consider:

We want the same thing everywhere. All things being equal, we'd rather there be concord than not—with our children, coworkers, friends, municipal government, teams, and so on.

Without concord, we often only have the picture of what we want only.

It's concordant influence that makes someone want to be our friend, want to be at our birthday party, want to volunteer, want to care for mutual space, want to do us a favor, want to reconcile with us, and so on.

It's the same for what motivates us. When we have a genuine, shared smile with someone—we want to do things with or for each other.

When multiple people feel humor for the same reason, the alignment of evaluations is an expression of concord.

This gives some context for what we've been talking about throughout this book: humor is the mechanism of concordant influence. Humor is the power that drives internal changes. The way this happens—outside of coercion or inertia—is through humor.

That's the end of this chapter!

Chapter summary

Humor outcomes can help us parse motivation while sidestepping descriptive terms. (Compare "rude" to revealing a personal view.) This lets us better understand situations that include negative outcomes and humor.

  • People intend humor outcomes, which helps us understand why someone made the comments or actions they did.
  • By focusing on who is feeling what, we can pay attention to who is feeling humor and who is feeling shame.
  • Humor is a positive emotion. If we feel shame, it's because we're feeling shame—not because we're feeling humor. If we're feeling humor and shame, the positive component is from the humor, the negative component is from the shame.

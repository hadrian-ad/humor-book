Most humor is low-intensity

Most of the humor we feel isn’t very intense. Sometimes our humor crests into laughter, but most of the time, our humor happens at lower intensities—like how we bounce, hum, smile, talk excitedly, whistle, and so on more often than we laugh:

And even when our humor crests into laughter, our laughter is still bookended by lower-intensity humor—like below, where the peak of loud laughter is bookended by talking excitedly and bouncing.

If we think of humor is only the peak moments of intense humor (loud laughter), we’ll end up ignoring most of the humor we feel.

Consider:

At first, “talk excitedly” doesn't seem like humor. But we "talk excitedly" is often a composite of humor tells—when someone talks excitedly, they’re socially integrating, socially tightening, having high energy, thinking fast, raising speaking register, and so on.

Blindness. If we only thought of sadness as sobbing, we’d be blind to most of the sadness people feel. Sadness sometimes crest into a climactic expression (sobbing or wailing), but it doesn't always.

Instead, we often have low-intensity sadness where we just feel dispirited, have low-energy, get tired easily, feel pessimistic, and so on. This is sadness too—whether or not sobbing is ever involved.

It's the exact same for humor. In the same way that sadness is more than just sobbing, humor is more than just laughter. We don't want to be blind to low-intensity humor—or to how low-intensity humor bookends moments of high-intensity humor.

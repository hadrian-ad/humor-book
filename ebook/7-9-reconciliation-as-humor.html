Reconciliation as humor

Humor family words

acceptance, accomplishment, achievement, appreciation, belonging, comfort, connection, delight, forgiveness, generosity, insight, joy, realization, reconciliation, release, revelation, relief, safety, success, welcome

Reconciliation is another word that describes periods of time where mistakes and OKs are generating humor:

Elementary-school Trevor is held down by his older brother, Craig.

Craig: *laughs*

Trevor: *terrified* “Craig! Stop! Stop!”

Craig: *laughs more*

- LATER –

Adult Craig: *sheepishly* “I was a bit of an asshole, wasn’t I?”

Adult Trevor: “You’re still an asshole, Craig!”

Craig and Trevor laugh. (←Craig and Trevor feel reconciliation↔︎mistakes are OK↔︎humor)

Middle-school Zoe had an awkward experience trying to befriend a boy that weighs heavily on her.

But one day after school, the text conversation crosses her mind. Not the biggest of deals was it? She gives a loud laugh, suddenly feeling cleansed. (←Zoe feels reconciliation↔︎mistake is OK↔︎humor)

Minh is waiting for Amaya at the coffee shop, and checks his phone again. Did she forget? Is she ghosting him? But then he sees her rush around the corner.

Amaya: “Minh! I’m so sorry! I went to the wrong Cafe 61 and let my phone die!”

Minh: (relieved) *smiles* “That’s okay!” (←reconciliation↔︎mistake is OK↔︎humor)

Amaya: *smiles* “Quick, let’s go in and get coffee and make up for lost time!” (←reconciliation↔︎mistake is OK↔︎humor)

Minh and Amaya were worried that they had frayed their social ties. When this mistake was balanced with an OK, they felt mutual reconciliation—humor. 

Since reconciliation is an emotion of humor, that means an apology is successful when it generates humor.

Owen: *after walking around for an hour* *finds Emma* “Emma, I’m sorry I was such an asshole. Like, really sorry. I got defensive. You were right, as usual, even when it’s hard for me. I didn’t mean what I said.” (←Owen apologizes)

Emma: *keeps typing* “Yeah, well. Thanks. I appreciate that. And, you know, on my end, sometimes people have told me… I lack tact.” (←Emma accepts apology, and reciprocates)

Owen: *sits down* *laughs* (←reconciliation↔︎humor) “Emma, I think you’re just great the way you are.”

Emma: *laughs* (←reconciliation↔︎humor) (pleased) “Okay. Let’s not overdo it there.”

- OR -

Owen: *after walking around for an hour* *finds Emma* “Emma, I’m sorry I was such an asshole. Like, really sorry. I got defensive. You were right, as usual, even when it’s hard for me. I didn’t mean what I said.” (←Owen apologizes)

Emma: *keeps typing* “Whatever, dude.” (←Emma doesn’t accept the apology)

Owen: *sits around awkwardly* “Emma, I…” (←no reconciliation↔︎no humor)

Emma: “No, I get it, you can’t be respectful to me. It’s cool, I totally understand. Let’s just move on, shall we.” (←no reconciliation↔︎no humor)

When we’re apologizing, it’s often hard to pay attention to this humor. We’re often intensely focused on other things—but once it’s pointed out, reconciliation is a familiar instance of humor.

Consider:

To successfully reconcile with someone, we both need to feel humor—but more specifically, feel humor on relevant topics. Owen can’t just make Emma laugh by imitating a professor and calling that shared humor reconciliation.

Instead, Owen and Emma have to activate specific topics related to the interpersonal rupture, and then feel humor on those specific topics. This gives their humor the situational integration that lets us call it reconciliation.

Emotional straightforwardness. Jokes are often emotionally straightforward compared to apologies. Reading a random joke from a jokebook might involve a few random topics—but an apology may involve many relevant topics:

With so much going on in complicated emotional states, we sometimes lose track of humor. But as we learn to look for it, it becomes easier and easier to register humor in these situations.

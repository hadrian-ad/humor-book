Censure is related to dress codes and fittedness

The humor dress code tells us when humor is vulnerable to justified censure.

When a humor attempt fits a situation, it’s seen as justified—and so is resistant to censure. When the humor attempt is doesn't fit the situation, it’s not seen as justified—and so is vulnerable to censure.

The dress code is the watershed line between resistance to censure and vulnerability to censure.

Emotion dress codes. We have dress codes for all our emotions—like how anger isn’t always tolerated or always censured. Instead, it depends on whether the anger is fitted or not.

When anger is fitted, it’s seen as justified—and we describe that justified anger as natural or righteous. When anger is instead unfitted, it’s seen as unjustified—and we describe that unjustified anger as a tantrum or thin-skinned.

The fittedness of anger depends on how much it fits the situation. It's more fitted to be angry because someone put our children in danger than it is because someone forgot the ketchup.

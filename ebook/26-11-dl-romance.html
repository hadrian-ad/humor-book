A deeper look Romance

We can use a humor bush to think about why our partner likes us so much, and vice versa—even if neither of us has ever cured a disease, modeled underwear, won an Olympic medal, or so on. This helps answer a question: if we can compare everyone to everyone, why would anyone still like anyone?

We already know the answer! Those metric comparisons are neither here nor there to our overall opinion. Instead, our overall opinion is based on our humor bush with them.

People feel bonded when they mutually and frequently feel intense feelings of acceptance, appreciation, belonging, comfort, connection, delight, forgiveness, generosity, insight, joy, reconciliation, release, safety, welcome, and so on. In other words, they're bonded when they have a large mutual humor bush!

We emotionally orient toward the things we have large humor bushes with, including our partner and friends—and having a large mutual humor bush is the same thing as two people having a high mutual opinion of each other.

This process is independent of any metric comparisons we could chose to make, which is why people everywhere love other people—not for their comparative successes, but for the positive emotional experiences they have with each other.

This is why people tend to say that "humor" is their most important trait in a partner. (“someone I can laugh with,” “someone who makes me laugh,” “someone to be weird with together,” “someone with a great sense of humor,” and so on.) We foremost want a humor bush with someone!

We can expand how we think about this using humor family words too. If we say we want "someone who accepts me," "someone I feel safe with," "someone I feel comfortable around," this is also a focus on humor, since feelings of acceptance, comfort, safety, and so on are also humor.

We develop positive opinions this way with romantic partners—but also with friends, coworkers, neighborhoods, apartment complexes, online gaming lobbies, yarn stores, and so on. That's because these positive opinions all happen the same way—through cumulative experiences of humor.

Promotion in relationships

We looked at comedy (prioritizing intensity), charm (prioritizing fittedness), and promotion (prioritizing status).

We sometimes prioritize status when considering romantic partners, instead of fittedness or intensity of humor. When we do this, we don't judge a partner by whether we feel connection and delight with each other—but on whether we think they're status-increasing or not.

We may hear or think statements like “we would look so good together,” “I want someone my parents would approve of,” “they're someone who will impress my friends,” and so on.

When we prioritize increasing a status-metric, we fall into metric comparisons—which by themselves do not predict good experiences, and are vulnerable to the endless variety of metric comparisons that may float around at different times.

People who prioritize status instead of prioritize a mutual humor bush often end up with brittle relationships, where their opinions of each other can quickly sour—since those relationships weren't grounded on a large mutual humor bush, but something more friable.

The same thing can happen anytime there's promotion chosen over a humor bush—whether a partner, job, style, activity, and so on.

Chapter summary

We emotionally orient toward positive emotional experiences—which means we can use humor over time as a proxy for our overall opinion of something.

  • We have a higher overall opinion of something we felt more humor over time with.
  • Our overall opinion of something comes from the presence of humor family words, humor outcomes, and humor tells—which we can aggregate over time.
  • Our opinions are based on our fitted humor with something, not simply a comparison of arbitrary metrics.

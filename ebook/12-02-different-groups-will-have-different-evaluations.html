Evaluations are different for different groups

In a play, characters may be upset—but the audience may laugh. To think about why characters and spectators have different emotional outcomes, we can use mistakes and OKs:

Sam and Jennifer are on set, and curtains rise.

Sam's character forget to buy the right cream cheese, and Jennifer's character is angry. (←characters have small OK and big mistake)

The audience howls with laughter at Sam's attempt to escape Jennifer's chilly rebuke. (←actors have big OK and big mistake)

Owen watches a clip from a show.

The farmers give the annoying jocks a barrage of insults. (←large mistake)

The jocks are stunned. (←jocks have small OK↔︎no humor)

Owen laughs. (←Owen has large OK↔︎humor)

It's the same thing for why things make us laugh when they happen to characters, but not to people:

Sam watches Fired Fireman 3 on his laptop. Chief is trying to get the household ready for school, but heâ€™s comically inept at it. As he runs to get the doorbell, he slips on a toy car left on the stairs.

Sam loves the slapstick in these things. (←Sam feels humor)

- OR -

Sam watches Jennifer slip on a toy car left on the stairs.

His heart wrenches in fear. (←Sam does not feel humor)

These diverging outcomes happen everywhere in our lives—like how we might laugh when someone steps in a puddle, even if they don't laugh.

What makes spectator entertainment different is that the priority is on spectators. This is a design priority that shapes how spectator entertainment is put together.

Consider:

What goes into a comedic play? Things that will audience laugh, not the actors. The priority shapes what is and isn't in the play.

This is why simply laughing at someone (they stepped in a puddle) isn't spectator entertainment—in this chapter, we're looking at things that are intentionally designed to focus on spectators.

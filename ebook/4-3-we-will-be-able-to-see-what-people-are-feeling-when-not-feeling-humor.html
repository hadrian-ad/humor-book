What do people feel when not feeling humor?

When the size of mistakes and OKs are close to balanced, we feel humor. When they're not balanced, we don't feel humor.

But there are two ways these scales can not be balanced. The scales can either be mistake-predominant (mistake is larger than the OK) or OK-predominant (OK is larger than the mistake):

Above, someone starts with mistake-predominant evaluations, moves into humor, and then moves into OK-predominant evaluations.

Informal precision. We'll be using these evaluation graphs throughout the rest of this book—but informally. Like any sketch, they're used to quickly convey the gist of something.

We've used numbers to think about the size of mistakes and OKs—but our goal wasn't to imply precision, but to show that these evaluations are fluid. Once we accomplish that goal and see evaluations as fluid and changing, the numbers aren't so important.

In the rest of the book we won't use numbers for mistakes and OKs. Our evaluations will be informally described as "small," "medium," or "large," and our graphs will be informal and sketch-like.

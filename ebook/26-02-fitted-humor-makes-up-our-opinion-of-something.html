Our opinion is based on fitted humor

If there are two math teachers at a high school, students won't have the same opinion of their classes—even if those math teachers have identical lesson plans.

Instead, the students will have a higher opinion of whichever class better generates more fitted humor. This will be the teacher that:

  • presents the content in a way that creates insight
  • pushes students the right amount so that they feel accomplishment
  • validates honest efforts, and helps students feel appreciation
  • structures tests well, and allows for feelings of success
  • decorates a room well, and allows students to feel belonging
  • acknowledges students, and can help them feel welcome
  • allows group bonding, and lets students feel connection

(and so on)

When students think back to how good or bad the class was, they'll think about their humor bush. If they how frequently felt appreciation, belonging, connection, insight, welcome, and so on, they'll have a good opinion of the class.

Consider:

Sometimes it doesn’t seem accurate to say that humor makes up our opinion of teachers—in part because we're so used to thinking of comedy. (Prioritizing intensity.) But it makes more sense when we're able to think about charm. (Prioritizing fittedness.)

Sometimes our favorite teachers were the cool, funny, or outrageous ones—but more specifically, we liked best the teachers who best helped us experience humor family words, like achievement, delight, generosity, insight, welcome, and so on. Their ability to do this may or may not have overlapped with our stereotypical ideas of coolness or funniness.

If a class rarely feels fitted humor, they’ll rarely feel insight, accomplishment, appreciation, success, belonging, welcome, connection, and so on—and the lack of humor family words gives students a bad opinion of the course.

Orientation. Our humor bushes are our overall understanding of someone or something's ability to help modulate the situation in a way that's effective and builds group cohesion.

Our emotional orientation is toward this fitted humor. That's because all things being equal, we prefer something that makes us feel more accomplishment then less—more acceptance than less—more insight than less—and so on.

This orientation toward fitted humor (efficient and cohesive humor) is what drives our personal opinion of something—our overall opinion is how much something fits this emotional orientation.

An overall opinion isn't based on specific comparisons

Our spoken opinions usually have two parts to them. One is an overall opinion of the experience. Another is a metric comparison—comparing one metric to another:

Justin: "How was the wine tasting?"

Heather: *smiles* “A bit pricey." (←a metric comparison of cost) "But great!" (←an overall opinion)

Zoe: “You took Cod: The Rise of the International Fish with Professor Schmidt, right? Worth it?”

Owen: *smiles* “I love Professor Schmidt! That course was way more interesting than it had any right to be." (←an overall opinion) "Don’t know when I’ll use that knowledge, but now I know all about Basque fishermen." (←a metric comparison of utility of knowledge)

Dave: “What did you think of Pencils?”

Justin: “Honestly, it was pretty dry." (←an overall opinion) "Tremendous scholarship though, just tremendous.” (←a metric comparison of quality of scholarship)

Justin is reading evaluations.

Anonymous: “I liked the course." (←an overall opinion) "but we spent too much on Browning honestly” (←a metric comparison of syllabuses)

Emma: “How was the Tennis for Beginners event? Should I do it?”

Zoe: *smiles* “I didn’t learn much about tennis." (←a metric comparison of instruction) "But it was kind of fun.” (←an overall opinion)

We're often so used to making metric comparisons that we sometimes confuse them with our overall opinion—but a comparison may or may not overlap with our opinion, especially since there are many different comparisons we could make.

Consider:

When we get pizza with our best friend and have a great conversation, we have a great time. We may have fond memories of that pizza place (overall opinion)—even if that pizza place was worse than most other pizza places in terms of quality, service, decour, and so on. (Metric comparisons.)

If we feel fitted humor toward something, we’ll think of it positively—which isn't the same thing as saying it compares well to everything in every possible comparison.

Kids movies. As kids, we grew humor bushes with the movies we watched over and over. We had a good opinion of them! Since the humor bush often continues into our adult lives, we continue having a positive opinion of those movies.

What happens if someone tells us those movies were low-quality compared to other kids movies of the time, and definitely low-quality compared to kids movies of today? (Metric comparison of movie qualities) We’d probably agree—but agreeing with a metric comparison isn't relevant to our overall opinion. Our overall opinion is a humor bush, which may or may not overlap with various metric comparisons.

Similarly, if Zoe prefers Pittsburgh to Seattle, would Zoe change her opinion once Emma points out that Pittsburgh is poorer and smaller than Seattle? (Metric comparisons of cities wealth and population.) No—Zoe would agree with the comparison, but her overall opinion of Pittsburgh is her humor bush. Comparative metrics about wealth or size has nothing to do with her overall opinion, which is based on how often and intensely she feels humor family words on the topic of 'Pittsburgh.'

We see people interchange opinions with metric comparisons all the time. "You should be happier now (overall opinion) because you're making more money (metric comparison)." But this is often disconnected reasoning—like ignoring units on an equation and expecting the results to always make sense.

Consider:

Sometimes we think our opinion should be based on metric comparisons, like comparative quality or comparative wealth. In this view, we'd think that everyone, everywhere, should prefer Seattle to Pittsburgh because of a select few comparative qualities.

Setting aside problems with picking a few metrics for that—that's not functionally how we form opinions! Our opinions are our cumulative amount of humor we feel with things, due to our emotional orientation to fitted humor.

This creates a fundamental starting point for thinking about opinions and the relevance of metric comparisons.

Humor bushes allow comparisons

To think more about how our opinion is based on humor bushes, we can compare two variations of Heather—one that frequently generates low-intensity humor, and the other that doesn’t:

Heather 1: “… And so if we multiply by the denominator, that gives us…” (grandly) “seven!” *draws big seven* *enthusiastically caps marker* (←displays humor tells)

Xiaowei: (stressed) *watching* (←minor and small humor tells↔︎grows humor bush)

- OR -

Heather 2: “And so if we multiply by the denominator like this we get seven.”

Xiaowei: (stressed and bored) (←no humor tells↔︎doesn’t grow humor bush)

Heather 1 *walks in* *pauses* (calmly) (warmly) “Good morning, class.”

Class: *habitually* (pleasantly) “Good morning, Ms. Johnson.” (←class feels belonging and welcome↔︎grows humor bush)

Heather: “Please, take out your laptops.”

- OR -

Heather 2: *walks in* (frazzled) “Sorry I’m two minutes late, I got caught up in talking to… you know what, never mind, it doesn’t matter, let’s take our laptops and…”

Lina: *opens notebook* (←no humor tells↔︎doesn’t grow humor bush)

Lina and Xiaowei walk into Heather 1's class. There are plants everywhere and nice windows with trees outside. The walls are a light green, and there’s a giant poster that says “apple pi.” They sit down comfortably. (←Lina and Xiaowei feel comfort and belonging↔︎grows humor bush)

- OR -

Lina and Xiaowei walk into Heather 2's class. It’s white and empty, just like every other classroom. They sit down at their grey plastic desks. (←no humor tells↔︎doesn’t grow humor bush)

Both Heather 1 and Heather 2 are teaching the exact same content. Students would have a higher opinion of the heather than overall grows a larger humor bush.

Consider:

While we looked at “Heather 1 and Heather 2,” we can do the same thing with any comparison—like between “PE teacher #1 and PE teacher #2,” “social studies class and math class,” "middle school and high school," and so on.

In any comparison, students will have a high opinion of the things they have a large, fitted humor bush with, and have a low opinion of things they don’t.

If students have a well-fitted humor bush in a class, they’ll remember that class or teacher as effective, enjoyable, fun, great, recommendable, and so on. If the students don’t have a well-fitted humor bush in a class, they’ll remember the class or teacher as controlling, creepy, dull, off, weird, and so on.

Unfitted. Sometimes teachers annoy us when they ignore fittedness and focus on intensity. This can happen when they intuitively know how important humor is, but don't have a strong ability or understanding of fitted humor.

In these cases, we can see their humor attempts as wasting time with things like comics, funny voices, pointless games, long gimmicks, wisecracks, cringy videos, and so on. This isn't a problem with humor, though—we're not feeling humor, but annoyance! The teacher has a problem with irritation, not humor.

Even if we did laugh, we can still censure them—since we felt their humor attempts don't meet or exceed our sense of the situation's standard of efficiency and cohesion. We found it irrelevant and time-consuming!

If a teacher wants to build a humor bush with their students, the teacher will have to not be censured by frustration or disapproval.

Consider:

Dress codes are one reason teachers can't act like our friends. Since they're our teacher, our need for efficiency and cohesion goes up—our dress code goes up!

This means we censure humor attempts from our teachers that we would tolerate in our friends—and teachers that try be our friend are making a fundamental error in how our interactions work.

We prioritize evaluatory information

Evaluatory information is the first thing we look for—because we care about evaluatory information more than we do content information:

For each photo, we probably looked first at the faces, and only then tried to infer what they were evaluating—if we tried to find this out at all. That's because we're most interested in evaluatory information.

Consider:

Our default priority is evaluatory information. We automatically soak up evaluatory information, and do so first, before paying attention to content information.

This prioritization is what makes us all automatically form emotion networks—it happens first, before anything else. Since it's always there, our emotion network makes up reactive context for everything we do in our lives.

If we remove people or other evaluators from pictures, we not only remove not only most of the information—but also the kind of information that we usually find most interesting!

Majority. The majority of the information we share with each other is usually evaluatory information. The content information is like a nucleus that evaluatory information gathers around:

When Emma suggested putting minimal effort into an assignment and getting ice cream instead, the actual content information is pretty small (“ice cream?”). But there's a lot of evaluatory information around it—in this case, several people's-worth of evaluations.

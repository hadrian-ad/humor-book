Emotion networks are why we can communicate without words

Emotion networks can help us look past words and speaking. This helps us see a deeper foundation to how people interact with each other.

We can do all kinds of interactions wordlessly, like when we:

  • assemble things together (without speaking)
  • assign tasks with a specified degree of urgency (without speaking)
  • claim a section of a beach (without speaking)
  • express affection (without speaking)
  • fan out to search for something (without speaking)
  • flirt with someone (without speaking)
  • indicate something with a specified degree of permissiveness (without speaking)
  • organize ourselves on a bus (without speaking)
  • show that weâ€™re unbothered (without speaking)
  • show support (without speaking)
  • walk around busy streets (without speaking)
  • welcome someone (without speaking)

(and so on)

All this happens because of our emotion network—which exchanges information whether or not we're using words. It's more foundational than speech.

This is often easiest to see in dramatic circumstances, like when:

  • awareness of a shocking event trickles from one end of the bar to everyone else (without spoken explanation)
  • belligerent attitudes harden people into groups (without spoken declaration)
  • fear travels throughout a city (without spoken reasoning)
  • festive attitudes draw people together (without spoken invitation)
  • good news ripples through a classroom (without spoken announcement)
  • a restaurant suddenly falls silent (without spoken declaration)
  • uncertainty sweeps through a train (without spoken announcement)
  • worried crowds soften into release and celebration (without spoken announcement)

(and so on)

It's worth noticing above that we usually think evaluations are more important than the context. That's because our evaluations are foundational—they come first.

Consider:

If we're about to go into a bar, we don't care so much what people are talking about. We instead care about how they're talking about things—whether they're afraid, belligerent, laughing, relaxed, and so on.

Similarly, if a bar we're in suddenly goes silent, we'll go silent too—since our first concern isn't what happened, it's how people feel about what happened.

Emotion networks are the social or evaluatory medium that everything in our lives happens in—and is the medium that transmits our humor, or shifts our evaluations into humor.

Consider:

This isn't specific to humor—it's the same for any emotion. Mistake-predominate emotions can also happen simply by exchanging looks or listening to angry people. Words are sometimes involved, but not always.

Medium. Emotion networks are important! We can't ignore them—trying to learn about humor without mentioning emotion networks would be like trying to learn about fish without mentioning water.

When we think about the people in our lives who have been good at making others laugh, we'll notice that they often have a field-like quality of influence on people around them—part of their good understanding of the overall emotional situation.

In other words, they're skilled at being able to see, interpret, and act within these emotion networks! This skill is inseparable from their broader skill at making others laugh. It's a skill we can improve ourselves through attention and practice.

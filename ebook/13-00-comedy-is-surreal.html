Chapter 13

Surreal

In this chapter, we'll look at how spectator entertainment is surreal.

It's obvious that fight scenes are surreal:

Owen and Emma are watching the trailer for Blood Soul Revenge.

The hero, Rage Man, guns down dozens of henchmen while they ineffectively return fire. Then a missile explodes near him, throwing Rage Man against a concrete wall—but he gets up in time to behead a charging henchmen with a keyboard. (←action is surreal)

Emma: “You know, I hear your body just liquidates if you’re close enough to be blasted off your feet by an explosion.” (←action is surreal)

Owen: *laughs* “Yeah, but the dude’s not even wearing earplugs, so he’s clearly made of sterner stuff!” (←action is surreal)

Emma: (thoughtfully) “Do you think you could really decapitate someone with a keyboard?” *laughs* (←action is surreal)

Emma and Owen know this is surreal—they don't go around thinking that they could elegantly decapitate henchmen with keyboards.

Consider:

It doesn't matter to Emma and Owen that Blood Soul Revenge is surreal. They aren't looking for realism for realism’s sake—since they want the movie to entertain them, not to teach them how to fight with keyboards.

But while it’s often obvious to us that action scenes are surreal, we don’t always think of comedy scenes as being equally surreal—but they are!

Emma, Owen, and Zoe are watching clips from Fired Fireman 4.

Chief is unpacking on the moon base. He opens his luggage, and the bong he had tried to throw away rolls out.

Chief: (aghast) *picking it up* “Oh no! I brought the wrong suitcase!”

Right then, the colonel walks in. Her eyes narrow as Chief gulps.

Emma and Owen laugh.

Zoe: *amused* "The actress playing the colonel is so young. How'd she get put in charge of security on the world's first moonbase? So unrealistic!" (←comedy is surreal)

Emma: "What's so unrealistic is that she's so HOT, argh!" *flops back on the sofa in mock distress* (←comedy is surreal)

Owen: (amused) "Well, I think it's most unrealistic that they're on the moon!" (←comedy is surreal)

Above, the comedy scene is surreal. In the same way Rage Man's actor can't be like Rage Man (use grit to survive missiles), Chief's actor can't be like Chief (be somewhere important when unqualified and avoiding discipline).

For similar reasons, Owen can't survive damage like Rage Man, or fail his way through a moonbase. Just because we can present a surreal thing doesn't mean it can be experienced 1:1 by real people.

We'll be talking about this surreality throughout this chapter.

Consider:

There's nothing wrong with surreal things—instead, we can be wrong if we don't spot surreal things.

Partly, it's a matter of apples and oranges. There are two things: acting in a fight scene, and actually fighting someone. If Owen confuses the two, he can make inaccurate comparisons or judgements.

It's the same for spectator entertainment. There are two skills: producing a funny conversation scene, and actually generating humor in a day-to-day conversation. These are apples and oranges.

Coherent and surreal. When the audience watches Rage Man punch through concrete in the opening scene of Blood Soul Revenge, the audience knows this is surreal—people can't punch through concrete.

But even though it's a surreal premise, Rage Man's strength can still be coherent to that premise. Rage Man can punch through concrete throughout the movie, making his strength both surreal and coherent. But it's still surreal!

This is often obvious for physical things, like punching through concrete. But it's the same for emotions too.

If Rage Man is always angry, the surreal emotional premise is that he always has a large mistake and small OK. But just because the movie is coherent to this premise, doesn't mean it's realistic—since real people are calm from time to time.

This is relevant to comedies, where the character's emotional reactions may be coherent to a surreal emotional premise—and we shouldn't take it as more realistic than it is.

Dress codes and censure are always present

While dress codes and censure are obvious in extreme examples (abandoning attention to mission control versus not), the same dynamic happens all across our lives.

Dress codes and censure are why groups often can censure as a group, like when we:

  • encounter awkward pauses around the dinner table
  • exchange looks with each other when the teacher makes an uncomfortable joke
  • find everyone dismissive of our social media post
  • find random pointless stories from our spouse fun, but random pointless stories from the guy in the line in front of us annoying
  • cringe when watching an uncomfortable scene in a comedy
  • make eye contact with others when someone is weird in public
  • share looks with passengers when someone is laughing on the bus too loudly
  • know who in the group everyone finds irritating
  • register who is censuring who in a meeting
  • find our post censured online by immediate consensus

(and so on)

In all these cases, some people have arrived at an shared understanding of the humor dress code—which gives them a shared basis to censure someone breaking the dress code.

Censure. “Censure” and “censor” are similar sounding words that mean different things. To censure is to condemn, while to censor is to suppress communication.

While censure and censor are overlapping concepts, generally, censure comes from within emotion networks, in the form of desynchronization—while censorship comes from outside an emotion network, as something imposed from without.

We don't have to censure unfitted humor

When humor or a humor attempt is below dress code, we don't have to censure it. Instead, it just means we have the option to.

Often, there's no reason to censure humor or humor attempts:

Owen: “What if we…” *laughs* “…shit all over the classroom?” *laughs harder* (←unfitted humor)

Emma: *laughs* “Class, I’ve never read…” *laughs* “…such mush… before…” (←unfitted humor)

Owen: *crying from laughter* “Such slippery prose…” (←unfitted humor)

Owen and Emma: *whoop with laughter* (←unfitted humor)

Owen: “Think fast, Zo!” *flicks a sunflower seed at her* (←unfitted humor attempt)

Zoe: “Return fire! Fire the catapults!” *loads spoon with grape* (←unfitted humor attempt)

No one above had any reason to censure humor—so no one did.

Consider:

If we imagine we're the only onse in a situation with a high dress code, we're the only ones who can bring censure—so if we don't, there's no censure.

When something is below dress code, that doesn't mean we will or should censure it—it just means that if we censure it, others will find that censure justified:

Zoe happily hugs a stranger. (←unfitted humor attempt)

Stranger: "Um." (←stranger chooses to censure Emma)

- OR -

Stranger: (surprised) *happily hugs back* (←stranger doesn’t choose to censure Zoe)

Emma: “Trevor was dressed like a carrot today.” (←unfitted humor attempt)

Zoe: (warningly) “Emma.” *laughs* (←Zoe chooses to censure Emma)

- OR -

Zoe: “Oh my gosh! You’re right!” *laughs* (←Zoe doesn’t choose to censure Emma)

Tucker pokes his head out of his office to talk to Amber. There are a million things going on at once.

Tucker: “Okay. Pun-time. Here we go: I’m a big fan of whiteboards. I find them totally re-markable!” (←unfitted humor attempt)

Amber grimaces. (←Amber chooses to moderately censure Tucker)

Yasmin: *listening* *thinking* "Good." (←Yasmin sees Amber’s decision to censure as justified)

- OR -

Amber gives a frazzled laugh. (←Amber chooses to mildly censure Tucker)

Yasmin listens to the exchange. Tucker can be so annoying. (←if Amber had decided to censure Tucker, Yasmin would have seen the censure as justified)

Jasper is doing stand-up.

Jasper: “Okay, okay. So here’s another joke. Let’s see. So there are two recovering alcoholics, right? And they sit down. And they’re like—let’s get our act together and finally be rock stars, like we always wanted. So they start trying to write a song. But they can’t get past the first two bars!”

The audience guffaws, and Sam laughs too—nothing like a joke in slightly bad taste.

As the laughter dies down, the audience catches the tail end of a groan. It causes people to chuckle, and Jasper raises his hands in good-natured self-defense. (←someone censures an unfitted humor attempt, and people support the censurer)

- OR -

The audience guffaws, and Sam laughs too—nothing like a joke in slightly bad taste. (←no one bothers to censure an unfitted humor attempt)

Above, Sam doesn’t bother to censure an unfitted humor attempt himself. But if someone censures it, Sam will see their censure as justified.

Consider:

That doesn’t mean Sam would approve of censure of any strength. Instead, Sam would see censure as justified in proportion to how unfitted it was.

Since Jasper’s humor attempt wasn’t particularly unfitted, Sam would see a good-natured groan as a justified intensity of censure—but would see yelling at the stage as an unjustified intensity of censure.

Normalcy. Desynchronization and resynchronization is a normal part of being around others. Censure and dress codes are just how we group and collaborate:

Emma: “Where do you want to eat? The Loaded Gyro?”

Zoe: *exaggerated grimace* (←Zoe censures Emma)

Owen: (compromising) “How about Humble Pie?” (←Owen notices censure and uses it to adjust)

Emma: *lights up* “Let’s do it!” (←emotion network resynchronizes and agrees on a course of action)

Above, Zoe’s censure (exaggerated grimace) wasn’t received by Emma as harsh—it was just part of a collaborative, back-and-forth process that allowed evaluations to be shared and reacted to. This allowed a group to reach a consensus in just a few seconds.

Censure is just a normal part of how conversations and groups work. Like trying to balance a scale, it's usually neither annoying nor notable—just how a task is done. It's only only occasionally that the same mechanism becomes large enough to become confrontational.

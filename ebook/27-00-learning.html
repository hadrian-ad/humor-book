Chapter 26

Learning

Last chapter, we looked at how fitted humor and humor bushes together constituted our opinion of something.

In this chapter, we'll look at how fitted humor and humor bushes are involved in learning.

Humor is how we learn—when we learn, we feel humor. More specifically, humor is the process of incorporating new perspectives or framings:

Justin: *lecturing* *reading off slides* “Joseph Conrad who wasn’t actually a native speaker of English was one of the finest novelists in English literature and was famous for his maritime perspective and exploration of commercial and cultural systems that would later be called globalization…”

Emma: *thinking* “This is so dull!” (←Emma feels no humor↔︎Emma does not incorporate a new perspective)

Zoe: *thinking* “Wait—English wasn’t his first language?” (←Zoe catches a mistake, but doesn’t have time or help to match it with an OK)

- OR -

Justin: *lecturing* “Joseph Conrad was one of the finest English novelists—but there’s something surprising about that.” (←Justin primes audience for a mistake) “Anyone have any guess on what his native language was? Emma?”

Emma: “Uhh… not English?”

Justin: “Right! Any guesses? Owen?”

Owen: “… German?”

Justin: “Good guess! But no!” (←Justin primes for an even bigger mistake) *class is listening* (←humor tells like loss of situational awareness, social tightening, focusing on present) “It was Polish! He didn’t learn English till he was 21!” (←mistake is revealed)

Zoe: (surprised) *laughs* (←Zoe feels humor↔︎Zoe incorporates a new perspective)

Justin: “What do you think about that—Minh?”

Minh: “Um. It’s surprising.” *smiles sheepishly* (←Minh feels humor↔︎Minh incorporates a new perspective)

Justin: “Yeah. That would be like if someone in this room decided to start learning Polish today—and then went on to become the best Polish author in history!” *smiles* *class smiles and briefly chatter* (←class feels humor↔︎incorporates a new perspective)

In order to effectively create new perspectives and framings in his students, Justin thinks of his teaching as a series of humor attempts. When he does this, he’s better able to acceptably criticize (perspectives), share new perspectives, support arguments, reveal personal views, and so on.

This is why his successful teaching generates humor, while his unsuccessful teaching does not.

Consider:

When the class doesn’t feel humor, it’s a bit like pouring water over a stone—the potential new framings or perspectives are there, they just slide off them. When the class does feel humor, we know our class is actually incorporating new framings.

Learning. We don’t always notice the relation between humor and learning, in part because we often only notice humor where we expect to find it. We'll scan ourselves for humor while watching a comedy show, but not while doing math homework.

But once it’s pointed out, we notice that we do feel humor from solving math problems. This isn't surprising—since any kind of problem generates mistakes and lowers our OK.

As we come to understand and eventually solve a problem, we shrink the mistake and raise our OK, and eventually they'll balance. This is true for bouldering, jokes, math problems, logic puzzles, box packing, riddles, way-finding, and so on—all of which generate humor for similar reasons.

When we don't feel humor at math homework, it's because we feel it's rote busywork, or we feel we're flailing through it. But when we're actually overcoming problems, and gaining insight, and feeling things click into place—when we're learning and incorporating new perspectives—we feel humor.

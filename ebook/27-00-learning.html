Chapter 27

Learning

Last chapter, we looked at how fitted humor and humor bushes together constituted our opinion of something.

In this chapter, we'll look at how fitted humor and humor bushes are involved in learning. Humor is how we learn—when we learn, we feel humor.

Emma is trying to finish a homework problem, but her solution isn't quite working. She skims forums until she discovers what she was doing wrong. She had missed a step! Everything makes sense. She smiles. (←Emma learns↔︎humor)

Yasmin spends an hour clicking on links on the StormConQuest Wiki. She smiles as she reads about the troll brides. The lore is so fun. (←Yasmin learns↔︎humor)

Jessica: *reading trivia on her bottle of sweet tea* “Emma! Did you know sharks can lose 100 teeth a day!” *beams* (←Jessica learns↔︎humor)

Emma: *not really listening* “Really?” (←Emma doesn't learn↔︎no humor)

Jessica: *looking out the window* (amazed) *smiling* "I thought the full moon was always on a Thursday!" (←Jessica learns↔︎humor)

Elias: *laughs* (←Elias learns↔︎humor) "What! Jess, you are so fucking stupid sometimes!"

Justin devours the Complete History of Pencils. The personages involved in the early modern age are so fascinating! (←Justin learns↔︎humor)

Alina is talking on the phone with her younger brother, Artem.

Alina: “I’m sorry things are rough right now. Lots of people have troubles after high school. There’s no shame in being depressed. Listen, I have to go, but I’ll call you soon.”

She hangs up. Artem sits back.

Artem: (thunderstruck) “I have depression!?” *laughs* (←Artem learns↔︎humor) “That makes so much sense. Why didn’t I think of that before?”

Above, people encounter things and experience acceptance, accomplishment, achievement, apprecitation, comfort, insight, realization, release, revelation, safety, and so on—they're learning!

We'll be talking about this throughout this chapter.

Learning. We don’t always notice the relation between humor and learning, in part because we often only notice humor where we expect to find it. We'll scan ourselves for humor while watching a comedy show, but not while doing math homework.

But once it’s pointed out, we notice that we do feel humor from solving math problems, which isn't surprising—since any kind of problem generates mistakes and lowers our OK.

Our mistakes and OKs start to balance as we come to understand and eventually solve a problem. This is true for bouldering, delivering jokes, solving math problems, working through logic puzzles, encountering riddles, packing boxes, way-finding, and so on—all of which generate humor for similar reasons.

If we don't feel humor at math homework, it's because we feel it's rote busywork, or because we feel we're flailing through it. But when we're actually overcoming problems, and gaining insight, and feeling things click into place—we feel humor.

Humor attempts

In this book, we've looked at how you can intend to make someone smile by telling them a joke ("chant of my generation!") or by complimenting them ("gosh you're cute") or even being rude. ("This is terrible") How can we talk about all this?

In this book, we'll call intentions to generate humor humor attempts.

consider:

Whenever we try to make someone show humor tells, feel humor family words, reach humor tripwires—we're making a humor attempt.

If a friend tells us a joke, the humor attempt is broader than just the words of that joke—it's everything. It's intonation, pacing, posture, facial expression, broader situation, and so on.

That means the joke itself would just be part of the humor attempt.

So if a hundred people tell the same joke, they're using one joke but making a hundred different humor attempts—since each person would be:

  • talking to a different person
  • using a different inflection
  • have a different relation to the joke
  • speaking at different times of day

(and so on)

It's the same for other things that intend someone to feel humor. Bringing our partner tea can be part of a humor attempt—if our intention is for them to smile or loosen and ease tension and experience belonging, comfort, delight, and so on.

Consider:

If our partner smiles at the tea, it's a successful humor attempt—if they frown with disappointment, it's an unsuccessful humor attempt. Regardless of outcome, it's still a humor attempt if we intended humor.

The phrase "humor attempts" gives us a way to talk collectively about everything we do to generate smiles or laughs— including the ones we don't really have names for, like bringing tea, massaging shoulders, giving sincere compliments, giving sincere criticism, making eye contact, and so on.

Why do we need the phrase "humor attempt"? A "humor attempt" describes intention first. (To make us feel humor.) By descripting intention first, we don't have to worry about describing it with a genre word.

For instance, the words "joke" or "insult" don't imply a specific intention. We can intend sadness from a joke, just like we can intend laughter from an insult.

So when the tutor says "this is terrible," what matters isn't whether it's a joke or an insult—what matters is the intention. An "successful high-velocity humor attempt" will tell us more about "this is terrible" than trying to sort through words like "joke" or "insult."

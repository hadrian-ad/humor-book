Humor outcomes determine fittedness

We can use humor outcomes as indicators of fittedness, in addition to topics and humor family words.

Humor outcomes

acceptably criticize, achieve catharsis, avoid conflict, change negative emotions into positive ones, control a situation, create rapport, deal with taboo topics, ease tensions, give new perspectives, handle complaints, help one become accepted, make a point, open up a new topic of conversation, reduce defensiveness, reveal a personality trait, reveal a personal view, support an argument, survive in high stress situations, symbolize close ties.

Humor outcomes help us frame questions about fittedness with narrative clarity.

For instance:

Instead of asking “when does humor fit?” we can ask more tangible questions—like “when does avoiding conflict fit?” or handling a complaint, reducing defensiveness, symbolizing close ties, and so on.

When Justin makes a humor attempt, we can use humor outcomes to see in what ways it fits the situation:

Justin’s walking out of the English department when Dave catches him.

Dave: *smiling* “Justin! The department’s going to get some drinks tonight. You should come!”

Justin: *shaking head* *smiling* "Thanks, but I’d better pass. The mountain of sobriety, you know, Tantalus and Sisyphus. Maybe another event!” (←Justin reveals a personality trait)

Dave: *laughs* “Sure, I get it. Maybe another time, another event then!”

In this example, Justin wants to reveal a personality trait—he’s sober. With enough situational relevance, Dave will see this humor attempt as fitted:

But revealing a personal trait isn’t the only humor outcome in this example—Dave also sees Justin’s humor attempt as trying to avoid conflict (while declining an invitation), deal with a taboo topic (alcoholism), support an argument (that he has a valid excuse to decline), and so on:

By comparison, if Justin makes a funny voice and goes “NOOOooooOO… thank you,” Dave wouldn't describe that humor attempt as straightforwardly dealing with taboo topics or avoiding conflict—and so would be less fitted.

Consider:

Dave’s humor in response to Justin’s humor attempt (*laughs* "sure, I get it.") is also fitted. Dave wasn’t laughing at content, like alcoholism—Dave was laughing because he reached humor outcomes.

Dave felt an argument was supported, he gained a new perspective, he feels Justin revealed a personality trait, he feels rapport has been created, and so on.

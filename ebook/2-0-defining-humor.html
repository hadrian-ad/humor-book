Chapter 2

Humor Pattern

In this chapter, we'll be looking at the universal pattern to when and why we feel humor. We can put this pattern most simply as:

Humor is mistake that’s evaluated as OK.

We'll explain this in detail soon. But first, we can look at examples to get a general sense of how this pattern works:

Yasmin puts on her glasses for the first time. She can see leaves! Clouds have texture! She beams and laughs. (←Yasmin evaluates a mistake as OK↔︎humor)

Riley sees a squirrel on a pumpkin and laughs. (←Riley evaluates a mistake as OK↔︎humor)

Sam: (baffled) “What’s so funny?” (←Sam evaluates a mistake as not OK↔︎no humor)

Jennifer sees the squirrel and laughs too. (←Jennifer evaluates a mistake as OK↔︎humor)

Minh is playing with his baby nephew, and covers his face with his hands.

Baby: (confused) *mouth drops* (←no humor)

Minh: *pulls hands aside* (enthusiastically) "Peek-a-boo!"

Baby: (delighted) *laughs* (←baby evaluates a mistake as OK↔︎︎humor)

Trevor walks into class and trips, and the cereal he’s carrying falls down his shirt.

At first his classmates are shocked. But when they see that he’s not hurt, they smile and laugh with relief. (←Trevor’s classmates evaluate a mistake as OK↔︎humor)

Trevor, on the other hand, is embarrassed. His shirt is dirty, and he stalks to the bathroom, decidedly unamused. (←Trevor evaluates a mistake as not OK↔︎no humor)

In each example, someone felt humor or not based on their evaluation of a mistake as OK or not OK.

Humor is a mistake that’s evaluated as OK.

We're using words like "mistake" and "OK" in new ways. We'll be looking more precisely at what these words mean over the next few sections.

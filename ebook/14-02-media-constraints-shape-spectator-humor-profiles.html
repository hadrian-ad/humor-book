Priorities that shape humor profiles

Spectator entertainment makes choices about how it wants to convey evaluatory information.

A movie doesn't have to prioritize realism of humor—instead a movie may:

  • Prioritize pacing. When we laugh in conversation, the laughter may take up a substantial proportion of the conversation. But for pacing reasons, movies don't want laughter to take up the same proportion of a scene.
  • Prioritize camera shots. If a movie wants a clear shot of a face, the director will instruct the actor not to put their hands near their heads when pretending to laugh. This prevents a common humor tell from being present in many scenes:
  • Prioritize characterization. In a movie, it can be charming to see the roguish character smirk as they receive an award—but it can be off-putting in real life. In reverse, it can be charming to see someone beam at an award ceremony in real life, but might feel like weak characterization in a movie:
  • Prioritize what's flattering on camera. Genuine smiles contort our faces. Some movies rarely let actors or characters blink on screen, let alone scrunch their eyes when they smile.

Sometimes we think movies are supposed to show realistic humor—but movies aren't trying to be realistic for realism's sake.

It's obvious that Star Wars isn't trying to present realistic space battles for realism's sake—realism isn't the priority!

For exactly the same reason, movies aren't trying to present realistic emotions for realism's sake—realism also isn't the priority.

Limitations. One reason spectator humor profiles are so distinct is that we simply can't convincingly act out high-intensity humor on command!

We can't convincingly act out our own laugh on command—let alone imitate someone else's laughter. While we can sometimes caricature, we can't convincingly replicate intense laughter.

Directors and actors will shape presentations of humor to what's physically possible to do, as well as the skills of the actor—so if a lead actor can't act out laughter, it won't be scripted. We don't always notice this about movies, but once it's pointed out it's hard to unsee!

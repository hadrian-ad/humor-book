Things can be charming

We can find anything charming, not just people. We can feel frequent, fitted humor around:

  • events
  • festivals
  • flowers
  • metaphors
  • packaging
  • places
  • sound effects
  • typography
  • user interfaces
  • walks

(and so on)

These things can have a charm-quality, since they tilt us toward humor outcomes.

We feel humor from these things for the same reason we've seen throughout this book. When we encounter them, we:

  • shift our evaluations of mistakes and OK into balance
  • have opportunity and reason to relax, loosen, feel optimistic, lose situational awareness, and so on
  • feel delight, insight, welcome, and so on
  • are able to avoid conflict, ease tension, feel an argument has support, and so on

When this happens many times in association with something (cookies, a nursury, a baseball stadium, a lak, a greenhouse), or happens for a long time due to the integration of our expectations—we describe those things as charming.

Consider:

We said that fitted humor is socially effective and connective. That's why charming people seem socially effective and connective—it's their skill at fitted humor.

Of course, the same is true not just of people, but of anything—places or events included. If everyone smiles whenever we gather around freshly baked cookies, baker as well as the cookies will seem socially effective.

It's why we can also feel cookies, nurseries, baseball stadiums, and so on are agents of social cohesion, just like we feel charming people are.

Integrated. We previously talked about how some expectations become integrated—when something deviates from integrated expectations, it takes a long time for our expectations to adjust.

People or things that can make us feel fitted humor on topics with integrated expectations are often seen as charming—as this creates a large amount of fitted humor.

If Zoe spends a lot of her time in a city with lots of concrete and asphalt, going into an urban nursery will be a charming experience. If we're used to thinking of us as bad at something, an unexpected genuine compliment will be a charming experience.

Things can be charming

We can find anything charming. It's not just people we ca feel frequent, fitted humor around—but also:

  • events
  • festivals
  • flowers
  • metaphors
  • packaging
  • places
  • sound effects
  • typography
  • user interfaces
  • walks

(and so on)

These things can have a charm-quality, since they tilt us toward humor outcomes.

We find all kinds of things charming:

Owen goes home to Connecticut for Christmas. His mother bakes his favorite Christmas cookies and they laugh and talk as they wait for them to cool. (←Owen frequently feels humor family words and reaches humor outcomes around freshly baked cookies↔︎Owen finds cookies charming)

Zoe goes to her favorite plant nursery. She pauses to look down a beautiful alley. Every time she comes here, she just feels relaxed, calm, and happy. (←Zoe frequently feels humor family words and reaches humor outcomes when at a particular plant nursery↔︎Zoe finds this place charming)

Trevor goes to a baseball game, and admires the park as he takes his seat. The entire thing feels like a low-grade party. He has a smile on his face at all the familiar sights, sounds, and smells. (←Trevor frequently feels humor family words and reaches humor outcomes at a baseball game↔︎Trevor finds attending baseball games charming)

Heather goes to see her relatives up north. Walking by the lake, she pauses to admire the familiar childhood sight of a sailboat on the lake. She relaxes and gives a half-smile as she looks out at the lake. (←Heather frequently feels humor family words and reaches humor outcomes around a lake↔︎Heather finds a lake charming)

Justin visits a greenhouse and feels like he's transported to a magic kingdom. He spends hours walking around, admiring everything—even small things, like the perfect two green lines in the walkway. (←Justin frequently feels humor family words and reaches humor outcomes when visiting something beautiful↔︎Justin finds his visit to the greenhouse charming)

We feel humor from these things for the same reason we've seen throughout this book. When we encounter them, we:

  • shift our evaluations of mistakes and OK into balance
  • have opportunity and reason to relax, loosen, feel optimistic, lose situational awareness, and so on
  • feel delight, insight, welcome, and so on
  • are able to avoid conflict, ease tension, feel an argument has support, and so on

In this book, when this happens many time in association with something, we describe that thing as charming.

Consider:

We said that fitted humor is socially effective and connective. That's why charming people seem socially effective and connective—it's their skill at fitted humor.

Of course, the same is true not just of people, but of anything—places or events included. If everyone smiles whenever we gather around freshly baked cookies, the cookies and the baker will also seem socially effective.

It's why we can also feel cookies, nurseries, baseball stadiums, and so on are agents of social cohesion, just like charming people.

Comedic things. In the same way a festival may be charming (making constant charm attempts), a show may be comedic (making constant comedy attempts).

Comedic things might be funny sound effects (“sproooinng-ng-ng-ng!!”), trampolines, oversized bouncy balls, penguins, farts and so on, which have an association with prioritizing intensity of humor, not fittedness.

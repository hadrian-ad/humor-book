Each humor tell is expressed many ways

Each humor tell has an endless variation in how it can be expressed—like laughter:

In this book, we’ll referring to all of these exhales and inhales generically as laughter.

So in our examples, we'll say that people *laugh*—but that doesn't usually mean they had some kind of cliche, high-intensity laughter. (“Hahahahahahah!”) Instead, it usually refers to a laugh that’s less intense and in endless variety—like “pff!” “ah!” “heh!”, and so on.

We can see this in some examples:

Trevor: *playing StormConQuest* (frustrated) “I can’t beat the Ogre Time Magistrate. I’ve been trying for hours. It’s just not possible.”

Yasmin: “Did you buy armor potions?”

Trevor: *pause* (confused) “Do his magic arrows do physical damage?”

Yasmin: *smiles* “Half physical, half magical.”

Trevor: *laughs* (←laugh-like exhale like uuahh! or aahh!) “Fuuuck me.”

Owen: *telling joke* “… and that’s why they always called him… Moo-shroom!”

Emma: *laughs* (←laugh-like exhale like pfff or pssh)

Jessica: *reading trivia on her bottle of sweet tea* “Emma! Did you know sharks can lose 100 teeth a day!” *laughs* (←laugh-like exhale like hm! or Hhuh)

Emma: *not really listening* “Really?”

Zoe is browsing the bookstore when she picks up a book on meditation. She reads the book title: Wherever You Go, There You Are. She thinks about that for a second, puzzled. Then she laughs. (←laugh-like exhale out the nose)

Owen is watching Fired Fireman. After Chief fails to stop the Master Arsonist, he becomes depressed and no one knows where he is. Finally, Victoria discovers he’s been living in a garbage bin the past month.

Victoria: (happy) (concerned) “Chief! We’ve been so worried about you. We know you did everything you could. You don’t need to hide from us."

Chief: “Um. I prefer the term ‘tactical self-ostracization.”

Owen: *laughs* (←laugh-like exhale like tss! or ah! or a snort)

In our day-to-day conversations, we don’t always have an overall word for sounds like “hm!” or “huh!” or “fff!!” But in this book, we’ll just call them laughter.

In the same way a half-smile and a full smile are different intensities of the same overall thing (a smile)—hm! and hahahahah! are different intensities of the same overall thing (a laugh).

Consider:

We’ll mostly use the generic *laughs* throughout this book to indicate all kinds of laughter. This keeps our examples easy to read, even though it does cut out specific information about that humor's duration, intensity, and so on.

It's the same for many humor tells too—characters in this book will generically *smile* to indicate all kinds of situationally specific and nuanced smiles.

Inflection. Like any humor tell, how we laugh conveys a lot of nuance about the intensity and duration of our humor. ("Heh" versus "HAH!" and "hahahahaha" versus "HAHAHAHAHA!") But how we laugh also conveys whether our evaluations are tilted toward mistake- or OK-predominance.

If our evaluations aren't perfectly balanced, our laugh can show that we're leaning toward the OK-predominant zone (like Jessica's "hm!"), or whether we're leaning toward the mistake-predominant zone (like Trevor's "uuahh!"):

Jessica's and Trevor's evaluations fell on different sides of the watershed line of highest-intensity humor—which was conveyed by how they laughed.

This isn't just true for laughs, but for all humor tells—smiles can be angry or sad (humor mingled with mistake-predominant emotions), or smiles can be bored or social (humor mingled with OK-predominant emotions).

Consider:

Sometimes we have words for blended emotion tells. A "grimace" and a "dreamy look" are both often smiles—only a grimace is a smile tilted toward mistake-predominance, just like a dreamy look is a smile tilted toward OK-predominance.

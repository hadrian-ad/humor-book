Chapter 16

Negativity

In this chapter, we'll continue looking at humor outcomes—including at how they help us think about why people try to generate humor:

Santiago and Damien are walking through the halls.

Santiago: “Well. We lost every game but two this season. I feel like that’s some kind of achievement!”

Damien: *laughs* “Uh-huh, sure man.”

To make narrative or motivational sense of why this humor is here, we can pick humor outcomes that seem to clearly describe this situation—Santiago and Damien gained a new perspective, changed negative emotions into positive ones, and symbolized close ties.

Consider:

We could use other humor outcomes too—if Santiago and Damien had never brought up their losing streak before, we could describe Santiago as trying to deal with (mildly) taboo topics and to open new topics of conversation.

Since we already have an intuitive understanding about the motivations and goals behind trying to give a new perspective and symbolize close ties, we have an intuitive understanding of the motivations and goals behind this attempt to generate humor.

We'll be talking about this and more throughout this chapter.

File compression. In a previous chapter, we said that evaluatory information increased information density—that emotions let us say more with less. We can see this by imagining Santiago and Damien as surreally emotionless. This decreases the information density of their speech—requiring more words to say the same things:

Santiago: (rationally) "I am going to make an argument: I think it’s time we stop remembering our season as being a negative experience. Now that it’s in the past, we can clearly see it wasn’t as consequential as it felt at the time. Instead, we should treat it as an experience that, if accepted, can give us a new perspective on the relative importance of success—and on the relation of our feelings to objective conclusions.”

Damien: “Interesting. I am not yet convinced, but the premise merits further thought.”

Santiago: “Also, given that the median win/loss ratio was higher than ours, it’s not literally wrong to call our season exceptional—just maybe not exactly in a way that brings conventional prestige.”

Damien: *thinks it over* “Yes. I am in partial concurrence, although I think you purposely downplay counterpoints.”

Santiago: “I am glad you partially concur. Does this show close ties?”

Damien: “Yes.”

The real Santiago and Damien communicated all this in just a few words! That's because they increased the information density of the interaction by adding evaluatory information (humor tells)—and so their humor made the exchange more efficient.

This increased efficiency is why we sometimes find communication that generates humor to be effective, incisive, light, memorable, multilayered, quick, expansive, influential, and so on—and why, when people try to remove or preclude humor, we sometimes find their communication to be ponderous, ineffective, off-putting, and so on.

An overall opinion isn't a comparison

We sometimes confuse a metric comparison with an overall opinion—but they're not the same thing.

Emma: “How was the Tennis for Beginners event? Should I do it?”

Zoe: *smiles* “I didn’t learn much about tennis." (←a metric comparison of instruction) "But it was kind of fun.” (←an overall opinion)

Zoe grew a moderate humor bush with the Tennis for Beginners event. That means her overall opinion was mostly good. ("Kind of fun.") Since she enjoyed being outside, meeting new people and trying new things, she overall felt:

  • accomplishment (at having tried something new)
  • appreciation (of the outdoors or people she met)
  • connection (with new people)
  • generosity (at spending time at something new)
  • insight (at a subculture or game)
  • release (from undergoing and finishing exercises)

(and so on)

Ostensibly, she was there to learn about tennis. But overall, despite not learning much about tennis, she felt enough humor here and there to have a good overall opinion of the experience.

But having a good overall opinion of the event doesn't mean Zoe loses her ability to make comparative judgements about the event:

Emma: *quizzing* "Did you learn a lot about tennis?" (←Emma asks for a metric comparison)

Zoe: "No."

Emma: "Did it prepare you for more engagement with the sport?" (←Emma asks for metric comparison)

Zoe: "No."

Emma: "Was time used efficiently?" (←Emma asks for a metric comparison)

Zoe: "Good god, no."

Emma: "Was it a good use of money?" (←Emma asks for metric assessment)

Zoe: "No, I would have learned more from a free YouTube video." (←Emma asks for a metric comparison)

Emma: "But how was it, overall?" (←Emma asks for overall opinion)

Zoe: *smiles* "Kind of fun." (←Zoe gives overall opinion)

Emma could have asked Zoe endless numbers of comparative questions. These might be metric comparisons of price, effort, outcome, and so on. But these comparisons are different than our overall opinion of something.

Our opinion is how we emotionally experience something—not how we set up comparisons before or after the fact. That's because our broadest emotional relation to something is our humor bush.

Consider:

Metric comparisons and overall opinions can overlap. If Zoe was desperately hoping to learn about tennis, it might have been a large mistake and a small OK to realize it wasn't a cost- or time-effective way to learn tennis. This may have prevented most of her humor, and she would have had a negative overall opinion.

In this case, one metric assessment ("efficiency of learning") would overlap with her overall opinion ("bad"). But even though this metric assessment would overlap with her overall opinion in this case, that doesn't make them the same thing.

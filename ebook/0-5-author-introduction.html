Introduction Teaching Humor

It is more tiring to stand and watch a procession than to be part of it.

– Peter Opsvick, Rethinking Sitting

For background, I am a normal sitter. So when I started a book called Rethinking Sitting, I thought—surely, I would be safe from revolutionary insight. Nope! I was stunned. Once again, I found out: there's nothing obvious about the things right in front of our noses.

There were patterns and perspectives I had completely missed about sitting. The book showed me these by simply giving me a guided tour of my own observations. Like a magic wand being waved, everything starting clicking into place, hidden things became suddenly self-evident, and it was like a new world was created.

I aim to do something similar for you here, but for humor. You probably have completely normal experiences with smiling and laughing: that's great. Those experiences are the raw materials we'll be using to come to understand everything better.

Like with sitting and other things of daily life, learning about humor is first and foremost a trick of noticing. With a bit of catalytic insight, a chain reaction of understanding will shoot through out heap of observations, helping us make sense of what's been in front of us all along.

We'll be covering humor in its full breadth: the smiles we make while sad and angry; the laughter after cruelty, desperation, or kisses; the bright eyes of joy. We'll cover the whens and whys, the magnitudes and outcomes, and clarify a variety of common confusions. It'll be fun, and very interesting! You'll probably smile as we go through all this.

By the end, I hope that you walk away energized and transformed: the world will have more depth, more joy, more agency.

A world content to not know humor

I am a humor teacher. That doesn't mean I directly teach people to be "funnier." Instead, I guide people through systems of observation. My goal is to grow their sensititivity of understanding. That means you'll become wiser about smiling and laughing on the strength of your own experiences.

We need teachers of humor! Once you know what to look for, there's a strange state of affairs: a world content with not knowing much about humor.

  • In The Librarian's Guide to Homelessness—an otherwise crunchy, useful book—there's a throwaway line line where the author says (paraphrasing, but not by much) "by the way, humor is the most important thing of all" and then moves on. Why hide this power, brother?
  • In an introduction to a book of poetic history, Robert Graves briefly mentions that a sense of humor is required to not go mad and to cure oneself of heartbreak—before not elaborating and moving on. Does he take credit card, or...?
  • In Custer Died for Your Sins, a collection of essays by an indigenous humorist, the author remarks that "laughter encompasses the limits of the human soul." It's a nice enough statement—but does it mean much?

For all these authors, if you pressed them on what, exactly, they meant by "humor," and to articulate why and how it's important—it's likely there's nothing actually there, other than the most basic observation of all, which is that smiling and laughing feels really good, and does a lot.

This is in no way a criticism of these specific authors. The truth is that vanishingly few people are ready to explicate humor in any practical, fleshed-out sort of way.

It gives rise to a number of silly, interlinked myths, which bother me to no end—including the persistent one that humor can't be taught. That, of course, describes the speaker more than it describes the subject: "humor can't be taught (by me)."

Since freshman year of high school, this has bothered me—that what we sometimes currently call "humor" was like a blank spot on the map. And it became increasingly strange that it was common to meet people with intuitive excellence in smiling and laughing—but very rare to find people who could articulate anything about it beyond a surface level.

It doesn't have to be this way! And maybe more importantly, it shouldn't be that way. We can instead simply treat humor like anything else, and learn about it.

You can teach and learn humor

Most of us never had any formal training in "humor," which makes us suspicious of the whole idea that humor can be taught at all. Partly, this rests on the widespread consensus that humor doesn't need instruction, since it's personal and intuitive. Bunk!

We know this isn't true by thinking about other things. There are supportive art courses (even though art is personal)—and deep places to go through geometry (even though squares are intuitive in some ways). It would be a poor world if there were no art or geometry teaching, on the reasoning that it was pointless!

It's the same for humor. Like art or geometry, there's nothing unusual about humor being both profoundly personal and intuitive—while also being able to be supported with good instruction and explored with guided reasoning.

A world without belonging

Most people haven't been to a comedy club, but they have been lonely. Look out almost any window and you can see the physical infrastructure and consequence of the loneliness crisis. Things have been hollowed out or plucked apart at the seams.

If we're going to heal places and people from the root causes of the alienation and loneliness crises—or, as I prefer to positively call it, the belonging and connection crisis—we're going to need large-scale reconstruction and retraining. But this can't be done through jargon and astract thinking. Instead, it has to be based on something that's tangible and understood by everyone—and seen as common sense.

I do community-building work where I meet thousands of people a year. I constantly draw on my decades of independent humor fieldwork and theorizing. And I am continually struck by the need almost everyone has for humor education. It's not just that complex, integrated skills around humor are off the table—it's that even answering basic questions like "why did they smile?" was beyond most people's ability.

Imagine you're on a first date and you're a bit nervous and awkward. Then you both see something and laugh. You can feel that connection, right? (Or feel the opposite, the disconnect from not laughing.)

Now imagine something a bit bigger: you're in your first week in a new city, or new job, and you're a bit nervous and awkward. Whose resposibility is it for creating those smiles and laughs? Well, the responsibility is yours, obviously—but it's also an all-hands on deck thing too. Your neighbors play a role, the architects, the engineers, the landscapers.

It all creates a milleu where we can start to see the need for a shared humor science. unfortunately, this science is so absent that we don't always notice it's not there!

Consider:

I'm sure you know the story of the Three Blind Men, who come across an elephant, but can't generalize from one touch what it is they've encountered:

  • one man touches a tail and declares the elephant is a rope
  • another man touches a leg and declares the elephant is a tree trunk
  • another touches an ear and says the elephant is the drape

We do this with humor all the time! We all notice that laughing sometimes seems to relate to truth, and sometimes to lies—sometimes to kindness, and sometimes to cruelty—sometimes to insight, and sometimes to drivel. What kind of elephant is this?

Ending humor illiteracy, together.

Due to pervasive mythology and double standards around humor, combined with the minimal weight given to humor in formal institutions—I don't think it's an exaggeration to say that humor is one of the most important subjects that's the least taught. The result is widespread illiteracy in humor.

I see the consequences of humor illiteracy everywhere:

  • We get confused. We encounter smiling or laughing that perplexes us. Sometimes humor can be troubling or upsetting, which is compounded by perplexity.
  • Description feels insufficient. Our articulation skills about what humor "means" or "does" feels underdevoleped. We often simply don't talk about it, or try but trail off or offer an incomplete statement.
  • Our practical framework is limited. We maybe hate talking to strangers, and don't have a sense of how to interact with them in a way that could produce mutual smiles. We don't have a framework for how or why this happens, or how we could improve or evaluate or experiences.
  • Our parsing skills are missing. We can't pick apart why we are smiling or laughing, especially in a way that's conveyable to others.
  • We have sampling biases. When we think of "humor" we think of comedy shows, even though the majority of smiling and laughing in our lives happens away from those things.
  • We rely on circular statements. Definitions of humor and related words are notoriously circular. ("Why is it funny? Because I laughed. Why did I laugh? Because it's funny.") This often happens when genre terms are used as explanatory mechanisms. ("Why is it a farce? Because I laughed. Why did I laugh? Because it's a farce.")
  • We struggle to make design choices. Everything we do has potential downstream impacts on smiling or laughing, but it can be hard to think about this. If someone asks us to pick between several different tables to maximize smiling and laughing, we may not feel competent to make this choice, or clear what, if anything, their framing means.
  • We accept myths around humor. We tend to have unexamined ideas about humor, like that some people are born funny, and that some people aren't.

(and so on)

Humor illiteracy is a problem! Luckily, it's easy to get a handle on everything—something with positive ramifications everywhere in our lives.

Humor is not more abstract than anything else: a note on power

I want to emphasize an important general point around humor and abstraction. Smiling and laughing are very real things! Physical things! They exist like rocks and trees! They're events, like rain or budding! Even so, we're often told or are given the impression that laughter and smiling are somehow too abstract to be known or taken into account.

As is obvious as soon as it's pointed out: that's nonsense! Just imagine someone saying that crying was too abstract to be taken into account.

This forced incoherence—that the tangible, everyday experience of laughing is also so abstract it's not actually thinkable—creates a sense of powerlessness. It's a bit like someone telling you that a rose is an unthfahomable metaphor and let's leave it at that. It hurts to empty our gardens that way.

I think this is part of why serious instruction on humor has resonated with so many people—It's a way of filling our gardens. I've found that people are ready for the stability, connective resonance, and strength of orientation that comes from studying smiling and laughing. I wager you're ready too!

The journey ahead

This book is more than a quick summary. Instead, I want you to actually learn to think with and use a particular system of humor. (As you probably suspect, there is no "one" way to talk about humor—this is just one way I find particularly useful and beautiful.)

To that end, this book is arranged like a course. Since the chapters are cumulative and build on each other, it's best to read this book straight through, without skipping around.

You'll learn a productive theory of humor, and apply some coherence to what's often an anarchic, incommensurate, or equivicating zone of definitions, genres, assumptions, and more.

I want to clarify what this book is not. It's not a scholarly book—I'm not going to catch you up on how various academic disciplines have thought about humor.

That means this isn't a book about psychology, neurochemistry, computational biology, large language models, literary history, commercialization, or other approaches one might take to humor.

Instead, this is a book of field research and observational philosophy. It's about what's knowable about humor just by observation and ordered thinking.

I've developed this method of teaching humor over the course of two decades—and I am beyond excited that you're about to start. By the end of this book, I hope you'll be a philosopher of smiling and laughing, just like you were always meant to be—and come to see, as I do, that the study of humor is the root of all good knowledge.

Laughter makes things emotionally vivid

We often find the most resonant parts of spectator entertainment to be scenes of characters laughing together:

Owen is watching Rick & Morty:

Testy rivals have been forced to collaborate and develop mutual respect for each other.

Zeep: (with sincerity) "If this works, drinks are on me."

Rick: "If drinks are on you, you're going to need a second mortgage." *leans in* *whispers* "I'm an alcoholic."

Zeep: *leans in* (shared disclosure) "Opium addict!"

Zeep and Rick share a cathartic, injured, bonding laugh. (←shared humor)

Owen laughs. For how aggressive and random this show can be, it's also sometimes surprisingly emotionally substantive.

Above, characters briefly balance mistakes and OKs, which allows them to feel mutual acceptance, comfort, connection, forgiveness, generosity, insight, reconciliation, release, revelation, relief, safety, and so on.

This variety of humor family words, shared between two people, makes the scene feel emotionally rich to Owen. The way the characters share an evaluatory transitions feels meaningful, coherent, relatable, and integrated.

Consider:

Owen appreciates this scene not because it says anything profound about addiction or other subjects—but because the shared laughter between the characters creates a more engaging emotional story.

For instance, the emotional plot of this episode includes enemies becoming friends becoming enemies again. The intensity of this emotional plot partly relies on the quality of their shared humor in this moment.

Since it's hard for live-action characters to laugh convincingly on-screen, let alone laughing together—these emotionally resonant scenes tend to either be flat or absent from live-action media.

Do people go "ha ha ha?" Not really—not any more than cars go "vroom!" or falling pancakes go "splat!"

That's why people in the United States tend to write "ha ha ha," but many people in Europe tend to write "ah ah ah" instead—and why the convention in Brazil for a while was to write "kkkkkkk." Since these are just conventions for indicating laughter, so they vary.

Consider:

What sound do cats make? English speakers say cats go "meow," but German speakers say cats go "miau,"—and Japanese speakers "nya."

These aren't meant to be "real" noises cats make—since cats make all kinds of nontranscribable noises. Instead, "meow," "miau," "nya," and so on are just abstract conventions. It's the same with written laughter.

There's no exact way to transcribe even a single instance of laughing—let alone one way to transcribe all the variations of a laugh. Something like "ha ha ha" is just how some reference laughter in writing.

Sometimes people take these conventions too seriously. "Moo" is the written word for the noises cows makes—which isn't how a cow literally sounds. But if we take written conventions too seriously, we start expecting cows to literally be saying "moo" over and over again—which is a bit like expecting a pancake to say "splat!" when it falls onto the floor.

Something similar happens with laughter. Actors sometimes literally say "ha ha ha" because that's how it's written—like how Santa actors will often literally say "ho ho ho" because that's how it's written.

"Ho ho ho" was a written convention for Santa, since it conveyed more of a good-natured, full-body laugh than other vowels. ("Ha ha ha," "huh huh huh," "hee hee hee," and so on.) But actually saying "ho ho ho" aloud instead of laughing is a bit odd—like an actor literally saying "zzz" when pretending to sleep, or a video editor adding the spoken word "bam!" to the sound of a discharging gun.

There's nothing inherently wrong with saying "ho ho ho"—since, like any spectator humor profile, "ho ho ho" serves various purposes outside of being realistic. However, we can be wrong if we lose sight that these spectator humor profiles are surreal—and not literally laughter.

We have patterns of humor tells that comprise humor profiles. Something analagous happens in spectator entertainment to create spectator humor profiles. These convey evaluatory information in a way that supports the medium or audience.

  • We can usually distinquish between spontaneous reactions and goal-oriented reactions.
  • When we become familiar with different spectator humor profiles, they start seeming normal—but normal is different than "real." These spectator humor profiles aren't perfect mirrors of our lives—they're surreal.
  • Live-action actors can't always produce realistic laughs on demand—and while voice actors can't either, repetitive noises effectively convey evaluatory information in animation.

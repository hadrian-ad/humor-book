Author's Postface Conclusion

You finished the book! I think congratulations are in order—it's an achievement!

It's no exaggeration to say that, at this point, you know more about humor than most people.

You've maybe already started noticing this heightened awareness in action—you see casual instances of humor illiteracy in others, like explanations that don't go anywhere, or smiles that only you noticed.

It’s my hope that, where it comes to humor, you feel your stance has improved—feet further apart, more stable-footed. And beyond that, that you feel there's something dignified and wise in trying to think all this through.

And, happiest result of all (to me) would be if you felt the seed of harmony between yourself and the ideas in this book—that catalytic insight was planted that will grow and grow and lead to a potent, self-sharpened variation within you.

The natural unit of a smile

Had I not observed that Purblinde men have discoursed well of sight, and some without issue, excellently of Generation; I that was never master of any considerable garden, had not attempted this subject.

– Thomas Brown, Hydriotaphia

This book took over six years of writing, and many more years of trying to find something—a sort of crazy synthesis of vivid but strange books, shuttered dance halls, careful observation of places of uncommon gathering. I kept feeling that I didn't quite have my finger on something BIG that would help make sense of everything.

Everyone has a different journey, but for me, good knowledge that encompassed dignity, joy, and wisdom was hard to come by—and devastatingly hard to digest even if I did come by.

I wanted to make something had a sort of walk-in-the-woods feeling, where you come back with the refreshed feeling that there are things new, elegant, and important, maybe right in front of us, all the time.

And so I tried to create human-scaled instruction in the much-downplayed natural unit of a smile—a unit of measurement whose absence has caused much amazement to so many people. (Amazement: "lost in a maze; unable to adjust one's own conditional structures.")

The lunar clarity of Warren Shibles

There are very few books about the lie. In this respect it will be an adventure into the practically unknown. Once this world is entered no one will return the same as before.

– Warren Shibles, Lying: A Critical Analysis

I wanted to give some notes of gratitude to a very peculiar academic, Warren Shibles—who had an indelible influence on my intellectual life. He wrote many, many books on subjects like humor, emotions, ethics, aesthetics, lies, time, metaphor, death, language, and so on—with a simplicity of style that straddled trite and profound.

His books were so different. I would read a few pages of his books and then stop and sulk for a week as I grappled with them. Eventually, I would digest them and elements of them would become part of me. I'd stop sulking, read a bit more, and the process would repeat.

He had a tenacious love of precise language, which somehow had an immense freedom to it. He could dive into bodies of literature while dismissing them calmly, and would casually drop world-challenging implications without any reader-friendly polish at all.

He was weird, no doubt about that. There was a divergent tilt to his styles and examples that gave everything an unfamiliar, almost otherworldly lens. Diving into his archives, you can read the tensions he kept up with the people around him—precise responses to the casual incoherencies of his more Zeitgeist-attuned coworkers.

One of Shible's books was called Time: A Critical Analysis for Children—a book that would have deeply scarred me as a child, which I know because it kind of did as an adult.

Shibles tried to demonstrated the non-existence of time (as popularly understood) via stick figures drawn in MS Paint and somewhat frightening phrases like "it's banana o'clock!"

As a high schooler, I encountered Shibles’ book on humor, which was the parent to this book. It made me into a kind of sleeper agent of humor ethnography—I walked around thinking everyone had competing models of humor and emotions in their head.

I never met him, but a chance encounter with his monumental (and monumentally strange) Online Humor Reference Guide set me on a life of thinking and teaching about humor. His fingerprints are all over Humor in Various Moods and Meters, from his basic model to some of his spare style to some of his phrasing for (what I called) humor outcomes.

It's amazing that a book like his slipped through the cracks to reach me, like a little squirt of jelly through a wall. I was not exploring weird books at the time—I was on a strictly normative diet of "classic" literature. But Wikipedia had a citation, and that citation led me to a book self-published in 2008, and that book was by early Internet pioneer Warren Shibles. And so this insurgent information was available to me for free.

Nearly twenty years later, I am also able to self-publish my own work in the same theme—and with gratitude to the self-publishers and editors before me, including Shibles, whose lunar clarity of thought has resonated so creatively-destructively with me.

One of my goals in writing this book was to try to replicate the feeling I had while reading one of Shible's books—only with more careful stewardship of human examples, and a more focused through line of thought. Hopefully, this book produced quite a bit less sulking than I felt at his books, and quite a bit more wonder at where the paths all can lead.

Recapitulation

This book detailed an analytical model of emotions, with an emphasis on humor. People have evaluatory perspectives, which form emotions and drive human dynamics. We developed standardized ways of verbalizing all this using ordinary language.

  • Humor is an emotion that occurs in discoverable patterns
  • Humor refers to a broad set of possible configurations of behavior, which isn't limited to just smiling and laughing.
  • Humor is part of how we communicate information-assessments with ourselves and others.
  • Humor is acceptance, connections, insight, joy, and so on.
  • Humor is the mechanism of concordance.
  • Humor is the emotional orientation of our lives.

This is one of many possible ways to talk about positive emotions in our lives. Some benefits of this model is how it balances coherence, tangibility, standardization, and usability with the requirement that we not lose sight of the soul of the thing.

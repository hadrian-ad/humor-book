The natural unit of a smile

Humor in Various Moods and Meters was an experimental textbook that was both exhilarating and exhausting to produce—the word passion comes to mind, meaning to “to struggle and feel pain.” It was a passion project!

This book was certainly long in the womb. It took over six years of writing, which was an effort of crazy synthesis of everything: my reading of vivid but strange books, my puzzling over shuttered dance halls, my community work through cafes and folk schools—and, looming over it all, the long feeling that something, something BIG, wasn’t quite right in everything.

Everyone has a different journey, but for me, good knowledge that encompassed dignity, joy, and wisdom was devestatingly hard to not just come by, but to come to agree with. I am sure I am not the only person my age who grew up with one foot in hustle-bootstrap-improvement culture and the other in joyless intellectualism.

And so I wanted to share a step-by-step chain of reasoning, such that it might not just itself constitute this knowledge, but transmit it to others as well. A sort of walk in the woods feeling, where one comes back with an epiphany.

What would lead to gentle acceptance of many of this books more dangerous ideas—like that smiles and laughter aren't just for comedy clubs, and aren't mysterious things we're exempt from caring about? That seeing connection is a vital part of living, and the downplaying of which is poor intellectual hygiene?

And so I tried to create human-scaled instruction in the much-downplayed natural unit of a smile—a unit of measurement whose absence has caused much amazement to so many people. (Amazement: "lost in a maze; unable to adjust ones own conditional structures.")

The lunar clarity of Warren Shibles

I wanted to give some notes of gratitude to a very perculiar academic, Warren Shibles. He wrote many, many books on subjects like humor, emotions, ethics, aesthetics, lies, time, metaphor, death, language, and so on.

He had a style of thoughts and writing that was normative-presenting but not. There was an autistic tilt to his styles and examples that gave everything an unfamiliar, almost otherworldly lense, with a simplicity of style that straddled trite and profound. He would casually drop world-challenging implications, and either disregarded or was incapable of reader-amenable polish.

One of Shible's books was called Time: A Critical Analysis for Children—a book that would have deeply scarred me as a child, which I know because it kind of did as an adult.

Shibles tried to demonstrated the non-existence of time (as popularly understood) via stick figures drawn in MS Paint and somewhat frightening phrases like "it's banana o'clock!"

His books were so different. I would read a few pages of his books and then stop and sulk for a week as I grappled with them. Eventually, I would digest them and elements of them would become part of me. I'd stop sulking, ead a bit more, and the process would repeat.

As a high schooler, I encountered Shibles’ book on humor, which was the parent to this book—I adopted his basic model for this book, and took it further in ways that made sense to me.

I never met him, but a chance encounter with his self-published Online Humor Reference Guide (via Wikipedia citation) set me on a life of thinking and teaching about humor. Twenty years later, I am able to self-publish my own work—with gratitude to the self-publishers before me, including Shibles whose lunar clarity resonated so deeply to me.

One of my goals was to stylize my book with more careful stewardship of human examples, with a more focused line of thought that I hoped would producing less sulking, and quite a bit more wonder.

Recapitulation

This book detailed an analytical model of emotions, with an emphasis on humor. We learned to notice evaluatory information in the dynamics between people, and developed standardized ways of verbalizing all this using ordinary language.

  • Humor is an emotion that occurs in discoverable patterns
  • Humor refers to a broad set of possible configurations of behavior, which isn't limited to just smiling and laughing.
  • Humor is part of how we communicate information-assessments with ourselves and others.
  • Humor is acceptance, connections, insight, joy, and so on.
  • Humor is the the mechanism of concordance.
  • Humor is the emotional orientation of our lives.

This is one of many possible ways to talk about positive emotions in our lives. It tries to balance the need for coherence, tangibility, and standardization with the requirement that we not lose sight of the soul of the thing.

This work is not the end: a second volume in this series is in production, which will continue where this book leaves off. It follows things to even greater detail and application.

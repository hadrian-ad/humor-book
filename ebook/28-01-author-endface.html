The long look for something

Humor in Various Moods and Meters was an experimental textbook that was both exhilarating and exhausting to produce. I had to write, illustrate, format, test, and do all the varied tasks that goes into creating a book—the word passion comes to mind, in its non-cant meaning of “to struggle and feel pain.” It was a passion project!

This book was certainly long in the womb. It took over six years of writing, which was an effort of crazy synthesis of everything: my reading of vivid but strange books, my puzzling over shuttered dance halls, my community work through cafes and folk schools—and, looming over it all, the long feeling that something BIG wasn’t quite right in everything.

The natural unit of a smile

Everyone has a different journey, but for me, good knowledge that encompassed dignity, joy, and wisdom was devestatingly hard to come by. I had the double burden of struggling to digest the good knowledge I did come by—I am sure I am not the only person my age who grew up with one foot in hustle-bootstrap-improvement culture and the other in joyless intellectualism. In these frameworks, words like "acceptance" and "connection" were fuzzy terms outside the framework I used to understand the world, or replaced by creepy repackaging. ("Brand loyalty.")

As I found other systems of thinking, I started wonder what would better lead to gentle acceptance of some of the more dangerous ideas in the book—like that smiles and laughter aren't just for comedy clubs, and aren't mysterious things we're exempt from caring about? That seeing connection is a vital part of living, and the downplaying of which is poor intellectual hygiene?

One of my goals was to create a step-by-step chain of reasoning, such that it might not just itself constitute good knowledge, but transmit it to others as well. I wanted readers to have a sort of walk in the woods feeling, where one comes back with an epiphany that they feel everywhere—the unquestioned sense that there's something new, elegant, and important, and the desire to go find or make more of that feeling.

And so I tried to create human-scaled instruction in the much-downplayed natural unit of a smile—a unit of measurement whose absence has caused much amazement to so many people. (Amazement: "lost in a maze; unable to adjust ones own conditional structures.")

The lunar clarity of Warren Shibles

There are very few books about the lie. In this respect it will be an adventure into the practically unknown. Once this world is entered no one will return the same as before.

– Warren Shibles, Lying: A Critical Analysis

I wanted to give some notes of gratitude to a very perculiar academic, Warren Shibles—my sort of lifelong frenemy. He wrote many, many books on subjects like humor, emotions, ethics, aesthetics, lies, time, metaphor, death, language, and so on.

While he continued to try to present his thoughts throughout his life in an academically normative style, he couldn't. There was an autistic tilt to his styles and examples that gave everything an unfamiliar, almost otherworldly lense. He had a remarkable independence from bodies of literature that others in his time had trouble stepping away from, and could casually drop world-challenging implications without any reader-friendly polish. He had a simplicity of style that straddled trite and profound.

One of Shible's books was called Time: A Critical Analysis for Children—a book that would have deeply scarred me as a child, which I know because it kind of did as an adult.

Shibles tried to demonstrated the non-existence of time (as popularly understood) via stick figures drawn in MS Paint and somewhat frightening phrases like "it's banana o'clock!"

Shibles: "It seems that we have no evidence that there is such a thing as time. There are only clocks. And if there is no time, clocks cannot measure time. Clocks do not tell time. They tick but do not talk.

He had a tenacious love of precise language relatively free from pedantry and formal logic. You probably picked up on his influence in this book. ("It is man tricking himself with time words which he misuses.")

His books were so different. I would read a few pages of his books and then stop and sulk for a week as I grappled with them. Eventually, I would digest them and elements of them would become part of me. I'd stop sulking, ead a bit more, and the process would repeat.

As a high schooler, I encountered Shibles’ book on humor, which was the parent to this book—I adopted his basic model for this book, and took it further in ways that made sense to me.

I never met him, but a chance encounter with his monumental (and monumentally strange) Online Humor Reference Guide set me on a life of thinking and teaching about humor.

I think about what enabled that to happen: Wikipedia, which shared a citation to Shible's book—and Shibles generously putting in the work to self-publish a reference book, as a pioneer explorer of the Internet late in his life. Twenty years later, I am also able to self-publish my own work—with gratitude to the self-publishers before me, including Shibles, whose lunar clarity of thought has resonated so deeply to me.

One of my goals was to stylize my book with more careful stewardship of human examples, with a more focused line of thought that I hoped would producing less sulking, and quite a bit more wonder at where the paths can lead.

Recapitulation

This book detailed an analytical model of emotions, with an emphasis on humor. Emotions were presented as evaluatory information, which drives human dynamics. We developed standardized ways of verbalizing all this using ordinary language.

  • Humor is an emotion that occurs in discoverable patterns
  • Humor refers to a broad set of possible configurations of behavior, which isn't limited to just smiling and laughing.
  • Humor is part of how we communicate information-assessments with ourselves and others.
  • Humor is acceptance, connections, insight, joy, and so on.
  • Humor is the the mechanism of concordance.
  • Humor is the emotional orientation of our lives.

This is one of many possible ways to talk about positive emotions in our lives. Some benefits of this model is how it balances coherence, tangibility, standardization, and usability with the requirement that we not lose sight of the soul of the thing.

This work is not the end: a second volume in this series is in production, which will continue where this book leaves off. It follows things to even greater detail and application.

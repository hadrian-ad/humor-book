We teach through humor attempts

When we think of learning as humor, that means teaching becomes humor attempts.

We can see this below, where the students learn more when Justin introduces mistakes on active topics and works to shift them to match an OK:

Justin: *lecturing* *reading off slides* “Joseph Conrad who wasn’t actually a native speaker of English was one of the finest novelists in English literature and was famous for his maritime perspective and exploration of commercial and cultural systems that would later be called globalization…”

Emma: *thinking* “This is so dull!” (←Emma feels no humor↔︎Emma does not incorporate a new perspective)

Zoe: *thinking* “Wait—English wasn’t his first language?” (←Zoe catches a mistake, but doesn’t have time or help to match it with an OK)

- OR -

Justin: *lecturing* “Joseph Conrad was one of the finest English novelists—but there’s something surprising about that.” (←Justin primes audience for a mistake) “Anyone have any guess on what his native language was? Emma?”

Emma: “Uhh… not English?”

Justin: “Right! Any guesses? Owen?”

Owen: “… German?”

Justin: “Good guess! But no!” (←Justin primes for an even bigger mistake) *class is listening* (←humor tells like loss of situational awareness, social tightening, focusing on present) “It was Polish! He didn’t learn English till he was 21!” (←mistake is revealed)

Zoe: (surprised) *laughs* (←Zoe feels humor↔︎Zoe incorporates a new perspective)

Justin: “What do you think about that—Minh?”

Minh: “Um. It’s surprising.” *smiles sheepishly* (←Minh feels humor↔︎Minh incorporates a new perspective)

Justin: “Yeah. That would be like if someone in this room decided to start learning Polish today—and then went on to become the best Polish author in history!” *smiles*

Class: *smiles* *briefly chatters* (←class feels humor↔︎incorporates a new perspective)

When Justin thinks of his teaching as a series of humor attempts, he’s better able to acceptably criticize (perspectives), share new perspectives, support arguments, reveal personal views, and so on.

This is why his successful teaching generates humor, while his unsuccessful teaching does not.

Consider:

When the class doesn’t feel humor, it’s a bit like pouring water over a stone—the potential new framings or perspectives are there, but they just slide off. When the class does feel humor on a relevant topic, we can know the class is actually incorporating new framings.

When Justin’s class doesn't feel humor, we'd characterize the class with the inverse of humor tells, humor family words, and humor outcomes:

  • If the class showed inverse humor tells, they would have low energy, tension, sluggish thinking, focus on the future (or past), pessimism, social avoidance, social fragmentation, formal behavior, disguising personal views, and so on
    • .
  • If the class showed inverse humor family words, then instead of feeling acceptance, they’d feel refusal; instead of accomplishment, failure; instead of appreciation, frustration; instead of insight, boredom, instead of realization, retrenchment; instead of relief, stress, and so on.
  • If the class showed inverse humor outcomes, they’d feel that arguments have not been supported, tension has not been eased, new perspectives have not been given, criticisms have not been handled, new topics of conversation have not been opened, conflict has not been avoided, and so on.

We have a variety of words to describe a class with inverse humor tells—daydreaming, inattentive, lazy, listless, unenthusiastic, unmotivated, and so on. We can be more encompassing by simply saying these classes lack fitted humor.

Concord. Before, we talked about how humor was the mechanism of concordant influence—with "concord" etymologically meaning "hearts (moving) together."

Consider:

Concordant influence (humor influence) was in-between coercive influence (mistake-predominant influence) and inertial influence (OK-predominant influence)

Concord is what makes humor outcomes humor outcomes. We can be forced to gain perspectives by touching a hot stove or by having to memorize pointless facts for a driver's license examination. These are coercive or inertial ways of "learning"—but in this book, there's only one concordant way to learn: humor.

Humor is how we feel an argument has been (concordantly) supported, new perspectives have been (concordantly) gained, other ideas have been (concordantly) criticized, complaints about other ideas have been (concordantly) handled, rapport has been (concordantly) created with us, and so on.

Zoe is reading a book about Aesopian fables.

A trumpeter marches into battle in the vanguard of an army, and uses his music to instill his comrades with courage. But in the course of battle, the trumpeter is captured and taken prisoner. Later, a council is assembled to decide what to do with the prisoners.

Trumpeter: *begging* "Do not put me to death. Not only have I not killed anyone, I don't even have any weapons! I carry with me only my trumpet."

Council: "That is only the more reason why we should take your life. For though you did not fight yourself, you stirred up others to do so."

Zoe smiles, thinking about it. (←Zoe concordantly incorporates a new framing↔︎Zoe concordantly learns↔︎humor)

- OR -

Zoe frowns. Killing seems so severe here. She prefers the animal fables that have a more abstract feeling to them. (←Zoe doesn’t concordantly incorporate new framings↔︎Zoe doesn't concordantly learn↔︎humor)

It's this concord that produces feeling of acceptance, achievement, comfort, insight, realization, revelation, safety, and so on, which constitute the flow of new perspectives through us, outside of coercion or inertia.

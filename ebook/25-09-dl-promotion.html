A deeper look Promotion

Moments of humor have both intensity and fittedness. Sometimes we give more weight to fittedness ("charm") and sometimes we give more weight to intensity ("comedy")—and these priorities shape how we judge humor.

But sometimes we prioritize another measure: status. When we prioritize status ("promotion"), we start judging humor as good or bad in strange ways:

Owen sees Zoe smile as she reads a paperback romance she got at the public library. (←Zoe feels humor)

Owen looks at the cover. There’s a dark-haired duke on a castle balcony with a sunset behind him.

Owen: *thinking* “Lame.” (←Owen prioritizes a measure of status, even over his observation of Zoe’s humor)

Owen flips through the book and finds himself amused at the dialogue between the haughty Duke and the spirited new horse trainer. (←Owen feels humor)

Embarrassed that he’s enjoying himself, Owen puts it back. (←Owen prioritizes a measure of status, even over his own humor)

Above, Owen felt humor himself, and he saw Zoe feel humor—which means the novel had decent intensity, fittedness, or both. It’s not until Owen gives priority to his own ideas about status that he decides that it’s “bad” humor.

There are a few problems with prioritizing status. One is that status is often tangential to the experience—a bit like judging someone’s cooking by how much we like their kitchen decor.

Another problem is that status is often entirely in our own heads—Owen is probably the only one of his social circle who worries that tolerating or briefly enjoying a romance novel is status-decreasing.

This means Owen is prioritizing his status for an audience of one. He’s essentially trying to impress himself!

Consider:

If we get into a habit of prioritizing personal ideas about status, even when no one cares, we can develop a strange relation to humor.

We tend to see people who prioritize status over fittedness or intensity to be dull, excruciating, narrow, self-serving, socially insecure, uncurious, uninteresting, and so on.

In the same way we feel this about others, others feel this about us!

The point isn’t that Owen needs to read books he doesn’t want to—the point is that he doesn’t need to have a backlash against himself for simply enjoying paging through a book. And he doesn’t need to look down on Zoe for enjoying what she enjoys.

Consider:

If Owen looked down on Zoe, he would be judging her based on his own ideas of status—which doesn’t really accomplish anything.

Owen doesn’t gain anything by prioritizing status when around Zoe—but he and Zoe do gain something when he doesn’t prioritize status. If Owen feels humor when Zoe feels humor, they can grow a mutual humor bush:

Owen: (sincerely) *smiling* “Looks like a fun book!”

Zoe: *smiles* “It is fun.” (←Zoe and Owen grow their mutual humor bush)

- OR -

Owen: *smiling* “Good book?”

Zoe: *smiles* “Shameless.” (←Zoe and Owen grow their mutual humor bush)

- OR -

Owen: *smiling* “The dialogue in this book is outrageous!”

Zoe: *smiles* “Isn’t it?!” (←Zoe and Owen grow their mutual humor bush)

When Owen and Zoe grow a mutual humor bush through mutual humor, they grow their ability to mutually characterize their relationship with mutual humor family words—like acceptance, connection, belonging, delight, generosity, welcome, and so on.

This mutual humor allows Owen to accrue greater respect from the people around him. By not prioritizing status, he opens the possibility of this mutual humor.

Consider:

Owen gains respect by treating Zoe the way he would want to be treated himself.

Owen wouldn’t want Zoe to feel shame for briefly enjoying something he enjoyed, or for her to feel condescension toward him when he enjoys what he enjoys.

This equal treatment is a foundational condition for mutual humor bushes.

If Owen doesn’t treat others as equals, and prioritizes his personal ideas of status over everything else, he’ll have to live with an internal censor in his head—one that’s constantly screening his humor for status, fighting against moments of humor, prompting feelings of anger or guilt, and so on.

This can pointlessly curtail the range and vividness of his experiences and relationships—without any benefit.

Chapter summary

Sets of topics will have an amount of humor compared to time. This ratio is their "humor bush." This helps us take a broad view of humor, even over intervals where there's no humor.

  • Humor bushes pair with humor outcomes to characterize a relationship. Whether we say someone always controls the situation or just(sometimes influences the situation depends on the size of humor bush.
  • The amount of humor over time can be fitted or unfitted.
  • Humor bushes sum evaluatory perspectives over time, which means different people will have different humor bushes toward each other.

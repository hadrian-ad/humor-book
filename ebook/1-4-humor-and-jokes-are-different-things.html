This book focuses on humor, not jokes

Instead of talking about humor (emotions), we sometimes instead drift into talking about humor (genres)—like "farces," "jokes," "irony," and so on. The problem with this is that we don't always laugh or smile at these things!

Like anything else, comedies, farces, jokes, irony, and so on can lead to humor—but they don’t always.

Since jokes, comedies, wittiness, satire, and so on can lead to any emotion outcome, they're not the same thing as humor:

Since we’re focusing on humor in this book, we won’t be focusing on jokes or satire—since those things aren’t humor.

Instead, we'll stay focused on when and why the emotion of humor happens.

Consider:

Thinking a joke will explain laughing is a bit like thinking a salad will explain eating.

Why can't a salad explain eating? For one, we can have a salad and not eat it—since a salad isn't the same thing as eating.

For another, if our goal is to explain "eating," we can distort or narrow our explanation if we focus on "eating a salad" instead of "eating" more generally.

Jokes and everything. We sometimes call everything we laugh at a joke: "We sometimes laugh at jokes. Therefore everything we laugh at is some kind of joke."

It's easy to see this as idiomatic by creating new idioms. If we started saying: "we sometimes buy coffee. Therefore, everything we buy is some kind of coffee”—we would never take this expression too literally!

It's strange to frame all buying around coffee for the same reasons it's strange to frame all laughing around jokes.

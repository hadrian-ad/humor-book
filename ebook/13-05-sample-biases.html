Accurately sampling humor

For many of us, when we hear the word "humor" we think of surreal things—like jokes, memes, satires, comedies, comedians, and so on.

While these things can be fun and wonderful—we also don't want to have a bias toward those things. If those were the only things we looked at, our understanding of humor lopsided.

Consider:

If we wanted to study warfare, we would lose our grounding if we only studied movies set in wartime.

Similarly, if we wanted to study smiling and laughing, we would also lose our grounding if we only studied our favorite comedies.

Where do we actually smile and laugh in the world, outside of spectator entertainment? Until it's pointed out, we often don't notice all the places we do:

Justin stops by Heather's place unexpectedly. Heather beams with delight to see him, and they embrace, smiling. (←humor)

Zoe's Uncle Jason helps her move into her apartment. Later, Zoe finds he left her a gift card to a nearby plant nursery.

She laughs in surprise. (←humor)

Justin and Heather are mountain biking when Justin flips on a root and tumbles down a small hill.

Heather: *jumps off bike* *scrambles down hill* “Oh my god! Justin! Are you okay?”

Justin stirs and sits up.

Justin: *adjusting helmet shakily* “I’m fine!”

They look at each other and start giggling with relief. (←humor)

Owen is about to cross the street but Zoe grabs him and pulls him back. A bus careens by, honking. Owen stands there, dumbfounded, knees shaking.

Owen and Zoe look at each other, and they both chuckle nervously. (←humor)

Trevor and Minh are walking into the student union. Minh pushes the door instead of pulls.

Trevor. *pause* "Moron."

Minh and Trevor both laugh. (←humor)

Above, we can see people laughing and smiling in a way that arise out of day-to-day situations—in ways that aren't always clearly represented in surreal spectator entertainment.

By being able to see and think about humor as it happens spontaneously, we'll gain access to improving at a variety of skills that require a spontaneous touch, like:

  • connecting with strangers
  • delighting our partner
  • leading a group
  • supporting a struggling friend
  • defusing an argument

(and so on)

To understand these moments well, we have to be able to set aside spectator humor to be able to see the situation with clear reality.

We know that reading about a feast isn't the same thing as being at a feast. Similarly, reading about a terrible event isn't the same thing as living thorugh that terrible event.

For the same reason, watching a witty conversation isn't the same thing as participating in a witty conversation—just like watching someone delight their partner isn't the same thing as delighting our partner.

We can't compare ourselves to—or straightforwardly learn from—surreal standards. Being able to compare apples to oranges correctly here matters in reaching a high competence in assessment and ability.

Play. The word illusion is an interesting one—and today has connotations with magic, mystery, deception, tricks, and so on. Of course, the etymology of the word gives a clearer picture—illusion came from a word that means "in play."

So football players have their illusion thorugh their game—children arranging dolls are composing an illusion—we pretend or daydream about being someone else through illusion. We're in play.

It's not that illusions that are bad themselves—since they're not. Instead, it can be bad when we're caught unaware by them. Then we risk the feeling of disillusionment—the sense of being expelled from a game we didn't know we were part of.

Even if we're not disillusioned, when we don't quite realize what's pretended coherence and what isn't—our thinking can become slanted in ways that make interacting with others harder than it has to be.

Accurately sampling humor

For many of us, when we hear the word "humor" we think of spectator entertainment—like jokes, memes, satires, comedies, comedians, and so on.

While these things can be fun and wonderful—we also don't want to have a bias toward those things. If those were the only things we looked at, our understanding of humor would be lopsided.

Consider:

If we wanted to study warfare, we would lose our grounding if we only studied movies set in wartime.

Similarly, if we wanted to study smiling and laughing, we would also lose our grounding if we only studied comedies.

Where do we actually smile and laugh in the world, outside of spectator entertainment? Until it's pointed out, we often don't notice all the places we do:

Justin stops by Heather's place unexpectedly. Heather beams with delight to see him, and they embrace, smiling. (←humor)

Zoe's Uncle Jason helps her move into her apartment. Later, Zoe finds he left her a gift card to a nearby plant nursery.

She laughs in surprise. (←humor)

Justin and Heather are mountain biking when Justin flips on a root and tumbles down a small hill.

Heather: *jumps off bike* *scrambles down hill* “Oh my god! Justin! Are you okay?”

Justin stirs and sits up.

Justin: *adjusting helmet shakily* “I’m fine!”

They look at each other and start giggling with relief. (←humor)

Owen is about to cross the street but Zoe grabs him and pulls him back. A bus careens by, honking. Owen stands there, dumbfounded, knees shaking.

Owen and Zoe look at each other, and they both chuckle nervously. (←humor)

Trevor and Minh are walking into the student union. Minh pushes the door instead of pulls.

Trevor. *pause* "Moron."

Minh and Trevor both laugh. (←humor)

Above, we can see people laughing and smiling in a way that arise out of day-to-day situations—in ways that aren't always clearly represented in surreal spectator entertainment.

By being able to see and think about humor as it happens spontaneously, we'll be able to improve at a variety of skills that require spontaneous action in actual emotional systems. These are skills like:

  • connecting with strangers
  • delighting our partner
  • leading a group
  • supporting a struggling friend
  • defusing an argument

(and so on)

To understand these moments well, we have to be able to set aside spectator humor—and see the situation with clear reality.

We know that reading about a feast isn't the same thing as being at a feast—or preparing one. Similarly, watching strangers meet isn't the same thing as meeting them.

For the same reason, watching a witty conversation isn't the same thing as participating in a witty conversation—just like watching someone delight their partner isn't the same thing as delighting our partner.

By seeing these as apples and oranges, we reach a higher competence in assessment and ability.

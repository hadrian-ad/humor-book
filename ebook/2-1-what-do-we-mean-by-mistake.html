What do we mean by "mistake"?

The first part of our humor pattern is a mistake.

Humor is a mistake that’s evaluated as OK.

In this book, a "mistake" is something we aren't expecting:

Justin and Heather are mountain biking when Justin flips on a root and tumbles down a small hill. (←mistake)

“Oh my god! Justin! Are you okay?” Heather calls, jumping off her bike and scrambling down to the body and bike below.

Timothy calls Jennifer.

Timothy: “Jennifer, I know Sam said otherwise, but I checked with the insurance company. They said the treatment is still experimental. They won’t cover it.” (←mistake)

Damien checks his pocket.

Damien: “Where’s my wallet? Maybe I left it at the bus stop!” (←mistake)

He gets off at the nearest stop and runs back, panicking.

Sarah sits down disconsolately.

Sarah: (dispirited) *to self* “I can’t believe she’d cheat on me.” (←mistake)

Elias sees Jessica come in carrying a big box.

Elias: “Jess, be careful, don’t knock the table…!”

The table is bumped, and a clay statue falls over. One of the arms shatters. (←mistake)

Yasmin realizes her boyfriend has been quiet for an hour. Didn’t he say he was just going to the kitchen for some leftovers? (←mistake)

She suddenly has a bad feeling.

Xiaowei pulls up to his high school. He's dropping his friend Lina off for the opening day of her play.

Xiaowei: “Break a leg!" (teasing) “Also. You look nervous!”

Lina gives him the finger on the way out. (←mistake)

Sam walks into the bedroom, tying his tie.

Sam: “Jen, the kids were eaten by wolves.” (←mistake)

Sabrina opens her email from her supervisor.

Email: “You’re being let go. Don’t come in on Monday.” (←mistake)

The examples above aren’t examples of humor—they're just examples of what we're calling mistakes. Mistakes are just one part of our pattern of humor.

Consider:

We labeled mistakes above based on the perspective of the people in the examples. The characters thought they were mistakes—which may or may not match what we, as readers, feel are mistakes. We’ll do this throughout this book.

Expectations. A more formal term for a "mistake" would be a deviation from our expectations. We have expectations for how things should be, and sometimes things deviate from those expectations.

This isn't about whether something is good or bad—instead, it's about whether something conforms to our expectations.

For instance:

Since we expect an egg to have one yolk, it’s a deviation from our expectations to find an egg with two yolks—or an egg with no yolk.

Similarly, if we thought we knew the billing, it’s a deviation from our expectations to get an unexpected fee—or get an unexpected discount.

It doesn’t matter whether we feel these deviations from our expectations are good or bad. A mistake is when our expectations don't match what we actually encounter.

For now, we’ll be using these terms interchangeably (mistake = deviation from our expectations). We’ll mostly use “mistake,” since it’s shorter and less cumbersome.

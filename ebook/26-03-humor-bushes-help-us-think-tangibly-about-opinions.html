Humor bushes can demystify opinions

Sometimes, we encounter advice, complaints, criticisms, evaluations, and so on that can seem arbitrary, confusing, mysterious, and so on. (“I just… like it.”)

It can be hard to think through these opinions:

Alina: “What do you like about GrosTech’s music?”

Artem: *shrugs* “I dunno. I just… like it.”

Alina: “It makes me uncomfortable that you like something but you can’t say why.” (←Alina struggles to make sense of opinions)

Justin: *complaining to Heather* “Each semester some of my evals say that my courses are 'dull.' And that’s all they say! If they would just, you know, use more words, maybe I could understand what they mean!” (←Justin struggles to make sense of opinions)

Justin finds a characterization of his course as “dull” to be opaque. He thinks that if someone doesn’t connect “dull” to something specific, he can't know what the actual problem is.

But he doesn't actually need to know specifics to make sense of it. Instead, he needs to recognize it as an overall opinion—which is a humor bush. A humor bush isn't about any one thing specifically—it's about everything about his class.

Consider:

If the student actually disliked something in particular, they would have said something specific. (“The quiz formats were unfair.”) The student is instead conveying a general opinion, about everything.

This general opinion is the general opinion (“dull”), and can’t be replaced by specific anecdotes. In part, that's because there isn't just one problem and so isn't just one thing to fix—the student wishes they felt more achievement, comfort, connection, delight, insight, realization, welcome, and so on. This is an open-ended problem with open-ended solutions.

The student felt their humor bush with the class or with Justin was unfitted—they felt they didn’t feel enough humor family words or reached humor outcomes easily enough, and so had an overall negative opinion ("dull").

Feedback. If we get an overall positive opinions ("I liked it" "interesting" "great!"), we don’t usually think that these overall positive opinions are confusing, unknowable, or can’t be understood without specific examples. Instead, we take it as a holistic opinion.

However, we sometimes do the opposite with an overall negative opinions (“dull”)! Instead of taking it holistically, we assume there must be specific things negative opinions refer to—and are maybe unknowable or not valid otherwise. But we should treat negative opinions like positive opinions—where overall opinions are seen as overall opinions.

An overall opinion is an average of overall humor. The presence or absence of humor over many experiences composes our overall opinion of something, and we can't explain a humor bush through a single example.

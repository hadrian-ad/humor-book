Introduction Teaching Humor

It is more tiring to stand and watch a procession than to be part of it.

– Peter Opsvick, Rethinking Sitting

One of my favorite books is called Rethinking Sitting. It took something I had a lot of experience with (sitting)—and then bam hit me with a lightning bolt. It was like a magic wand: a touch of catalytic insight, and everything was different.

This book aims to do something similar, but for humor. We've all been smiling (and reacting to smiles) since before we could speak. We have a literal lifetime of raw data—what's sometimes missing, including in ways we don't always realize we're missing, is some catalytic insight to organize our observations.

That's what we'll be doing here. I'll be pointing out things about smiling and laughing that won't be new to you exactly, but might still be surprising. These are things that, once seen, can't be unseen. I want this new vision to transform your experience in the world.

We'll be covering humor in its full breadth: the smiles we make while sad and angry; the laughter after cruelty, desperation, or kisses; the bright eyes of joy. We'll cover the whens and whys, the magnitudes and outcomes, and clarify a variety of common confusions. It'll be fun, and very interesting! You'll probably smile as we go through all this.

I am a humor teacher. What I do isn't exactly the same thing as teaching people to be "funnier." Instead, I guide people through systems that grow their sensitivity to their own lifelong experiences with humor.

While I was trained as an environmental historian and professional writer, my interest has always been in reknitting people together—and in the large-scale social reconstruction needed to heal places and people from the loneliness epidemic that's hollowed out life in North America.

As I volunteered, worked, and observed in this context, I was continually struck by the need almost everyone had for humor education. It wasn't just that complex, integrated skills around humor were off the table—even answering basic questions like "why was that funny?" was beyond most people's ability.

The lack of a humor science has been a root obstacle to so many things, including simply making it hard to even describe experiences of alienation or belonging. This is an obstacle this book aims to remove.

Most of us grew up with no instruction in humor, and never had any formal training in "humor." This makes most people suspicious of the whole idea that humor can be taught at all. Partly, this rests on the widespread consensus that humor doesn't need instruction, since it's personal and intuitive. Bunk!

We know this isn't true by thinking about other things: there are supportive art courses (even though art is personal), and deep places to go through geometry (even though squares are intuitive in some ways). It's the same for humor, which can be both profoundly personal and intuitive—while also being supported with good instruction and explored with guided reasoning.

It's not an exaggeration to say that humor is one of the most important subjects that's the least taught. The result is widespread illiteracy in humor. Often, this illiteracy leads to weak backfill explanations from comedy. It's like studying pizza when the goal is to study eating.

I see the consequences of humor illiteracy everywhere. It comes as:

  • periodic confusion or missed information
  • a loss of practical skills around connection and understanding
  • poor design choices that struggle to see people clearly

(and so on)

In a general sense, that's obvious when it's pointed out, we're often told that very real things (laughter, smiling) are somehow too abstract to be known or taken into account. It's a forced incoherency that creates a sense of powerlessness.

I think this is part of why serious instruction on humor has resonated with so many people. I've found that people are ready for the stability, connective resonance, and strength of orientation that comes from studying smiling and laughing. I wager you're ready too!

This book is more than a quick summary. Instead, I want you to actually learn to think and use one system of humor. To that end, this book is arranged like a course. Please don't skip to the chapter on dirty humor—the chapters are cumulative and build on each other.

You'll learn productive theories of humor, and apply some coherence to what's often an anarchic or incommensurate zone of definitions, genres, assumptions, and more.

I want to clarify a few things that this book is not. First, it's not a scholarly book—I'm not going to catch you up on how various academic disciplines have thought about humor.

Second, this isn't a book about neurochemistry, computational biology, large language models, literary history, commercialization, or other approaches one might take to humor.

Instead, this is a book of field research. It asks and aswers: what's knowable about humor just by observation and ordered thinking.

I've developed this method of teaching humor over the course of two decades—and I am beyond excited that you're about to start. By the end of this book, I hope you'll come to see, as I do, that the study of humor is the root of all good knowledge.

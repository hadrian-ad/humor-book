We often prioritize fittedness

We often appreciate moments of humor, even when the humor isn't particularly intense:

Sam: *gets up* “You know what. I’ll come with you!”

Jennifer: “Oh, you don’t have to.”

Sam: *smiling* “Well I want to.”

Jennifer: *smiles* (←high-fittedness humor↔︎Jennifer feels connection, delight, generosity, relief, safety, and so on) *puts on coat*

Trevor watches everyone enter class with a burrito. Zoe enters and hands him one.

Zoe: “I don’t think you’re in any of the group chats, so I didn’t want you to miss out on the free burritos down the hall!”

Trevor: *face lights up* “Well… thanks! That’s really nice.” *smiles* *unwraps burrito* (←high-fittedness↔︎Trevor feels acceptance, delight, welcome)

Trevor is talking to Minh about Yasmin. She has her job offer in another city and he was accepted to grad school, and he doesn’t know how to balance any of that with their relationship. Minh takes a sip of beer, listening.

Trevor continues, talking about his lab, and how he’s suddenly less interested in research and has been thinking of going into industry, which is confusing to him. Minh continues to listen as Trevor talks. Trevor finally sighs and smiles with a feeling of relief.

Trevor: “Thanks for listening, man.”

Minh: “Yeah, no problem. You have an interesting set of stuff going on. Let me know whatever I can do to help.”

Trevor: *smiles* “Well. Thanks.” (←high-fittedness humor↔︎Trevor achieves catharsis, eases tension, creates rapport, survives stress, and so on)

Justin is reading the Anabasis, by Xenophon—a war account from the 4th century BCE.

Cyrus is leading an army to overthrow the king of Persia, and he maintains the loyalties of his mercenaries and retainers through compliments, flattery, and gifts.

Some soldiers confront him, since they're worried he’s promising more than he can give them. Cyrus assures the soldiers that they’re wrong—there’s actually too much to give to people!

Cyrus: “Consequently I am more afraid of not having enough friends for what I can give—than of not having what to give, if things go well, to all my friends.”

The soldiers, satisfied, leave. (←high-fittedness humor↔︎soldiers feel acceptance, appreciation, connection, delight, generosity, insight, reconciliation, relief, safety, and so on)

Justin smiles and stretches. He wishes he was able to reform the Persian Empire for eternal glory!

Lina is reading a book of fables.

A wolf came upon a lamb and wanted to eat it, but felt he should give an excuse first.

Wolf: "Why, last year you grossly insulted me."

Lamb: "That's impossible, sir, for I wasn't born then."

Wolf: "Well, you continually feed in my pastures and steal my fodder."

Lamb: "That can't be, sir, for I haven't yet drunk anything but my mother's milk."

Wolf: (annoyed) "Look, I'm not going without my dinner."

And so the wolf sprang upon the lamb and devoured it without more ado.

Lina smiles grimly. (←high-fittedness humor↔︎Lina feels an argument has been supported, she gained new perspectives, taboo topics were dealt with, something was acceptably criticized, and so on—we'll feel acceptance, appreciation, delight, insight, realization, release, revelation, and so on)

In the examples above, we'll call this humor as good or bad depending on which measure we're prioritizing:

  • If we judge first by intensity, then these would be bad to okay moments of humor.
  • If we judge first by fittedness, these would all be okay to good moments humor.
Consider:

The point here isn't that we have to pick just one measure. The point is instead to notice that there are multiple measures to humor—which allows for multiple kinds of judgement.

Discussions of humor can seem dimensionless and flat if we only have one measure for humor (intensity). We can create some depth perception to how we think about humor by adding in a second measure (fittedness).

Nameless. In the examples above, people cared about fittedness first, and intensity second.

  • Lina foremost was hoping the fable would make her feel wiser (which was why she was reading fables and not an anthology of Sunday comics)
  • Cyrus was foremost hoping his soldiers would feel convinced and satisfied (which was why he argued with and flattered them, instead of just drinking merrily with them)
  • Trevor was foremost hoping to feel some kind of catharsis or relief by talking with Minh (which was why he hoped Minh would take him seriously, not joke about being bored)
  • Zoe foremost wanted Trevor to feel included (which was why she brought him the burrito, instead of teasing him for his bad luck not having a burrito)

(and so on)

In all of these cases, success was marked by humor. At the same time, it's the fittedness of the humor that mattered most, not intensity.

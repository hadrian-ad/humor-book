A deeper look Emotions

Since we grew up with words like "humor" and "emotions," we often find it hard to imagine a world without them—a bit like imagining a world without words like "tree" or "clouds."

But surprisingly, both humor and emotions are relatively new words. Their modern meanings didn't actually exist for most of history in any language.

Let's go over a quick history of both of these words:

Humor

"Humor" originally meant "juice of a plant or animal." So we can squeeze the humors from apples (apple juice), and squirrels are full of squirrel humors.

Consider:

Words like humor, humid, humidify, humidor, humus, and so on all share the same root—of "moist, wet."

Humor didn't have any association with laughter at all—for the same reason "juice" doesn't have association with laughter today.

It wasn't until just a few hundred years ago that the word "humor" started being used not for laughter specifically but for emotions generally. That's when the word "humor" was used by English-speakers as terminology for popular personality modeling systems. (The four humors, or four fluids.)

Eventually, the combinations of fluids (humors specifically) was seen as an extraneous step, and "humor" started to directly mean something like "malleable medium of disposition" (humor generally)—often with a capricious subtext. ("The king's humor," "The baby was in bad humor yesterday, but is in good humor today!”)

Over the next two hundred years or so, the meaning of humor shifted from "the malleable medium of disposition" into all the things it also means today.

These overlapping modern meanings of "humor" tend to relate to laughter from different angles—including "laughter," "cause of laughter," "weirdness associated with what sometimes causes laughter," "laughter associated with weirdness," "jokes in general," "interpersonal ability to generate laughter," "a feeling associated with laughter," and so on.

These modern meanings coexist with older meanings, making the word a very broad tent—a word that shrunk from a general meaning to a more specific one, before expanding again.

For instance:

We can feel the presence of this history with the word humor, since the word can still carry poetic notes of sadness, madness, rejection, capriciousness, emotional medium, energy, and so on. ("The sisters were instilled with periodic bouts of plaintive humor.")

Emotion

The word “emotion” was originally just a variation of the word “motion.” Like (e)rupture and rupture, (e)motion was just motion with an emphatic or expelling e- in front.

This is why early uses of emotion were about motion:

  • an earthquake was (e)motion in the ground (the ground shakes, has motion)
  • an audience pouring out of the stadium was (e)motion in the crowd (they moved together, com-motion)
  • a letter could cause a reader’s (e)motion (like pacing, from internal feelings)

(and so on)

From there, the word eventually developed into its current sense of “cogitated feelings, especially strong ones (that cause movement or expressive action).”

Consider:

In an essay from 1904, we can see a middle point in the development of the word:

I was able to walk on, but very slowly, so strongly was I still possessed by the interior emotion.

The reference to an "interior" emotion is dated and redundant: unlike in 1904, today there are only interior "emotions".

Sometimes, we use the word “emotion” expansively beyond movement—like to refer to all inner life. But when we look, we can see the meaning of “emotion” constantly drifting back to motion.

If a director says “let’s do the scene again, this time with more EMOTION!”—they don’t mean more vivid internal sensations. The director mean more emoting, more expressive action in the body and face.

Similarly, we call cats emotionless, not because cats don't have feelings, moods, opinions, personality, preferences, reactions, and so on, because they obviously do—but because their faces don’t move. Cats can’t emote in the same way dogs can with their moving (emotional) faces.

Consider:

It's the same for people. When a novelist describes someone as "standing emotionless" they often mean they have a stony poker face ("motionless")— not that there's nothing happening inside them.

The shift

One reason "humor" and "emotion" took on their new meanings was because of a metaphor—the metaphor that reactions and interpretations were liquid. This metaphor that caught several words in its wake, and gave them new meanings.

Since humors (liquids) were particularly emotional (fluid), these two words, humor and emotion, changed together from literally describing fluids and movement to metaphorically describing cause of laughter and feelings.

Consider:

We have plenty of remnants of this metaphor cluster, like when we talk about emotions as something we are "filled with" that can be "let out" or "expelled" (emoted).

This way of speaking wouldn't have had as much power with older ways of describing experiences. In the Latin language, emotions were described with affect. Affect meant "to have done to," "to have been connected in such a way."

If a view affected us (with beauty), there's nothing to expel or let out—since their model of experiences and evaluations was not based on a metaphor of internal fluids, but external influence.

The point of all this isn't to chase history so much as it is to give us some distance from the modern meanings of these words. These words dominate how we think about ourselves—even when they're often confusing!

When we can step back and accept that these words are a bit strange, nebulous, and inconsistent today, we have more freedom to treat these words as one tool in a bigger toolbox—we aren't locked forever into one way of how we think about ourselves

Chapter summary

The general pattern to humor is made up of elements called humor tells. But there are individual patterns of humor within this general pattern—someone's humor profile.

  • Everyone has a unique humor profile. The more familiar we are with someone's humor profile, the more we can infer about their emotion state.
  • Even though humor profiles and variations of humor tells vary over time and place, it's all related to the same underlying process (mistakes and OKs) and involves a similar list of humor tells.
  • Humor tells are humor. We sometimes think of an emotion as a centralized thing ("first one thing, than many possible things"), but humor is a description of decentralized behavior instead ("many possible things first, then one description").

The Humor Book

About

In some ways, humor is the world's best and most interesting puzzle. How do we explain the most, most elegantly? We solve this by collecting clues from our day-to-day lives.

This makes a study of humor feel familiar, expected, unlocking, and surprising—all at once. It's like an equally-balanced certainty matrix: regarding humor, there are known knowns, known unknowns, unknown knowns, and unknown unknowns.

A study of humor mingles all these things together more than perhaps any other subject. It's not new—we have endless observations and experiences—we just haven't always put everything on the table. When we do so, it's a bit like unfolding our life for re-examination and puzzle-solving.

Everyone should learn about humor

You should learn more about humor. It doesn't matter if you're "good" or "bad" at humor—because everyone should know more about humor.

Humor substantially contributes to our "aura of ability"—our collective ability to:

  • form acquaintances and grow closer to them
  • to make successful arguments
  • to ask for and give aid
  • to interpret situations
  • to create
  • to be resilient

(and so on)

These are things we never finish improving at. Which is why everyone should learn about humor.

The elephant in the room

One of the challenges to learning about humor is just how big it is. It's a bit like the story of the Blind Mice and the Elephant. The blind mice come across a mysterious object, and each touch it to see what it is:

  • One blind mouse runs up the leg and says “it’s a tree trunk!”
  • Another goes up the tail, and says “it’s a rope!”
  • The last clambers around the ear and says “it’s a curtain!”

This is like humor! At different times, we've probably come across humor as:

  • sometimes comedy, sometimes tragedy
  • sometimes frivolous, sometimes important
  • sometimes mean, sometimes kind
  • sometimes related to anxiety, sometimes to confidence
  • sometimes related to shame, sometimes to pride
  • sometimes related to failure, sometimes to success
  • sometimes related to jokes, sometimes to straight-talk
  • sometimes about lies, sometimes about truth
  • sometimes about randomness, and sometimes about insight

(and so on)

What sort of elephant is this? In some ways, it's like having a jigsaw puzzle in front of us, but no box—how to we fit everything together?

Putting things harmoniously together is something we'll be doing throughout this book. And we'll eventually be able to step back and finally see the whole picture of humor clearly.

Dispelling myths

Along the way, we'll be freeing ourselves of some of the baggage we have about humor—a bunch of myths we don't always question, but don't quite make sense when actually laid out in sunlight.

These myths are probably pretty familiar to us:

  • I haven't been taught humor before. Therefore, humor can't be taught.
  • Some people seem naturally funny. Therefore, I can't become funnier.
  • Comedians are funny. Therefore, comedians are the standard to which everyone should be compared to, humorwise.
  • Some humor seems trivializing. Therefore, all humor is trivializing.
  • We sometimes laugh at jokes. Therefore everything we laugh at is a joke.
  • Funny movies sometimes have clever and witty scripts. Therefore, to make people laugh, I have to be clever and witty, like a script.

(and so on)

These informal logic fallacies can cause us a lot of trouble, so we'll be taking some time to iron these out. By untying these knots in our thinking, we'll find our thinking about humor becomes much lighter, truer, and more powerful.

This is a course on humor

Some humor books are fun because they expertly tell anecdotes in an amusing style. This humor book is fun because it's a textbook. You know what I mean?

This book is a course: it teaches something—an analytical model of humor. Then it expands this model into an observational system to make sense of what's happening around us, and why.

We'll use this model as a methodology for producing smiles and laughs—and as a tool we can use to draw inferences about what the absence or presence of laughter means about people, events, and places.

We'll go over all this at a pace and sequence that genuinely allows us the learn these things. We won't just talk about them, but actually learn them—by using thousands of vignettes, hundreds of graphs, dozens of case studies, and plenty of picture and video examples.

The goal is to finish the course fully comfortable with an elegant, organized, and productive worldview of humor.

My fieldwork on humor

Over the years, I've been an after school teacher, barista, canvasser, cashier, dance teacher, event coordinator, facilitator, greeter, tutor, and so on. In each of these positions, you sink or swim on your ability to generate smiles and laughs!

And like everyone in these roles, I was constantly around coworkers who were better than I was at generating smiles and laughs—it was often astonishing what they could do. I took a lot of notes, studying what worked, what didn't, when, on who, what happened after, and why all that might be.

These observations make up the theory in this book, which are grounded in day-to-day experiences.

I think of these observations as a kind of "fieldwork." A lot of interesting studies of humor are more like "bookwork"—they're structural analysis of comedies or satires, and aren't always grounded on the experiences of people. They also tends to leans toward description ("the eight kinds of jokes") rather than explanation ("the process of why we laugh at things in general")

In my experience, "explanation + fieldwork" fast tracks us to the central mysteries of humor, and brings us face-to-face with the questions about humor that are hardest, most interesting, and most productive.

Research

I did relatively little secondary research on humor for this book. In many ways, it's an independent look at things.

That means this book is not a good way to be introduced to academic or professional scholarship on humor and humor-adjacent topics—whether psychological, computational, neurological, or so on. This has upsides and downsides, depending on your goals.

The notable academic resource that I did use for this book was Warren Shibles' Online Humor Reference Guide—without question one of the weirdest books ever written. I'm grateful Shibles' book zapped my brain with its rays at an impressionable age. It set me on a path of humor-thinking in high school, which I've been running down since.

Everyone should learn about humor

You should learn more about humor. It doesn't matter if you're "good" or "bad" at humor—because everyone should know more about humor.

When we don't know analytical and applied humor methods, all sorts of bad things happen.

On a collective level, we end up in groups that are unable to create joyful experiences—as anyone who has taken a joyless class or attended a joyless meeting can attest.

On an individual level, we end up with difficulties of action and decisions. Humor substantially contributes to our "aura of ability:" our collective ability to form acquaintances and grow closer to them—to make successful arguments—to ask for and give aid—to interpret situations—to create—to be resilient—and so on. There's no reason to limit our aura of ability.

So, in short, everyone should learn about humor. And the resources to do so should be clear, accessible, coherent, practical, straightforward, and so on—like what's attempted in this book.

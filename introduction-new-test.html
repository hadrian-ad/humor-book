One of my favorite books, Rethinking Sitting, takes something ostensibly obvious and settled (chairs, sitting)—and hits it with a lightning bolt. A touch of catalytic insight and snap! the picture goes into focus, and at that point we realized it had been blurry all along.

Many of our observations are waiting to come alive like this—and, when they do, how we look at the world is permanently changed.

I'm sure you can see where I'm going with this: we'll be doing something similar to humor in this book. My goal is for you to notice things that, once seen once, can't ever be unseen—and for you to smile as this happens. It'll be fun!

I am a teacher of humor. My goal is to guide people through their own observations of smiling and laughing to help them see things that have been right in front of their eyes. This isn't exactly like learning to be funny—it's something broader and much more inclusive.

Most of us grew up with little exposure humor education—and then usually only unsophisticated and rudimentary ideas on it. For instance, you've probably taken a geometry class—but probably never a humor class.

The consequences of this kind of illiteracy are too long to list. I think it's part of why serious instruction on humor resonates with so many people. People are ready for the power and strength of orientation that comes from studying smiling and laughing.

I grew up as an independent theorist of humor, which happened exactly by accident. In high school, I stumbled across the monumental (and monumentally weird) Online Humor Reference Guide by Warren Shibles—and in the scattered, contradictory chapters there started in me a little fire that led to two decades of observing smiles and laughters everywhere I went.

My large body of fieldwork and notes was collected not from performers and movies but from day-to-day interactions, where smiles and laughs are common and, in my opinion, much more interesting.

These observations and notes often directly make up the examples used in this book, like this one:

Jessica: *to a stranger* “Oh, I love your style!”

Yasmin turns red, beams, and laughs. (←Yasmin smiles and laughs)

Yasmin: (pleased) Thanks!”

I followed this by developing an instructive sequence to help others notice and make sense of these smiles and laughs. After much practice and refinement, I've produced it in this book here.

I'm hear to tell you—it's completely normal for everyone to learn about humor. There are patterns and organizations to our thinking that are obvious once pointed out, but are not easy to discover on our own. It's like anything else!

This book focuses on "humor dynamics"—that is, humor over time. We frown as we hear a joke, pause to think, laugh a bit, feel lightly amused for a while, forget about it, think about it later and laugh, tell a friend and have them get angry at us. There's a lot going on here! Understanding all of this is the goal of this book—humor dynamics.

As we do this, we'll learn how to generate humor with the help of things we don’t always think of as “humor”—like arguments, compliments, favors, gifts, gratitude, insults, looks, needling, rough-housing, teasing, timing, and so on. Sometimes we can also generate humor by choosing to do nothing at all.

Humor has a reputation for being unteachable or unlearnable—but it's not! Like anything else, humor is something that can be taught and learned. That's because humor happens as a coherent process—we can learn the universal pattern to humor, which lets us better predict, describe, and use humor.

This universal pattern of humor has been in front of us all our lives, which is why it'll seem both familiar and surprising at the same time. Since we've been observing humor all our lives, we have all the data we need to make sense of humor—we're usually just need some catalytic insight to put all these observations into the same system.

To provide this insight, this book will be both analytic and narrative. We'll use an analytical model of humor to stay organized in our thinking, while grounding this model to hundreds of narrative stories about daily life. Together, these two approaches will help us arrive at an elegant, comprehensive, and practical way of thinking about humor.

All this will be fun, in a serious way. Understanding humor is like putting together an enormous puzzle—a puzzle where the clues have been scattered everywhere, across all the hours of all our days. As these pieces come together, we'll not only better understand ourselves and others—we'll feel more freedom of action in the world.

We'll dive right in. Good luck! by the end, you'll know more about humor than just about anyone—and see firsthand that the more we know about humor, the more true improvement we find in the quality of our lives.

By the end, we'll want to be able to easily answer questions like: "why did I find that funny?" or "how could someone possibly laugh at that?!"

We'll learn other things too—including about how humor relates to things like seriousness, "darkness," appropriateness, connection, meanness, insight, and so on. In other words—we'll be learning a lot!

Hey! I'm Hadrian. I've been working on this book for several years, and I'm excited to be able to share it with more people!

I started this book when I realized there weren't many good teaching guides to humor—and the ones that did exist tended to be incoherent or narrow. Above all, I thought that they didn't really capture the full order and beauty there was to humor.

We'll be coming to grips with this order and beauty throughout this book, and it will be wildly fun. We'll find coherence where we thought was randomness, and potency in ourselves where we thought were limitations.

So what is this book about?

This book answers an initially puzzling question:"why do we sometimes laugh, and sometimes don't laugh?"

More broadly, this book is about all the questions we have about smiling and laughing—all the when, what, why, who, how, how much, how long, so what, what if, and so on. These answers together comprise what we'll be calling "humor."

At the outset, it's worth noting that this book is about much more than just comedy. We do sometimes laugh at jokes—but then again, we also sometimes laugh at insults, feasts, gifts, hugs, weather, vegetables, and so on. So why stick with jokes, when we could stick with laughter?

When we explain laughter, this will explain why we sometimes laugh at jokes—but also why we sometimes laugh at insults, feasts, gifts, hugs—and everything else besides. We'll find the simplest answer that explains the most things. Elephant in the room One of the challenges of learning about humor is just how big it is. It's a bit like the story of the Blind Mice and the Elephant, where each blind mouse declares what they think it is they're touching: One blind mouse runs up the leg and says “it’s a tree trunk!” Another goes up the tail, and says “it’s a rope!” The last clambers around the ear and says “it’s a curtain!” This is like humor, where at at different times, we come across humor as: sometimes comedy, sometimes tragedy sometimes frivolous, sometimes important sometimes mean, sometimes kind sometimes related to anxiety, sometimes to confidence sometimes related to success, sometimes to failure sometimes related to jokes, sometimes to straight-talk sometimes about truth, sometimes about lies sometimes about insight, and sometimes about randomness (and so on) What sort of elephant is this? In some ways, it's like having a jigsaw puzzle in front of us but no box. We have to arrange the pieces harmoniously together, and then see what the picture is. Preconceived notions As we get closer to assembling this Big Picture of humor, we'll be untangling many of our tangled knots about humor. These knots are fallacies in our informal logic—many of which we may recognize: "I can't teach others humor. Therefore humor can't be taught." "Some people are naturally funny. Therefore I can't be funnier." "Comedians are funny. Therefore, comedians are the standard to which we should all compare our humor skills too." "Some humor is trivializing. Therefore, all humor is trivializing." "We sometimes laugh at jokes. Therefore everything we laugh at is a joke." "While my mom and I laugh around each other a lot, she's never told a single joke. Therefore, she's bad at humor." "Funny movies sometimes have clever and witty scripts. Therefore, to make people laugh, I'd have to be clever and witty." "People laughed when I spoke up. Therefore I should feel shame." (and so on) When put starkly like this, it's often easy to see these conclusions don't follow. These are the kinds of things we'll want to unlearn—a process that will make our thinking much lighter, truer, and powerful. We'll be dispelling myths around humor—like that humor can't be taught. As these myths are broken down, we'll feel a bit like we're opening the curtains to the world—something that lets in more light and gives us a beautiful view.

About

Hey! I'm Hadrian, the author of this book. I'm a humor theorist with a specific interest in teaching humor. To everyone.

Everyone should learn about humor!

When I tell people they should learn about humor, sometimes they take it as a slight. They often reply with something like: "I'm a pretty normal person! I smile, I get jokes. So do I really need to learn more about humor?"

And my reply is always: yes! Everyone should learn more about humor. Humor is something with no skill ceiling and few borders. There's a dynamic of lifetime learning going on.

That's why the best and worst people at humor are both still learning about it—just like we are.

Even though this book starts "at the beginning," it's not remedial instruction. Instead, this book provides catalytic insight, helping us organize a lifetime of observations and experiences with humor into a coherent system.

Who is this course for?

This course has practical value to all kinds of people. From experience, I can say this course helps:

  • architects and interior designers (think about why some arrangements lead to more smiles over time than others)
  • facilitators and service workers (better handle conflict and create compromise)
  • musicians and painters (make conscious creative choices leading to smiles)
  • parents and children (better understand each other)
  • performers and public speakers (find new perspectives on their audience)
  • researchers and historians (use a coherent analytical model of humor)
  • romantic partners and family members (more broadly generate laughter in people they care about)
  • teachers and instructors (gain insights about their class and methods)
  • transplants and transfers (better form connections with strangers, neighbors, and coworkers)

(and so on)

If you'd like to improve at any of the skills or outcomes above, then this course is for you.

In this book, "humor" refers to the process through which smiles or laughs do or don't happen. That means this isn't a book about "jokes," but about something much broader instead.

For instance, we sometimes laugh at:

  • success
  • failure
  • hugs
  • violence
  • cute things
  • gross things
  • jokes
  • compliments
  • vegetables

(and so on)

This book will explain the presence or absence of laughter in all cases. This is inclusive to jokes, but also treats them as incidental. The real prize is understanding smiling and laughter generally.

This is a course on humor

This book isn't a series of amusing anecdotes—instead, it's a course. It teaches something: an analytical model of humor.

We'll expand this model into methodological and observational systems that will help us make sense of what's happening around us.

We won't just talk about these things, but actually learn them too—by using thousands of vignettes, hundreds of graphs, dozens and dozens of case studies, and plenty of picture and video examples.

The focus of this book will always be on realistic people involved in realistic situations. This will keep our analytical model grounded to our actual experiences.

The analytical model in this book is used as a means to an end. The "end" is greater intuitive understanding of humor. Once we arrive there, the "means" doesn't matter so much.

You'll gain a lot from this course

Over the decade I've spent observing and thinking about humor, I've come to find it's hard to overstate how important an understanding of humor is. That's because our skill at humor substantially and practically makes up our aggragate ability to:

  • ask for and give aid
  • adapt and be resilient
  • convince others or effectively share viewpoints
  • create things in ways that people (including ourselves) appreciate
  • enjoy new and old things
  • form acquaintances and grow closer to them
  • interpret situations
  • participate effectively in discussions and organizations

(and so on)

Since humor is tightly embedded into what makes all these things happen, learning about humor means learning about the process and methods in which all these things happen!

Our goal is to understand how this all works—and how to do it all better than we do right now. In that sense, this is kind of an exciting book—it's about increasing a kind of aura of ability.

There are many myths about humor—like that humor is abstract, unknowable, undefinable, unteachable, and so on. None of those are true. Usually, those statements describe the speaker more than they describe humor: "humor is unteachable (by me)."

Since humor is a coherent process, we can model it. We can use this model to describe and predict outcomes. This has transformational ramifications on how we see and use humor—leading to an improvement in our intuitive relation to humor.

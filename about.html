About

Hey! I'm Hadrian, the author of this book. I'm a humor theorist with a specific interest in teaching humor. To everyone.

Everyone should learn about humor!

When I tell people they should learn about humor, they sometimes take it personally. "I'm a pretty normal person!" they say, "I smile, I get jokes—so do I really need to learn more about humor?"

And my reply is always: Yes! Everyone should learn more about humor. Humor is something with few borders and no skill ceiling. There's a lifetime dynamic going on—we're never done learning about it.

Even though this book starts "at the beginning," it's not remedial instruction. Instead, this book provides catalytic insight, helping us organize a lifetime of humor observations and experiences into a coherent system.

Of course, outside of any practical value, I think this book is also quite interesting! In many ways, humor is like the world's most interesting puzzle. It's fun to see how it can all be elegantly fit together and explained.

In this book, "humor" refers to the process through which smiles or laughs do or don't happen. This means this isn't a book about "jokes," but about something much broader.

For instance, we sometimes smile or laugh at:

  • success
  • failure
  • hugs
  • violence
  • cute things
  • gross things
  • jokes
  • compliments
  • insults
  • vegetables

(and so on)

This book will explain the presence (or absence) of laughter in all cases. This is inclusive to jokes, but also treats them as incidental. The real prize is understanding smiling and laughter generally.

This is a course on humor

This book isn't a series of amusing anecdotes—instead, it's a course. It teaches something: an analytical model of humor. We'll use this model to create methods of describing, generating, and observing humor.

We won't just talk about these things, but actually learn them too—by using thousands of vignettes, hundreds of graphs, dozens and dozens of case studies, and plenty of picture and video examples.

We'll always focus on realistic people involved in realistic situations. This will keep our analytical model grounded to our actual experiences, and avoid getting weird or abstract.

This is a brisk course of moderate length. It takes most readers in the ballpark of 10 hours to complete. Most readers find the time spent to be enjoyable, interesting, empowering, and personally relevant.

The study of foundational skills

The formal study of humor takes us to some pretty important fundamentals. That's because humor is involved in our ability to:

  • ask for and give aid
  • adapt and be resilient
  • convince others or effectively share viewpoints
  • create things in ways that people (including ourselves) appreciate
  • enjoy new and old things
  • form acquaintances and grow closer to them
  • interpret situations
  • participate effectively in discussions and organizations

(and so on)

When we learn about humor, we learn about the process and methods in which all of these things happen—which in turn lets us do them better, and understand others better.

There are many myths about humor—like that humor is abstract, unknowable, undefinable, unteachable, and so on. None of those are true. Usually, those statements describe the speaker more than they describe humor: "humor is unteachable (by me)."

About

Hey! I'm Hadrian, the author of Humor in Various Moods and Meters. I'm a humor theorist with a specific interest in teaching humor. To everyone.

Everyone should learn about humor!

When I tell people they should learn about humor, they sometimes take it personally. "I'm a pretty normal person!" they say, "I smile, I get jokes—so do I really need to learn more about humor?"

And my reply is always: Yes! Everyone should learn more about humor. Especially the dynamics of humor—where it comes from, where it goes, what it does, and how things change because of it.

While we all have an intuitive understanding of humor, not many of us are able to put this intuition into words. Beyond that, humor is something with few borders and no skill ceiling—there's a lifetime interaction going on, and we're never done learning about it.

More interestingly, you'll find there are things about humor that you didn't know you didn't know—ideas of broad application and universal interest. We'll be discovering these things together throughout this book.

Even though this book starts "at the beginning," it's not remedial instruction. Instead, this book provides catalytic insight, helping us organize a lifetime of humor observations and experiences into a coherent system.

Okay, "humor dynamics"—what's that again?

What's the difference between studying garden flowers, and studying gardening? Between studying the names of trees and studying ecology? Well, one's the study of something—the other is the study of the system that that "something" happens in.

It's similar for humor dynamics. It's the difference between studying "jokes" and studying the the deeper system that does or doesn't produce smiles or laughter—including aspects of duration and magnitude, and evolving relations to people around us.

Using a math analogy, going from humor to humor dynamics is a bit like moving from humor-arithmetic to humor-algebra. Like moving into algebra, a whole new world of understanding and application opens.

That maybe sounds challenging—but it's actually only as challenging as the material makes it. This book is written so that it can be learned at a normal reading pace, like a normal book—without being made artificially challenging by the absence of examples or muddy language.

Humor isn't "about" jokes for the same reason gardening isn't "about" carrots. Instead, like gardening, humor is a process that is inclusive to a variety of things, but not defined by them.

Here, humor refers to the process to and function of smiles and laughs. This is something immensely broad—immensely broader than jokes—since we smile and laugh at all kinds of things, including when we:

  • recieve good hugs
  • hear slicing truths
  • see ugly dogs
  • hear flattering compliments about us
  • pick up funky vegetables in the store
  • see our friend walking outside
  • step into a beautiful room

and so on

Complicating all this is that we sometimes don't smile or laugh at those things. Understanding the presence or absence of laughter in all these cases is the prize.

When we have this prize, we'll understand why we sometimes do and sometimes don't laugh at jokes—but that will be incidental to a much broader understanding of smiling and laughing in general.

There are many myths about humor—like that humor is abstract, unknowable, undefinable, unteachable, and so on. None of those are true. Usually, those statements describe the speaker more than they describe humor: "humor is unteachable (by me)."

Myths around humor proliferate due to many disconnected conclusions. The reasoning here is often familiar when it's pointed out:

  • "I can't teach others humor. Therefore humor can't be taught."
  • "Some people are naturally funny. Therefore I can't be funnier."
  • "Comedians are funny. Therefore, comedians are the standard to which we should all compare our humor skills too."
  • "Some humor is trivializing. Therefore, all humor is trivializing."
  • "We sometimes laugh at jokes. Therefore everything we laugh at is a joke."
  • "Funny movies sometimes have clever and witty scripts. Therefore, to make people laugh, I'd have to be clever and witty."
  • "People laughed when I spoke up. Therefore I should feel shame."

    • (and so on)

When put starkly like this, it's often easy to see these conclusions don't follow. We'll be unlearning these incoherent statements in exchange for something elegant and explanative.

You can think of this book as a course

People usually have expectations when I tell them about my "humor book." Surely, they think, this book will dip into amusing personal anecdotes, and digress into jokes or gags. How wrong they are!

Instead, this is written a goal-oriented book that teaches something: an analytical model of humor. We'll use this model to create methods of describing, generating, and observing humor.

We won't just talk about these things, but actually learn them too—by using thousands of vignettes, hundreds of graphs, dozens and dozens of case studies, and plenty of picture and video examples.

We'll always focus on humor happening to people in real situations, over time—the dynamics of it all. This will keep our analytical model grounded to our actual experiences, and avoid getting weird or abstract.

It'll all be fun—not because it's full of well-styled anecdotes, but because it's fun to solve puzzles, improve skills, and see the world in refreshed ways are all fun.

This is a brisk course of moderate length. It takes most readers in the ballpark of 10 hours to complete. Most readers find the time spent to be enjoyable, interesting, empowering, and personally relevant.

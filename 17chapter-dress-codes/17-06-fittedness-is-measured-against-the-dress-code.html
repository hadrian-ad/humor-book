Comparing fittedness to dress code

We can put everything together to look at a few examples:

Zoe is browsing through the used bookstore for fun. (←Zoe doesn't need to be particularly efficient or cohesive↔︎browsing has low dress code)

She finds a book called Ferdydurke. (←humor attempt with low fittedness)

She smiles. (←low dress code and low fittedness↔︎fitted humor)

Owen is writing an email to Justin. For fun, he writes “He cries FERDYDURKE!” in the subject lines

Justin is amused and annoyed. Why doesn’t anyone ever read the email etiquette guidelines on the syllabus? (←Owen and Justin disagree on the humor dress code)

Above, there was disagreement on what the humor dress code should be, and so end up with different views on whether the subject line was fitted.

Consider:

Justin has to juggle emails from all twenty-five students—Owen just has to juggle emails from one professor. It's not surprising they have different initial estimates of efficiency and cohesion needs.

Tucker: *to Amber* “Okay. Pun-time. Here we go: I’m a big fan of whiteboards. I find them totally re-markable!” (←humor attempt with low fittedness)

It’s the end of the day. Amber can put up with Tucker's bad jokes while she packs up for the day. (←Amber thinks there's less need for efficiency and cohesion at the end of the day↔︎fitted)

- OR -

It's the busiest time of the day. Amber looks up, feeling a bit frazzled by everything. She does not have time for his bad jokes. (←Amber thinks there's more need for efficiency and cohesion during the middle of the day↔︎unfitted)

Tucker: *to Amber* “Amber, great work with setting up that meeting today. It was flawless. I don’t know what we’d do without you.” (←humor attempt with high fittedness)

It's the end of the day, and Amber smiles as she packs up to go home. (←Amber thinks there's less need for efficiency and cohesion at the end of the day↔︎fitted)

- OR -

Amber gives a quick smile. It's during the busiest part of the day, but she appreciates the comment. (←Amber thinks there's more need for efficiency and cohesion at the end of the day↔︎fitted)

Tucker’s pun had a low fittedness, since it wasn't on relevant topics and wasn’t trying to reach humor family words. Amber only thought it was fitted when there was a low dress code.

By contrast, Tucker’s affirmation had a high fittedness, since it was on relevant topics and was trying to make Amber feel appreciation, connection, belonging, delight, and so on.

Because the affirmation had a high fittedness, Amber thought it fit the emotional situation whether or not there was a high or low dress code.

Context. Sometimes we're surprised that humor attempts are recieved differently in different places—we think there should be a flat dress code everywhere.

It's worth noticing that this gives a double standard for humor that we don't give to other emotions. We're not surprised that an erotic photo or a long rant are treated one way in one situation, and another way in another.

For the same reason, we shouldn't be surprised that jokes or humor attempts are treated differently in one situation than another.

That's it for this chapter!

It's not surprising that there are places with high dress codes—like advocacy organizations, charities, classrooms, international offices, big-tent political parties, public agencies, and so on. These all are goal-oriented groups (need effectiveness) and have varied clients, customers, members, supporters, and so on (need cohesion).

This means that our humor dress code goes up as we're interfacting with these groups, and goes down again when we’re not. These groups want to avoid pointlesly fragmenting the emotion networks associated with them, while at the same time promoting effective actions.

From the outside looking in, we can feel a relative gap with their dress code—from the outside, we won't have the same need for efficiency or cohesion. This can make us criticize their humor dress codes, and feel that they would be stifling to incorporate into our own lives.

That's probably true! At the same time, we shouldn't judge humor dress codes by whether we should incorporate them into every situation. We're not supposed to incorporate those dress codes—they've been set for different needs than we have.

There may be good reasons to criticize an institution's humor dress code—but the fact that their dress code is different than our dress code at home by itself isn't a good reason.

Humor or humor attempts need a minimum amount of fittedness to be fitted. However, this minimum amount is constantly changing. This changing minimum amount is the "humor dress code."

  • Humor dress codes change as our needs for efficiency and cohesion change.
  • The humor dress code is the minimum standard for the complexity and coherence of our interactions. This doesn't weed out humor, so much as weed out the things that don't match the needs of the moment.
  • Humor dress codes are set in collaboration with everyone around us. If not everyone agrees, words are used to describe the dress code like flippant or snooty.

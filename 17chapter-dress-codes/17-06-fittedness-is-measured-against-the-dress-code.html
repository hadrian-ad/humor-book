Fittedness is measured against the dress code

We can put everything together to look at a few examples:

Zoe is browsing through the used bookstore for fun. (←Zoe doesn't need to be particularly efficient or cohesive↔︎browsing has low dress code)

She finds a book called Ferdydurke. (←humor attempt with low fittedness)

She smiles. (←low dress code and low fittedness↔︎fitted humor)

Owen is writing an email to Justin. For fun, he writes “He cries FERDYDURKE!” in the subject lines

Justin is amused and annoyed. Why doesn’t anyone ever read the email etiquette guidelines on the syllabus? (←Owen and Justin disagree on the humor dress code)

Above, there was disagreement on what the humor dress code should be, and so end up with different views on whether the subject line was fitted.

Consider:

Justin has to juggle emails from all twenty-five students—Owen just has to juggle emails from one professor. It's not surprising they have different initial estimates of efficiency and cohesion needs.

Tucker: *to Amber* “Okay. Pun-time. Here we go: I’m a big fan of whiteboards. I find them totally re-markable!” (←humor attempt with low fittedness)

It’s the end of the day. Amber can put up with Tucker's bad jokes while she packs up for the day. (←Amber thinks there's less need for efficiency and cohesion at the end of the day↔︎fitted)

- OR -

It's the busiest time of the day. Amber looks up, feeling a bit frazzled by everything. She does not have time for his bad jokes. (←Amber thinks there's more need for efficiency and cohesion during the middle of the day↔︎unfitted)

Tucker: *to Amber* “Amber, great work with setting up that meeting today. It was flawless. I don’t know what we’d do without you.” (←humor attempt with high fittedness)

It's the end of the day, and Amber smiles as she packs up to go home. (←Amber thinks there's less need for efficiency and cohesion at the end of the day↔︎fitted)

- OR -

Amber gives a quick smile. It's during the busiest part of the day, but she appreciates the comment. (←Amber thinks there's more need for efficiency and cohesion at the end of the day↔︎fitted)

Tucker’s pun had a low fittedness, since it wasn't on relevant topics and wasn’t trying to reach humor family words. Amber only thought it was fitted when there was a low dress code.

By contrast, Tucker’s affirmation had a high fittedness, since it was on relevant topics and was trying to make Amber feel appreciation, connection, belonging, delight, and so on.

Because the affirmation had a high fittedness, Amber thought it fit the emotional situation whether or not there was a high or low dress code.

That's it for this chapter! We'll talk about humor dress codes next.

Sometimes we think there should be a flat dress code everywhere, and we're surprised when humor attempts are recieved differently in different places. We think a humor attempt that's good among friends should be good in a classroom or on a public social media profile.

It's worth noticing that this gives a double standard for humor that other emotions don't have. We know that attempts to make others feel affection, anger, exuberance, frustration, guilt, pleasure, sadness, and so on are treated differently in different situations.

We're not surprised that an erotic photo or a long rant, or an expensive gift is treated one way when shared in a lecture hall, and another when shared with our partner. And for similar reasons, we shouldn't be surprised when an erotic or angry joke is also treated one way in one place, and another way in another.

Humor is a regular emotion—it's not surprising that emotion attempts are reacted to different in different places.

Similary, it's not surprising that we encounter places with high dress codes—like advocacy organizations, charities, classrooms, international offices, big-tent political parties, public agencies, and so on. These all are goal oriented groups (need effectiveness) and have varied clients, customers, members, supporters, and so on (need cohesion).

This means that our humor dress code goes up while actively participating in these groups, and goes down again when we’re not. They want to avoid pointlesly fragmenting the emotion networks associated with them, while at the same time promoting effective actions.

From the outside looking in, we won't have the same understanding of the needs for efficiency or cohesion—and so we may criticize how their humor dress code seems to us.

To be fitted, a humor attempt needs a minimum amount of fittedness. We're this minimum fittedness the "humor dress code."

  • Emotion networks arrive at consensus on the situational requirement for efficiency and cohesion—the humor dress code. As the need for effectiveness and cohesion can vary quickly, humor dress codes also vary quickly.
  • We can censure humor or humor attempts that fall below the humor dress code. We tolerate degrees of censure proportional to the deficiency of fittedness relative to the humor dress code.
  • We experience desynchronization as censure. This feedback allows for calibration toward harmony on an emotion network. This is a normal, often gentle part of interactions. Censure only occasionally becomes confrontational or high stakes.

Consider:

Our humor dress code is the minimum standard for the complexity and coherence of our interactions—and this standard is constantly changing as the emotional situation changes.

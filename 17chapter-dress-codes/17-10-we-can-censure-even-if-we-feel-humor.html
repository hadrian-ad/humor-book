The Humor Book

We can censure even if we feel humor

We can censure humor or a humor attempt, even if we feel humor:

Zoe hugs a stranger.

Stranger: (confused) *smiles* "Um." (←stranger can feel humor and censure at the same time)

Jasper does a cringey fake accent, and Sam and a stranger laugh. They meet each other’s eyes, shaking their head a bit. (←Sam and a stranger can feel humor and censure at the same time)

Emma: “Trevor was dressed like a carrot today.”

Zoe: “Emma.” *laughs* (←Zoe can feel humor and censure Emma at the same time)

Owen is writing an email to Justin. For fun, in the subject line he writes “He cries FERDYDURKE!”

Justin is amused and annoyed. (←Justin can feel humor and censure Owen at the same time)

Owen, Zoe, and Emma are studying in the library. Emma keeps opening and closing her mouth.

Owen: *smiling* “What are you doing?”

Emma: “I’m pretending I’m a penguin. Do you think penguins have teeth?”

Owen: (confused) “Teeth?”

Emma: (curiously) *opens her mouth*

Owen and Zoe laugh.

Owen: *laughs* “Do you think robins and crows have teeth too?”

Emma: *laughs* “But penguins are, I don’t know… different!”

Owen: *laughs* “Different how?”

Emma: *laughs* “They’re teethy! I don’t know!”

Zoe has been laughing, but shakes her head.

Zoe: *smiling* (regretfully) “C’mon, we should stay focused.” (←Zoe can enjoy unfitted humor, while also censuring it)

Jessica and Tom are washing dishes at the dish pit.

Jessica: *offers sprayer* “Sprayer?”

Tom: (mock confused) “But I hardly know her!”

Jessica: (confused) “What?”

Tom: (embarrassed) “Spray 'er? I hardly know ‘er.”

Jessica: *gets it* *laughs* (vaguely disapproving) (←Jessica censures Tom even though she laughed)

Tom: *apologetically* “Sorry.” (←Tom censures himself)

Jessica: “No, you’re all good. I laughed.” (←Jessica equates humor with fittedness)

Tom: “No, I should probably keep vaguely misogynistic jokes to a minimum at work.” (←Tom censures himself again.)

Jessica: “Well. Probably!”

Above, just because Jessica felt humor doesn’t mean a humor attempt can’t also be unfitted. That’s because we can feel an emotion of humor at the same time our judgement can be that it’s unfitted.

Consider:

This is obvious with other emotions. We might feel anger, but that by itself doesn’t tell us anything about whether the anger is fitted or not. Simply having an emotion in our emotional state doesn’t mean it’s fitted.

We often censure ourselves too:

Emma: (sad) “I dropped my coffee on the way here”

Zoe: *laughs* *restrains laughter* (←Zoe censures herself to resynchronize with Emma) “Sorry. That sucks.”

When Zoe censures herself, that doesn’t mean she thinks her humor was “bad”—she’s just resynchronizing with her emotion network. Zoe is showing her commitment to maintaining a mutually agreeable standard of efficiency and cohesion.

Rule-breaking. Unfitted humor attempts can be fun, important, and useful—when they avoid censure. Sometimes, these can be useful even when they're censured. For instance, sometimes:

  • a shocking joke breaks the ice
  • a needling comment leads to a productive conversation
  • unexpected swearing brings people together
  • an off-color joke sets off a redeeming riff
  • a random joke starts a conversation
  • an out-of-the-blue invitation starts a friendship

(and so on)

We often observe and admire instances of this that are done well, and want to learn to do them ourselves. Counterintuitively, we learn to do these things well by first learning the formal rules well. This gives us the emotional sophistication to productively bend the rules.

In many ways, this is like real dress codes, for clothes. The better we understand the rules, the better we can break those rules in ways others appreciate instead of finding erratic or underwhelming.

We're allowed to censure unfitted humor

We censure humor or humor attempts by not synchronizing fully with someone.

In most cases, this is often simply not expressing an equal amount of humor tells:

Emma sees Owen selling candy in the hallway.

Emma: (surprised) “You’re selling candy?” *laughs* “What are you, a girl scout?”

Owen: (annoyed) “No.” (←Owen withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Owen censures Emma by not feeling humor↔︎Owen thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Owen, Zoe, and Emma are studying in the library. Emma keeps opening and closing her mouth.

Owen: “What are you doing?”

Emma: “I’m pretending I’m a penguin. Do you think penguins have teeth?”

Zoe: “I don’t know.” (←Zoe withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe censures Emma by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Jennifer: *checks phone* They do a daily check-in on my dad at his home. He always texts me after, but he’s late today.”

Amber: “Check-in every day at the nursing home?” *smiles* “Is it like a Black Death cart that goes door-to-door?”

Jennifer: “Um. It’s just staff knocking on the door.” (←Jennifer withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Jennifer censures Amber by not feeling humor↔︎Jennifer thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Emma pulls out her phone, but is stupefied to see she accidentally grabbed Owen’s phone. Zoe doubles over with laughter at Emma’s expression.

Emma: (stupefied) “It’s not funny! Where’s my phone?”

Zoe laughs harder, and Emma gets more and more annoyed.

Emma: “I wish you would take this seriously.” (←Emma withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Emma censures Zoe by not feeling humor↔︎Emma feels the humor attempt was below dress code)

Zoe: *smiling* “Okay, okay, sorry.”

Tucker pokes his head out of his office to talk to Amber.

Tucker: “Okay. Pun-time. Here we go: I’m a big fan of whiteboards. I find them totally re-markable!”

Amber: “Ha ha Tucker, not bad, but you know I’m pretty busy.” (←Amber withholds synchronization by expressing less humor↔︎Amber censures Tucker by not feeling humor↔︎Amber thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

Emma and Owen are watching videos of a prankster tricking people into walking into poles. Emma and Owen guffaw at each successful prank. But Zoe isn’t so amused by them. She just tilts her head and twists her mouth instead of laughing. (←Zoe withholds synchronization by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe censures Emma and Owen by not feeling humor↔︎Zoe thinks the humor attempt was below dress code)

We experience desynchronization as censure because we want others to synchronize with us—or for others to let us synchronize to them.

Essentially, someone is sharing an evaluatory perspective and by showing disagreement with that perspective, we censure them.

Consider:

In this book, “censure” is just withheld synchronization. That's often a lighter definition of censure than how we use the word in day-to-day conversations.

Under this definition, most of the day-to-day censure we receive isn't a big deal to us—it's just something done by everyone, including friends or family, in ways we think of as neither good nor bad, but just how things work.

Censure. “Censure” and “censor” are similar sounding words that mean different things. To censure is to condemn, while to censor is to suppress communication.

  • Censure: if a group of academics publish a letter condemning us, we have been censured. If our parents tell us it was inappropriate to make that humor attempt about Grandma, we have been censured. If people on social media react poorly to the joke we post, we have been censured. If our friend vaguely smiles at our joke in a way we feel is unenthusiastic, we have been censured.
  • Censor: if a government commands an organization remove something we wrote, we have been censored. If a militia threatens us with violence if we continue airing a show, we have been censored. If a forum blots out swear words, the forum has censored those words.

Generally, censure comes from within emotion network, in the form of desynchronization—while censorship is something imposed on an emotional network from outside of it.

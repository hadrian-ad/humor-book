We feel it's justified to censure unfitted humor

The humor dress code tells us when humor is vulnerable to censure.

Consider:

This is like real dress codes, for clothes. If the dress code is "all black," we know when others will find censure justified. ("Yellow? Did you miss the memo?")

If a humor attempt is above the dress code, it's resistant to censure. If a humor attempt is below the dress code, it's vulnerable to censure.

The dress code sets when censure of humor or humor attempts feels justified.

When someone feels humor or a humor attempt is below dress code (not meeting a minimum standard for efficiency and cohesion), they feel censure of it is justified. If they feel it's above dress code (meeting a minimum standard for efficiency and cohesion), they'll feel censure of it is not justified.

Consider:

We used an extreme examples above (abandoning mission control versus not), but the same dynamic happens all across our lives.

People may cheer when donuts are brought into a classroom, or grin or stretch during a conversation—and we can intuitively imagine moments where these are either unfitted and fitted—where censure of them feels justified or not.

Emotion dress codes. We have dress codes for all our emotions. For instance, just like humor, anger isn’t always tolerated or always censured—instead, it depends on whether the anger is fitted or not.

When anger is fitted, it’s seen as justified—and we describe that justified anger as natural or righteous. When anger is instead unfitted, it’s seen as unjustified—and we describe that unjustified anger as petty, a tantrum, thin-skinned, whining, and so on.

The fittedness of anger depends on how much it fits the situation. If we’re angry because someone put our children in danger, it's more fitted than if we’re angry because someone forgot the ketchup. We have a similar awareness of situational fittedness for any emotion, including humor or sadness.

20. Dress codes

In previous chapters, we've looked at fittedness—how well humor or humor attempts fit in a situation:

In this chapter, we'll be looking at how much fittedness is needed in a given situation—what we'll call the humor dress code:

Consider:

We can understand humor dress codes with an analogy to money—if we have $5, is that enough or not enough?

The question doesn't make much sense until we also know what we want to buy! If we want to buy candy, $5 is enough—but if we want to buy pizza, $5 is not enough.

We’ll be doing the same thing with fittedness. Without also knowing the humor dress code, fittedness it doesn't tell us much.

We’ll be going over this in greater detail throughout this chapter.

Fitted and unfitted. In this book, something that's "fitted" is above dress code, while something that's "unfitted" is below the dress code.

This means what fits and what doesn't is relative to a dress code. A humor attempt with fittedness of "5" will be unfitted if the dress code is "6," but fitted if the dress code is "4":

Being "fitted" or "unfitted" is a relative term to the dress code. That means it's not enough to know fittedness by itself—since we also need the dress code.

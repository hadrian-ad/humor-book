The Humor Book

Fittedness is measured against the dress code

Similar things can be fitted or unfitted based on the situation's humor dress code:

Zoe is browsing through the used bookstore for fun. (←browsing has low dress code)

She finds a book called Ferdydurke. (←humor attempt with low fittedness)

She smiles. (←low dress code and low fittedness↔︎fitted humor)

- OR -

Owen is writing an email to Justin. (←writing to superior has high dress code)

For fun, in the subject lines he writes “He cries FERDYDURKE!” (←humor attempt with low fittedness and high dress code)

Justin is amused and annoyed. Why doesn’t anyone ever read the email etiquette guidelines on the syllabus? (←humor attempt with low fittedness and high dress code↔︎unfitted)

Amber is at the receptionist's deck. Tucker pokes his out of his office.

Tucker: *to Amber* “Okay. Pun-time. Here we go: I’m a big fan of whiteboards. I find them totally re-markable!” (←humor attempt with low fittedness)

It’s the end of the day. Amber is packing up to go home, relieved the day is over. She can put up with Tucker's bad jokes. (←low dress code and low fittedness↔︎fitted)

- OR -

Amber looks up, feeling a bit frazzled by everything. It's during the busiest part of the day. She does not have time for his bad jokes. (←high dress code and low fittedness↔︎unfitted)

Amber is at the receptionist's deck. Tucker pokes his out of his office.

Tucker: *to Amber* “Amber, great work with setting up that meeting today. It was flawless. I don’t know what we’d do without you.” (←humor attempt with high fittedness)

It’s the end of the day. Amber is packing up to go home, relieved the day is over. She smiles at Tucker's validation. (←low dress code and high fittedness↔︎fitted)

- OR -

Amber gives a quick smile. It's during the busiest part of the day, but she appreciates the comment. (←high dress code and fittedness↔︎fitted)

Tucker’s pun had a low fittedness, since it wasn't on relevant topics and wasn’t trying to reach humor family words. It was only fitted when there was an even lower dress code.

By contrast, Tucker’s affirmation had a high fittedness, since it was on relevant topics and was trying to make Amber feel appreciation, connection, belonging, delight, and so on. Because the affirmation had a high fittedness, it fit the emotional situation whether there was a high or low dress code.

Formation. Dress codes are so omnipresent that they can be hard to notice. But dress codes are always around, everywhere—which is why we:

  • encounter awkward pauses around the dinner table
  • exchange looks with each other when the teacher makes an uncomfortable joke
  • find our social media post quickly censured by dismissive reactions
  • find random pointless stories from our spouse fun, but random pointless stories from the guy in the line in front of us annoying
  • cringe when watching an uncomfortable scene in a comedy
  • make eye contact with others when someone is weird in public
  • share looks with passengers about someone laughing on the bus too loudly
  • know who in the group everyone finds irritating
  • register who is censuring who in a meeting find our post censured online by immediate consensus

(and so on)

Humor dress codes are different than humor “guidelines” that we verbally discuss. Asking comedians to not make genocide jokes is a guideline—while the dress code is how an audience would actually respond to specific humor attempt with genocide content.

All intensities of humor matter

Humor tells convey intensity, like how a smile can be low-intensity (light smile) or high-intensity (beaming):

Sometimes we only focus on the more-intense half of the spectrum—like broad smiles that crest into laughter. But we should also be able to see the less-intense half of the spectrum too:

The importance of low-intensity humor is obvious in our day-to-day life, even if low-intensity humor isn't what we always first think of when asked to think abstractly about "humor:"

Zoe and Emma are watching a show.

The main character is attending a gifted therapy session, and isn't taking it seriously. She starts talking about a man she's obsessed with.

Therapist: "Are you in love with him?"

Fleabag: *laughs* "No!"

Therapist: "Why do you find that funny."

Fleabag: (amused) (sad) "I don't know... I just... no."

Therapist: "Not a romantic?"

Fleabag: "No."

A. Therapist: "Just a girl with no friends and an empty heart."

Fleabag: (taken aback)

B. Therapist: *smiles* "By your own description."

Zoe and Emma think the therapist is a weirdly compelling character.

In this book, we'll want to understand this low-intensity humor as well as high-intensity humor.

Accounting. We wouldn't be very good accountants if we didn't realize that cents were money—or if we thought we never had to account for them in any situation. Something similar happens with humor.

If we didn't realize that a twinge in our cheeks was a smile—or thought we never had to bother paying attention to these smiles—we would have some blind spots in our observations.

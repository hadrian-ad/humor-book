Emotions aren't something we "feel"

We experience emotions all the time without actually "feeling" anything:

  • we might not "feel" nervous, but we wring our hands.
  • we might not "feel" angry, but our hand is shaking.
  • we might not "feel" anxious, but we’re twirling our hair.
  • we might not "feel" bored, but we’re bouncing our leg.
  • we might not "feel" sad, but we want to go to bed earlier and earlier.
  • we might not "feel" jealous of our friend, but find ourselves being passive aggressive toward them.
  • we might not "feel" humor, but we’re smiling as we’re watching people out the window.

(and so on)

If we’re wringing our hands a lot before something stressful, it doesn't really matter if we don't "feel" nervous—we're nervous:

Emma: "Worried about your big date?"

Owen: (honestly) "No! I'm a bit surprised. I feel very calm."

Owen heads out, and Emma turns to Zoe.

Emma: "He went to the bathroom like ten times!"

Zoe: (worried) "He kept wringing his hands, too."

Above, Emma and Zoe understand Owen to be nervous, even though he sincerely thinks he doesn't "feel" nervous.

That's because Emma and Zoe know the absence of internal feelings isn't as important as the presence of behavior.

It's the same for humor. It doesn't really matter if we don't "feel" humor. If we're smiling and loosening while watching something—we're feeling humor.

That's because a "feeling" of humor isn't essential to there being humor—it's just one of many possible humor tells, which may or may not be present during humor.

Consider

We can set aside the idea that our emotional states are always supposed to have "internal feelings," and instead look at a broader pattern of actions, behavior, and decisions (emotion tells) to judge our emotional state.

Feeling. Sometimes we think emotions are supposed to feel like something, like an ambient sensation throughout our body—something that conclusively indicates "this is anger/fear/humor."

This generally uses a model of emotions we learned as children, where emotions were things that were inside us, and that happen in discrete and obvious notes, like from a piano.

As we get older, we're not always presented with alternative models to correspond to our growing ability to reflect on the world—leading to unsound or incomplete perspectives.

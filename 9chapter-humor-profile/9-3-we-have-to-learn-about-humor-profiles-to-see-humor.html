The Humor Book

We see more humor by learning about different humor profiles

The absence of a smile doesn't necessarily mean someone's not feeling humor. That's because the significance of a smile to humor depends on someone's humor profile.

When Zoe moved to Pittsburgh for school, she got to spend more time with her gruff Uncle Jason, who lived nearby and would often give her rides.

At first, she found spending time with him awkward, because he had such a sour face and silent demeanor. But by senior year, she was very comfortable chatting and spending time with him, in part because she found it increasingly easy to gauge his mood. Sometimes he was sour—but more often than not, he surprisingly good-humored. He had expressive eyebrows and small smiles that she came to find very expressive and welcoming. (←Zoe acclimates to a humor profile)

When Lisa is first hired at the office, Yasmin thought she was incredibly uptight, with a permanent scowl.

But after two years, Yasmin has a great time working with Lisa, and picks up on her mood by how fast she walks down the hall, how talkative she is, and the kind of eye-contact she makes. (←Yasmin acclimates to a humor profile)

When a new employee mentions Lisa's aggressive resting face, Yasmin is surprised.

Emma was originally impressed by how much her roommate smiled. Emma thought Jessica had a blissfully content and happy inner life.

After a while, Emma came to know that Jessica just smiled a lot—she even smiled when she cried! Emma found her simplistic initial impression of Jessica's smiling wasn't complicated enough. (←Emma acclimates to a humor profile)

Since smiles are common ways of expressing humor, we're often thrown off balance by their absence:

Alina is watching the crows gather sticks outside her window. She watches them with interest and gently grips her wrist.

Alina is autistic and rarely smiles—and people who don't know her often find the way she expresses her emotional state to be unintuitive. In the example above, is Alina feeling humor or mistake-predominant emotions?

At first, we might exclude humor, since her emotion tells don't "look" like humor—she's not laughing or grinning.

But she is expressing a few humor tells. She's lost wider situational awareness, focused on present, has high energy (relative to herself), reduces stress, touches self, and so on. Since she has several humor tells, we can't exclude humor from possibly being in her emotional state.

Consider:

Whether or not Alina is actually expressing humor depends on her humor profile. If we spent days with Alina, we'd start picking up on her emotional patterns. This would give us more confidence to decide whether Alina is expressing stress or humor, and to what intensity and proportion.

The character makes a joke and feels humor—even though she doesn't smile. After making the joke, she glances toward the other characters (social seeks), gives a (light) smile, speaks quickly (energy), taps another character on his head (touching others), swings her arms (loosening, informal movement), and breaks off the conversation (increased agency relative to before the joke).

The actor and writers gave this character a humor profile that doesn't usually include smiling or laughing. That doesn't mean she doesn't feel humor—she just expresses it in a way relative to her.

We can’t make precise judgements about the emotional states of people we just met—since humor tells are relative to each person’s humor profile. To make relative judgements about their emotions, we have to give attention to someone over time.

For instance, we may know our partner rarely talks to strangers, but strangers don't. So if our partner introduces themselves to a stranger on the train, we may be amazed at their high-intensity social integration (relative to them)—but the stranger can't make any judgement about the intensity of this humor tell.

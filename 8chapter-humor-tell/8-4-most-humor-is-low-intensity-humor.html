Most humor is low-intensity humor

Most of the humor we feel isn’t very intense. Sometimes our humor crests into laughter, but most of the time, our humor happens at lower intensities—like how we bounce, hum, smile, talk excitedly, whistle, and so on more often than we laugh:

And even when our humor crests into laughter, our laughter is still bookended by lower-intensity humor—like below, where the peak of loud laughter is bookended by talking excitedly and bouncing.

If we think of all humor as being the peak moments of intense humor (loud laughter), we’ll end up ignoring most of the humor we feel.

Consider:

At first, “talk excitedly” doesn't seem like humor. But we "talk excitedly" is often a composite of humor tells—when someone talks excitedly, they’re socially integrating, socially tightening, having high energy, thinking fast, raising speaking register, and so on.

This also helps us think about humor that's part of mixed emotional states. We may feel humor and anger; humor and boredom; and so on.

When this happens, we don’t always feel high-intensity humor. Instead, the humor may just be expressed as small smiles, fast thinking, temporary social seeking, and so on—which co-occur with mistake-predominant or OK-predominant emotion tells, like embarrassed smiles (humor and mistake-predominant emotions) or bored smiles (humor and OK-predominant emotions).

This helps us think about all moments of humor—not just high-intensity humor, but the more common moments of low-intensity humor and mixed-emotional states as well.

Blindness. If we only thought of sadness as sobbing, we’d be blind to most of the sadness people feel. Sadness sometimes crest into a climactic expression (sobbing or wailing), but it doesn't always.

Instead, our sadness may just be at a low-intensity where we just feel dispirited, have low-energy, get tired easily, feel pessimistic, and so on. This is sadness too—whether or not sobbing is ever involved.

We don't want to be blind to low-intensity sadness, whether it happens on its own or bookends moments of high-intensity sadness.

It's the exact same for humor. In the same way that sadness is more than just sobbing, humor is more than just laughter. We don't want to be blind to low-intensity humor—or to how low-intensity humor bookends moments of high-intensity humor.

The Humor Book

Humor tells are expressed in many different ways

Each humor tell has an endless variation in how it can be expressed—like laughter:

Variations of laughs

  • loud laughs
  • pff-laughs
  • scoff-laughs
  • tsk-laughs
  • explosive laughs
  • nose-laughs
  • hahs
  • hm!s
  • hehs
  • huhs
  • hurhs
  • cackles
  • chortles
  • chuckles
  • titters
  • snickers
  • giggles
  • guffaws
  • snorts

(and so on)

In this book, we’ll referring to all of these exhales and inhales generically as laughter.

When people *laugh* in our examples, it doesn't usually mean they had high-intensity laughter. (“Hahahahahahah!”) Instead, it usually refers to a laugh that’s less intense—like “pff!” “ah!” “heh!”, and so on.

We can see this in some examples:

Trevor: *playing StormConQuest* (frustrated) “I can’t beat the Ogre Time Magistrate. I’ve been trying for hours. It’s just not possible.”

Yasmin: “Did you buy armor potions?”

Trevor: *pause* (confused) “Do his magic arrows do physical damage?”

Yasmin: *smiles* “Half physical, half magical.”

Trevor: *laughs* (←laugh-like exhale like uuahh! or aahh!) “Fuuuck me.”

Owen: *telling joke* “… and that’s why they always called him… Moo-shroom!”

Emma: *laughs* (←laugh-like exhale like pfff or pssh)

Jessica: *reading trivia on her bottle of sweet tea* “Emma! Did you know sharks can lose 100 teeth a day!” *laughs* (←laugh-like exhale like hm! or Hhuh)

Emma: *not really listening* “Really?”

Zoe is browsing the bookstore when she picks up a book on meditation. She reads the book title: “Wherever You Go, There You Are.” She thinks about that for a second, puzzled. Then she laughs. (←laugh-like exhale out the nose)

Owen is watching Fired Fireman. After Chief fails to stop the Master Arsonist, he becomes depressed and no one knows where he is. Finally, Victoria discovers he’s been living in a garbage bin the past month.

Victoria: (happy) (concerned) “Chief! We’ve been so worried about you. We know you did everything you could. You don’t need to hide from us."

Chief: “Um. I prefer the term ‘tactical self-ostracization.”

Owen: *laughs* (←laugh-like exhale like tss! or ah! or a snort)

In our day-to-day conversations, we don’t always have an overall word for sounds like “hm!” or “huh!” or “fff!!” But in this book, we’ll just call them laughter.

In the same way a half-smile and a full smile are different intensities of the same thing (a smile)—hm! and hahahahah! are different intensities of the same thing (a laugh).

Consider:

We’ll mostly use the generic *laughs* throughout this book to indicate all kinds of laughter. Even though this generic word cuts out specific information about that humor's duration, intensity, and so on—it does keep our examples easy to read.

It's the same for many humor tells too—characters in this book will generically *smile*, even though all actual smiles are situationally specific and richly detailed.

Like any humor tell, how we laugh conveys a lot of nuance about the intensity and duration of our humor. ("Hahahahaha" versus "HAHAHAHAHA!") But how we laugh also conveys the proportion of our evaluations.

If our evaluations are cleanly balanced, we may laugh in a way that just shows humor. But if our evaluations aren't cleanly balanced, our laugh can show that we're leaning toward the OK-predominant zone (like Jessica's "hm!"), or that we're leaning toward the mistake-predominant zone (like Trevor's "uuahh!"):

Jessica's and Trevor's evaluations fell on different sides of the watershed line of highest-intensity humor. The specific inflection was conveyed through how they laughed.

This is the same for all humor tells, which each can be tinged with mistake-predominance or OK-predominance. We have angry or sad smiles (humor mingled with mistake-predominant emotions), and bored or social smiles (humor mingled with OK-predominant emotions).

Sometimes these blended emotion words are a bit disguised. A grimace is often a smile—and so is a dreamy look. These are words that bundle the generic word smile with an inflection toward mistake- or OK-predominant evaluations. Often, but not always, a grimace and a dreamy look are both smiles—but bundle smile with an inflection toward either mistake-predominant or OK-predominant evaluations.

The Humor Book

We can annotate our examples with humor tells

We previously used examples of humor family words and said they were humor. We can use humor tells to describe what this humor looked like:

Reconciliation

Emma: *keeps typing* “Yeah, well. Thanks. I appreciate that. And, you know, on my end, sometimes people have told me… I lack tact.”

Owen: *sits down* (←social tightening, loosening) *laughs* (←laughter, raised spirits) “Emma, I think you’re just great the way you are.” (←social integrating)

Emma: *laughs* (←laughter) (pleased) (←raised spirits) “Okay. Let’s not overdo it there.” (←social integrating)

Achievement

Lina gets an email that her college decision notification is ready. Her blood freezes. Heart pounding, she logs on to her account and clicks the ‘view decision update’ letter. Her mouth is dry while it loads. “Congratulations!” the letter says.

Lina leaps up and pumps her fist. (←high energy) “Mom, mom!” she yells, thumping down the stairs. (←seeking others, informal movement, energy, louder voice) “I got into Pitt!” She jumps up and down and dances, glowing with happiness. (←non-normative behavior, high energy, attempting to spread humor, focus on present, flushing) As her mom congratulates her, she puts on a celebratory plastic tiara, feeling invincibly happy. (←non-normative behavior, social tightening) They head out to get pizza, and she catches her reflection of the doors of the pizza place, and laughs as she examines herself. (←laughter, optimism) She spends the rest of the evening joking with her mom. (←effort to spread humor)

Safety

Trevor and Yasmin are watching Squeal III. A teenager explores an old mansion at night, and paused before a small jewelry box ominously on the table. “Don’t do it,” Trevor says, annoyed. The teenager slowly opens the box and a ragged ghost explodes out!

Trevor and Yasmin leap up. “Fuck!!” Yasmin says, heart thumping. She laughs, hand over her heart. (←Yasmin laughs, touches self, loosens, relaxes, and so on)

Trevor has his hands on his head. “Why can’t they just have the monsters and blood without the freaking jump scares?” he says. (←Trevor touches self, social seeks)

Humor tells help us spot when people are feeling humor family words. We show humor tells when we feel safety after a jump scare, achievement after a success, reconciliation after an apology, and so on.

Absence. Trevor didn’t show many humor tells after the jump scare—because he wasn’t really feeling much safety! Trevor mostly shows inverse humor tell:

  • instead of loosening, he tightens
  • instead of feeling optimism, he feels dread
  • instead of flushing, he pales

(and so on)

While Trevor also shows a few humor tells, like touching self and social seeking, these are also anxiety tells or fear tells. Emotion tells often count for more than one emotion—which means social seeking can double count as both a humor tell and mistake-predominance tell.

In this case, we’d say Trevor has some humor in his emotional state after the jump scare—but that he’s mostly feeling discomfort or irritation.

That's the end of this chapter! Next, we'll see how humor tells make up someone's humor profile.

Experiences of humor are clusters of co-occuring reaction elements called humor tells. These humor tells make up a pattern definition of humor.

  • We'll use a specific list of humor tells in this book: brightening, energy, expansive posture, eyes twinkling, flushing, higher speaking register, informal posture, informal movement, laughter, loosening, reduction of physical tension, relaxed posture, smiling, touching others, touching self, efforts to spread humor, fast thinking, fast speaking, louder voice, focusing on present, high energy, increased agency, loss of situational awareness, non-normative behavior, optimism, raised spirits, reduction of stress, social integration, social seeking, and social tightening.
  • Humor tells = humor = humor family words. These are all different ways of looking at the same thing—which is why they all carry humor qualities like intensity and duration, and each help us notice and describe low-intensity humor.
  • Humor tells combine with other emotion tells to make up our emotional state. For instance, humor tells and anger tells together mean we have humor and anger simultaneously in our emotional state.

One way we define things is through pattern definitions. Pattern definitions are much closer to how we actually define things than dictionary definitions—they’re why we can drive past ten barns and recognize them each as a “barn,” even though none of them look exactly alike.

To see how pattern definitions works, we can make a simple pattern definition for a barn. We’ll start by making a list of things we understand to be possible elements of barns:

Elements of our pattern definition:

  • silo
  • big double doors
  • stand alone structure
  • red
  • winch
  • fodder
  • made of wood
  • gambrel roof

(and so on)

Then, we assign weight to each element of a barn, since some things are more important to what a barn “is” than others. So we'll assign points for “barnness:”

Elements of our pattern definition:

  • silo (10) (←ten points toward “barnness”)
  • big double doors (5)
  • stand alone structure (3)
  • red (2)
  • winch (5)
  • fodder (3)
  • made of wood (2)
  • gambrel roof (5)

(and so on)

The final piece of a pattern definition is to set a point threshold for what we identify as a barn. Let’s say 15 points is the threshold where we tag a structure as being a barn. Now, if we come across three very different buildings, we’ll be able to classify each one as barn or not-barn:

Structure #1

  • silo (10)
  • winch (5)

= 15 points of barnness = “it’s a barn!”

Structure #2

  • stand alone structure (3)
  • red (2)
  • big double doors (5)
  • fodder (2)
  • made of wood (3)

= 15 points of barnness = “it’s a barn!”

Structure #3

  • stand alone structure (3)
  • made of wood (3)
  • gambrel roof (5)

= 11 points of barnness = “well it’s barn-like…”

When we combine dozens of pattern definitions for different structers, we have a flexible system for identification and classification.

We’ll have patterns in our head for barns, houses, monasteries, outhouses, sheds, warehouses, and so on—which we can mix and match to describe even buildings that are unique in all the world. (“Well, it looks like a mix between a barn and a… monastery?”)

There’s no official cosmic pattern definition for a barn. They’re just definitions we make up ourselves, out of everything we know about them. This means these pattern definitions vary person-by-person, based on our experiences.

Consider:

Someone who grew up in 19th century Japan would have a different pattern definition for “barn” than someone who grew up in 14th century France. Similarly, we would have a different pattern definition for barn someone who uses or researches barns.

The pattern definition we casually made above is just based on the barns we see in children’s books and stock photos—it's probably not a very good pattern definition for most practical purposes. As we gain more awareness about barns, we’d update and refine our definition.

In this chapter, we’ve been making a pattern definition of humor:

Elements of our pattern definition:

  • laughter (10)
  • smiling (5)
  • brightening (3)

(and so on)

We have a set of possible reaction elements, and we can decide which of these recation elements are part of the humor pattern and which are part of other emotional patterns. This lets us take an emotional state that’s unique in all the world and try to make sense of whether it has what we’ve patterned as “humor” in it.

This is why wecan see a hundred people feel humor a hundred times each, and still recognize it all as humor—even though none of those moments of humor are exactly alike. It’s for the same reason we can recognize a thousand different structures as barns, even if none of them are exactly alike!

We can use pattern definitions to think about what emotions are. We take the set of possible human reaction elements (“emotion tells”), and see which ones co-occur in distinguishable relationships. We give names to these patterns, like sadness or anger. But it’s important to notice that there isn’t an "official" definition to an emotion like anger, since there are so many ways to tweak our pattern definition of it.

For instance:

Maybe the convention fifty years ago was that anger required 25 points to be “anger”—but today the word anger only requires 15 points. Maybe a hundred years ago, blasphemous cursing was 15 points toward anger, but today is usually only 5 points toward anger.

Maybe one language has a very broad pattern for anger but another language splits this pattern up into two emotions. Maybe another language doesn't have a word for anger and uses a word like hurt instead.

In this book, we’re using a pattern definition of humor that’s broader than is often conventional—in part because we're also fitting in our experiences with low-intensities of humor. A pattern definition that's only used for belly-laughing will be different than a pattern definition that's also inclusive of a single exhale making up a very soft laugh.

We can also use pattern definitions to think about why achievement and generosity can be thought of as humor—even though we can somewhat distinguish between things like achievement and generosity. Humor family words are pattern definitions that sit within a broader pattern of humor—similar to how irritability and frustration are specific patterns within a broader pattern of anger.

Finally, a pattern definition for the word humor tends to exclude context—while humor family words explicitly include context as part of its pattern definition.

As we’ll see throughout this book, this kind of definition of humor will let us talk about ourselves and the world in some interesting ways—and think pragmatically about some of the underlying systems that make up our experiences.

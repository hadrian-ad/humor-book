The Humor Book

Focusing on humor outcomes can generate humor where jokes can't

When we want to generate humor in complex situations, we often have no other option but to use high-velocity comments.

That's because low-velocity comments can be mired by other priorities within complicated emotional situations. It's only high-velocity actions or comments that can break through these obstacles and rush the situation into humor outcomes:

Justin: *hands back an essay* “Not up to your usual standards, Emma.”

Emma: “Wow. And here I was thinking I had no standards!” (←Emma tries to make a joke more than she tries to handle his criticism)

Justin: (not having it) “I’ll expect more from your final essay.” (←Emma doesn’t reach any humor outcomes in Justin↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Justin: *hands back an essay* “Not up to your usual standards, Emma.”

Emma: (irritated) (tired) “Yeah? Well I have a life.” (←Emma tries to handle criticism)

Justin: *laughs* “Fair enough. I’ll look forward to your final essay.” (←Justin feels Emma has handled criticism, controlled a situation, been acceptably criticized, gained a new perspective↔︎humor)

Hana is trying to use her ID card to get into the lab, but it’s not working. She stands there stupidly, swiping her card, until Alina comes to help her.

Hana: (awkwardly) “That’s so weird. Usually I’m so much cooler and suaver!” (←Hana tries to make a joke more than she tries to create rapport)

Alina: “Hm.” *enters lab* (←Hana doesn’t reach any humor outcomes in Alina↔︎no humor)

Hana feels awkward. (←Hana doesn’t reach any humor outcomes with herself↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Hana is trying to use her ID card to get into the lab, but it’s not working. She stands there stupidly, swiping her card, until Alina comes to help her.

Hana: “Thanks, I was feeling pretty stupid standing there. What should I do about this door? It never opens for me.” (←Hana tries to build rapport with Alina)

Alina: “It’s been having troubles this week. Call one of us down, we’ll help you with the door.” (←Hana is unsure if she’s reached humor outcomes with Alina)

Hana smiles gratefully. (←Hana feels she has built rapport, become more accepted↔︎humor)

Zoe gets a few oil paintings accepted into a student gallery. Her parents come by to see.

Brad: (self-amused) “So you finally got your oil shop up and running, hey?” (←Brad tries to make a joke more than he tries to symbolize close ties)

Zoe: *rolls eyes* “Dad.” (←Brad doesn’t reach any humor outcomes with Zoe↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Zoe gets her oil painting into a student gallery.

Brad: “Gosh. I’m so proud of you, Zo!” (←Brad tries to make Zoe feel achievement)

Zoe: *smiles* “Thanks dad!” (←Zoe feels close ties, acceptance, achievement↔︎humor)

Amber pulls up over the crosswalk, which always bothers Jennifer.

Jennifer: (improvising) “If you keep running over those zebras, we’ll… uh, be able to start our own glue factory!” (←Jennifer tries to make a joke more than she tries to acceptably criticize Amber)

Amber: (peeved) “What?” (←Jennifer fails to reach any humor outcomes with Amber↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Amber pulls up over the crosswalk, which always bothers Jennifer.

Jennifer: “Stop pulling up to the crosswalk! So rude to the pedestrians!” (←Jennifer tries to acceptably criticize Amber)

Amber: *laughs* “Okay. Sure.” (←Amber feels she was acceptably criticized, and that Jennifer supported her argument well↔︎humor)

Jokes were easily deflected away from humor outcomes tripwires, since their low-velocity either allowed them to be easily countered, or else never got traction in the overall situation.

By contrast, when people focused on reaching humor outcomes, this higher-velocity alternative made them successful in their circumstances.

Generating humor in this way requires favorable conditions, judgment, luck, practice, skill, and so on. Often, the stakes are higher when we rush humor outcomes. So the point here isn’t that rushing humor outcomes is a magic method—because it’s not!

Instead, the point here is just to understand why and how humor happens, even in some initially confusing situations.

Consider:

High-velocity comments are good at generating all kinds of emotions. Even though in our examples the high-velocity comments lead to humor, we can easily imagine alternatives where the high velocity comments led to mistake-predominant emotions instead—where Justin was annoyed by Emma, Alina was dismissive of Hana, Zoe was embarrassed by Brad, and Amber was angry at Jennifer.

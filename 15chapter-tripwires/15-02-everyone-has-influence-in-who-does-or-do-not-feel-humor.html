The Humor Book

Everyone has influence in who does or doesn't feel humor

Emotional outcomes are shaped by everyone involved, since everyone involved are active participants in the situation.

This is why Heather has influence on whether or not Xiaowei reaches humor outcomes:

Heather: *hands on hips* “Xiaowei, when are you going to start looking for a job?”

Xiaowei: *playing a game* (listlessly) “When my shit turns blue.” (←Xiaowei pushes the situation toward controlling the situation, handling a complaint, and so on)

Heather: *sits down* *crosses legs* (calmly) “How are you feeling lately?” (←deflects the situation away from those tripwires)

Xiaowei: (annoyed) “Annoyed, right now.” (←Heather preempts Xiaowei from feeling he controlled the situation, handled a complaint, or avoided conflict↔︎no humor)

When Heather prevents Xiaowei from feeling like he's avoided conflict, controlled the situation, handled complaints, and so on—she prevents him from feeling humor.

Consider:

This isn’t to say that this is the “best” thing for Heather to do. We’re just looking at how the humor in a situation isn’t predetermined by one person. Instead, both people are involved in creating or preventing humor.

Xiaowei can't unilaterally handle a complaint because he's in a multilateral situation—Heather triangulates whether he feels he handles a complain or not, which is something outside his full control.

That's because humor is a multi-person process, in which everyone is involved.

Metaphors. Sometimes we think that generating humor is like kicking balls into an empty net. We sit in front of a comedian, and they try to kick balls into our net to make us laugh. This makes us as passive, stationary targets where their comments are "hit or miss" depending on their aim.

That metaphor sometimes works okay for some comedy shows. But it doesn't for most of our lives, because we're not always passive, stationary targets.

Instead, we're often on the field ourselves, defending the net, or trying to score on theirs. Other times there are many people on the field—sometimes we have the bigger team, sometimes the smaller team—sometimes there’s clear visibility, and sometimes there’s rain or fog—sometimes we’re playing for a prize, and sometimes everyone is just playing for fun.

We can use these metaphors to capture some of what it feels like to generate humor in interpersonal situations. While it changes depending on the situation, it often feels active and positional—more like playing a game of soccer than it does just practicing shooting goals.

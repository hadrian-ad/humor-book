The Humor Book

Tripwires help us see synchronization

We talked before about how evaluations on an emotion network can be synchronized or desynchronized.

When our evaluations are synchronized, we all see the situational ball as moving similarly—so synchronized people all feel humor at around the same time:

Middle school Emma is watching her brother Christian play StormConQuest.

As he sneaks up on an evil kobold, his torch hits an exploding barrel, suddenly flinging his character through the cavern walls in a glitchy mess. It makes Emma and her brother jump in surprise.

Emma laughs and laughs. (←Emma feels humor↔︎Emma feels she has gotten through a stressful situation)

Her brother laughs and laughs at the same time. (←Christian feels humor↔︎Christian feels he has gotten through a stressful situation)

By contrast, when we don't have synchronized evaluations, we don't see the situational ball as the same:

Trevor is carelessly cutting onions, and Yasmin tells him to be careful.

“Shit!” Trevor drops the knife and puts his finger into his mouth. “I almost severed my pinky!”

Yasmin comes over, ashen-faced. Then Trevor waves his hand. Just joking. He laughs at her reaction. (←Trevor feels humor↔︎Trevor feels he avoided conflict)

She stares at him, furious. (←Yasmin doesn't feel humor↔︎Yasmin doesn’t feel Trevor avoided conflict)

Heather: *hands on hips* “Xiaowei, when are you going to start looking for a job?”

Xiaowei: *playing a game* (listlessly) “When my shit turns blue.”

Heather: *throws hands up* (←Heather feels Xiaowei created conflict , exerbated her complaint, and that both of them lost control of a situation↔︎no humor)

Xiaowei: *laughs* (←Xiaowei feels he avoided conflict, handled her complaint, and that he controlled the situation↔︎humor)

Consider:

We're looking at evaluatory perspective. Sometimes we share the same evaluatory view of something, and so we react similarly (synchronized evaluations)—sometimes we realize we don't share the same view, and so react differently (desynchronized evaluations).

We can use humor outcomes to think narratively about this. If we both feel someone did or didn't handle criticisms, we'll react similarly—but if one of us laughs and the other frowns, we'll have different evaluatory perspectives on whether someone handled criticisms.

Resynchronize. When Trevor pranks Yasmin, we can describe Trevor’s humor as a “good” experience for Trevor—even at the same time we also see his humor outcomes as related to a "bad" experience for Yasmin, since she felt angry and hurt.

We treat others as equals when we care about synchronizing our evaluations. So if Trevor wants to treat Yasmin as an equal, he has to both notice and care about their desynchronized evaluations. By resynchronizing, they'll eventually feel mutual humor—mutual reconciliation.

This resynchronization will probably best be initiated and guided by Trevor, since he took the action that led to desynchronization in the first place. To do so, he’ll have to bring their situational balls back into greater alignment. This means converging on a shared evaluatory perspective.

The first step is for Trevor to notice their diverging emotional states. The next step is to give equal weight of consideration to Yasmin’s evaluations as he does to his own evaluations. If both people put in this effort, they'll become become more and more resynchronized.

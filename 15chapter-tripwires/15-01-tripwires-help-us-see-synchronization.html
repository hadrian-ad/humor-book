The Humor Book

Tripwires help us see synchronization

When our evaluations are synchronized, the situational ball moves similarly for everyone—so synchronized people all feel humor at around the same time:

Middle school Emma is watching her brother playing StormConQuest.

As he sneaks up on an evil kobold, his torch hits an exploding barrel, suddenly flinging his character through the cavern walls in a glitchy mess. It makes Emma and her brother jump in surprise.

Emma laughs and laughs. (←Emma feels humor↔︎Emma feels she has gotten through a stressful situation)

Her brother laughs and laughs at the same time. (←Christian feels humor↔︎Christian feels he has gotten through a stressful situation)

By contrast, when people don't have synchronized evaluations, the situational ball isn’t the same:

Trevor is carelessly cutting onions, and Yasmin tells him to be careful.

“Shit!” Trevor drops the knife and puts his finger into his mouth. “I almost severed my pinky!”

Yasmin comes over, ashen-faced. Then Trevor waves his hand. Just joking. He laughs at her reaction. (←Trevor feels humor↔︎Trevor feels he avoided conflict)

She stares at him, furious. (←Yasmin doesn't feel humor↔︎Yasmin doesn’t feel Trevor avoided conflict)

We saw the same thing with Xiaowei and Heather. Xiaowei felt he avoided conflict, handled her complaint, and controlled the situation—and so he felt humor. By contrast, Heather felt he created conflict, exerbated her complaint, and lost control of a situation—and so she didn’t feel humor.

Consider:

We talked before about how evaluations on an emotion network can be synchronized or desynchronized. Humor outcomes help us make this idea more tangible. ("Do both people feel a complaint has been handled?")

If one person feels someone handled a complaint and the other doesn't, they're not emotionally synchronized. They have desynchronized evaluations—they don't share an evaluatory perspective.

Resynchronize. When Trevor pranks Yasmin, we can describe Trevor’s humor outcomes as a “good” experience for Trevor. We can do this even at the same time we also see his humor outcomes as related to "bad" experiences for Yasmin, since she felt angry and hurt.

We treat others as equals when we care about arriving at synchronized evaluations. So if Trevor wants to treat Yasmin as an equal, he has to both notice and care about their desynchronization. By resynchronizing, they'll eventually feel mutual humor—mutual reconciliation.

This resynchronization will probably need to be initiated and guided by Trevor, since he took the action that led to desynchronization in the first place. To do so, he’ll have to bring their situational balls back into greater alignment—they'll have to converge on a shared evaluatory perspective.

The first step is for Trevor to notice their diverging emotional states. The next step is to give equal weight of consideration to Yasmin’s evaluations as he does to his own evaluations. If both people put in this effort, they'll become become more and more re-synchronized.

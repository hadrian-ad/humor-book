The Humor Book

It often takes many steps before we feel humor

Humor is something we develop a situation toward. This is how all humor happens, even though it’s sometimes a one-step process and sometimes a multi-step process:

In both cases above, humor takes time—it's something the situation develops toward. Humor never happens instantaneous, even though it sometimes happens very quickly. But sometimes the development toward humor takes a long time with many steps.

This is obvious with a humor outcome like support an argument—since we can't always support an argument in one sentence. That's because the person we’re talking with may:

  • disagree with us at first
  • not understand the argument
  • ask for clarification
  • need more context
  • give a counterpoint
  • take time to think it over

(and so on)

To push them into a humor outcome, we have to be aware of how their tripwire is guarded, and be responsive to them. It’s only after a shared development of the situation that they’ll feel we’ve supported our argument:

Trevor: *playing StormConQuest* (frustrated) “I can’t beat the Ogre Time Magistrate. I’ve been trying for hours. It’s just not possible.”

Yasmin: “Did you buy armor potions?” (←Yasmin tries to support an argument)

Trevor: *pause* (confused) “Do magic arrows do physical damage?” (←Trevor doesn’t feel Yasmin has supported an argument)

Yasmin: *smiles* “Half physical, half magical.” (←Yasmin tries to support an argument)

Trevor: *laughs* “Fuuuck me.” (←Trevor feels Yasmin has supported an argument)

Yasmin: *pained* “So why do you think sixteen-year-olds should be able to vote?”

Trevor: “Because why not?” (←Trevor tries to support an argument)

Yasmin: “Because they have no life experience! Their brains aren’t developed!” (←Yasmin doesn’t feel Trevor has supported an argument↔︎no humor)

Trevor: “But we let adults with intellectual disabilities vote, right? So it’s not really about development.” (←Trevor tries to support an argument)

Yasmin: “But they have no life experience.” (←Yasmin doesn’t feel Trevor has supported an argument↔︎no humor)

Trevor: “But that’s arbitrary? Some dude who’s sixty would say that about someone our age.”

Yasmin: *laughs* (←Yasmin feels Trevor has supported an argument) *shakes head in frustration* “Where have you been hearing all this, Trevor?”

It’s the same for all humor outcomes, like easing tension, handling complaints, creating rapport, and so on. Sometimes, we reach these outcomes quickly—but other times, we have to develop the situation that way.

If we thought a humor attempt had to be successful or not from the first sentence, we’d be playing a game with the wrong rules—and we’d often struggle to support arguments, handle complaints, create rapport, and so on:

Humor takes some situational assembly—and it’s often helpful to think of humor as the conclusion that we work toward, rather than beginning that starts something.

Since all humor outcomes are connected, a rising tide lifts all boats: improving at supporting arguments, revealing personal views, and so on improves our skill at humor—and improving our skill at humor improves our ability to support arguments, reveal personal views, and so on.

We can use humor outcomes as methods of generating humor. This lets everything become much more tangible and practical.

If we ask: "How do I generate humor?" we can give ourselves an answer with a humor outcome:

  • “How do I generate humor? By supporting an argument”
  • "How do I generate humor? By revealing personal views"
  • "How do I generate humor? By helping someone become accepted"

and so on

This downsizes an abstract question into something more specific—and something we already have an idea about how to do. We already have an idea on how to support arguments and reveal personal views—and we can enroll these as part of our overall humor skill.

So if we're writing a script and want some levity, instead of saying "what could this character say that would be funny?" we could instead say: "what could this character say to reveal a personal view?"

Yasmin is re-watching Serenity.

Jayne: "Captain's right." *loads gun* "Can't be thinking on revenge if we're gonna get through this."

Zoë: (with grim certitude) "Do you really think any of us is gonna get through this?"

Jayne: *looks around at people struggling to load their guns* (hopefully) "Well, I might." (←Jayne reveals personal view, reveals personality trait)

Yasmin: *laughs* (←Yasmin feels Jayne revealed personal view, revealed personality trait)

Yasmin always appreciated Jayne's character. He's one of those non-funny funny characters.

More importantly, if we're around people and want to make them laugh, instead of "trying to be funny" we can reconfigure our goal by trying to reach humor outcomes like "trying to support an argument."

Amber: "Cats are way better than dogs. They sit on your lap and shit in a box!"

Yasmin: *smiles* (←Yasmin feels Amber supported her argument) "You're just wrong!"

This method of using humor outcomes sets questions about humor and humor-generation into managable framings.

It often takes many steps before we feel humor

Humor is something we develop a situation toward. Sometimes this happens quickly, in what seems like a single step—other times it happens more slowly, over what seems like many steps:

In both cases above, humor isn't happening instantaneously—humor happens after time. Even when humor happens quickly, it's not instantaneous.

It's obvious it takes time to reach a humor outcome—since we can't always support an argument in one sentence. That's because the person we’re talking with may:

  • disagree with us at first
  • not understand the argument
  • ask for clarification
  • need more context
  • give a counterpoint
  • take time to think it over

(and so on)

To push them into a humor outcome, we have to be aware of how their tripwire is guarded, and be responsive to those guards. It’s only after a shared development of the emotional situation that they’ll feel we’ve supported our argument:

Trevor: *playing StormConQuest* (frustrated) “I can’t beat the Ogre Time Magistrate. I’ve been trying for hours. It’s just not possible.”

Yasmin: “Did you buy armor potions?” (←Yasmin tries to support an argument)

Trevor: *pause* (confused) “Do magic arrows do physical damage?” (←Trevor doesn’t feel Yasmin has supported an argument)

Yasmin: *smiles* “Half physical, half magical.” (←Yasmin tries to support an argument)

Trevor: *laughs* “Fuuuck me.” (←Trevor feels Yasmin has supported an argument)

Yasmin: *pained* “So why do you think sixteen-year-olds should be able to vote?”

Trevor: “Because why not?” (←Trevor tries to support an argument)

Yasmin: “Because they have no life experience! Their brains aren’t developed!” (←Yasmin doesn’t feel Trevor has supported an argument↔︎no humor)

Trevor: “But we let adults with intellectual disabilities vote, right? So it’s not really about development.” (←Trevor tries to support an argument)

Yasmin: “But they have no life experience.” (←Yasmin doesn’t feel Trevor has supported an argument↔︎no humor)

Trevor: “But that’s arbitrary? Some dude who’s sixty would say that about someone our age.”

Yasmin: *laughs* (←Yasmin feels Trevor has supported an argument) *shakes head in frustration* “Where have you been hearing all this, Trevor?”

It’s the same for all humor outcomes. Sometimes, we reach these outcomes quickly—but other times, we have to develop the situation that way toward easing tension, handling complaints, creating rapport, and so on.

If we thought a humor attempt had to be successful from the first sentence, we’d be playing a game with the wrong rules—and we’d often struggle to support arguments, handle complaints, create rapport, and so on:

Humor takes some situational assembly—which is why it’s often helpful to think of humor as the conclusion that we work toward, rather than beginning that starts something.

Consider:

Since all humor outcomes are connected, a rising tide lifts all boats: improving at supporting arguments, revealing personal views, and so on improves our skill at humor—and improving our skill at humor improves our ability to support arguments, reveal personal views, and so on.

Answers. We can use each humor outcomes as a method of generating humor. So if we ask: "How do I generate humor?" we can answer ourselves with a humor outcome:

  • “How do I generate humor? By supporting an argument”
  • "How do I generate humor? By revealing personal views"
  • "How do I generate humor? By helping someone become accepted"

and so on

This lets us take an abstract question ("how do I generate humor?") and downsize it into something tangible—since we already have an idea on how to support arguments and reveal personal views. We can enroll these ideas as part of our overall humor skill.

That's the end of this chapter! Next, we'll look at the "appropriateness" of humor.

When thinking about generating humor, we have two different approaches:

  • An emotion-first approach, which treats a humor outcome as a consequence of humor. ("If they laugh, they'll reach a humor outcome.")
  • An outcome-first approach, which treats humor as a consequence of a humor outcome. ("If they reach a humor outcome, they'll laugh")

These two approaches let us talk about the same thing in different ways:

  • Emotion-first: “If he laughs, I’ll have handled his criticism”
  • Outcome-first: “If I handle his criticism, he’ll laugh”
  • Emotion-first: “If I feel humor, I’ll have dealt with a taboo topic”
  • Outcome-first: “If I deal with a taboo topic, I’ll feel humor”
  • Emotion-first: “If they feel humor, I’ll have eased their tensions.”
  • Outcome-first: “If they ease their tensions, they’ll feel humor.”

Essentially, this is like solving X = Y. Since they have the same value, we can solve the equation by approaching either end.

If we think we can only solve for X, we'll limit our approach. When we realize we can also solve for Y, we'll have much greater flexibility in how we think about different situations.

For instance:

If we're welcoming a new person to our team, we know we'll want them to smile or laugh. To help them do so, we could focus on the things we associate with smiling or laughing—jokes, funny voices, goofy icebreakers, and so on.

That's an emotion-first approach, which tries to achieve an outcome by first thinking about an emotion.

Alternatively, we could focus on the outcome we want—so we'd focus on the things we asossociate with creating rapport, symbolizing ties, easing tension, and so on. This calls to mind acts of inclusion, compliments, displays of equality, gifts, hospitality, warm welcomes, and so on—all of which will create smiles and laughs.

That's an outcome-first approach, which tries to generate humor by first thinking about an outcome.

The point of all this isn't that there are "two ways" of going about things—it's instead to help jog our thinking into new perspectives how we go about making emotional actions in our lives.

We can use a spatial metaphor to think about "reaching" humor outcomes. This gives an intuitive way of thinking about why humor does or doesn't happen.

  • We can think narratively about humor's triggering conditions by thinking of humor outcomes as something the situation develops towards.
  • Our evaluations are shaped by the direct and indirect participation of everyone. That means that everyone has a role in whether or not humor outcomes are reached on various topics.
  • Humor outcomes are reached over time. Since our evaluations don't shift instantaneously, it sometimes takes time and effort to shift evaluations on a topic in certain ways.

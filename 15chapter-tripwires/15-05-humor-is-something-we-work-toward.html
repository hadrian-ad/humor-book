The Humor Book

Humor is something we work toward

Humor outcomes are things we often have to work towards, over time. That means we don’t have to immediately give up pushing toward a humor outcome if we don’t get there right away.

We know this with polite refusals. If we offer a guest something to drink, we know we don’t have to immediately stop if they politely decline. Instead, we know we sometimes push a little more:

Yasmin goes to the bathroom, leaving Trevor sitting awkwardly at the table with her mother and siblings. There’s an awkward pause.

Then Yasmin’s mother gets up. “Want some tea, pop, water?” she asks. (←Yasmin’s mother tries to makes Trevor feel accepted)

Trevor: *shakes head* “No thank you, I’m good.” (←Trevor resists this humor outcome with a polite refusal)

Yasmin’s mother: “No really, you’d make me feel happy to get you something. What would you like?” (←Yasmin’s mother applies more pressure)

Trevor: *smiles* “Well, I guess I wouldn’t mind some soda.” (←Trevor is pushed across the become accepted tripwire)

While this is familiar for polite refusal, the exact same thing is true for impolite refusals too—we don’t need to take an impolite refusal at face value:

Heather: *hands on hips* “Xiaowei, when are you going to start looking for a job?” (←Heather tries to open a new topic of conversation)

Xiaowei: *playing a game* (listlessly) “When my shit turns blue.” (←Xiaowei makes an impolite refusal)

Heather: *changes tact* “Did you hear about Justin’s brother’s carpet shop?” (←Heather tries again to open new topic of conversation)

Xiaowei: (amused) “A carpet shop, mom?” (←Heather has opened a new topic of conversation)

Heather: *smiles* “What? Never imagined yourself working at a carpet shop?” (←Heather and Xiaowei create rapport with each other)

The difference between the polite and impolite refusals is smaller than it seems at first. They’re both part of positional movement within a situation. One person is pushing the situation toward a humor outcome and another trying to deflect from that outcome.

This partnered motion within a situation is how all humor happens, everywhere. There’s a give-and-take and push-and-pull until outcomes are reached—a process that happens over time and that sometimes generate humor.

No thanks. When Yasmin's mother offers Trevor a drink, her actual goal has nothing to do with the drink—she wants Trevor to feel belonging, comfort, welcome, and so on. The offer is just a means to this end.

If Trevor just says "I'm not thirsty," Yasmin's mother will just offer something else—because she hasn't reached her goal of Trevor expressing humor tells.

If Trevor wants to decline a drink effectively, he has to do so with humor tells. If he says "no thanks" while briefly smiling, focusing on the people around him, raising his speaking register, loosening, socially tightening, and so on—Yasmin’s mother will see that he feels accepted, which was her goal.

At that point, she'll stop insisting on a drink—and Trevor has now effectively declined.

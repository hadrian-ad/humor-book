The Humor Book

Humor is something we work toward

It sometimes takes work to reach humor outcomes. And we don't have to give up pushing for a humor outcome if we don't reach it right away.

We know this with polite refusals. If someone politely declines our offer of something to drink, that doesn't mean we always immediately stop.

Instead, we know that in some situations we sometimes push a little more:

Yasmin goes to the bathroom, leaving Trevor sitting awkwardly at the table with her mother and siblings. There’s an awkward pause.

Then Yasmin’s mother gets up. “Want some tea, pop, water?” she asks. (←Yasmin’s mother tries to makes Trevor feel accepted)

Trevor: *shakes head* “No thank you, I’m good.” (←Trevor resists this humor outcome with a polite refusal)

Yasmin’s mother: “No really, you’d make me feel happy to get you something. What would you like?” (←Yasmin’s mother applies more pressure)

Trevor: *smiles* “Well, I guess I wouldn’t mind some soda.” (←Trevor is pushed across the become accepted tripwire)

While this is familiar for polite refusal, it's the exact same thing for impolite refusals too:

Heather: *hands on hips* “Xiaowei, when are you going to start looking for a job?” (←Heather tries to open a new topic of conversation)

Xiaowei: *playing a game* (listlessly) “When my shit turns blue.” (←Xiaowei makes an impolite refusal)

Heather: *changes tact* “Did you hear about Justin’s brother’s carpet shop?” (←Heather tries again to open new topic of conversation)

Xiaowei: (amused) “A carpet shop, mom?” (←Heather has opened a new topic of conversation)

Heather: *smiles* “What? Never imagined yourself working at a carpet shop?” (←Heather and Xiaowei create rapport with each other)

The difference between the polite and impolite refusals is smaller than it seems at first. They’re both part of positional movement within a situation—one person is pushing the situation toward a humor outcome and another trying to deflect from that outcome.

Consider:

This partnered motion within a situation is how all humor happens, everywhere. There’s a give-and-take and a push-and-pull until outcomes are reached—a process that takes time and often involves resistance and tension before emotional outcomes are reached.

Declining. When Yasmin's mother offers Trevor a drink, she doesn't want him to take the drink—she instead wants Trevor to feel belonging, comfort, welcome, and so on. The offer is just a means to this end.

So if Trevor tries to decline by saying "I'm not thirsty," Yasmin's mother will just offer something else (snack, cushion, or so on)—because she wants to reach her goal of Trevor expressing humor tells.

To decline a drink effectively, Trevor has to satisfy Yasmin's mother's goal by showing humor tells. He'll have to pair his "no thanks" with briefly smiling, focusing on the people around him, raising his speaking register, loosening, socially tightening, and so on—which will let Yasmin’s mother will see that he feels accepted.

With her goal reached, she'll stop insisting on a drink—and Trevor has now effectively declined.

Consider:

Notice that declining and accepting an offer have the same requirement. It's not enough to simply take a drink to accept it, for the same reason it's not enough to simply refuse it to decline it.

When there's a goal of helping us reach a humor outcome, we have to express humor tells whether we accept or decline an offer.

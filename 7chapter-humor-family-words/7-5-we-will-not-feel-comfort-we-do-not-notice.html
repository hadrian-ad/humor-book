The Humor Book

We won’t feel comfort we don’t notice

We feel humor when we feel comfort. But if we’re not aware that we’re feeling comfort, we won’t feel humor:

Riley spends all day in front of the TV while Jennifer’s on vacation. She lies on the sofa, utterly relaxed, and transfixed by her tablet and the rainy window, mouth open and blank-faced.

Sam: “Are you just going to lie there all day?”

Riley: “Uh-huh.” (←Riley doesn’t notice something comfortable↔︎no mistake means the mistake can’t be OK↔︎no humor)

Sam: “Ready to leave, Riley? How was your time with Maddie?”

Riley: *heading out the door* “Maddie has a beanbag! It was so comfortable!! Why isn’t our house comfortable?” (←Riley notices something comfortable, and so experiences it vividly)

Sam: *getting into car* “Our house is comfortable, sweetheart! We have a sofa, chair, beds, swing…” (←Sam doesn’t understand why Riley feels comfort at comfortable things at Maddie's house, but not at similar things at home)

Riley: “I felt so comfortable at Maddie’s, I could have spent all day in her chair!”

Sam: (vaguely annoyed) “But you spend all day on the sofa at home!”

Riley: “It’s not the same.” (←Riley has perceptual camouflage on chairs at home)

When Riley first jumps on the couch, she may have felt comfort. But over time, the experience of being on the sofa accrues perceptual camouflage, and she stops feeling comfort.

It won’t be until something changes—like she gets off the sofa or changes position—that she’s likely to notice things again. 

Consider:

When Riley first jumps on the sofa, she has an active topic where a mistake is evaluated as OK—she feels comfort. But eventually, she acclimates to the mistake, and the topic stops generating humor. Eventually, the topic becomes inactive, and so can't generate humor. 

At that point, Riley isn't evaluating this topic for the same reason she's not evaluating topics related to her being safe, short, young, Pennsylvanian, able to run, or the fact that she could one day walk on Mars. Even though those are all true, she simply has no reason to be evaluating these things—those topics are dormant or uncreated. 

This is also why Riley will easily feel comfort when she goes to a friend’s house. Nothing there has perceptual camouflage, so she’ll experience everything vividly.

The things we come to expect accrue perceptual camouflage. This happens with our senses, like how we eventually stop hearing the sounds from the nearby highway—but it also happens with our emotions too.

We stop noticing our emotional state when it becomes expected, the way things just are. This is like when we're:

  • stressed for an entire year and not really be aware of it—until something changes in our emotional state.
  • employed for horrible work that we despise at first—but soon become numb to it.
  • sad for so long that we don't notice—until we start suddenly crying.
  • having a powerful emotional experience in a new hobby to be a powerful emotional experience—until it soon feels like everything else we do on a weekly basis.
  • angry all the time—until it eventually just seems the normal way we are

(and so on)  

It's harder to reflect on our emotional state when it's been consistent for a long while—which is what happened on a smaller scale with Riley, where the same amount of comfort slowly lead to less and less comfort over time.

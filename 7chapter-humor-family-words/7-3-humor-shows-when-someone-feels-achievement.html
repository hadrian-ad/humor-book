Humor marks a sense of achievement

An "objective" achievement and an emotion of achievement are different things: 

Zoe gets third place in her track meet. Emma leans over the fence to congratulate her, grinning. (←Emma feels achievement↔︎mistake is OK↔︎humor)

But Zoe feels disappointed. She gives a weak smile and puts her hands on her hips, frustrated. (←Zoe doesn’t feel achievement↔︎mistake is not OK↔︎no humor)

Emma thinks getting third is an objective achievement. But that doesn’t mean Zoe will subjectively experience it as achievement.

Consider:

We can use humor to identify who is and isn’t feeling achievement. When Zoe got third place, it was Emma who felt achievement (feels humor), and Zoe who doesn’t feel achievement (doesn't feel humor). Humor helps us track how people are experiencing things around them.

An "objective achievement" and an "emotion of achievement" are different things. They sometimes overlap, but don't always:

 

This is why people sometimes feel achievement at failure, and sometimes don't feel achievement at success:

Santiago: (amused) “Well. We lost every game this season. I feel like that’s some kind of achievement!”  (←Santiago feels achievement↔︎mistake is OK↔︎humor)

- OR -

Santiago: (bitter) “We lost every game this season. I feel that’s some kind of achievement.” (←Santiago doesn’t feels achievement↔︎mistake is not OK↔︎no humor)

Lina leaps up and pumps her fist. “Mom, mom!” she yells, thumping down the stairs. “I got into Pitt!” She jumps up and down and dances, glowing with happiness. (←Lina feels achievement↔︎mistake is OK↔︎humor)

- OR -

Lina opens her decision email. “Huh, I got in.” She tabs back to the game she’s playing. (←Lina doesn’t feel achievement↔︎mistake is not OK↔︎ no humor)

Stephen: *smiling* “I made $1.2 million in Q1.” (←Stephen feels achievement↔︎mistake is OK↔︎humor)

- OR -

Stephen checks a notification from his accountant—everything seems normal. He goes back to his work. (←Stephen doesn’t feel achievement↔︎no mistake means there’s nothing to match the OK↔︎no humor)

This is true for all our humor family words—like how people may feel realization when we think they "shouldn't" feel realization. ("How did you not know that?") 

Perspective and achievement. We can use mistakes and OKs to think about why someone does or doesn't feel achievement at a track meet.

When Zoe gets third place, her mistake is larger than her OK. She had expected to do better (large mistake), and felt that this had negative personal consequences to how she or others saw her (small OK).

But someone who gets fourth place in that contest may feel achievement (feel humor) if they did better than they expected (large mistake) and felt that this didn't have much risk of negative personal consequences (large OK).

This is why someone with a worse result (fourth place) can feel more achievement than someone with a better result (third place). We can use mistakes and OKs to think about this, because humor and achievement refer to the same emotion.

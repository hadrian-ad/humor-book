The Humor Book

Humor family words have intensity and duration

Humor has intensity and duration—and since humor family words are context-specific words for humor, humor family words also have intensity and duration:

Zoe is browsing the bookstore when she picks up a book on meditation. She reads the book title. “Wherever You Go, There You Are.” She thinks about that for a second, puzzled. Then something clicks.

She gives a nose exhales. (←Zoe feels brief, low-intensity realization)

- OR -

She smiles and laughs and feels good for the next ten minutes. (←Zoe feels long, high-intensity realization)

Lina gets an email that her college decision notification is ready. Her blood freezes. Heart pounding, she logs on to her account and clicks the ‘view decision update’ letter. Her mouth is dry while it loads. “Congratulations!” the letter says.

Lina grins, and goes back to finish her game. When she goes downstairs, she tells her mom and smiles as she congratulates her. (←Lina feels moderately-long, moderately-intense achievement)

- OR -

Lina leaps up and pumps her fist. “Mom, mom!” she yells, thumping down the stairs. “I got into Pitt!” She jumps up and down and dances, glowing with happiness. She’s flushed and delighted all evening. (←Lina feels long, high-intensity achievement)

Yasmin: *getting ready* (thinks) “I can’t believe I have to blow off Trevor just because Stephen and Tucker wants me to come into work this weekend. Tucker’s an entitled asshole, and Stephen thinks I’m like some kind of slave. God I hate this job. But I have to stick with it, because this is the only way I’ll ever be able to one day have a nice life. I have no choice!”

She stops to think about that, and then laughs. “Wow,” she says. “Maybe I should quit.”

She has a laughing fit, before dread comes back. (←Yasmin feels short, high-intensity realization)

- OR -

A weight falls off her, and she laughs again. She laughs on and off all morning. Why had this never occurred to her? (←Yasmin feels long, high-intensity realization)

Emma has been hunched over her laptop for six straight hours. She gives a start when Zoe starts massaging her shoulders.

Emma smiles appreciatively. (←Emma feels short, low-intensity appreciation and comfort)

- OR -

It feels so deeply good that Emma starts giggling.

Zoe: (concerned) “Emma, this is like massaging a boulder.”

Emma smiles and can’t stop exclaiming how good it feels, and keeps laughing—and she smiles for the rest of the hour. (←Emma feels long, high-intensity comfort)

Emma had been uncomfortable for so long that her discomfort had become a durable expectation—it was part of how she expected the world to be. The strength of this expectation controls how long she feels comfort.

Consider:

In all these examples, we can use whichever humor family words seem accurate. For instance, when Lina gets accepted, she doesn’t just feel achievement— we could also describe her humor as comfort, insight, generosity, relief, release, and so on.

Humor family words describe an emotional zone

These book uses an emotional model with three zones: the mistake-predominant zone; OK-predominant zone; and the mistake- and OK-balanced zone (humor):

Humor family words are what go into the balanced (humor) zone:

mistake-predominant emotions (afraid, ashamed, bummed, confused, excited, irritated, focused, grieving, jealous, perplexed, sad, stressed, terror, worried, and so on.)

balanced emotions (humor) (acceptance, accomplishment, achievement, appreciation, belonging, comfort, connection, delight, forgiveness, generosity, insight, joy, realization, reconciliation, release, revelation, relief, safety, success, welcome, and so on )

OK-predominant emotions  (apathetic, bored, calm, certain, content, convinced, detached, leisurely, listless, neutral, relaxed, stagnant, satisfied, tranquil, unexcited, and so on)

Our humor family words describe when the sizes of mistakes and OKs balance. This helps us see where these humor emotions are, and when, relative to other emotions.

We often associate humor with spoken eloquence—but eloquence isn't really related to most of the humor in our day-to-day lives:

  • a classmate can make us feel achievement by just saying “congratulations!”
  • a friend can make us feel belonging, welcome, and so on just by smiling at us
  • a romantic partner can make us feel safety just by holding our hands

These moments of humor didn’t require eloquence—since eloquence isn't as related to humor as we sometimes think.

Consider:

Through out this chapter, we saw examples that didn't use words at all and so couldn’t have involved spoken eloquence. When Jennifer laughs as she explores her resort room, eloquence was neither here nor there.

This is true even for things like apologies, which at first seem to rely on verbal sophistication. But when we stop to think about it, whether or not we feel reconciliation depends less on verbal sophistication and more on things like:

  • awareness
  • attention on the other person
  • honest disclosure
  • sincerity
  • understanding of the relevant topics

(and so on)

This is obvious with children, who can have “interpersonal eloquence” even though they can’t have much spoken eloquence:

Riley: *upset* “Mom? I’m sorry. I didn’t mean to break it. Maybe I can make a new one.” (←Riley conveys sincerity, honesty, understanding, and so on without spoken eloquence)

Jennifer: “Too bad! Not eloquent enough!” (←declined apology↔︎no reconciliation↔︎no humor)

- OR -

Jennifer: “Aw Riley. It’s okay.” (←reconciliation↔︎humor) *hugs her* “Let’s just be more careful next time, okay?”

Riley: *still upset* “Okay.” (←no reconciliation↔︎humor)

Jennifer: “No really. Things break sometime. Dad and I break things sometimes. It just teaches us to be more careful.”

Riley: *smiles* (←reconciliation↔︎humor) “Dad broke the computer!”

Jennifer: *smiles* (←reconciliation↔︎humor) “Remember that? And he learned to be more careful.”

The same thing applies to all our humor family words, where "interpersonal eloquence" is often more important than simple verbal eloquence:

Sam is examining his nearly bald head in the mirror. He rubs it thoughtfully. Jennifer sees him do this, and she grabs him by the arm.

Jennifer: *squeezing him* “You look so handsome.”

Sam gives a releasing series of small laughs, and leans against her affectionately. (←acceptance↔︎humor)

Jennifer could make Sam feel humor without a speech full of literary allusions and clever turns of phrase—since eloquence is neither here nor there for most of the humor in our lives.

In fact, the speech that makes us feel humor in our day-to-day lives is usually un-eloquent speech—either because clumsy speech is itself evaluated as mistake, or because humor degrades our ability to speak eloquently:

Owen: “What if we…” *laughs* “…shit all over the classroom?” *laughs harder*

Emma: *laughs* “Class, I’ve never read…” *laughs* “…such mush… before…”

Owen: *crying from laughter* “Such slippery prose…” (←our ability for verbal eloquence declines as our humor becomes more intense)

Owen and Emma: *whoop with laughter* (←eloquent speech is rarely relevant to feeling humor)

Owen: *telling joke* “So anyway, the priest walks into the bar…” *laughs* “… and he meets the pope…” *laughs harder* “… he meets the pope and…” *laughs harder* (←our ability for verbal eloquence declines as our humor becomes more intense)

Emma and Zoe laugh at Owen’s inability to tell the joke. (←eloquent speech is rarely relevant to feeling humor)

Zoe: *not enunciating* “So we should really matcha jamb colchially.” (confused) *pause*

Emma laughs. Zoe starts laughing too. (←eloquent speech is rarely relevant to feeling humor)

Emma: *laughing* “No, matcha jamb bolchially.”

Zoe: *laughing* “I said colchially, dammit!”

Emma: *laughing harder* “… colchially?!” (←our ability for verbal eloquence declines as our humor becomes more intense)

Sam walks into the kitchen and puts a hand on Jennifer’s shoulder. He sees her bagel without cream cheese.

Sam: (surprised) “Oh, it’s the thing.”

Jennifer: *laughs* “Well, I wouldn’t just... you know…” (←eloquent speech is rarely relevant to feeling humor)

Sam: *laughs* *sits down* “Of course not!”

Jennifer: *amused* “One of these days…” *gestures*

Sam: *smiles* (ironically wistful) “One day…” (←most interactions aren't verbally eloquent, especially as we feel more humor)

All this means that improving our skill at humor isn’t really about improving verbal eloquence—most of the time it's about improving something else. (Interpersonal eloquence.)

It also means that we can have excellent humor skills whether or not we feel eloquent—since most humor isn't about eloquence.

As we start to see achievement, connection, generosity, and so on as humor, it gets easier to think about how everyone has ways of being good at humor—since the only route to humor doesn't involve magically becoming more eloquent.

The word "humor" is generic, and doesn't imply any context. To think narratively about humor, we can bundle humor and context together by using humor family words. This lets us approach humor with more ordinary language.

  • We'll use a standardized list of humor family words in this book: acceptance, accomplishment, achievement, appreciation, belonging, comfort, connection, delight, forgiveness, generosity, insight, joy, realization, reconciliation, release, revelation, relief, safety, success, and welcome. 
  • Humor family words are interchangeable with the word "humor." That means humor family words and humor have the same qualities as humor—including intensity and duration.
  • Humor family words are subjective. Whether or not we feel achievement or comfort doesn't depend on any technical metrics—it depends on our individual evaluations of mistakes and OKs.

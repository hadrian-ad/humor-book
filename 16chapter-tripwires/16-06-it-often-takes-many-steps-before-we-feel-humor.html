It can take several steps to reach tripwires

Humor is something we develop a situation toward. Sometimes this happens quickly, in what seems like a single step—other times it happens more slowly, over what seems like many steps:

In both cases above, humor happens after time. Even when humor happens quickly, it's not instantaneous.

It's obvious it sometimes takes a while to reach a humor outcome—like how we can't always support an argument in one sentence. That's because the person we’re talking with may:

  • disagree with us at first
  • not understand the argument
  • ask for clarification
  • need more context
  • give a counterpoint
  • take time to think it over

(and so on)

To push them into a humor outcome, we have to be aware of how and why their tripwire is guarded, and be responsive to those guards. It’s only after a shared development of the emotional situation that they’ll feel we’ve supported our argument:

Trevor: *playing StormConQuest* (frustrated) “I can’t beat the Ogre Time Magistrate. I’ve been trying for hours. It’s just not possible.”

Yasmin: “Did you buy armor potions?” (←Yasmin tries to support an argument)

Trevor: *pause* (confused) “Do magic arrows do physical damage?” (←Trevor doesn’t feel Yasmin has supported an argument)

Yasmin: *smiles* “Half physical, half magical.” (←Yasmin tries to support an argument)

Trevor: *laughs* “Fuuuck me.” (←Trevor feels Yasmin has supported an argument)

Yasmin: *pained* “So why do you think sixteen-year-olds should be able to vote?”

Trevor: “Because why not?” (←Trevor tries to support an argument)

Yasmin: “Because they have no life experience! Their brains aren’t developed!” (←Yasmin doesn’t feel Trevor has supported an argument↔︎no humor)

Trevor: “But we let adults with intellectual disabilities vote, right? So it’s not really about development.” (←Trevor tries to support an argument)

Yasmin: “But they have no life experience.” (←Yasmin doesn’t feel Trevor has supported an argument↔︎no humor)

Trevor: “But that’s arbitrary? Some dude who’s sixty would say that about someone our age.”

Yasmin: *laughs* (←Yasmin feels Trevor has supported an argument) *shakes head in frustration* “Where have you been hearing all this, Trevor?”

It’s the same for all humor outcomes, like easing tension, handling complaints, creating rapport, and so on. Sometimes, we reach these outcomes quickly—but other times, we have to develop the situation that way over initial guardedness.

If we thought a humor attempt had to be successful from the first sentence, we’d be playing a game with the wrong rules—and we’d often struggle to support arguments, handle complaints, create rapport, and so on:

Humor takes some situational assembly—which is why it’s often helpful to think of humor as the conclusion that we work toward, rather than beginning that starts something.

That's the end of this chapter! Next, we'll look at the "appropriateness" of humor.

We can use each humor outcomes as a method of generating humor. So if we ask: "How do I generate humor?" we can answer ourselves with a humor outcome:

  • “How do I generate humor? By supporting an argument”
  • "How do I generate humor? By revealing personal views"
  • "How do I generate humor? By helping someone become accepted"

(and so on)

This lets us take an abstract question ("how do I generate humor?") and downsize it into something tangible—since we already have an idea on how to support arguments and reveal personal views.

Loosely, we have two different approaches when thinking about generating humor:

  • An emotion-first approach, which treats a humor outcome as a consequence of humor. ("If they laugh, they'll reach a humor outcome.")
  • An outcome-first approach, which treats humor as a consequence of a humor outcome. ("If they reach a humor outcome, they'll laugh")

These two approaches let us talk about the same thing in different ways:

  • Emotion-first: “If he laughs, I’ll have handled his criticism”
  • Outcome-first: “If I handle his criticism, he’ll laugh”
  • Emotion-first: “If I feel humor, I’ll have dealt with a taboo topic”
  • Outcome-first: “If I deal with a taboo topic, I’ll feel humor”
  • Emotion-first: “If they feel humor, I’ll have eased their tensions.”
  • Outcome-first: “If they ease their tensions, they’ll feel humor.”

Essentially, this is like solving X = Y. We can solve the same equation by approaching either end. In our lives, we tend to be most familiar with emotion-first thinking—which is a bit like thinking we could only ever solve for X.

But when we realize we can also solve for Y, we'll have much greater flexibility in how we think about different situations.

For instance:

If we're welcoming a new person to our team, we know we'll want them to smile or laugh. If we frame our question as "how can we make them smile or laugh" (emotion-first), we'll call to mind a set of things like jokes, funny voices, goofy icebreakers, and so on.

Alternatively, if we frame our question as "how can we symbolize close ties" (outcome-first), this calls to mind a different set of things—maybe a thoughtful gift, individualized time, hospitality, displays of equality, and so on.

By reframing the question to center around humor outcomes, we can think beyond our usual associations to other things that still produce smiles and laughs when done successfully.

The point of all this isn't that there are "two ways" of going about things—it's instead to help jog our thinking into new perspectives how we go about making emotional actions in our lives.

It's also a way of thinking about how we can find synergies from a network of humor skills: since all humor outcomes are connected, improving at supporting arguments, revealing personal views, and so on improves our skill at humor—and improving our skill at humor improves our ability to support arguments, reveal personal views, and so on.

In this sense, a rising tide lifts all boats—improving at humor makes us better at all these different humor outcomes, especially as we gain greater awareness to the sources and breadth of humor.

Humor has triggering conditions. We can think about these conditions narratively with humor outcomes, including using a spatial metaphor to think about how a situation does or doesn't develop toward these triggering conditions.

  • Our evaluations are shaped by the direct and indirect participation of everyone. That means that everyone has a role in whether or not humor outcomes are reached, and on what topics.
  • We can generate humor by simply focusing on reaching a humor outcomes—whether or not this strikes as as stereotypically "funny."
  • Humor outcomes are something a situation develops toward. This means we sometimes have to take time working toward them. It's sometimes helpful to think of humor as more as a conclusion or destination than as a beginning.

The Humor Book

Focusing on reaching humor outcomes

We can generate humor without even thinking about “humor.”

When Sam focuses entirely on symbolizing close ties with Jennifer, he's also focusing on generating humor:

Sam: “I love you.” (←Sam tries to symbolize close ties)

Jennifer: *smiles* (←Jennifer feels Sam has symbolized close ties↔︎humor) “I love you too!”

Jennifer is about to head out to the grocery store.

Sam: *gets up* “You know what. I’ll come with you!”

Jennifer: “Oh, you don’t have to.”

Sam: *smiling* “Well I want to.” (←Sam tries to symbolize close ties)

Jennifer: *smiles* (←Jennifer feels Sam has symbolized close ties↔︎humor) *puts on coat*

Sam: “Remember when we went to Chicago?” (←Sam tries to symbolize close ties)

Jennifer: *smiles* “What made you think of that?” (←Jennifer feels Sam has symbolized close ties↔︎humor)

Sam generates humor in Jennifer—he helps her feel acceptance, connection, comfort, and so on.

Consider:

Sam didn't do this by focusing on funny voices, jokes, puns, silly dances, wit, or so on—instead, Sam just focuses on symbolizing close ties. If he successfully reaches this outcome with Jennifer, she feels humor.

Similarly, Jessica can make customers laugh without involving anything she usually associates with humor—she can instead just focus on reaching humor outcomes:

Jessica: *to a customer* *setting down condiments* “Oh, I love your style!” (←Jessica goes straight toward humor outcome tripwires)

Yasmin: *turns red* *brightens* *beams* “Oh. Thanks!” (←Yasmin feels Jessica’s revealed a personal view, changed her negative emotions into positive ones, supported an argument, created rapport, and so on)

Jessica made Yasmin feel humor without needing to use jokes or even think about humor. That's because a compliment pushes Yasmin directly toward humor outcomes.

Consider:

When we realize we can use the outcome we want to reach as its own guide for how to get there, we exponentially broaden the methods we have available to generate humor.

Associations. Sometimes we have such strong associations with certain methods used to generate humor (jokes, roasts, slapstick, so on) that our thinking becomes bound by them. To loosen our thinking, we can see those things as middlemen.

We can appreciate live roasts when we understand them as, on balance, vehicles of symbolizing close ties or acceptably criticizing. But, of course, if we want to symbolize close ties with our partner, we don't have to have the middleman of a roast—we can just symbolize close ties.

Learning to see the "middleman" aspect to formats helps us see them more clearly, and not confuse a means for the ends.
